## üìù Understanding Mistake as a Defense

### ‚öñÔ∏è Definition of Mistake

**Literal Meaning**: 
Mistake means **commission or omission of an act ignorantly or unintentionally** causing injury.

**Legal Meaning**:
- Unconscious ignorance or forgetfulness of a fact
- Past or present fact material to the act
- A belief about circumstances that is incorrect

**Nature of Defense**:
- Mistake is one of the **defenses available** to accused
- Gets exemption from criminal liability
- Negates the existence of particular intent or foresight
- Law looks at **believed facts**, not real facts

### üîë Fundamental Maxim

**"Ignorantia facti excusat, ignorantia juris non-excusat"**

**Meaning**: 
- **Ignorance of fact excuses**
- **Ignorance of law does NOT excuse**

**Rationale**:
- Person cannot be blamed for not knowing facts
- But everyone is presumed to know the law
- "Ignorance of law is no excuse"

---

## üìñ Section 14 BNS: Act Done by Person Bound or Justified by Law

### ‚öñÔ∏è Provision (Previously Section 76 IPC)

**Section 14 BNS**: "Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who is, or who by reason of a **mistake of fact** and not by reason of a mistake of law in **good faith** believes that he is, **bound by law** to do it."

### üß© Essential Ingredients

#### **1Ô∏è‚É£ Mistake of Fact**

**Requirement**: The person must act by reason of **mistake of fact**.

**NOT Mistake of Law**: Section specifically excludes mistake of law.

**Key Point**: Person must be mistaken about **factual circumstances**, not about legal provisions.

#### **2Ô∏è‚É£ Good Faith**

**Requirement**: The act must be done in **good faith**.

**Definition (Section 2(10) BNS)**:
"A thing is said to be done in **good faith** where it is in fact done honestly, whether it is done negligently or not."

**Good Faith Requires**:
- Honesty of intention
- **Due care and attention**
- Acting as a reasonable person would

**Test**: Even if person is honest, must act with due care and caution. Degree of reasonableness in care exercised.

#### **3Ô∏è‚É£ Belief of Being Bound by Law**

**Requirement**: Person believes himself **bound by law** to do the act.

**"Bound by law"** means:
- Person thinks he is under **legal compulsion**
- Legal duty or legal obligation to do the act
- Must do it as required by law

**Examples**:
- Police officer executing warrant
- Public servant carrying out lawful order
- Person obeying court order

---

### üìñ Illustration from Section 14

**Illustration 1**:
- A, a soldier, fires on a mob by order of his superior officer in conformity with commands of law
- A has committed no offense

**Analysis**:
- A believes he is **bound by law** to follow superior's lawful order
- Act done in good faith
- Even if order later found unlawful (mistake of fact), A protected

**Illustration 2**:
- A, a public servant, seizes property believing he is bound by law to seize it
- Later found property should not have been seized (mistake about facts)
- If A acted in good faith, he is protected

---

## üìñ Section 17 BNS: Act Which a Person is Justified by Law in Doing

### ‚öñÔ∏è Provision (Previously Section 79 IPC)

**Section 17 BNS**: "Nothing is an offence which is done by any person who is **justified by law**, or who by reason of a **mistake of fact** and not by reason of a mistake of law in **good faith** believes himself to be justified by law, in doing it."

### üß© Essential Ingredients

#### **1Ô∏è‚É£ Mistake of Fact** (Same as Section 14)

The person must act by reason of mistake of fact, not mistake of law.

#### **2Ô∏è‚É£ Good Faith** (Same as Section 14)

The act must be done in good faith with due care and attention.

#### **3Ô∏è‚É£ Belief of Being Justified by Law**

**Requirement**: Person believes himself **justified by law** in doing the act.

**"Justified by law"** means:
- Person thinks there is **legal justification** for his action
- Not that he is under compulsion, but that he has legal right to do it
- Act is legally permissible or authorized

**Examples**:
- Person exercising right of private defense
- Parent disciplining child (within legal limits)
- Person making citizen's arrest

---

### üìñ Illustration from Section 17

**Illustration 1**:
- A, under influence of madness, attempts to kill B
- B, in exercise of right of private defense, kills A
- B believed justified by law in killing to protect himself
- B has committed no offense

**Illustration 2**:
- A sees two persons fighting
- A separates them believing he is justified by law to prevent breach of peace
- Even if A was mistaken about circumstances, if acted in good faith, protected

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è Distinction: Section 14 vs Section 17

### üìä Comparative Analysis

| **Aspect** | **Section 14 BNS (Sec 76 IPC)** | **Section 17 BNS (Sec 79 IPC)** |
|------------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Key Phrase** | "**Bound by law**" | "**Justified by law**" |
| **Belief** | Under legal **compulsion** | Legal **justification** exists |
| **Nature** | Legal **duty** to do act | Legal **right** to do act |
| **Examples** | Police executing warrant, soldier following order | Private defense, preventing breach of peace |
| **Mindset** | "I must do this by law" | "I may do this by law" |
| **Obligation** | Mandatory compliance | Permissive authorization |

### üîë Common Elements

**Similarities Between Section 14 and 17**:
1. Both require **mistake of fact** (not mistake of law)
2. Both require **good faith**
3. Both provide complete immunity from criminal liability
4. Both look at **believed circumstances**, not actual circumstances

### üéØ Key Difference

**Section 14**: Person believes himself **bound by law** (thinks he **must** do it)

**Section 17**: Person believes himself **justified by law** (thinks he **may** do it)

## üìä 3 Conditions for Mistake Defense

For an accused to get protection on ground of mistake, **three conditions** must be fulfilled:

### **1Ô∏è‚É£ State of Things Believed Would Justify Act**

**Condition**: The state of things believed to exist would, **if true**, have justified the act done.

**Meaning**: If facts were as accused believed them to be, act would have been lawful.

**Example**:
- A believes Z is a burglar attacking
- A uses force to defend (actually Z was innocent passerby)
- **IF** Z had actually been burglar, force would be justified
- Condition satisfied

### **2Ô∏è‚É£ Mistake Must Be Reasonable**

**Condition**: The mistake must be **reasonable** in the circumstances.

**Meaning**: A reasonable person in accused's position would have made same mistake.

**Test**: 
- Not what accused thought
- But what a **reasonable person** would have thought in same circumstances

**Example of Unreasonable Mistake**:
- A sees B in distance and thinks B is thief
- Without checking or reasonable grounds, A shoots B
- This is unreasonable - A should have verified

### **3Ô∏è‚É£ Mistake Must Relate to Fact, Not Law**

**Condition**: The mistake must relate to **fact**, not to **law**.

**Meaning**: 
- Ignorance of fact excuses
- Ignorance of law does NOT excuse

**Why Law is Excluded**:
- If mistake of law allowed, every accused would claim ignorance
- Difficult for prosecution to prove accused knew the law
- Would encourage ignorance of law
- Principle: "Everyone is presumed to know the law"

---

## üî¥ Mistake of Fact vs ‚ö™ Mistake of Law

### **Mistake of Fact**

**Definition**: Mistake about **factual circumstances** or situation.

**Legal Effect**: **CAN be a defense** if conditions satisfied.

**Examples**:
1. Person takes someone else's property thinking it is his own
2. Police officer arrests wrong person believing he is the accused
3. Person believes spouse is dead (actually alive) and remarries

### **Mistake of Law**

**Definition**: Mistake about **what the law is** or **legal consequences**.

**Legal Effect**: **NOT a defense** (general rule).

**Maxim**: "Ignorantia juris non excusat" - Ignorance of law is no excuse.

**Examples**:
1. Person thinks bigamy is not crime (mistake about law - NO defense)
2. Person believes certain act is legal but it is not (mistake of law - NO defense)
3. Person misunderstands legal provision (mistake of law - NO defense)

---

### üìå 4 Exceptions: When Mistake of Law CAN Be Defense

In **limited circumstances**, mistake of law can serve as defense:

#### **Exception 1: Law Not Published**

**When**: The law under which person is charged was **not published**.

**Rationale**: Cannot expect person to know unpublished law.

**Case**: **R v. Bailey** - Accused was away at coast of Africa when statute passed. Could not have known about it. Court held him guilty, but this was criticized. Modern law recognizes this as valid defense.

#### **Exception 2: Statute Later Overturned or Held Unconstitutional**

**When**: Person relied upon statute that was **later declared unconstitutional or overturned**.

**Rationale**: Person cannot be blamed for following what appeared to be valid law at time.

#### **Exception 3: Relied Upon Judicial Decision**

**When**: Person relied upon **judicial decision** interpreting the law.

**Rationale**: Court's interpretation of law creates reasonable belief about what law is.

#### **Exception 4: Relied Upon Official Interpretation**

**When**: Person relied upon interpretation by **appropriate official** (e.g., government officer, legal advisor).

**Rationale**: Reasonable to rely on official guidance about what law requires.

## üèõÔ∏è Important Case Laws

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **R v. Tolson** (1889) - Leading Case on Mistake of Fact

#### **Facts**:
- Appellant married in September 1880
- Husband went missing in December 1881
- She was told husband was on ship that was **lost at sea**
- **Seven years later**, believing husband dead, she **married another man**
- First husband turned up sometime after second marriage
- Charged with offense of **bigamy**

#### **Issue**:
Whether she could claim defense of mistake of fact?

#### **Held**:
**NOT GUILTY** - She was afforded defense of mistake.

#### **Reasoning**:
1. She believed in good faith that her first husband was dead
2. It was **reasonable in the circumstances** to believe he was dead
   - Seven years had passed
   - Told ship was lost at sea
   - No word from husband for 7 years
3. This was **mistake of fact** (believed he was dead)
4. NOT mistake of law (she knew bigamy was illegal)
5. If her belief had been true (if he was actually dead), second marriage would have been lawful

#### **Principle Established**:
**Reasonable mistake of fact** can be defense if:
- Belief was honest and reasonable
- If facts were as believed, act would be lawful
- Act done in good faith

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **R v. Prince** (1875) - Mistake Must Be Reasonable

#### **Facts**:
- Prince took a girl **below 16 years** of age
- Took her **without consent of parents**
- Believed the girl was **above 16 years**
- This is offense in England under Offences Against Persons Act, 1861

#### **Issue**:
Whether mistake about girl's age was defense?

#### **Held**:
**CONVICTED** - Mistake not a defense.

#### **Reasoning**:
1. Prince did NOT act in **good faith**
2. He **failed to make enquiries** to find out actual age of girl
3. A reasonable person would have verified girl's age before taking her
4. His mistake was **unreasonable**
5. Even though no mens rea for specific age element, convicted

#### **Principle Established**:
Mistake defense requires:
- **Reasonable mistake** (not just any mistake)
- **Due care and attention** must be exercised
- Cannot claim mistake if failed to verify facts

**Distinction from Tolson**:
- **Tolson**: Reasonable to believe husband dead after 7 years, no way to verify
- **Prince**: Unreasonable not to verify girl's age when possible

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **R v. Bailey** - Law Not Published

#### **Facts**:
- Accused was away at coast of Africa
- A statute was passed by British Parliament
- Accused could not have known by any means about passing of statute
- Charged under that statute

#### **Held**:
Court held him **guilty**, disallowing his plea of want of knowledge of law.

#### **Criticism**:
This decision has been criticized. Modern law recognizes that:
- If law is not published or not accessible, person cannot be expected to know it
- This should be exception to "ignorance of law is no excuse"

#### **Principle**:
Generally, ignorance of law is no excuse, BUT unpublished law creates difficulty.

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ **R v. Wheat and Stock** - Mistake of Law Not Excuse

#### **Facts**:
- Accused was **illiterate**
- He was **misinformed** that he had been granted divorce
- Subsequently he remarried
- His first wife charged him for **bigamy**

#### **Held**:
Court held him **guilty** of bigamy and convicted him.

#### **Reasoning**:
1. This was **mistake of law**, not mistake of fact
2. He was mistaken about his **legal status** (whether divorced or not)
3. Ignorance of law is no excuse
4. Even though he was illiterate and misinformed, no defense

#### **Distinction from Tolson**:
- **Tolson**: Mistake of **fact** (believed husband was dead) - Defense available
- **Wheat**: Mistake of **law** (believed he was divorced) - NO defense

#### **Principle**:
**Mistake of law is NOT a defense**, even if:
- Person is illiterate
- Person was misinformed by others
- Person genuinely believed he had legal right

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ **State of West Bengal v. Shew Mangal Singh** - Good Faith Defense

#### **Facts**:
- Subordinate police officer opened fire
- Acted in pursuance of **orders of Commissioner of Police**
- Firing caused death of some persons
- Police personnel fired on mob by order of superior officer

#### **Issue**:
Whether subordinate officer protected by Section 76 (now Section 14 BNS)?

#### **Held**:
Supreme Court held:
- If order of superior officer was **justified and lawful**, no further enquiry needed
- Officer acted in **conformity with commands of law**
- Officer acted in **good faith**
- Section 76 (now Section 14) protection available

#### **Reasoning**:
1. Officer believed he was **bound by law** to follow superior's order
2. Act done in **good faith**
3. Even if mistaken about circumstances, protected if acted honestly with due care

#### **Principle Established**:
**Good faith requires**:
1. **Honesty** of intention
2. **Due care and attention**
3. Three factors for due care:
   - Nature of act committed
   - Magnitude and importance of act
   - Facility person has for exercise of care and attention

---

## üìã Good Faith: Essential Element

### ‚öñÔ∏è Definition of Good Faith

**Section 2(10) BNS** (Previously Section 52 IPC):

"Nothing is said to be done or believed in **good faith** which is done or believed without **due care and attention**."

### üîë Requirements of Good Faith

#### **1Ô∏è‚É£ Honesty**

- Must act with honest intention
- Not with malice or bad motive
- Genuine belief in righteousness of act

#### **2Ô∏è‚É£ Due Care and Attention**

**"Due Care"** means:
- Degree of **reasonableness** in care exercised
- Not utmost care, but **sufficient care**
- What a **prudent and reasonable person** would consider adequate

**Three Factors for Due Care**:

1. **Nature of Act**:
   - Seriousness of act
   - Consequences of act
   - Type of act being done

2. **Magnitude and Importance**:
   - How important is the act?
   - What is at stake?
   - Level of caution required

3. **Facility for Exercise of Care**:
   - What means does person have to verify?
   - What opportunity to exercise caution?
   - What is reasonably possible in circumstances?

### ‚ùå What is NOT Good Faith

**Good faith does NOT exist if**:
- Act done with malice or ill-will
- Act done with recklessness or gross negligence
- No attempt made to verify facts when verification was possible
- Act done with knowledge that it might be wrong

### **Examples with Analysis**

#### **Example 1: Protected by Mistake Defense**

**Scenario**:
- A is a police officer
- A receives information that B (wanted criminal) is in house C
- A goes to house C with warrant for B's arrest
- A arrests person thinking he is B
- Later found person was innocent D, who looks like B

**Analysis**:
- ‚úÖ Mistake of **fact** (mistaken identity)
- ‚úÖ **Reasonable** mistake (D looked like B)
- ‚úÖ Done in **good faith** (A verified information, had warrant)
- ‚úÖ If D had actually been B, arrest would be lawful
- ‚úÖ A believed he was **bound by law** to execute warrant

**Result**: A protected by **Section 14 BNS** - NO criminal liability

---

#### **Example 2: NOT Protected**

**Scenario**:
- A sees B walking on road at night
- A thinks B looks suspicious
- Without any verification or reasonable grounds, A attacks B thinking B is thief
- B is actually innocent person walking home

**Analysis**:
- ‚úÖ Mistake of fact (mistaken identity)
- ‚ùå **NOT reasonable** mistake (no grounds to believe B was thief)
- ‚ùå NOT done in **good faith** (no due care - should have verified)
- ‚ùå Even if B had been thief, A had no right to attack without attempting arrest

**Result**: A **NOT protected** - Liable for assault

---

#### **Example 3: Mistake of Law - NO Defense**

**Scenario**:
- A is getting married
- A believes that if he registers marriage, divorce from previous wife not needed
- A marries B without divorcing first wife
- Charged with bigamy

**Analysis**:
- ‚ùå Mistake of **law** (mistaken about legal effect of registration)
- Not mistake of fact (knew he was still married to first wife)
- Ignorance of law is no excuse

**Result**: A **NOT protected** - Guilty of bigamy

## üìö Summary

**Mistake** can be defense to criminal liability if:
- Relates to **fact** (not law)
- Is **reasonable**
- Done in **good faith** (honesty + due care)
- If facts were as believed, act would be lawful

**Sections**:
- **Section 14**: Bound by law (legal compulsion)
- **Section 17**: Justified by law (legal justification)

**Maxim**: "Ignorance of fact excuses, ignorance of law does not excuse"

**Key Cases**:
- **Tolson**: Reasonable mistake of fact = Defense
- **Prince**: Unreasonable mistake = NO defense
- **Wheat**: Mistake of law = NO defense

**Good Faith**: Requires honesty AND due care based on:
- Nature of act
- Magnitude and importance
- Facility to exercise care
