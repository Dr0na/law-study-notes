**Q1-CVM-10M:** Explain the concept of "sapinda relationship" under Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. How is it calculated?

#### **INTRODUCTION**

Sapinda relationship is a crucial concept under Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, governing marriage prohibitions based on degrees of blood relationship. Defined under Section 3(f) and made a condition for valid marriage under Section 5(iv), sapinda relationship prevents marriages between persons related within certain degrees, thereby safeguarding against consanguineous marriages.

---

### **DEFINITION [Section 3(f)]**

Section 3(f) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 defines sapinda relationship as follows:

"Two persons are said to be 'sapindas' of each other if one is a lineal ascendant of the other within the limits of sapinda relationship, or if they have a common lineal ascendant who is within the limits of sapinda relationship with reference to each of them."

**Limits Specified:**
- **3 degrees** through father (excluding first generation)
- **5 degrees** through mother (excluding first generation)

---

### **MEANING AND ORIGIN**

**Etymology:**
"Sapinda" is Sanskrit word meaning:
- "Sa" = same/common
- "Pinda" = body/particles
- Literally: "Connected by particles of same body"

**Religious Significance:**
- In Hindu tradition, pinda (rice balls) offered during funeral ceremonies (shraddha)
- Persons offering pinda to same ancestor are sapindas
- Traditionally determined funeral rite participation

---

### **HOW TO CALCULATE SAPINDA DEGREES**

**Rule of Calculation:**
1. **Exclude first generation** (self and immediate parent)
2. **Count upward** to ascertain degrees

---

### **CALCULATION THROUGH FATHER'S LINE (3 Degrees)**

**Step-by-Step:**

```
Self (A)
‚Üì
Father (EXCLUDED - First Generation)
‚Üì
Grandfather (1st Degree)
‚Üì
Great-grandfather (2nd Degree)
‚Üì
Great-great-grandfather (3rd Degree)
```

**Within 3 Degrees Through Father:**
- A and Grandfather = Sapindas (1 degree)
- A and Great-grandfather = Sapindas (2 degrees)
- A and Great-great-grandfather = Sapindas (3 degrees)
- A and Great-great-great-grandfather = NOT sapindas (exceeds 3 degrees)

---

### **CALCULATION THROUGH MOTHER'S LINE (5 Degrees)**

**Step-by-Step:**

```
Self (A)
‚Üì
Mother (EXCLUDED - First Generation)
‚Üì
Maternal Grandfather (1st Degree)
‚Üì
Mat. Great-grandfather (2nd Degree)
‚Üì
Mat. GG-grandfather (3rd Degree)
‚Üì
Mat. GGG-grandfather (4th Degree)
‚Üì
Mat. GGGG-grandfather (5th Degree)
```

**Within 5 Degrees Through Mother:**
- A and Maternal Grandfather = Sapindas (1 degree)
- A and up to 5th degree ancestor = Sapindas
- Beyond 5 degrees = NOT sapindas

---

### **PRACTICAL EXAMPLES**

**Example 1:**
Can A (male) marry his father's sister (paternal aunt)?

**Calculation:**
```
A ‚Üí Father (excluded) ‚Üí Grandfather (1) ‚Üí Grandfather's children include father and aunt
```
Father's sister is within 3 degrees through father = **Sapinda relationship**
**Answer:** NO, unless custom permits.

**Example 2:**
Can A marry his mother's mother (maternal grandmother)?

**Calculation:**
```
A ‚Üí Mother (excluded) ‚Üí Maternal Grandmother (1)
```
Within 5 degrees through mother = **Sapinda relationship**
**Answer:** NO, unless custom permits.

**Example 3:**
Can A marry his cousin (father's brother's daughter)?

**Calculation:**
```
A ‚Üí Father (excluded) ‚Üí Grandfather (1) ‚Üí Uncle ‚Üí Cousin
```
Within 3 degrees = **Sapinda relationship**
**Answer:** NO, unless custom permits.

---

### **PROHIBITION UNDER SECTION 5(iv)**

**Statutory Provision:**
"The parties are not sapindas of each other, unless the custom or usage governing each of them permits of a marriage between the two"

**Effect:**
Marriage between sapindas is **VOID** under Section 11.

---

### **EXCEPTION - CUSTOM**

**Custom May Permit:**

In certain communities, marriages within sapinda relationship are permitted by custom.

**Example: South Indian Custom**
- Cross-cousin marriages (mother's brother's daughter)
- Traditionally acceptable
- Custom must be proved

**Requirements for Valid Custom:**
- Must be ancient, certain, reasonable
- Continuous and uniform observance
- Must apply to both parties
- Burden of proof on party claiming custom

**Case Law:**
**Veerappa Setty v. Veeramma (AIR 1955 Mad 291):**
Custom permitting sapinda marriage must be strictly proved.

---

### **WHY 3 AND 5 DEGREES?**

**Historical Rationale:**

**Father's Line (3 Degrees):**
- Agnatic (male line) succession important
- 3 generations sufficient for property purposes
- Religious rituals performed up to 3 generations

**Mother's Line (5 Degrees):**
- Maternal relationship given wider recognition
- Acknowledges maternal ancestry importance
- Extended family includes maternal relations more broadly

**Genetic Rationale:**
Sufficient genetic distance to minimize risk of genetic disorders.

---

### **CASE LAWS**

**Madhavrao v. Raghavendrarao (AIR 1946 SC 33):**
- Sapinda relationship calculated strictly per Section 3(f)
- Marriage within prohibited sapinda degrees is void
- No relaxation without valid custom

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

Sapinda relationship under Hindu Marriage Act represents an important prohibition based on degrees of blood relationship. The 3-degree rule through father and 5-degree rule through mother, calculated by excluding the first generation, creates a clear mathematical formula for determining prohibited marriages. This concept, rooted in Hindu religious tradition of pinda offerings, serves modern purposes of preventing consanguineous marriages and genetic disorders while maintaining family and social order. The exception for custom acknowledges regional diversity, particularly South Indian cross-cousin marriage traditions, while requiring strict proof to prevent abuse. Understanding sapinda calculation is essential for determining validity of Hindu marriages.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 2

**Q2-CVM-10M:** Discuss the age requirement for a valid Hindu marriage and consequences of violation.

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

#### **INTRODUCTION**

Section 5(iii) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 prescribes minimum age requirements for parties to a Hindu marriage. This provision, aimed at preventing child marriages and ensuring maturity of consent, mandates that bridegroom must have completed 21 years and bride must have completed 18 years at the time of marriage. Violation of this condition renders marriage voidable, and additional penal consequences follow under the Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006.

---

### **SECTION 5(iii): STATUTORY PROVISION**

**Text:**
"The bridegroom has completed the age of twenty-one years and the bride, the age of eighteen years at the time of marriage"

**Requirements:**
- **Bridegroom (male):** Must have completed 21 years (attained 21st birthday)
- **Bride (female):** Must have completed 18 years (attained 18th birthday)
- Age calculated at **time of marriage**

**Recent Developments:**
Legislative proposals to increase bride's minimum age to 21 years to ensure:
- Gender parity
- Educational opportunities
- Career development
- Informed consent

---

### **RATIONALE FOR AGE REQUIREMENT**

**1. Maturity of Consent:**
- Ensure parties capable of understanding marriage implications
- Informed decision-making
- Psychological maturity

**2. Physical Readiness:**
- Physical maturity for marital relations
- Safe childbearing age for women
- Health considerations

**3. Educational Development:**
- Complete basic education
- Career planning
- Economic independence

**4. Prevention of Exploitation:**
- Protect minors from forced marriages
- Prevent child marriages
- Safeguard vulnerable children

**5. Constitutional Values:**
- Article 15(3): Special provision for children
- Article 21: Right to life and dignity
- Article 45: Education for children

---

### **CONSEQUENCES OF VIOLATION**

#### **A. UNDER HINDU MARRIAGE ACT**

**Effect on Marriage:**
Marriage is **VOIDABLE** under Section 12(1)(c).

**Who Can Seek Annulment:**
Only the **minor party** (party who was underage at marriage).

**Time Limit:**
Within **2 years** of attaining majority age (i.e., before turning 20 for bride, 23 for groom).

**Procedure:**
- File petition for decree of nullity
- Court satisfied that age requirement violated
- Decree of nullity passed

---

#### **B. PROHIBITION OF CHILD MARRIAGE ACT, 2006**

**Additional Provisions:**

**1. Voidable Nature:**
- Child marriage voidable at option of contracting party who was child
- Can petition before attaining majority, OR
- Within 2 years after attaining majority

**2. Penal Consequences:**

**For Adult Male:**
- If male adult (above 18) marries minor: **2 years imprisonment** and/or **fine up to ‚Çπ1 lakh**

**For Person Performing Marriage:**
- Person performing, conducting, directing, or abetting child marriage
- **2 years imprisonment** and/or **fine up to ‚Çπ1 lakh**

**For Parents/Guardians:**
- Promoting or permitting child marriage
- **2 years imprisonment** and fine

**3. Child Marriage Prohibition Officers:**
- Appointed to prevent child marriages
- Powers to stop child marriages
- Can file complaints

---

#### **C. REGISTRATION CONSEQUENCES**

**Difficulty in Registration:**
- Marriage registrars require age proof
- Many states refuse registration if age requirement not met
- Acts as practical deterrent

---

### **PROOF OF AGE**

**Acceptable Documents:**
1. **Birth Certificate** (most reliable)
2. **School Leaving Certificate**
3. **Passport**
4. **Aadhaar Card**
5. **Affidavit** (if documents unavailable)
6. **Medical Examination:**
   - Ossification test
   - Radiological examination
   - Dental examination

**Burden of Proof:**
- On party alleging underage to prove it
- If challenged, respondent must prove age

**Case Law:**
**Smt. Shewanti v. State of Haryana (2014):**
Medical test (ossification test) can determine age when documents unavailable.

---

### **BARS TO ANNULMENT**

**Time Limit:**
Strict 2-year limit after majority.

**Approbation:**
If minor party, after attaining majority:
- Lives with other party
- Affirms marriage
- Has children
May be deemed to have accepted marriage.

**Rationale:**
Prevents indefinite uncertainty about marriage status.

---

### **SECTION 16: LEGITIMACY OF CHILDREN**

**Important Protection:**

Even if child marriage annulled, children born are **legitimate** under Section 16 if:
- Conceived before decree
- Parties didn't know facts making marriage void/voidable

**Rights:**
Children can claim:
- Maintenance
- Succession to parents' property

**Rationale:**
Protect innocent children from consequences of invalid marriage.

---

### **COMPARATIVE POSITION**

**International Perspective:**
- Many countries: 18 years for both
- Some: 21 years for both
- Trend toward gender-neutral age requirements

**Indian Reform:**
Proposal to make it 21 for both, achieving:
- Gender equality
- Better educational outcomes
- Reduced maternal mortality

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

The age requirement under Section 5(iii) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 serves as crucial safeguard against child marriages, ensuring that parties possess maturity necessary for informed consent and marital responsibilities. The voidable nature of underage marriages, with 2-year window for annulment, balances protection of minors with stability of marriages ratified by continued cohabitation after attaining majority.

The dual regime under HMA and Prohibition of Child Marriage Act provides both civil remedy (annulment) and criminal deterrence (prosecution), reflecting society's strong commitment to ending child marriages. Section 16's protection of children's legitimacy demonstrates humane approach of protecting innocent offspring.

As India moves toward gender parity in marriage age and stricter enforcement through registration requirements and prohibition officers, the age requirement provisions continue to evolve, contributing significantly to social reform and protection of children's rights.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 2

**Q2-CVM-10M:** Explain the mental capacity requirements for a valid Hindu marriage under Section 5(ii).

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

#### **INTRODUCTION**

Mental capacity to understand the nature and consequences of marriage is an essential requirement for a valid Hindu marriage. Section 5(ii) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 prescribes three sub-conditions relating to mental capacity that must be satisfied by both parties. Violation of these conditions renders the marriage voidable at the option of the aggrieved party under Section 12(1)(b).

---

### **SECTION 5(ii): STATUTORY PROVISION**

**Text:**
"Neither party‚Äî
(a) is incapable of giving a valid consent to it in consequence of unsoundness of mind; or
(b) though capable of giving valid consent, has been suffering from mental disorder of such a kind or to such an extent as to be unfit for marriage and the procreation of children; or
(c) has been subject to recurrent attacks of insanity"

---

### **THREE SUB-CONDITIONS**

#### **A. INCAPACITY TO GIVE VALID CONSENT [5(ii)(a)]**

**Meaning:**
Party must be capable of:
- Understanding nature of marriage
- Comprehending duties and responsibilities of marriage
- Realizing consequences of entering into matrimony
- Giving free and informed consent

**Incapacity Due To:**
- Severe mental illness
- Intellectual disability
- Unsoundness of mind
- Conditions preventing comprehension

**Not Covered:**
- Temporary confusion
- Nervousness at ceremony
- Minor anxiety

**Test:**
At time of marriage, was person capable of understanding what marriage entails?

**Illustration:**
A suffers from severe mental disability unable to understand concept of marriage. A cannot give valid consent. Marriage with A would be voidable.

---

#### **B. UNFIT FOR MARRIAGE AND PROCREATION [5(ii)(b)]**

**Requirements:**
- Party has mental disorder, AND
- Disorder is of such kind/extent that party is unfit for:
  - **Marriage**, AND
  - **Procreation of children**

**Both Conditions Necessary:**
Not sufficient if unfit for marriage alone or procreation alone. Must be unfit for both.

**Mental Disorders Include:**
- Severe schizophrenia
- Psychotic disorders
- Conditions preventing discharge of marital obligations
- Conditions preventing capacity for procreation

**Illustration:**
B suffers from severe mental disorder making B incapable of understanding marital relationship and also preventing capacity for having children. Marriage would be voidable on this ground.

---

#### **C. RECURRENT ATTACKS OF INSANITY [5(ii)(c)]**

**Requirements:**
- Party has suffered recurrent attacks of insanity
- Pattern of repeated episodes
- Not single isolated incident

**Meaning of "Recurrent":**
- Multiple episodes
- Periodic occurrences
- Established pattern

**Proof Required:**
- Medical history
- Psychiatric records
- Expert testimony
- Evidence of multiple episodes

**Case Law:**
**Lila Gupta v. Laxmi Narain (AIR 1978 Del 32):**
- Medical evidence essential to prove recurrent insanity
- Mere eccentric or unusual behavior insufficient
- Pattern of insanity must be clearly established with medical proof

**Illustration:**
C has suffered three documented episodes of acute psychosis requiring hospitalization in past five years. This constitutes recurrent attacks of insanity.

---

### **CONSEQUENCE OF VIOLATION**

**Legal Effect:**
Marriage is **VOIDABLE** under Section 12(1)(b).

**Who Can Petition:**
Only the **other party** (aggrieved party who is of sound mind).

**Time Limit:**
No specific time limit mentioned (unlike impotence which has 1-year limit).

**Bars to Relief [Section 12(2)]:**
Petition may be dismissed if:
- Petitioner knew of mental condition at time of marriage, AND
- Consented to marriage despite knowledge, AND
- Parties cohabited with free consent after petitioner had knowledge

**Rationale for Bars:**
If knowing party accepts condition and cohabits, suggests willingness to continue marriage despite mental condition.

---

### **PROOF REQUIREMENTS**

**Medical Evidence Essential:**

Courts require:
1. **Expert psychiatric testimony**
2. **Medical reports and records**
3. **History of treatment**
4. **Psychiatric evaluation**
5. **Hospitalization records** (if any)

**Burden of Proof:**
- On petitioner alleging mental incapacity
- Must be proved by preponderance of probabilities
- Standard: Civil standard (balance of probabilities)

**Case Law:**
**Bimala Bose v. Satyendra Nath Bose (AIR 1952 Cal 396):**
- Person of unsound mind cannot give valid consent
- Marriage voidable
- Medical evidence required to establish condition

---

### **RATIONALE FOR MENTAL CAPACITY REQUIREMENT**

**1. Consent Foundation:**
- Marriage requires free and informed consent
- Mental capacity prerequisite for valid consent

**2. Marital Obligations:**
- Marriage involves duties and responsibilities
- Requires mental capacity to discharge obligations

**3. Protection of Parties:**
- Protects mentally incapable person from exploitation
- Protects other party from marrying incapable person unknowingly

**4. Children's Welfare:**
- Capacity for procreation relevant
- Parenting requires mental capacity

---

### **MODERN CONSIDERATIONS**

**Mental Health Awareness:**
- Greater understanding of mental disorders
- Reduced stigma
- Better diagnosis and treatment

**Ethical Issues:**
- Rights of persons with mental disabilities
- UN Convention on Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- Balance between protection and autonomy

**Treatable Conditions:**
Many mental conditions now treatable. Courts consider:
- Current status
- Treatment prospects
- Ability to manage with support

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

Section 5(ii) of Hindu Marriage Act ensures that both parties to marriage possess mental capacity necessary for understanding marriage, giving valid consent, and discharging marital and parental responsibilities. The three sub-conditions comprehensively cover various aspects of mental capacity, from basic consent capability to fitness for marriage and procreation, to patterns of insanity.

Making such marriages voidable (rather than void) respects the agency of the aggrieved party while requiring medical evidence prevents frivolous claims. The provisions balance protection of vulnerable persons with disability rights, ensuring that marriage is entered into with full mental capacity while recognizing that with support and treatment, many persons with mental health conditions can lead fulfilling marital lives.
