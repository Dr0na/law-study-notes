**Q1-SCH-10M:** Explain the Mitakshara School of Hindu Law with special reference to its five sub-schools.

#### **INTRODUCTION**

The Mitakshara School is one of the two major schools of Hindu Law, founded by Vijnaneshwara's 11th-century commentary on Yajnavalkya Smriti. Applicable to most of India except Bengal and Assam, it established key concepts of birthright, coparcenary, and survivorship that fundamentally shaped Hindu inheritance law. The school further developed into five regional sub-schools, each with distinctive features while maintaining core Mitakshara principles.

---

### **BASIC INFORMATION**

**Name:** "Mitakshara" = "Measured in words" or "Brief commentary"

**Author:** Vijnaneshwara (11th Century CE)
- Court scholar of Chalukya King Vikramaditya VI of Kalyan

**Text Basis:** Commentary on Yajnavalkya Smriti
- Systematically explained and interpreted Yajnavalkya's verses

**Geographical Application:**
- Covers majority of India
- All states except Bengal and Assam

---

### **FIVE SUB-SCHOOLS**

**Mnemonic: "BMBD-P"**

### **1. BENARAS SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:** Uttar Pradesh, parts of North India

**Text:** Mitakshara text itself most closely followed

**Characteristics:**
- Most orthodox and conservative
- Strict interpretation of ancient texts
- Followed Vijnaneshwara's commentary closely
- Daughters excluded from coparcenary (before 2005 Amendment)
- Traditional approach to women's rights

**Significance:**
Set standard for North Indian Hindu Law interpretation.

---

### **2. MITHILA SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:** Bihar, northern Jharkhand

**Principal Texts:**
- **Vivada Ratnakara** by Chandeswara
- **Vivada Chintamani** by Vachaspati Mishra

**Characteristics:**
- More liberal interpretation than Benaras
- Greater recognition of women's rights
- Emphasis on custom and local usage
- Balanced approach between tradition and flexibility

**Significance:**
Provided middle path between orthodox and liberal interpretations.

---

### **3. BOMBAY (MAHARASHTRA) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:** Maharashtra, Gujarat, parts of Madhya Pradesh

**Principal Text:**
- **Vyavahara Mayukha** by Nilakantha

**Characteristics:**
- Progressive approach
- Recognized certain daughter's rights even before statutory reform
- Balanced traditional texts with practical considerations
- Influenced by regional customs of Maharashtra

**Judicial Significance:**
- Bombay (now Mumbai) High Court follows this sub-school
- Important commercial center led to practical approach
- Many landmark judgments from Bombay HC

---

### **4. DRAVIDA (MADRAS) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:** Tamil Nadu, parts of Andhra Pradesh, Kerala

**Principal Text:**
- **Smriti Chandrika** by Devanna Bhatta

**Characteristics:**
- **Most liberal** of all Mitakshara sub-schools
- Extensively recognized widow's rights
- Acknowledged married daughter's maintenance rights
- Permitted cross-cousin marriages by custom
- Widow could inherit property absolutely in circumstances

**Distinctive Features:**
- Cross-cousin marriages (mother's brother's daughter, father's sister's daughter) permitted by custom
- Women's property rights more extensive
- Adaptation to South Indian customs

**Significance:**
Demonstrated Hindu Law's flexibility and regional adaptability.

---

### **5. PUNJAB (CUSTOMARY LAW) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:** Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Delhi

**Characteristics:**
- **Heavily influenced by local customs**
- **Punjab Custom (Riwaj-i-Am)** given significant weightage
- Agricultural land governed by customary law
- Concept of "ancestral land" more restricted
- Sons-only succession to agricultural land by custom

**Historical Statutes:**
- Punjab Laws Act, 1872 (now repealed)
- Recognized customary law prominently

**Modern Position:**
After Hindu Succession Act, 1956 and 2005 Amendment:
- Statutory law now prevails
- But customary influences remain in non-statutory matters
- Cultural practices continue

---

### **COMMON PRINCIPLES ACROSS SUB-SCHOOLS**

Despite variations, all Mitakshara sub-schools share:

1. **Birthright Concept:**
   - Son acquires interest by birth
   - Father's power limited

2. **Coparcenary:**
   - Four-degree rule
   - Joint ownership concept

3. **Survivorship:**
   - On death, share to surviving coparceners

4. **Joint Family:**
   - Recognition of joint family system
   - Ancestral vs self-acquired distinction

---

### **MODERN RELEVANCE**

**Hindu Succession Act, 1956:**
- Unified succession rules
- Reduced practical differences between sub-schools
- Statutory provisions override customary variations

**2005 Amendment:**
- Daughters made coparceners
- Applied to all sub-schools
- **Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020):** Clarified retrospective application

**Continuing Relevance:**
- Coparcenary property matters
- Joint family property issues
- Partition rights
- Regional customary practices in marriage/ceremonies

---

### **COMPARISON OF SUB-SCHOOLS**

| **Sub-School** | **Region** | **Text** | **Approach** |
|---------------|----------|----------|--------------|
| Benaras | UP, North | Mitakshara | Orthodox |
| Mithila | Bihar | Vivada Ratnakara | Moderate |
| Bombay | Maharashtra | Vyavahara Mayukha | Balanced |
| Dravida | South India | Smriti Chandrika | Liberal |
| Punjab | Punjab | Riwaj-i-Am | Customary |

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

The Mitakshara School, through its foundational commentary and five regional sub-schools, created a comprehensive legal framework that governed most of India. Each sub-school adapted Mitakshara principles to regional needs, with Dravida being most liberal and Benaras most orthodox. The core principles of birthright, coparcenary, and survivorship unified them despite regional variations.

While statutory codification through Hindu Succession Act, 1956 and 2005 Amendment has reduced practical differences, understanding Mitakshara and its sub-schools remains essential for comprehending coparcenary concepts, joint family property, and regional legal traditions. The school's influence continues in modern Hindu Law, demonstrating successful synthesis of ancient juristic wisdom with evolving social needs.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 2

**Q2-SCH-10M:** Discuss the doctrine of "birthright" and "survivorship" under Mitakshara School. How have statutory reforms affected these concepts?

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

#### **INTRODUCTION**

Two fundamental doctrines distinguish Mitakshara School from Dayabhaga: the doctrine of birthright and the doctrine of survivorship. These concepts revolutionized Hindu inheritance law by giving sons present interest in ancestral property from birth and determining succession through survivorship rather than regular inheritance. Understanding these doctrines is crucial for comprehending Mitakshara coparcenary system and its evolution through statutory reforms.

---

### **PART I: DOCTRINE OF BIRTHRIGHT**

**Concept:**
Under Mitakshara, a son acquires an **interest by birth** in coparcenary (ancestral) property.

**Key Features:**

**1. Automatic Acquisition:**
- Interest accrues **immediately upon birth**
- No act of father required
- No registration or formality necessary
- Automatic operation of law

**2. Present Interest (Not Expectancy):**
- Son has present, immediate interest
- Not mere hope of inheritance (spes successionis)
- Can claim and enforce rights during father's lifetime

**3. Ancestral Property:**
Birthright applies only to **ancestral property**:
- Property inherited by father from father, grandfather, or great-grandfather
- Property inherited up to three degrees

**Self-Acquired Property:**
Father's self-acquired property: No birthright; father has absolute control.

---

**LEGAL CONSEQUENCES OF BIRTHRIGHT:**

**A. Father's Limited Power:**
- Father cannot alienate coparcenary property without son's consent
- Exception: Legal necessity or benefit of estate

**B. Son Can Challenge:**
- Son can challenge unauthorized alienation
- Sale/gift without necessity voidable

**C. Partition Right:**
- Son can demand partition as matter of right
- Force division of coparcenary property

**D. Right Survives Father:**
- If father dies, son still has share
- Share passes to son's heirs

---

**CASE LAWS:**

**Suraj Narain v. Iqbal Narain (AIR 1953 All 221):**
- Son's birthright cannot be defeated by father's alienation without legal necessity

**Gurupad Khandappa Magdum v. Hirabai (AIR 1978 SC 1239):**
- Father has limited power to alienate coparcenary property
- Must be for legal necessity or benefit of estate

---

**ILLUSTRATION:**

**Scenario:**
```
Grandfather G dies leaving property
‚Üì
Father F inherits (becomes ancestral for F's sons)
‚Üì
Son S is born
‚Üì
S immediately gets birthright in property F inherited
‚Üì
F cannot sell without S's consent (unless necessity)
```

---

### **2005 AMENDMENT IMPACT ON BIRTHRIGHT:**

**Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005:**
- Extended birthright to **daughters**
- Daughters become coparceners by birth equal to sons
- Interest accrues from birth
- All coparcenary rights

**Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020):**
- Supreme Court: Daughter's birthright applies retrospectively
- Rights accrue irrespective of father being alive or dead
- Landmark judgment for women's property rights

---

### **PART II: DOCTRINE OF SURVIVORSHIP**

**Concept:**
On death of a coparcener, his share does not pass by **succession** but by **survivorship** to surviving coparceners.

**Principle:**
Interest of deceased coparcener automatically expands and merges with shares of surviving coparceners.

**Key Features:**

**1. Automatic Operation:**
- No will required
- No succession formalities
- Instant expansion

**2. Only Among Coparceners:**
- Applies only between coparceners
- Not to other family members

**3. Per Capita Division:**
- Share distributed equally among survivors
- Not per stirpes

---

**HOW SURVIVORSHIP WORKS:**

**Illustration:**
```
A, B, C, D are coparceners (each 1/4 share)
‚Üì
D dies (no Class I heirs)
‚Üì
D's 1/4 share merges by survivorship
‚Üì
A, B, C now each have 1/3 share
```

**Formula:**
Surviving coparceners = Deceased's share √∑ Number of survivors
Each survivor gets equal portion of deceased's share added to own.

---

**EXCEPTION TO SURVIVORSHIP:**

**Hindu Succession Act, 1956:**

If deceased coparcener leaves **Class I heirs**:
- Widow
- Son, daughter
- Mother
- Son's children, daughter's children, etc.

Then survivorship does NOT apply. Property passes by **succession** to Class I heirs.

**Rule:**
Survivorship applies only if NO Class I heirs.

**Illustration:**
```
A, B (son), C (son) are coparceners
‚Üì
B dies leaving widow W and daughter D (Class I heirs)
‚Üì
NO survivorship
‚Üì
B's share passes to W and D by succession
(NOT to A and C by survivorship)
```

---

**RATIONALE FOR SURVIVORSHIP:**

**1. Unity of Property:**
- Coparcenary property held in common
- No defined individual shares
- Community of interest

**2. Keeping Property in Male Line:**
- Historically, kept property in agnatic (male) line
- Prevented fragmentation

**3. Simplicity:**
- Automatic operation
- No succession formalities
- No probate required

---

### **CONTRAST WITH SUCCESSION**

| **Aspect** | **Survivorship** | **Succession** |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Trigger** | Death of coparcener | Death of owner |
| **To Whom** | Surviving coparceners | Heirs (as per law/will) |
| **Nature** | Automatic expansion | Transfer/inheritance |
| **Formality** | None required | Succession certificate/probate |
| **Scope** | Only coparcenary property | All property |
| **School** | Mitakshara | Dayabhaga + Modern Law |

---

### **PART III: STATUTORY REFORMS**

#### **HINDU SUCCESSION ACT, 1956**

**Impact on Birthright:**
- Retained for coparcenary property
- But succession rules unified
- Class I heirs concept introduced
- Exception to survivorship created

**Impact on Survivorship:**
- Modified by Class I heirs provision
- If Class I heirs exist, they inherit
- Pure survivorship applies only if no Class I heirs

---

#### **2005 AMENDMENT - REVOLUTIONARY CHANGE**

**Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005:**

**Section 6 Amended:**
- Daughters made coparceners by birth
- Equal to sons in all respects
- Interest from birth in coparcenary property

**Impact on Birthright:**
- Daughters now have birthright
- Equal share
- Can demand partition
- Father cannot alienate without daughter's consent

**Impact on Survivorship:**
- Daughters' shares also pass by survivorship
- Or to their Class I heirs

**Retroactive Application:**
**Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020) 9 SCC 1:**
- Three-judge bench held: Rights accrue from 9th September 2005 regardless of when father died
- Daughter has rights even if father died before 2005
- Landmark for women's property rights

---

### **MODERN POSITION**

**What Remains:**
- Birthright concept continues (now for daughters too)
- Coparcenary system operational
- Survivorship applies (with Class I exception)
- Partition rights exercisable

**What Changed:**
- Gender equality achieved
- Daughters equal coparceners
- Modified survivorship (Class I heirs)
- Unified succession rules

**Practical Relevance:**
- Property disputes still invoke these concepts
- Courts apply birthright and survivorship principles
- Partition cases common
- Understanding essential for legal practice

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

The doctrines of birthright and survivorship are cornerstone principles of Mitakshara School, creating unique property system where sons (now daughters too) acquire present interest by birth and shares pass by survivorship to surviving coparceners. While Hindu Succession Act, 1956 modified pure survivorship by introducing Class I heirs exception, and 2005 Amendment extended birthright to daughters achieving gender equality, these Mitakshara concepts remain central to understanding coparcenary property and joint family law. They represent sophisticated juristic thought balancing individual rights with family cohesion, though now reformed to align with constitutional values of equality and women's rights.
