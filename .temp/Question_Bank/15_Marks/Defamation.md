### **DEFAMATION**

#### **Introduction**

Defamation is an offense against reputation under the Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023, defined in **Section 356 BNS** (previously Section 499 IPC). It recognizes that **reputation** is a valuable asset and protects individuals against false imputations that harm their reputation. Defamation can be both a **civil wrong** (tort) and a **criminal offense**, with the criminal law addressing more serious cases where public interest demands prosecution. The law carefully balances **protection of reputation** with **freedom of speech**, providing ten specific exceptions where statements that might otherwise be defamatory are not punishable.

---

### **I. DEFINITION (Section 356 BNS)**

**"Whoever, by words either spoken or written, or by signs or by visible representations or otherwise, makes or publishes any imputation concerning any person intending to harm, or knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm, the reputation of such person, is said, except in cases hereinafter excepted, to defame that person."**

---

### **II. ESSENTIAL INGREDIENTS OF DEFAMATION**

---

### **1Ô∏è‚É£ IMPUTATION**

**Requirement**: There must be an **imputation** (statement, assertion, or allegation) concerning a person

**"Imputation"**: Any statement or suggestion attributing something to person

**Forms of Imputation**:
- **Words spoken** (oral - slander)
- **Words written** (written - libel)
- **Signs** (gestures, symbols)
- **Visible representations** (pictures, cartoons, videos)
- **Otherwise** (any other form of communication)

**Modern Forms**:
- Social media posts
- WhatsApp messages
- Emails
- Online articles
- Memes, videos

---

### **2Ô∏è‚É£ CONCERNING A PERSON**

**Requirement**: Imputation must be **concerning a specific person**

**Who Can Be Defamed**:
- Living **natural persons**
- **Companies** or associations (can be defamed)
- **Dead persons** (in some circumstances if affects living relatives)

**Identification**:
- Person must be **identifiable** from imputation
- Need not be named explicitly
- If reasonable person can identify, sufficient

**Example**: "The corrupt minister from Maharashtra" - if only one minister fits, identifiable.

---

### **3Ô∏è‚É£ INTENT TO HARM OR KNOWLEDGE/BELIEF IT WILL HARM**

**Two Alternative Mental States**:

**(a) Intending to Harm Reputation**:
- **Positive intention** to damage reputation
- Deliberate malice

**(b) Knowing or Having Reason to Believe It Will Harm**:
- **Knowledge** that statement will harm reputation
- OR reasonable belief that it will harm
- May not intend, but aware of consequence

**Test**: Did accused know or should have known statement would harm reputation?

**Key Point**: Either intention OR knowledge/belief sufficient.

---

### **4Ô∏è‚É£ HARM TO REPUTATION**

**Requirement**: Imputation must harm or be likely to harm **reputation**

**"Reputation"**: Estimation in which person is held by others

**What Harms Reputation**:
- Lowers person in estimation of others
- Exposes person to hatred, contempt, or ridicule
- Causes person to be shunned or avoided
- Injures person in profession or trade

**Test - Objective Standard**: 
Would statement lower person's reputation in eyes of **right-thinking members of society**?

**Not**: What particular person feels, but objective assessment.

---

### **III. 10 EXCEPTIONS TO DEFAMATION**

Section 356 provides **ten exceptions** where statements are NOT defamation:

---

### **Exception 1: Imputation of Truth for Public Good**

**When**: Imputation of truth which public good requires to be made or published

**Requirements**:
1. Imputation must be **true**
2. Publication for **public good**

**Examples**:
- Exposing corruption of public official
- Revealing criminal activities
- Publishing facts about public figure's misconduct

---

### **Exception 2: Public Conduct of Public Servants**

**When**: Opinion on conduct of public servant in discharge of public functions

**Requirements**:
1. Must be about **public servant**
2. Conduct must be in **discharge of public duties**
3. Must be **opinion** (not false fact)

**Example**: Criticizing minister's policy decisions

---

### **Exception 3: Conduct of Person in Public Questions**

**When**: Opinion on conduct of person touching **public question**

**Requirements**:
1. Person involved in public affairs
2. Conduct relates to public question

**Example**: Criticizing activist's role in public movement

---

### **Exception 4: Publication of Court Reports**

**When**: Substantially true report of proceedings of court

**Requirements**:
1. Report of **court proceedings**
2. **Substantially true** (need not be verbatim)
3. In good faith

**Example**: Newspaper reporting court judgment

---

### **Exception 5: Merits of Case Decided**

**When**: Opinion on merits of case decided by court or on conduct of witnesses/parties

**Requirements**:
1. About decided case
2. Opinion on merits or conduct
3. In good faith

**Example**: Legal commentary on court decision

---

### **Exception 6: Merits of Public Performance**

**When**: Opinion on merits of any performance submitted to public judgment

**Requirements**:
1. Performance submitted to public
2. Opinion on merits

**Examples**:
- Book review
- Movie review
- Art criticism
- Performance critique

---

### **Exception 7: Censure of Person's Conduct**

**When**: Censure passed in good faith by person having lawful authority

**Requirements**:
1. Passed in **good faith**
2. By person with **lawful authority**

**Examples**:
- Teacher criticizing student's conduct
- Employer's performance review
- Parent disciplining child

---

### **Exception 8: Accusation Preferred in Good Faith**

**When**: Accusation preferred in good faith to authorized person

**Requirements**:
1. Accusation made in **good faith**
2. To person with **authority** to inquire/remedy
3. For protection of interests

**Example**: Complaint to police about suspected crime

---

### **Exception 9: Imputation for Protection of Interests**

**When**: Imputation made in good faith for protection of interests of person making it or another

**Requirements**:
1. Made in **good faith**
2. For **protection** of legitimate interests

**Example**: Warning friend about dishonest person

---

### **Exception 10: Caution for Public Good**

**When**: Caution intended for good of person to whom conveyed or public good

**Requirements**:
1. Intended as **caution**
2. For good of recipient or public

**Example**: Warning public about quack doctor

---

### **IV. DISTINCTION: Criminal vs Civil Defamation**

| **Aspect** | **CRIMINAL DEFAMATION** | **CIVIL DEFAMATION** |
|------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Nature** | Public wrong/offense | Private wrong/tort |
| **Objective** | Punishment of offender | Compensation to victim |
| **Remedy** | Imprisonment + fine | Damages (monetary) |
| **Initiation** | Criminal complaint/prosecution | Civil suit |
| **Proof** | Beyond reasonable doubt | Preponderance of probabilities |
| **Parties** | State vs Accused | Plaintiff vs Defendant |
| **Result** | Conviction/acquittal + punishment | Damages awarded or not |
| **Focus** | Public interest, peace | Private injury, compensation |

---

### **V. PUNISHMENT (Section 356 BNS)**

**Defamation**:
- Imprisonment up to **2 years**, OR
- Fine, OR
- Both

**Printed/Engraved Defamation**:
- Imprisonment up to **2 years**, OR
- Fine, OR
- Both

**Nature**:
- Non-cognizable
- Bailable
- Compoundable

---

### **Conclusion**

Defamation under Section 356 BNS protects individuals' reputation while balancing freedom of speech through ten carefully crafted exceptions. The offense requires imputation concerning a person with intent to harm or knowledge that it will harm reputation, made through various forms of communication. The ten exceptions ensure that genuine public interest disclosures, fair opinions on public matters, court reports, and good faith communications are not stifled, thus maintaining equilibrium between individual reputation rights and societal need for free expression. The distinction between criminal and civil defamation reflects that while reputation is valuable, not every defamatory statement warrants criminal prosecution - only those affecting public interest or particularly serious in nature. Understanding these exceptions is crucial as they define the boundaries of permissible speech while protecting against malicious attacks on reputation.
