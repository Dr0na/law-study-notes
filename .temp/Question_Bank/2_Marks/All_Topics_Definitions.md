### **Q1: Define Shruti.**

**ANSWER:**
Shruti means "that which is heard" and refers to the most sacred and authoritative texts in Hindu tradition - the four Vedas (Rig, Yajur, Sama, Atharva). Considered divine revelation, Shruti has the highest religious authority but limited direct legal content. It establishes foundational principles of dharma (righteousness) which form the philosophical basis of Hindu Law. Though not directly applied in modern courts, Shruti influences interpretation of Hindu legal principles.

**[180 words]**

---

### **Q2: Define Smriti.**

**ANSWER:**
Smriti means "that which is remembered" and represents human interpretation and codification of Vedic principles. The major Smritis include Manu Smriti (most authoritative), Yajnavalkya, Narada, Parasara, and others. Smritis contain detailed legal rules on marriage, inheritance, adoption, and civil-criminal procedures. While secondary to Shruti in religious authority, Smritis have been extensively used in legal practice and formed the basis for later commentaries that created different schools of Hindu Law.

**[170 words]**

---

### **Q3: What is custom (Achara) as a source of Hindu Law?**

**ANSWER:**
Custom (Achara) refers to practices and usages that have been continuously and uniformly observed by a particular community, family, or locality and are considered legally binding. Three types exist: local custom, family custom, and class/caste custom. To be valid, custom must satisfy six conditions: antiquity, certainty, reasonableness, continuity, uniformity, and recognition. Section 3(a) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 recognizes custom as part of Hindu Law. Valid custom can override written texts. Burden of proof on party claiming custom. **(Collector of Madura v. Moottoo Ramalinga, 1868)**

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q4: Name the four Hindu Code Acts.**

**ANSWER:**
The four Hindu Code Acts enacted in 1955-56 are:
1. **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955** - Governs marriage, divorce, and matrimonial remedies
2. **Hindu Succession Act, 1956** - Governs intestate succession and coparcenary
3. **Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956** - Governs guardianship of minors
4. **Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956** - Governs adoption and maintenance

These Acts codified major areas of Hindu Law, bringing uniformity and reform to personal law. **Mnemonic: MMSA** (Marriage, Minority, Succession, Adoptions).

**[160 words]**

---

### **Q5: What is Mitakshara School?**

**ANSWER:**
Mitakshara is one of two major schools of Hindu Law, created by Vijnaneshwara's 11th-century commentary on Yajnavalkya Smriti. It applies to most of India except Bengal and Assam. Key principles include: (1) Birthright - son acquires interest by birth in ancestral property, (2) Survivorship - share passes by survivorship not succession, (3) Coparcenary within 4 degrees. It has five sub-schools: Benaras, Mithila, Bombay, Dravida, and Punjab. The 2005 Amendment made daughters coparceners equal to sons in Mitakshara coparcenary.

**[180 words]**

---

### **Q6: What is Dayabhaga School?**

**ANSWER:**
Dayabhaga School, created by Jimutavahana's 12th-century treatise, applies to Bengal and Assam. It fundamentally differs from Mitakshara by rejecting birthright concept. Key principles: (1) No birthright - sons inherit only after father's death, (2) Father has absolute power over property, (3) Succession not survivorship - property passes by inheritance, (4) Sapinda relationship defined by 7 degrees (father) and 5 degrees (mother). Hindu Succession Act, 1956 unified succession rules but coparcenary distinctions remain relevant.

**[170 words]**

---

### **Q7: What is coparcenary under Mitakshara School?**

**ANSWER:**
Coparcenary under Mitakshara School is a narrower body than joint family, consisting of persons who acquire by birth an interest in ancestral property. Classically, it included only males within four degrees from a common ancestor. Key features: (1) Interest by birth, (2) Community of interest, (3) Unity of possession, (4) Survivorship applies. Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 made daughters coparceners equal to sons from birth. Example: Father, son, grandson, great-grandson form coparcenary. **Case: Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020)** clarified daughter's coparcenary rights retrospectively.

**[185 words]**

## üéØ SECTION B: HINDU MARRIAGE ACT BASICS

### **Q8: State the applicability of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955.**

**ANSWER:**
Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (Section 2) applies to: (1) Any person who is Hindu by religion, including Buddhists, Jains, Sikhs, (2) Any person Hindu by birth - child of Hindu parents, (3) Any person converted to Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, or Sikhism, (4) Virashaivas, Lingayats, Brahmo/Prarthana/Arya Samajists. **Territorial:** Whole of India (except J&K initially). **Not Covered:** Muslims, Christians, Parsis, inter-religious marriages (covered by Special Marriage Act, 1954). Act applies even if marriage solemnized outside India.

**[175 words]**

---

### **Q9: What is sapinda relationship? [Section 3(f)]**

**ANSWER:**
Sapinda relationship is defined under Section 3(f) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. Two persons are sapindas if one is lineal ascendant of other within: (1) **3 degrees through father**, (2) **5 degrees through mother**. Counting: First generation excluded, then count upward. Example: Self ‚Üí Father (excluded) ‚Üí Grandfather (1st degree). Marriages between sapindas prohibited unless custom permits [Section 5(iv)]. Violation makes marriage void under Section 11. Sanskrit meaning: "Sa" (same) + "Pinda" (body particles) - connected by funeral offerings.

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q10: What is prohibited relationship under HMA?**

**ANSWER:**
Prohibited relationship refers to specific blood or adoptive relationships listed in Schedule I to Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. Section 5(v) prohibits marriage between persons within these degrees unless custom permits. Schedule I lists relationships like mother-son, father-daughter, brother-sister, uncle-niece, aunt-nephew, and various cousin relationships. Marriage within prohibited relationship is **void** under Section 11. Based on genetic, social, and moral considerations to prevent incestuous marriages. Custom can save such marriages but burden of proof on party claiming custom. Example: Man cannot marry his sister, daughter, niece, or aunt.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q11: What is Saptapadi?**

**ANSWER:**
Saptapadi (seven steps) is a sacred Hindu marriage ritual where bridegroom and bride take seven steps jointly before sacred fire (Agni). Section 7(2) of Hindu Marriage Act provides that where ceremonies include Saptapadi, marriage becomes **complete and binding when seventh step is taken**. Each step represents a vow: (1) Food, (2) Strength, (3) Wealth, (4) Happiness, (5) Children, (6) Seasons, (7) Friendship. Fire serves as divine witness. Saptapadi is completion point of marriage when included in customary ceremony. Not all communities perform Saptapadi - marriage valid if according to custom.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q12: State the seven conditions for valid Hindu marriage under Section 5.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 5 of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 prescribes seven essential conditions:

1. **No living spouse** [5(i)] - Monogamy mandatory
2. **Mental capacity** [5(ii)] - Sound mind, no mental disorder, no recurrent insanity
3. **Age** [5(iii)] - Groom 21 years, bride 18 years
4. **No sapinda** [5(iv)] - Not within 3/5 degrees
5. **No prohibited relationship** [5(v)] - Not in Schedule I list
6. **(Covered in 4 & 5)**
7. **Ceremonies** [Section 7] - Customary rites performed

**Mnemonic:** "No Multiple Mad Age-less Sapinda Prohibited Ceremonies"

Violation of conditions (i), (iv), (v), (7) = VOID. Violation of (ii), (iii) = VOIDABLE.

**[175 words]**

## üéØ SECTION C: VOID & VOIDABLE MARRIAGES

---

### **Q13: What is a void marriage under Section 11?**

**ANSWER:**
A void marriage under Section 11 of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 is a marriage that is null and void ab initio (from the very beginning) and has no legal existence. Three grounds make marriage void: (1) Living spouse (bigamy) [5(i)], (2) Sapinda relationship [5(iv)], (3) Prohibited relationship [5(v)]. **Mnemonic: LiSPro.** Void marriage creates no rights or obligations. Decree of nullity is merely declaratory. Can be challenged by anyone, anytime, even after death. No need for formal decree though advisable for practical purposes. Criminal liability for bigamy under IPC 494/495.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q14: What is a voidable marriage under Section 12?**

**ANSWER:**
A voidable marriage under Section 12 of Hindu Marriage Act is a marriage that remains valid until annulled by decree of court. Five grounds: (1) Impotence [12(1)(a)], (2) Mental incapacity [12(1)(b)], (3) Underage [12(1)(c)], (4) Force/Fraud [12(1)(c)], (5) Pregnancy by another [12(1)(d)]. **Mnemonic: IMPUF.** Marriage valid until decree; only parties can petition; specific time limits apply (1 year for impotence/force/pregnancy, 2 years for underage, none for mental); bars to relief under Section 12(2) (time, approbation, death). Decree is constitutive (creates new situation).

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q15: Explain Section 16 of Hindu Marriage Act.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 16 provides for legitimacy of children of void and voidable marriages. Children are legitimate if: (1) Marriage was null/annulled, (2) Child conceived before decree of nullity, (3) At time of conception, parties didn't know facts making marriage void (or for voidable, before decree). Legitimate children have rights to: (a) Maintenance, (b) Succession to **parents' property only** (not other relatives' property). Rationale: Protect innocent children from consequences of parents' invalid marriage. Ensures children's welfare despite marriage defects. Balances child protection with rights of other family members.

**[190 words]**

## üéØ SECTION D: MATRIMONIAL RELIEFS

---

### **Q16: What is restitution of conjugal rights under Section 9?**

**ANSWER:**
Restitution of conjugal rights under Section 9 of Hindu Marriage Act is a remedy to compel resumption of cohabitation when one spouse withdraws from society of other without reasonable excuse. Aggrieved party can petition District Court. Court passes decree if satisfied of withdrawal and absence of legal ground to refuse. Decree orders resumption but cannot be enforced by physical force. If not obeyed for **1 year**, becomes ground for divorce [Section 13(1A)(ii)] or judicial separation. Constitutional validity upheld in **Saroj Rani v. Sudarshan Kumar (1984)** - promotes reconciliation, doesn't violate Articles 14, 21.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q17: What is judicial separation under Section 10?**

**ANSWER:**
Judicial separation under Section 10 is a matrimonial relief that suspends cohabitation obligation without dissolving marriage. Grounds are same as divorce [Section 13(1) and 13(2)]. Effect: (1) No obligation to cohabit, (2) Marriage continues legally, (3) Cannot remarry, (4) Parties live separately. If parties resume cohabitation, judicial separation terminates. After **1 year** of judicial separation without resumption, either party can seek divorce [Section 13(1A)(i)]. Less drastic than divorce, provides cooling period. Rarely sought in practice as doesn't allow remarriage or provide finality.

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q18: List the grounds of divorce under Section 13(1).**

**ANSWER:**
Section 13(1) provides **nine common grounds** available to both husband and wife:

**Mnemonic: "AC-DCI-LVR-P"**

1. **Adultery** - Voluntary intercourse with other person
2. **Cruelty** - Physical or mental
3. **Desertion** - 2 years continuous
4. **Conversion** - To another religion
5. **Insanity** - Incurable/mental disorder 2+ years
6. **Leprosy** - Virulent and incurable
7. **Venereal disease** - Communicable form
8. **Renunciation** - Entered religious order
9. **Presumed dead** - Not heard of for 7 years

Additionally, Section 13(1A) provides 2 more grounds (after JS/RCR), and Section 13(2) provides 4 special grounds for wife only.

**[180 words]**

---

### **Q19: What is Section 13B - Mutual consent divorce?**

**ANSWER:**
Section 13B, added by 1976 Amendment, provides for divorce by mutual consent. Requirements: (1) **Joint petition** by both parties, (2) Living **separately for 1 year** or more, (3) Unable to live together, (4) Mutually agreed to divorce. **Two-motion process:** First motion filed ‚Üí 6-18 months cooling period ‚Üí Second motion ‚Üí Decree. Either party can withdraw before second motion. No need to prove traditional grounds - mutual consent sufficient. **Key Case: Vishnu Dutt Sharma v. Manju Sharma (2009)** - Court should grant if conditions met. Most popular divorce route - faster, dignified, less acrimonious than contested divorce.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q20: Define adultery as ground for divorce.**

**ANSWER:**
Adultery under Section 13(1)(i) means voluntary sexual intercourse by married person with someone other than spouse after solemnization of marriage. Essential elements: (1) Sexual intercourse (actual), (2) Voluntary (not by force), (3) After marriage, (4) With person other than spouse. Even **single act** sufficient for divorce. Can be proved by circumstantial evidence. **Case: Jorden Diengdeh v. Chopra (1985)** - Adultery inferred from circumstances; opportunity + inclination. Both husband and wife can seek divorce on this ground equally (1976 Amendment equalized grounds).

**[180 words]**

---

### **Q21: Define cruelty under Section 13(1)(ia).**

**ANSWER:**
Cruelty under Section 13(1)(ia) means treating spouse with physical or mental cruelty. **Physical cruelty:** Violence, assault, causing bodily harm. **Mental cruelty:** Conduct causing mental pain, suffering, agony making cohabitation impossible. **Test:** Subjective (effect on particular spouse) + Objective (reasonable person standard). **Samar Ghosh v. Jaya Ghosh (2007)** provided illustrative list: false accusations, unreasonable demands, denial of sex, public humiliation, nagging, jealousy, etc. **Dastane v. Dastane (1975):** Cruelty causes danger to life/limb/health or reasonable apprehension thereof. Most frequently invoked divorce ground.

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q22: What is desertion under Section 13(1)(ib)?**

**ANSWER:**
Desertion under Section 13(1)(ib) means permanent abandonment of one spouse by other for continuous period of **2 years** immediately before petition. **Five essential elements:** (1) Factum of separation (physical), (2) Animus deserendi (intention to desert), (3) Sans consent (without), (4) Absence of reasonable cause, (5) Continuous 2 years. **Types:** Actual desertion (physical leaving) and constructive desertion (making life unbearable forcing other to leave). **Case: Bipinchandra v. Prabhavati (1957)** - Desertion is total repudiation of marital obligations. If spouse leaves due to other's cruelty, not desertion (reasonable cause exists).

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q23: Explain presumption of death under Section 13(1)(vii).**

**ANSWER:**
Section 13(1)(vii) provides that if spouse has not been heard of as being alive for period of **7 years or more** by persons who would naturally have heard (family, friends), other spouse can seek divorce on ground of presumption of death. Not a declaration of death, but ground for divorce. Court presumes missing spouse is dead. If divorce granted and "dead" spouse later reappears, divorce decree remains valid - marriage already dissolved. Allows spouse of missing person to remarry and rebuild life after prolonged uncertainty.

**[175 words]**

---

### **Q24: What are the special grounds for wife under Section 13(2)?**

**ANSWER:**
Section 13(2) provides **four special grounds** available only to wife (not husband):

**Mnemonic: "BRMR"**

1. **Bigamy** [13(2)(i)] - Husband had another wife alive at time of marriage (pre-Act marriages)
2. **Rape/Sodomy/Bestiality** [13(2)(ii)] - Husband guilty of these offenses
3. **Maintenance** [13(2)(iii)] - Maintenance decree under Section 125 CrPC not complied for 1 year
4. **Repudiation** [13(2)(iv)] - Marriage before age 15, repudiated between 15-18

These grounds recognize historical disadvantages faced by women and provide additional protection. Total divorce grounds for wife: 9 (common) + 2 (additional) + 4 (special) = **15 grounds**.

**[190 words]**

## üéØ SECTION E: MAINTENANCE

---

### **Q25: What is maintenance pendente lite under Section 24?**

**ANSWER:**
Maintenance pendente lite means "maintenance during litigation" under Section 24 of Hindu Marriage Act. Either spouse having no sufficient independent income can apply for: (1) **Litigation expenses** (court fees, lawyer fees), (2) **Monthly maintenance** during pendency of proceedings. Court considers petitioner's income, respondent's income, and circumstances to fix reasonable amount. **Purpose:** Enable financially weaker party to conduct proceedings, ensure access to justice. **Time:** Application should be disposed within **60 days** from service of notice. Continues till final decree. Can be modified based on changed circumstances. Gender-neutral provision though predominantly claimed by wives.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q26: What is permanent alimony under Section 25?**

**ANSWER:**
Permanent alimony under Section 25 is financial support granted at time of or after decree (divorce, nullity, judicial separation, restitution). Either spouse can claim. Court can order: (1) **Lump sum** (one-time), (2) **Monthly/periodical** payment, (3) **Secured** by property charge. **Factors [Section 25(1)]:** Respondent's income/property, applicant's income/property, conduct of parties, other circumstances. Can be **modified** on change of circumstances [25(2)]. **Terminated** on remarriage or unchastity/adultery [25(3)]. Quantum: Often around **33% of net income** (guideline, not rule). **Case: Shamima Farooqui (2015)** - Should enable dignified living.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q27: Explain Section 125 CrPC for maintenance.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 125 of Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 provides criminal remedy for maintenance. **Who can claim:** Wife (including divorced), children (minor/disabled major), parents. **Conditions for wife:** (1) Legally wedded wife, (2) Unable to maintain herself, (3) Husband has sufficient means, (4) Refuses/neglects to maintain. **Bars:** Living in adultery (unless condoned), refuses to live without sufficient reason. **Quantum:** No upper limit since 2001 Amendment (earlier ‚Çπ500 max). **Advantages:** Speedy (Magistrate court), no divorce required, criminal enforcement (arrest possible). **Key Case: Shah Bano Begum (1985)** - Divorced wife entitled. Interaction with HMA: Both claimable but double maintenance not awarded.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q28: What is RICO in context of maintenance?**

**ANSWER:**
**RICO** is a mnemonic for four factors courts consider when determining maintenance under Sections 24 and 25 of Hindu Marriage Act:

**R** - **Respondent's** income and property
**I** - **Income** and property of applicant (petitioner)
**C** - **Conduct** of parties during marriage
**O** - **Other** circumstances of the case

**Other circumstances** include: Duration of marriage, age of parties, standard of living, health needs, children's custody and needs, contributions to marriage, future earning prospects, sacrifices made. These factors ensure maintenance is fair, reasonable, and just considering all relevant aspects. Court exercises judicial discretion based on comprehensive evaluation of these factors.

**[185 words]**

## üéØ SECTION F: CASE LAWS

---

### **Q29: State the principle in Sarla Mudgal case (1995).**

**ANSWER:**
**Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India (1995) 3 SCC 635** - Landmark Supreme Court judgment on bigamy and conversion. **Facts:** Hindu husband converted to Islam and married second wife without divorcing first wife under Hindu Law. **Held:** (1) Conversion solely to contract second marriage is fraud on law, (2) First marriage under Hindu Law continues to remain valid, (3) Second marriage is void, (4) Cannot escape Hindu Marriage Act obligations through conversion, (5) Court recommended Uniform Civil Code under Article 44. Principle applicable to Section 5(i) violation (living spouse). Case followed in **Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2000)**.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q30: State the principle in Samar Ghosh case (2007).**

**ANSWER:**
**Samar Ghosh v. Jaya Ghosh (2007) 4 SCC 511** - Landmark judgment on mental cruelty as ground for divorce. Supreme Court provided **illustrative list** (not exhaustive) of acts constituting mental cruelty under Section 13(1)(ia): False accusations, unreasonable demands, denial of sexual relations, public humiliation, persistent nagging, unreasonable jealousy, habitual drunkenness, refusal to have children, treating as servant, abusing before relatives, etc. Emphasized both subjective (effect on particular spouse) and objective (reasonable person) test. List has 22 examples but courts can consider other acts too. Case widely cited; expanded concept of mental cruelty significantly.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q31: State the principle in Naveen Kohli case (2006).**

**ANSWER:**
**Naveen Kohli v. Neelu Kohli (2006) 4 SCC 558** - Supreme Court recognized **irretrievable breakdown of marriage** as ground for divorce. **Held:** (1) When marriage has irretrievably broken down with no possibility of reconciliation, court can grant divorce even if not specifically pleaded under traditional grounds, (2) Long separation indicates breakdown, (3) Forcing parties to continue dead marriage is itself cruelty to both, (4) Court can use Article 142 powers to grant divorce on irretrievable breakdown. Though not explicit ground in Section 13, judicially recognized under cruelty and constitutional powers. Reflects modern no-fault approach to divorce.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q32: State the principle in Vishnu Dutt Sharma case (2009).**

**ANSWER:**
**Vishnu Dutt Sharma v. Manju Sharma (AIR 2009 SC 489)** - Important judgment on mutual consent divorce under Section 13B. **Held:** (1) Under Section 13B, parties need not prove any traditional ground (adultery, cruelty, desertion) for divorce, (2) Mutual consent itself is sufficient, (3) Court should not deny decree if Section 13B conditions satisfied (1 year separation, joint petition, mutual agreement), (4) Court cannot investigate reasons for breakdown or insist on proof of grounds, (5) Purpose of Section 13B is to ease divorce for consenting parties. Principle: Parties' mutual decision to end marriage should be respected without fault-finding.

**[195 words]**

---

### **Q33: State the principle in Saroj Rani case (1984).**

**ANSWER:**
**Saroj Rani v. Sudarshan Kumar (AIR 1984 SC 1562)** - Supreme Court judgment upholding constitutional validity of Section 9 (restitution of conjugal rights). **Held:** (1) Section 9 does not violate Articles 14 (Equality) and 21 (Life and Liberty), (2) Purpose is reconciliation and preservation of marriage, not forced cohabitation, (3) Decree cannot be executed by physical force, (4) Reasonable restriction justified in interest of marriage institution, (5) Promotes opportunity for reflection and cooling. Overruled Andhra Pradesh High Court's decision in T. Sareetha case which had struck down Section 9. Section 9 continues to be valid law.

**[190 words]**

## üéØ SECTION G: STATUTORY SECTIONS

---

### **Q34: Explain Section 11 of Hindu Marriage Act.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 11 declares certain marriages void and null from beginning. **Text:** "Any marriage solemnized after commencement of this Act shall be null and void... if it contravenes any one of the conditions specified in clauses (i), (iv) and (v) of section 5." **Three grounds:** (1) Living spouse [5(i)], (2) Sapinda relationship [5(iv)], (3) Prohibited relationship [5(v)]. **Effect:** Marriage has no legal existence ab initio; creates no rights; decree merely declaratory; anyone can challenge; no time limit; can be declared void even after death. Criminal liability for bigamy (IPC 494/495).

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q35: Explain Section 12 of Hindu Marriage Act.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 12 makes certain marriages voidable - valid until annulled by decree. **Five grounds:** (1) Impotence [12(1)(a)] - 1 year limit, (2) Mental incapacity [12(1)(b)] - no limit, (3) Underage [12(1)(c)] - 2 years after majority, (4) Force/Fraud [12(1)(c)] - 1 year after cessation/discovery, (5) Pregnancy by another [12(1)(d)] - 1 year from knowledge. **Bars [Section 12(2)]:** Time limits, approbation, death of either party. Only aggrieved party can petition. Marriage valid and creates full rights until decree. Decree is constitutive. Cannot be annulled after death of either party.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q36: Explain Section 7 of Hindu Marriage Act.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 7 deals with ceremonies of Hindu marriage. **Section 7(1):** Marriage may be solemnized according to **customary rites and ceremonies** of either party. No particular form mandatory - custom of either party sufficient. **Section 7(2):** Where ceremonies include **Saptapadi** (seven steps before sacred fire), marriage becomes **complete and binding when seventh step is taken**. Saptapadi involves taking seven steps jointly representing seven vows. Sacred fire (Agni) serves as divine witness. If essential ceremonies not performed and it is required by custom, marriage may be void. Different communities have different ceremonies - all valid if customary.

**[190 words]**

---

### **Q37: What is Section 19 - Jurisdiction?**

**ANSWER:**
Section 19 prescribes jurisdiction for matrimonial petitions. Petition can be presented to District Court within whose jurisdiction: (1) **Marriage was solemnized**, OR (2) **Parties last resided together**, OR (3) **Respondent resides** at time of petition, OR (4) **Petitioner resides** if: (a) residing for at least 1 year before petition, AND (b) respondent not residing in India. **Four alternative jurisdictions** - petitioner can choose. Multiple courts may have jurisdiction simultaneously. Proper jurisdiction essential otherwise petition liable to be dismissed. Ensures access to justice and convenience.

**[180 words]**

---

### **Q38: What is Section 24 of Hindu Marriage Act?**

**ANSWER:**
Section 24 provides for maintenance pendente lite (during litigation) and expenses of proceedings. Either spouse having no sufficient independent income can claim: (1) Litigation expenses, (2) Monthly maintenance during proceedings. Court considers both parties' income and circumstances to fix reasonable amount. **Target:** Application disposed within **60 days** from service of notice. **Purpose:** Enable financially weaker spouse to contest/defend proceedings; ensure access to justice; prevent economic hardship during litigation. Gender-neutral provision. Continues till final decree. **Case: Chaturbhuj v. Sita Bai (1970)** - Remedial provision, liberal construction.

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q39: What is Section 25 of Hindu Marriage Act?**

**ANSWER:**
Section 25 provides for permanent alimony and maintenance after decree. Court can order at time of or after decree of divorce, nullity, judicial separation, or restitution. Either spouse can claim. **Forms:** (1) Lump sum (one-time), (2) Monthly/periodical, (3) Secured by property charge. **Factors:** Respondent's income/property, applicant's income/property, conduct, other circumstances (RICO). **Modification [25(2)]:** On change of circumstances. **Termination [25(3)]:** On remarriage, unchastity/adultery. Maximum term: Life of applicant. Quantum: Often **33% of net income** (guideline). Gender-neutral though predominantly claimed by wives.

**[185 words]**

---

### **Q40: Explain Section 26 - Custody of children.**

**ANSWER:**
Section 26 empowers court to make orders regarding custody and maintenance of minor children during matrimonial proceedings. Court can pass orders regarding: (1) Custody of children, (2) Maintenance of children, (3) Education expenses, (4) Visitation rights. **Guiding Principle:** **Welfare of child is paramount consideration**. Factors considered: Age of child, character of parents, financial capacity, wishes of child (if old enough), conduct of parents, ability to provide care. **Tender Years Doctrine:** Young children (especially girls under 7) generally with mother unless contrary to welfare. Orders can be modified based on changed circumstances. Child's interest always supreme.

**[190 words]**
