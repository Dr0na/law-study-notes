## ‚ùì QUESTION 1

**Q1-SRC-5M:** Explain custom as a source of Hindu Law. What are the essential conditions for a valid custom?

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

**INTRODUCTION:**
Custom (Achara) is one of the ancient sources of Hindu Law and has significant legal recognition. Section 3(a) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 recognizes custom and usage as part of Hindu Law.

**DEFINITION:**
Custom refers to practices and usages that have been continuously and uniformly observed by a particular community, family, or locality and are considered legally binding.

**TYPES OF CUSTOM:**
1. **Local Custom:** Confined to particular locality
2. **Family Custom:** Peculiar to particular family
3. **Class/Caste Custom:** Applicable to particular caste/community

**SIX ESSENTIAL CONDITIONS FOR VALID CUSTOM:**

**Mnemonic: "ACRCU-R"**

1. **Antiquity:** Must be ancient and immemorial (long-standing)
2. **Certainty:** Must be clear, definite, and ascertainable
3. **Reasonableness:** Not opposed to public policy, morality, or statute
4. **Continuity:** Continuously observed without interruption
5. **Uniformity:** Uniformly followed by community
6. **Recognition:** Recognized by community as binding obligation

**BURDEN OF PROOF:**
Party claiming benefit of custom must prove its existence and validity.

**CASE LAW:**
**Collector of Madura v. Moottoo Ramalinga (1868):** Privy Council held custom must be proved as fact; burden on party claiming custom.

**LEGAL POSITION:**
Valid custom can override written texts. Section 5(iv) and 5(v) of HMA allow custom to save marriages within sapinda/prohibited relationships.

**CONCLUSION:**
Custom remains a vital source of Hindu Law, especially in matters of marriage ceremonies and certain regional practices, provided it satisfies the six essential conditions.

**Authority:** Highest religious authority; considered divine and infallible.

1. **Manu Smriti** (Most authoritative)

4. Gautama, Apastamba, Vasistha Smriti

- Detailed rules on marriage, inheritance, adoption

|-----------|-----------|------------|

Shruti and Smriti represent foundation and practical codification of Hindu Law respectively. While Shruti provided divine principles, Smriti translated them into workable legal rules, together forming the classical basis of Hindu Law.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 3

**Q3-SCH-5M:** Explain the Mitakshara School of Hindu Law.

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

**INTRODUCTION:**
Mitakshara is one of the two major schools of Hindu Law, applicable to most of India except Bengal and Assam.

**BASIC INFORMATION:**

**Author:** Vijnaneshwara (11th Century CE)
**Text:** Commentary on Yajnavalkya Smriti
**Meaning:** "Measured in words" or "Brief commentary"
**Area:** Most of India (except Bengal, Assam)

**FIVE SUB-SCHOOLS:**

**Mnemonic: "BMBD-P"**
1. **Benaras** (UP, North India)
2. **Mithila** (Bihar)
3. **Bombay** (Maharashtra, Gujarat) - Vyavahara Mayukha
4. **Dravida/Madras** (South India) - Smriti Chandrika
5. **Punjab** (Punjab, Haryana)

**KEY PRINCIPLES:**

**1. Coparcenary:**
- Narrower body than joint family
- Males within 4 degrees from common ancestor
- 2005 Amendment: Daughters also coparceners

**2. Birth Right:**
Son acquires interest **by birth** in ancestral property.

**3. Survivorship:**
On coparcener's death, share passes by **survivorship** to surviving coparceners (not by succession).

**4. Joint Family Property:**
- Ancestral property: All coparceners have birthright
- Self-acquired: Individual's separate property

**5. Partition:**
Any coparcener can demand partition as right.

**MODERN IMPACT:**
Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 made daughters coparceners equal to sons, significantly impacting Mitakshara principles.

**CASE LAW:**
**Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020):** Clarified daughter's coparcenary rights retrospectively.

**CONCLUSION:**
Mitakshara School's concepts of birthright, coparcenary, and survivorship fundamentally shaped Hindu inheritance law, though statutory reforms have modified classical principles to ensure gender equality.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 4

**Q4-SCH-5M:** Write a short note on Dayabhaga School of Hindu Law.

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

**INTRODUCTION:**
Dayabhaga is the second major school of Hindu Law, applicable in Bengal and Assam regions.

**BASIC INFORMATION:**

**Author:** Jimutavahana (12th Century CE)
**Text:** Original treatise (not commentary)
**Meaning:** "Division of Inheritance"
**Area:** Bengal (West Bengal) and Assam

**FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE:**
Rejects Mitakshara's doctrine of birthright and survivorship.

**KEY PRINCIPLES:**

**1. NO BIRTH RIGHT:**
- Sons do NOT acquire interest by birth
- Father has absolute ownership during lifetime
- Sons inherit only after father's death

**2. SUCCESSION, NOT SURVIVORSHIP:**
- Property passes by succession (inheritance) upon death
- Not by survivorship
- Heirs take according to succession rules

**3. SAPINDA RELATIONSHIP:**
- Through father: Up to **7 degrees**
- Through mother: Up to **5 degrees**
- Determines inheritance and marriage prohibitions

**4. FATHER'S ABSOLUTE POWER:**
- Can alienate property freely
- No need for son's consent
- Sons have no present interest (only expectancy)

**5. BETTER POSITION FOR WIDOWS:**
- Widow inherits equal to son
- Better rights than in classical Mitakshara

**COMPARISON WITH MITAKSHARA:**

| **Aspect** | **Mitakshara** | **Dayabhaga** |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Birth Right | Yes | No |
| Succession | Survivorship | Succession |
| Father's Power | Limited | Absolute |
| Area | Most of India | Bengal, Assam |

**MODERN POSITION:**
Hindu Succession Act, 1956 unified succession rules, reducing practical distinctions. However, coparcenary concepts still differ.

**CONCLUSION:**
Dayabhaga School's rejection of birthright and adoption of succession principles distinguished it fundamentally from Mitakshara, though modern legislation has reduced practical differences between the two schools.

## ‚ùì QUESTION 5

**Q5-SRC-5M:** Discuss legislation as a source of Hindu Law with special reference to Hindu Code Acts.

### **üìã MODEL ANSWER:**

**INTRODUCTION:**
Legislation has emerged as the supreme source of Hindu Law in modern times, overriding all other traditional and customary sources. The Hindu Code Acts of 1955-56 marked the most significant codification of Hindu Law.

**SUPREMACY OF LEGISLATION:**
- Statutes override Shruti, Smriti, customs, and precedents
- Represent will of people through Parliament
- Provide uniformity and certainty

**CASE LAW:**
**Commissioner of Wealth Tax v. R. Sridharan (1976):** Statutory law supersedes personal law.

**THE HINDU CODE ACTS (4 Acts):**

**Mnemonic: "MMSA" or "1955, then 3 in 1956"**

**1. Hindu Marriage Act, 1955:**
- Codifies marriage law
- Conditions for valid marriage
- Void and voidable marriages
- Divorce and matrimonial remedies
- Maintenance provisions

**Revolutionary Changes:**
- Introduced divorce (not in classical law)
- Mandatory monogamy
- Minimum age prescribed
- Matrimonial reliefs provided

**2. Hindu Succession Act, 1956:**
- Governs intestate succession
- Class I and Class II heirs
- 2005 Amendment: Daughters coparceners

**3. Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956:**
- Guardianship of minors
- Natural and legal guardians
- Court's powers

**4. Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956:**
- Adoption procedure codified
- Women's right to adopt
- Maintenance provisions

**WHY LEGISLATION IS SUPREME:**

1. **Democratic Legitimacy:** Enacted by elected Parliament
2. **Uniformity:** Applies across India
3. **Certainty:** Clear, written rules
4. **Reformability:** Can be amended
5. **Social Justice:** Removes discrimination
6. **Constitutional Compliance:** Ensures fundamental rights

**CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS:**
- **Article 44:** Directive to secure Uniform Civil Code
- **Article 372:** Continuation of existing laws

**CONCLUSION:**
Legislation, particularly the four Hindu Code Acts, has become the primary and supreme source of Hindu Law, successfully codifying, reforming, and modernizing traditional Hindu law while maintaining its essential character. The Acts represent triumph of legislative reform in personal law, bringing uniformity, certainty, and gender justice to Hindu Law.
