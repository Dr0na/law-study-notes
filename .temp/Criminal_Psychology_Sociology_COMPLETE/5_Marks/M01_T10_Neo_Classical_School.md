# Neo-Classical School: Key Modifications

**Module:** 01 - Crime, Criminal and Criminology  
**Marks:** 5  
**PYQ Reference:** 2021 (Oct), 2020 (May), 2019 (Dec), 2018 (Oct)  
**Frequency:** 4 | **ROI:** 7/10 | **Priority:** ⚠️ MEDIUM

---

## **BACKGROUND**

- Emerged in late 18th to early 19th century
- Reaction to rigidity of Classical School
- Introduced flexibility while retaining core Classical principles
- Bridge between Classical and Positivist approaches

---

## **KEY MODIFICATIONS (5)**

---

### **1. Recognition of Individual Circumstances**
- Consideration of mental state of offender
- Recognition of diminished responsibility
- Age factor (children treated differently)
- First-time vs repeat offenders distinction

---

### **2. Mitigation and Aggravation**

**Mitigating Circumstances:**
- Mental illness or insanity
- Infancy (young age)
- Provocation by victim
- Duress or coercion

**Aggravating Circumstances:**
- Premeditation and planning
- Extreme cruelty
- Multiple victims
- Repeat offending

---

### **3. Judicial Discretion**
- Judges given discretion in sentencing within limits
- Punishment can be individualized
- Consider specific circumstances of each case
- Balance certainty with flexibility

---

### **4. Modified Free Will**
- Acknowledged free will not absolute
- Some circumstances limit choice
- Mental capacity affects responsibility
- Degrees of culpability recognized

---

### **5. Partial Focus on Criminal**
- Shift from pure crime-focus to considering offender
- Balance between act severity and offender's circumstances
- Beginning of individualization in sentencing

---

## **COMPARISON**

| Aspect | Classical | Neo-Classical |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Free Will | Absolute | Modified |
| Punishment | Fixed | Flexible |
| Mental State | Ignored | Considered |
| Age | Not considered | Children different |

---

## **CONCLUSION**

Neo-Classical School modified Classical rigidity by recognizing individual circumstances, introducing judicial discretion, and acknowledging that free will is not absolute. These modifications made criminal justice more humane and flexible while retaining Classical principles of legality and proportionality. Modern systems reflect Neo-Classical influence in individualized sentencing and consideration of mitigating factors.
