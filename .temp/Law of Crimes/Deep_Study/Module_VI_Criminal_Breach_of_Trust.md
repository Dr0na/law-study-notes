## üìù Introduction

Criminal Breach of Trust (CBT) is an important offense against property under BNS 2023, covered under **Sections 316-319** (previously Sections 405-409 IPC). It involves a **breach of trust relationship** where a person who is entrusted with property or dominion over property dishonestly misappropriates or converts it. Unlike theft, where property is taken without consent, CBT involves **betrayal of confidence** reposed in the accused. The offense strikes at the foundation of trust relationships in society and commercial transactions.

---

## üìñ Section 316 BNS: Definition

### ‚öñÔ∏è Complete Provision (Previously Section 405 IPC)

**"Whoever, being in any manner entrusted with property, or with any dominion over property:**

**(a) dishonestly misappropriates or converts to his own use that property, or**

**(b) dishonestly uses or disposes of that property in violation of any direction of law prescribing the mode in which such trust is to be discharged, or of any legal contract, express or implied, which he has made touching the discharge of such trust, or**

**(c) willfully suffers any other person so to do,**

**commits criminal breach of trust."**

## üß© 4 Essential Ingredients of Criminal Breach of Trust

---

### **1Ô∏è‚É£ ENTRUSTMENT OF PROPERTY OR DOMINION OVER PROPERTY**

#### **A. Entrustment - The Foundation**

**Requirement**: There must be **entrustment** of property or dominion over property to the accused

**"Entrustment"**: 
- Property delivered or handed over to accused
- Accused given custody, control, or authority over property
- Relationship of trust created

**Two Forms of Entrustment**:

**(i) Entrustment with Property**:
- Actual physical **custody** given to accused
- Accused has property in his possession

**Example**: 
- Customer deposits money with banker
- Principal hands goods to agent
- Employer gives cash to employee

**(ii) Entrustment with Dominion Over Property**:
- **Control or authority** over property given
- May not have actual physical custody
- Power to manage or deal with property

**Example**:
- Power of attorney to manage property
- Trustee managing trust property
- Executor administering estate

---

#### **B. Characteristics of Entrustment**

**1. Lawful Entrustment**:
- Property must be entrusted **lawfully**
- Not stolen or taken without permission
- Voluntary transfer of custody/control

**2. Fiduciary/Trust Relationship**:
- Relationship of trust between parties
- Could be as agent, trustee, executor, employee, banker, etc.
- Confidence reposed in accused

**3. Ownership NOT Transferred**:
- Accused gets only **custody/control**, NOT ownership
- Property still belongs to entruster
- Accused holds property for benefit of owner

**4. In Any Manner**:
- Entrustment can be by any means
- Express agreement OR implied
- Written contract OR oral understanding

---

#### **Importance of Entrustment**

**Case Law: Ramaswamy Nadar v. State of Madras** (AIR 1957 SC)

**Held**: "Entrustment is **very important** in cases of criminal breach of trust. Unless there exists entrustment, there can be **no offense** under Section 405 IPC (now Section 316 BNS)."

**Principle**: Entrustment is **sine qua non** (essential condition) for CBT.

---

### **2Ô∏è‚É£ DISHONEST MISAPPROPRIATION OR CONVERSION**

#### **A. Three Prohibited Acts**

**Act (a): Dishonest Misappropriation**

**"Misappropriation"**: 
- Setting apart property for oneself
- Treating entrusted property as one's own
- Using property for own purposes

**Examples**:
- Agent using sale proceeds for personal expenses
- Employee using employer's petty cash for own needs
- Trustee using trust funds for personal investment

---

**Act (b): Dishonest Conversion to Own Use**

**"Conversion"**:
- Changing nature or character of property
- Dealing with property as if owner
- Disposing of property

**Examples**:
- Trustee selling trust property and using proceeds
- Banker transferring customer's money to own account
- Agent selling principal's goods and keeping money

---

**Act (c): Dishonest Use/Disposal in Violation of Direction or Contract**

**"In violation of"**:
- Direction of law prescribing mode of trust discharge, OR
- Legal contract (express or implied) regarding discharge of trust

**Examples**:
- Executor using estate property contrary to will
- Trustee investing funds in prohibited investments
- Agent depositing money in wrong bank contrary to instructions

---

#### **B. "Dishonestly" - Essential Element**

**Section 23 BNS**: "Dishonestly" means intention to cause:
- **Wrongful gain** to oneself or another, OR
- **Wrongful loss** to another

**Key Elements**:
- Accused knows he has no legal right to use property
- Intends to gain or cause loss wrongfully
- Acts contrary to trust reposed

**Test**: Did accused act with knowledge that he was violating trust and causing wrongful gain/loss?

---

### **3Ô∏è‚É£ PROPERTY SUBJECT TO ENTRUSTMENT**

**Requirement**: Property misappropriated must be:
- The **same property** entrusted, OR
- **Proceeds** of that property, OR
- **Substitutes** for that property

**Key Point**: Cannot breach trust over property never entrusted

**Examples**:

‚úÖ **Property Entrusted**:
- Agent entrusted with goods worth Rs. 1 lakh
- Agent sells goods (lawful act)
- Agent misappropriates Rs. 1 lakh sale proceeds
- CBT of **proceeds** (which represent entrusted goods)

‚úÖ **Dominion Over Property**:
- Trustee managing property portfolio
- Trustee sells one property and buys another (authorized)
- Trustee misappropriates new property
- CBT of property under his dominion

---

### **4Ô∏è‚É£ VIOLATION OF TRUST**

**Requirement**: Use/disposal of property must be:
- In violation of **direction of law**, OR
- In violation of **legal contract** (express or implied)
- Touching discharge of trust

**Direction of Law**:
- Statutory provisions governing trust relationship
- Example: Trust Act provisions, Company law provisions

**Legal Contract**:
- **Express contract**: Written agreement, specific instructions
- **Implied contract**: Understanding about property use, duties implied by relationship

**Examples**:

**Violation of Law**:
- Executor disposing of estate before probate
- Trustee investing in prohibited securities

**Violation of Contract**:
- Agent instructed to deposit in Bank A, deposits in Bank B
- Employee using petty cash contrary to office rules
- Trustee selling property contrary to trust deed

## üìä DISTINCTION: Criminal Breach of Trust vs Theft

| **Aspect** | **THEFT** | **CRIMINAL BREACH OF TRUST** |
|------------|----------|------------------------------|
| **Entrustment** | ‚ùå No entrustment - taken without permission | ‚úÖ Property **entrusted lawfully** |
| **Consent** | Without consent | With consent (entrusted) |
| **Possession** | Takes from **possession of another** | Already in **lawful possession** |
| **Relationship** | No prior relationship | **Fiduciary/trust relationship** |
| **Trust** | No trust violated | **Breach of trust** relationship |
| **Taking** | Accused **takes** secretly | Accused **already has** property |
| **Manner** | Stealthily | By misappropriation/conversion |
| **Example** | Pickpocketing | Agent misusing client money |
| **Section** | Section 303 BNS | Section 316 BNS |
| **Punishment** | Up to 3 years | Up to 3 years (simple), Life (aggravated) |

---

### **Key Distinction Explained**

**THEFT**:
- Property taken **without owner's knowledge or consent**
- No prior relationship
- Taking is the crime

**CRIMINAL BREACH OF TRUST**:
- Property **lawfully entrusted** to accused
- Trust relationship exists
- **Betrayal** of trust is the crime

**Classic Distinction**:
- In **theft**, accused has **no right** to possess property initially
- In **CBT**, accused has **right to possess** but no right to misappropriate

## üìñ Types of Criminal Breach of Trust (Aggravated Forms)

### **Section 317 BNS: CBT by Carrier, etc.**

**Previously Section 407 IPC**

**Who**: Carrier, wharfinger, or warehouse-keeper

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** + fine

**Rationale**: Enhanced trust - entrusted professionally to transport/store

**Example**: Warehouse-keeper dishonestly sells stored goods

---

### **Section 318 BNS: CBT by Clerk or Servant**

**Previously Section 408 IPC**

**Who**: Clerk or servant

**In Respect Of**: Master's property

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** + fine

**Rationale**: Breach of employment trust

**Example**: Employee stealing or misusing employer's property

---

### **Section 319 BNS: CBT by Public Servant, Banker, Merchant, Agent**

**Previously Section 409 IPC**

**Who**:
- Public servant
- Banker
- Merchant
- Factor
- Broker
- Attorney
- Agent

**Punishment**: **Life imprisonment**, OR imprisonment up to **10 years** + fine

**Rationale**: Highest trust - public confidence, professional relationship

**Examples**:
- Banker misappropriating customer's deposits
- Public servant misusing government funds
- Agent misappropriating principal's money

---

### **Comparative Punishment**

| **Type of CBT** | **Section** | **Punishment** |
|----------------|------------|---------------|
| Simple CBT | 316 BNS | Up to 3 years |
| By Carrier/Warehouse-keeper | 317 BNS | Up to 7 years |
| By Clerk/Servant | 318 BNS | Up to 7 years |
| By Public Servant/Banker/Agent | 319 BNS | **Life or 10 years** |

## üèõÔ∏è Important Case Laws

### **1. Ramaswamy Nadar v. State of Madras** (AIR 1957 SC)

**Issue**: Whether entrustment necessary for CBT?

**Held**: **Entrustment is essential** - without entrustment, no CBT offense

**Principle**: Words "converts to his own use" necessarily connote that accused has used or dealt with property in **derogation of rights of owner**.

---

### **2. Jaswantrai Manilal Akhaney v. State of Bombay** (1956 SCR 483)

**Issue**: Nature of relationship in CBT?

**Held**: Relationship is between **transferor and transferee**

**Clarification**:
- Transferor remains **owner** of property
- Only **legal custody** entrusted with accused
- Transferee acquires **special interest**, not ownership
- No right to dispose contrary to terms

**Principle**: Accused gets custody, not ownership - any contravention is CBT.

---

### **3. State of Orissa v. Bishnu Charan Muduli** (1985 Cr LJ 1573)

**Facts**: Head Constable forcefully took articles from boatman who recovered them from dead body, kept them dishonestly

**Held**: Guilty of CBT under Section 404 IPC

**Principle**: Dishonestly keeping property of deceased person constitutes CBT.

---

### **4. Sadhupati Nageswara Rao v. State of A.P.** (AIR 2012 SC)

**Issue**: What must prosecution prove in CBT cases?

**Held**: Prosecution must prove:
1. Accused was **entrusted** with property
2. Accused was **duly bound** to account for property
3. Accused committed **criminal breach** of that trust

**Principle**: All three elements must be established beyond reasonable doubt.

## üìö Summary

**Criminal Breach of Trust** (Section 316 BNS):
- Requires **entrustment** of property (custody/dominion)
- Accused **dishonestly misappropriates** or converts
- Property must be that which was entrusted
- Acts in **violation** of law or contract

**4 Essential Ingredients**:
1. Entrustment (lawful transfer of custody/control)
2. Dishonest misappropriation/conversion
3. Property entrusted or its proceeds
4. Violation of direction of law or legal contract

**Key Distinction from Theft**:
- **Theft**: No entrustment, taken without consent
- **CBT**: Lawfully entrusted, then betrayed

**Aggravated Forms**: Higher punishment for:
- Carrier/Clerk/Servant (7 years)
- Public Servant/Banker/Agent (**Life or 10 years**)

**Rationale**: Greater trust reposed, greater punishment for breach
