## üìù Introduction

Defamation is an offense that protects **reputation**, which is recognized as a valuable asset and quasi-property right. The law recognizes that reputation is earned through years of honest living and can be destroyed by malicious falsehoods. Defamation balances **protection of reputation** with **freedom of speech and expression** (Article 19(1)(a) of Constitution), providing specific exceptions where public interest or legitimate discourse prevails over individual reputation. Section 356 BNS carefully defines what constitutes defamation and provides **ten exceptions** where otherwise defamatory statements are not punishable.

---

## üìñ Section 356 BNS: Definition

### ‚öñÔ∏è Complete Provision (Previously Section 499 IPC)

**"Whoever, by words either spoken or written, or by signs or by visible representations or otherwise, makes or publishes any imputation concerning any person intending to harm, or knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm, the reputation of such person, is said, except in the cases hereinafter excepted, to defame that person."**

---

## üß© 4 Essential Ingredients

### **1Ô∏è‚É£ IMPUTATION**

**Requirement**: There must be an **imputation** (statement, assertion, allegation) concerning a person

**"Imputation"**: Any statement or suggestion attributing something to person

**Forms**:
- **Spoken words** (oral defamation - slander)
- **Written words** (written defamation - libel)
- **Signs** (gestures, symbols)
- **Visible representations** (pictures, cartoons, caricatures, videos)
- **Otherwise** (any other form)

**Modern Forms**:
- Social media posts (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram)
- WhatsApp messages/forwards
- Email communications
- Online articles/blogs
- YouTube videos
- Memes and digital content

---

### **2Ô∏è‚É£ CONCERNING A PERSON**

**Requirement**: Imputation must **concern a specific person**

**Who Can Be Defamed**:
- **Living natural persons** (individuals)
- **Companies** or incorporated associations
- **Dead persons** (in certain circumstances if affects living relatives)

**Identification Required**:
- Person must be **identifiable** from imputation
- Need not be explicitly named
- If reasonable person can identify, sufficient

**Examples**:
- "The CM of State X is corrupt" - if only one CM, identifiable
- "A senior minister" - if context makes clear who, identifiable

**Cannot Defame**:
- Government or public bodies as such
- Large unidentifiable groups

---

### **3Ô∏è‚É£ INTENT TO HARM OR KNOWLEDGE/BELIEF IT WILL HARM**

**Two Alternative Mental States**:

**(a) Intending to Harm Reputation**:
- Positive intention to damage reputation
- Malice toward person
- Deliberate aim to injure

**(b) Knowing or Having Reason to Believe It Will Harm**:
- Knowledge that statement will harm reputation
- OR reasonable person would know it will harm
- May not actively intend, but aware of effect

**Test**: Did accused know or should have known that statement would harm reputation?

**Key Point**: Either **intention** OR **knowledge/belief** sufficient - not both required.

---

### **4Ô∏è‚É£ HARM TO REPUTATION**

**Requirement**: Imputation must harm or be likely to harm **reputation**

**"Reputation"**: 
- Estimation in which person is held by others in society
- Person's standing in community
- How person is regarded by right-thinking people

**What Harms Reputation**:
- **Lowers person** in estimation of others
- **Exposes person** to hatred, contempt, or ridicule
- Causes person to be **shunned or avoided**
- **Injures person** in profession, trade, or calling
- Affects person's **social standing**

**Test - Objective Standard**:
- Not what particular person feels
- What would **right-thinking members of society** think?
- Would reasonable person's estimation of subject be lowered?

**Examples**:
- Calling person "thief" or "murderer" (lowers estimation)
- Stating person engaged in immoral conduct (causes to be shunned)
- False allegations affecting professional reputation (injures in calling)

## üõ°Ô∏è 10 EXCEPTIONS TO DEFAMATION

Section 356 provides **ten exceptions** where statements that might otherwise be defamatory are NOT punishable:

---

### **Exception 1: Imputation of Truth for Public Good**

**When**: It is not defamation to impute anything which is **true** concerning any person, if it be for **public good** that the imputation should be made or published.

**Two Requirements**:
1. Imputation must be **TRUE**
2. Publication must be for **PUBLIC GOOD**

**Public Good**: Benefit to society, public interest

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Exposing **corruption** of public official
- ‚úÖ Revealing **criminal activities** of person
- ‚úÖ Publishing facts about public figure's misconduct affecting public
- ‚ùå Publishing person's private embarrassing truths not affecting public

**Test**: Is there public interest in knowing this truth?

---

### **Exception 2: Public Conduct of Public Servants**

**When**: It is not defamation to express in good faith any opinion whatever respecting **conduct of public servant** in discharge of **public functions**.

**Requirements**:
1. Must be about **public servant**
2. Conduct in **discharge of public duties**
3. **Opinion** (not false statement of fact)
4. Expressed in **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Criticizing minister's policy decisions
- ‚úÖ Questioning bureaucrat's administrative actions
- ‚úÖ Commenting on judge's handling of cases
- ‚ùå Making false factual allegations against personal life

**Rationale**: Democratic accountability requires public servants' official conduct be open to criticism.

---

### **Exception 3: Conduct Touching Public Questions**

**When**: It is not defamation to express in good faith any opinion respecting **conduct of any person touching any public question**.

**Requirements**:
1. Person involved in **public affairs**
2. Conduct relates to **public question**
3. Opinion expressed in **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Criticizing activist's role in public movement
- ‚úÖ Commenting on business person's role in public policy
- ‚úÖ Questioning celebrity's stand on social issue

**Rationale**: Public discourse on public questions must be free.

---

### **Exception 4: Publication of Court Reports**

**When**: It is not defamation to publish **substantially true report** of proceedings of Court.

**Requirements**:
1. Report of **court proceedings**
2. **Substantially true** (need not be verbatim)
3. Published in **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Newspaper reporting court judgment
- ‚úÖ Publishing court observations
- ‚úÖ Reporting evidence in trial

**Rationale**: Court proceedings are public, open justice principle.

---

### **Exception 5: Merits of Case Decided**

**When**: It is not defamation to express in good faith opinion on **merits of case decided** by Court or on **conduct** of witnesses/parties.

**Requirements**:
1. About **decided case** (not pending)
2. Opinion on merits or conduct
3. In **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Legal commentary on court decision
- ‚úÖ Criticizing witness's credibility in decided case
- ‚úÖ Analyzing party's conduct in completed trial

---

### **Exception 6: Merits of Public Performance**

**When**: It is not defamation to express in good faith opinion on **merits of any performance** submitted to judgment of public or on character of author.

**Requirements**:
1. Performance **submitted to public**
2. Opinion on merits or character as related to public performance
3. In **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Book review
- ‚úÖ Movie/play criticism
- ‚úÖ Art critique
- ‚úÖ Literary criticism
- ‚ùå Attacking author's private life unrelated to work

**Rationale**: Public performances invite public criticism - part of democratic discourse.

---

### **Exception 7: Censure by Lawful Authority**

**When**: It is not defamation for person having **lawful authority** to pass censure in good faith on conduct of another.

**Requirements**:
1. Person has **lawful authority** over another
2. Censure passed in **good faith**
3. Within scope of authority

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Teacher criticizing student's conduct
- ‚úÖ Employer's performance review
- ‚úÖ Parent disciplining child
- ‚úÖ Judge's remarks about lawyer's conduct

**Rationale**: Persons in authority must be able to perform duties including criticism.

---

### **Exception 8: Accusation Preferred in Good Faith**

**When**: It is not defamation to prefer accusation in good faith to authorized person for investigation.

**Requirements**:
1. Accusation made in **good faith**
2. To person with **authority** to inquire/investigate
3. For **protection** of interests

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Complaint to police about suspected crime
- ‚úÖ Report to employer about employee misconduct
- ‚úÖ Complaint to regulatory authority
- ‚ùå False accusation with malice

**Rationale**: Good faith complaints to authorities should not be chilled.

---

### **Exception 9: Imputation for Protection of Interests**

**When**: It is not defamation to make imputation in good faith for **protection of interests** of person making it or another.

**Requirements**:
1. Made in **good faith**
2. For protection of **legitimate interests**
3. Reasonable and proportionate

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Warning friend about dishonest person
- ‚úÖ Informing employer about employee's misconduct
- ‚úÖ Alerting family about unsuitable marriage prospect

**Rationale**: Protecting legitimate interests by sharing information.

---

### **Exception 10: Caution for Public Good**

**When**: It is not defamation if imputation made in good faith, conveyed as **caution** for good of person to whom conveyed or public good.

**Requirements**:
1. Intended as **caution/warning**
2. For good of recipient **or public**
3. Made in **good faith**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Warning public about **quack doctor**
- ‚úÖ Alerting people about fraudulent schemes
- ‚úÖ Consumer warnings about dangerous products

**Rationale**: Public safety and consumer protection require warnings.

## üìä Criminal vs Civil Defamation

| **Aspect** | **CRIMINAL** | **CIVIL** |
|------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Nature** | Public wrong/offense | Private wrong/tort |
| **Objective** | **Punishment** of offender | **Compensation** to victim |
| **Remedy** | Imprisonment + fine | Damages (money) |
| **Initiation** | Criminal complaint/prosecution | Civil suit |
| **Proof** | Beyond reasonable doubt | Preponderance of probabilities |
| **Parties** | State vs Accused | Plaintiff vs Defendant |
| **Focus** | Public interest, maintain peace | Private injury, restitution |
| **Result** | Conviction/acquittal + sentence | Damages awarded or dismissed |

---

## üìä Punishment

**Simple Defamation** (Section 356):
- Imprisonment up to **2 years**, OR
- Fine, OR
- Both

**Printed or Engraved Defamation**:
- Imprisonment up to **2 years**, OR
- Fine, OR
- Both

**Nature**:
- **Non-cognizable** (police cannot arrest without warrant)
- **Bailable**
- **Compoundable**
