## üìò Introduction to Right of Private Defense

### ‚öñÔ∏è Fundamental Principle

**Right of Private Defense** is based upon the **instinct of self-preservation**, which is vested in every human being and has been recognized by law in all civilized countries.

### üîπ Legal Basis

**Section 34 BNS**: Nothing is an offense which is done in the exercise of the right of private defense.

### üß† Nature of the Right

**Preventive, Not Punitive**:
- It is a **defensive** right, not an offensive one
- Purpose: To **prevent harm**, not to punish wrongdoer
- Arises only when State protection unavailable immediately

**Doctrine of Necessity**:
- Rooted in need for self-preservation
- Recognized in all democratic societies
- Subject to reasonable limits

---

## üîë 2 Types of Right of Private Defense

The right of private defense is available in **TWO situations**:

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Right of Private Defense of BODY** (Sections 34-38)

**Purpose**: Protection of one's own body or body of another from danger

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **Right of Private Defense of PROPERTY** (Sections 39-44)

**Purpose**: Protection of one's own property or property of another

---

## üìò PART I: Right of Private Defense of BODY

### Section 34-38 BNS

---

### üîπ When Right Commences and Continues

**Section 35: Commencement and Continuance**

#### Commencement:
- Right commences as soon as **reasonable apprehension of danger** to body arises
- From **threat** of an offense
- Even if offense not yet commenced

**Example**:
- A, pointing gun at B, threatens to shoot
- B's right of defense commences immediately
- Need not wait for A to actually shoot

#### Continuance:
- Continues **as long as apprehension** of danger continues

**Cessation**:
- Right ceases when:
  - Danger has passed
  - Apprehension no longer reasonable
  - Assailant has withdrawn
  - Assailant is disabled

**üìå Important**: No right of defense against public servants acting in good faith in discharge of duty, even if legally erroneous

---

### üîπ 6 Situations When Right Extends to Causing DEATH

**Section 36: When extends to causing death**

The right of private defense of body extends to **voluntarily causing death** or any other harm to assailant in the following **SIX situations**:

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault Causing Reasonable Apprehension of Death**

**Situation**:
- Such assault as may **reasonably cause apprehension** that **death will otherwise be the consequence** of such assault

**Example**:
- A attacks B with sharp weapon at vital organ
- B may cause death of A to protect himself

**Rationale**: When life threatened, extreme defense justified

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault Causing Reasonable Apprehension of Grievous Hurt**

**Situation**:
- Such assault as may **reasonably cause apprehension** that **grievous hurt will otherwise be the consequence** of such assault

**What is Grievous Hurt**:
- Emasculation
- Permanent privation of sight
- Permanent privation of hearing
- Privation of any member or joint
- Destruction or permanent impairment of powers of any member or joint
- Permanent disfiguration of head or face
- Fracture or dislocation of bone or tooth
- Any hurt endangering life or causing severe bodily pain for 20 days

**Example**:
- A attacks B with acid
- B may cause death of A to prevent grievous hurt

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault with Intent to Commit Rape**

**Situation**:
- Assault with intention of **gratifying unnatural lust**
- Or committing **rape**

**Scope**:
- Protects against sexual assault
- Gender-neutral protection

**Example**:
- A attempts to rape B
- B may cause death of A in defense

**Rationale**: Protection of sexual autonomy and dignity

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault with Intent to Gratify Unnatural Lust**

**Situation**:
- Assault with intention of **gratifying unnatural lust**
- Includes sodomy and other unnatural sexual acts

**Example**:
- A attempts unnatural sexual act on B by force
- B may cause death of A to protect herself/himself

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault with Intent to Kidnap or Abduct**

**Situation**:
- Assault with intention of **kidnapping or abducting**

**Who Can Exercise**:
- Person being kidnapped/abducted
- Any other person witnessing such attempt

**Example**:
- A attempts to kidnap B's child
- B may cause death of A to prevent kidnapping

**Rationale**: Personal liberty protection

---

### 6Ô∏è‚É£ **Assault with Intent to Wrongfully Confine**

**Situation**:
- Assault with intention of **wrongfully confining a person**
- Under circumstances which may **reasonably cause apprehension** that person will be unable to have recourse to public authorities for release

**Example**:
- A forcibly drags B toward isolated location
- B may cause death of A if believes will be confined without access to help

**Key Element**: Fear of being unable to access public authorities

---

### üîπ When Right Does NOT Extend to Causing Death

**Section 37: When does NOT extend to causing death**

The right of private defense of body **NEVER extends to causing death** if:

#### 3 Limitations:

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Time to Seek Public Authority Protection**

**Rule**: If there is **time** to have recourse to protection of **public authorities**

**Example**:
- A threatens to assault B next week
- B has time to approach police
- B cannot claim right to kill A now

**Rationale**: State protection must be sought when available

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **Risk Only of Harm Other Than Listed**

**Rule**: If assault does not **reasonably cause apprehension** of:
- Death, OR
- Grievous hurt, OR
- Rape, OR
- Unnatural lust, OR
- Kidnapping/abduction, OR
- Wrongful confinement with inability to access authorities

**Example**:
- A threatens to slap B
- Simple hurt only
- B cannot cause death of A
- Can only use proportionate force

**Principle**: **Proportionality** - defense must be proportionate to attack

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **More Harm Than Necessary**

**Rule**: Right does not extend to inflicting **more harm than necessary** for defense

**Example**:
- A attacks B with fists
- B can stop A by pushing
- B cannot shoot A with gun
- That would be more harm than necessary

**Test**: **Necessity Test** - only harm necessary for defense permitted

---

### üìå Important Case Law

**Key Cases on Private Defense of Body**:

1. **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab**
   - Held: Right of private defense available even in case of mistake if belief honest and reasonable

2. **Vidhya Singh v. State of MP**
   - Held: Right must be exercised in good faith and without malice

3. **James Martin v. State of Kerala**
   - Held: Burden on accused to prove he acted in private defense

## üìò PART II: Right of Private Defense of PROPERTY

### Sections 39-44 BNS

---

### üîπ When Right Commences and Continues

**Section 40: Commencement and Continuance**

#### Against Theft:
- Commences: When **reasonable apprehension** of theft
- Continues: Till theft or attempt ceases
- Ceases: When property recovered or thief apprehended

#### Against Robbery:
- Commences: When robbery **commences**
- Continues: While offender in commission or immediate flight with stolen property
- Ceases: When offender apprehended or property recovered

#### Against Mischief or Criminal Trespass:
- Commences: When mischief or trespass **commences**
- Continues: While offender engaged in mischief or trespass
- Ceases: When offender ceases

---

### üîπ 6 Situations When Right Extends to Causing DEATH

**Section 41: When extends to causing death**

The right of private defense of property extends to **voluntarily causing death** in the following **SIX situations**:

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **ROBBERY**

**Definition**: Theft or extortion with use or threat of force

**Scope**:
- Right extends to causing death when defending against robbery
- Most dangerous form of property offense
- Involves violence or threat

**Example**:
- Armed robbers enter A's house
- A may cause death to prevent robbery

**Rationale**: Robbery inherently dangerous; involves threat to both person and property

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **House-Breaking by NIGHT**

**Definition**: House-breaking committed after sunset and before sunrise

**Why Night-Time Specifically**:
- Greater danger in darkness
- Difficult to identify perpetrator
- Limited access to help
- Increased vulnerability

**Example**:
- B breaks into A's house at 2 AM
- A may cause death of B

**Rationale**: Night-time intrusion creates heightened danger

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **Mischief by FIRE to Dwelling House**

**Definition**: Causing destruction or damage by fire to any building used as dwelling

**Scope**:
- Arson to dwelling house
- Endangers lives of occupants
- Causes extensive property damage

**Example**:
- A sets fire to B's house where B resides
- B or anyone may cause death of A to prevent

**Rationale**: Fire to dwelling poses extreme danger to life and property

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ **Theft, Mischief, or House-Trespass Under Aggravating Circumstances**

**Circumstances**:
- Committed by **night** AND
- Under circumstances causing **reasonable apprehension** of:
  - Death, OR
  - Grievous hurt, OR
  - Assault with intent to commit rape or unnatural lust

**Example**:
- A breaks into B's house at night with weapon
- Circumstances suggest danger to life
- B may cause death of A

**Key**: Night-time + reasonable apprehension of danger to person

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ **Mischief by Fire to Property (Not Dwelling)**

**Definition**: Causing destruction by fire to any property other than dwelling house

**Scope**:
- Fire to shops, warehouses, factories
- Agricultural produce, forests
- Vehicles, machinery

**Example**:
- A sets fire to B's factory
- B may cause death to prevent destruction

**Note**: Extends to any property, not just dwelling

---

### 6Ô∏è‚É£ **Mischief by Explosive Substance**

**Definition**: Causing mischief by explosive substance where likely to cause death, hurt, or damage to property

**Scope**:
- Bombing, explosives
- Extremely dangerous acts
- Indiscriminate harm

**Example**:
- A places bomb in B's property
- Anyone may cause death of A to prevent explosion

**Rationale**: Explosives create imminent danger to life and extensive property damage

---

### üîπ When Right Does NOT Extend to Causing Death

**Section 42: When does NOT extend to causing death**

The right of private defense of property **NEVER extends to causing death** unless:

#### 3 Conditions Must Be Met:

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **No Time for Public Authority**

**Rule**: Unless offense is **one of the SIX situations** listed in Section 41

**AND** there is **no time** to have recourse to protection of public authorities

**Example**:
- Simple theft in daylight when police nearby
- Cannot cause death
- Can approach police

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **Reasonable Apprehension of Danger to Person**

**Rule**: Unless reasonable apprehension that:
- Death, OR
- Grievous hurt, OR
- Rape/unnatural lust

will result if defense not exercised

**Example**:
- Theft of property when no danger to person
- Cannot cause death
- Only proportionate force

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **Proportionality Principle**

**Rule**: More harm than **necessary** must not be caused

**Test**: **Minimum Force Necessary**

**Example**:
- Simple trespass can be stopped by pushing
- Cannot shoot trespasser if pushing sufficient

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è General Principles Applicable to BOTH

### üîπ 6 Universal Principles

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **Good Faith Requirement**

**Rule**: Right must be exercised in **good faith**

**Meaning of Good Faith** (Section 52 BNS):
- With **due care and attention**
- Honest belief
- Without malice

**üìå Case**: If person honestly but mistakenly believes in danger, may claim right if belief reasonable

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **Proportionality Principle**

**Rule**: Force used must be **proportionate** to harm apprehended

**Test**: **No more harm than necessary**

**Examples**:
- ‚úÖ Push against push
- ‚úÖ Restraint against simple assault
- ‚ùå Gunshot against slap (disproportionate)

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **Preventive, Not Punitive**

**Rule**: Right is to **prevent** harm, not to **punish** offender

**Implications**:
- Cannot continue after danger passed
- Cannot chase and harm fleeing offender
- Not revenge or retaliation

**Example**:
- A attacks B
- B successfully defends and A flees
- B cannot chase and harm A
- Right ceased when danger passed

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ **No Right Against Public Servants**

**Rule**: No right of private defense against **public servants** acting in **good faith** in discharge of duty

**Even if**: Act technically illegal but done in good faith

**Rationale**: Prevent violence against law enforcement

**Exception**: If public servant acting in **bad faith** or clearly **exceeding authority**, limited right may exist

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ **Right Available to Third Parties**

**Rule**: Can exercise right in defense of:
- **Own** body/property
- Body/property of **any other person**

**Example**:
- A attacks B
- C (stranger) may defend B
- Extends to defense of strangers

**Rationale**: Society's collective interest in preventing crime

---

### 6Ô∏è‚É£ **Burden of Proof**

**Rule**: **Accused bears burden** to prove private defense

**Standard**: Preponderance of probability (not beyond reasonable doubt)

**What to Prove**:
- Reasonable apprehension of danger existed
- Force used was necessary and proportionate
- Acted in good faith

**üìå Case**: James Martin v. State of Kerala - Burden on accused clear

---

## üîÑ Exception to Murder

### üìå Exception 2 to Section 100 (Murder)

**Legal Principle**: If person **exceeds** right of private defense in **good faith**, offense becomes:

- **Culpable Homicide NOT amounting to Murder**
- Instead of Murder

**Conditions**:
1. Right of private defense existed
2. Right was **exceeded**
3. Excess was in **good faith**
4. Without intention to cause more harm than necessary

**Example**:
- A attacks B with stick
- B, in panic, shoots A dead
- Used more force than necessary
- But acted in good faith without malice
- Culpable Homicide, not Murder

**Punishment**: Lesser than murder (maximum 10 years instead of death/life)

## üìå Important Cases Summary

### 1. **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab**
- **Principle**: Honest and reasonable mistake doesn't defeat right
- **Held**: If accused honestly believed danger existed, right available

### 2. **Vidhya Singh v. State of MP**
- **Principle**: Good faith essential
- **Held**: Malice defeats right of private defense

### 3. **James Martin v. State of Kerala**
- **Principle**: Burden of proof
- **Held**: Accused must prove right existed on preponderance

### 4. **State of UP v. Ram Swarup**
- **Principle**: Proportionality
- **Held**: Force must be proportionate; excess defeats right

### 5. **Mohinder Pal Jolly v. State of Punjab**
- **Principle**: Preventive nature
- **Held**: Cannot continue after danger passed
