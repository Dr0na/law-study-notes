## üìù Understanding Conspiracy

### ‚öñÔ∏è Nature of Conspiracy

**Conspiracy** is an **inchoate crime** - an incomplete offense where the substantive crime may not be committed, yet criminal liability arises from the agreement itself.

**Special Character**: Conspiracy is primarily a **mental process** - a meeting of minds to do something unlawful.

---

## üìñ Section 61 BNS: Definition

### ‚öñÔ∏è Complete Provision (Previously Section 120A IPC)

**Section 61(1)**: "When two or more persons agree to do, or cause to be done‚Äî

**(a) an illegal act, or**

**(b) an act which is not illegal by illegal means,**

such an agreement is designated as a **criminal conspiracy**."

**Proviso**: "Provided that no agreement except an agreement to commit an offence shall amount to a criminal conspiracy unless some **act** besides the agreement is done by one or more parties to such agreement in pursuance thereof."

**Section 61(2) - Exception**: "It is immaterial whether the illegal act is the ultimate object of such agreement, or is merely incidental to that object."

## üß© 3 Essential Ingredients of Conspiracy

---

### **1Ô∏è‚É£ AGREEMENT BETWEEN TWO OR MORE PERSONS**

#### **Minimum Number Requirement**

**Two or more persons** must agree

**Key Points**:
- **Minimum 2 persons** essential
- Cannot have one-person conspiracy
- Maximum number unlimited

**Nature of Agreement**:
- Can be **express** (explicitly stated), OR
- Can be **implied** (inferred from conduct)
- Need not be formal written agreement
- Tacit understanding sufficient

---

#### **Proof of Agreement**

**Direct Evidence**: Rarely available
- Usually no written agreement for crime
- Meetings may be secret

**Indirect/Circumstantial Evidence**: Usually relied upon
- Can be inferred from:
  - Conduct of parties
  - Circumstances of case
  - Actions showing common purpose
  - Communications between parties

**Test**: Did parties have meeting of minds? Did they share common purpose?

---

#### **Case Law: Topandas v. State of Bombay** (1956)

**Principle**: Conspiracy is primarily a **mental process** - an agreement between two or more persons to do an illegal act.

**Held**: Direct evidence of agreement not necessary - can be inferred from circumstances.

---

### **2Ô∏è‚É£ AGREEMENT TO DO ILLEGAL ACT OR LEGAL ACT BY ILLEGAL MEANS**

#### **Two Categories of Conspiracy**

**Category (a): Agreement to Do ILLEGAL Act**

**Requirement**: Agreement is to do something **illegal**

**"Illegal Act"**: 
- Act forbidden by law
- Criminal offense under BNS or other laws

**Examples**:
- Agreement to commit murder
- Agreement to commit theft
- Agreement to commit dacoity
- Agreement to forge documents

**Key Point**: The **ultimate object** is illegal

---

**Category (b): Agreement to Do Legal Act by ILLEGAL Means**

**Requirement**: 
- Ultimate act/object itself is **legal**
- But **means** to achieve it are **illegal**

**Examples**:
- Agreement to recover debt (legal) by illegal confinement (illegal means)
- Agreement to win contract (legal) by bribing officials (illegal means)
- Agreement to get property (legal) by forging documents (illegal means)

**Key Point**: **End is legal**, but **means are illegal**

---

#### **Immaterial Whether Ultimate or Incidental**

**Section 61(2)** clarifies:
- Does NOT matter if illegal act is **ultimate object**
- OR if illegal act is merely **incidental** to object

**Example**:
- Primary object: Rob bank
- Incidental: May need to assault guard
- Both constitute conspiracy

---

### **3Ô∏è‚É£ OVERT ACT (With Important Exception)**

#### **General Rule - Overt Act Required**

**Proviso to Section 61(1)**:
"No agreement except an agreement to commit an **offence** shall amount to criminal conspiracy unless some **act** besides the agreement is done by one or more parties in pursuance thereof."

**Requirement**: 
- **Agreement alone** is NOT sufficient (general rule)
- Some **overt act** must be done
- By **one or more** parties to agreement
- **In pursuance of** the agreement (furtherance of conspiracy)

**"Overt Act"** means:
- Any act done to carry out conspiracy
- Need not be the substantive offense itself
- Any step toward execution sufficient
- Preparatory acts sufficient

**Examples of Overt Acts**:
- Buying weapons for planned robbery (preparation)
- Conducting reconnaissance of target (preparation)
- Traveling to location for crime (step toward execution)
- Any act showing conspiracy in motion

---

#### **EXCEPTION - No Overt Act Required**

**Exception**: If conspiracy is to commit offense punishable with:
- **Death**, OR
- **Life imprisonment**, OR
- **Rigorous imprisonment for 2 or more years**

Then **NO overt act required** - mere agreement is punishable.

**Rationale**: 
- Serious offenses pose grave danger
- Agreement itself shows serious criminal intent
- Law intervenes earlier for serious crimes

---

#### **When Overt Act Required vs Not Required**

**‚úÖ Overt Act REQUIRED** (for punishment):
- Conspiracy to commit simple theft (< 2 years RI)
- Conspiracy to cheat
- Conspiracy to commit mischief
- Conspiracy to cause simple hurt

**‚ùå Overt Act NOT Required** (agreement alone punishable):
- Conspiracy to commit **murder** (death/life punishment)
- Conspiracy to commit **dacoity** (‚â• 2 years RI)
- Conspiracy to wage **war** (death/life)
- Conspiracy to commit **robbery** (‚â• 2 years RI)

---

### **Formula**

```
For Most Offenses:
Agreement alone ‚Üí NOT punishable
Agreement + Overt Act ‚Üí PUNISHABLE

For Serious Offenses (Death/Life/RI ‚â•2 yrs):
Agreement alone ‚Üí PUNISHABLE
(No overt act needed)
```

## üìä DISTINCTION: Conspiracy vs Abetment

| **Aspect** | **CONSPIRACY** | **ABETMENT** |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Section** | Section 61 BNS | Sections 45-60 BNS |
| **Definition** | Agreement to do illegal act | Instigating, conspiring, or aiding |
| **Forms** | Only one form (agreement) | **3 forms** (instigation, conspiracy, aid) |
| **Persons** | Minimum **2 required** | Can be by **1 person** (instigation/aid) |
| **Agreement** | **Essential** | Not always (instigation, aid) |
| **Overt Act** | Generally **required** (except Exception) | Generally **NOT required** |
| **Nature** | Independent offense + form of abetment | Broader concept |
| **Punishment** | Same as offense OR 6 months | Same as offense (if committed) |
| **Example** | A & B agree to rob, take steps | A instigates B to rob |

---

### **Key Differences Explained**

**1. Dual Nature of Conspiracy**:
- Conspiracy has **dual character**:
  - (a) **Independent offense** under Section 61
  - (b) **Form of abetment** under Section 45(2)

**2. Number of Persons**:
- **Conspiracy**: Minimum 2 persons essential (agreement between parties)
- **Abetment**: Can be by single person (through instigation or aid)

**3. Agreement Requirement**:
- **Conspiracy**: Agreement is the essence, core element
- **Abetment**: Agreement not always needed (instigation and aid don't require agreement)

**4. Overt Act**:
- **Conspiracy**: Generally requires overt act (except serious offenses)
- **Abetment**: Generally no overt act required to punish abettor

---

## üìñ Punishment for Conspiracy

### **Section 61 BNS - Punishment Provision**

**Two Categories**:

**Category 1: Conspiracy to Commit Offense Punishable with Death/Life/RI ‚â•2 Years**
- Punished with **same punishment** as for that offense
- OR If no punishment specified: Imprisonment up to 6 months + fine

**Category 2: Conspiracy to Commit Other Offenses**
- Punished in **same manner** as if person had abetted offense
- Reference to abetment punishment provisions

**General Provision**: If no specific punishment, imprisonment up to **6 months** + fine + both

---

### **Examples**

**Conspiracy to Commit Murder**:
- Murder punishment: Death or life
- Conspiracy to commit murder: Same punishment possible

**Conspiracy to Commit Theft**:
- Theft punishment: 3 years
- Conspiracy to commit theft: Same as abetment OR 6 months

## üèõÔ∏è Important Case Laws

### **1. Topandas v. State of Bombay** (1956)

**Principle**: 
- Conspiracy is primarily a **mental process**
- Agreement is the **gist** of offense
- Can be inferred from **circumstances**
- Direct evidence not necessary

**Held**: Conspiracy can be proved by circumstantial evidence showing meeting of minds.

---

### **2. State of Maharashtra v. Som Nath Thapa** (1996)

**Issue**: Distinction between conspiracy as offense and as mode of abetment

**Held**: Conspiracy has **dual character**:
1. **Independent crime** (Section 61)
2. **Form of abetment** (Section 45)

**Principle**: Can be charged as conspiracy OR as abetment by conspiracy.

---

### **3. Kehar Singh v. State** (1988)

**Issue**: When does conspiracy continue?

**Held**: Conspiracy continues until:
- Object achieved, OR
- Agreement abandoned, OR
- Conspirators arrested

**Principle**: Once conspiracy formed, each conspirator liable for acts of others in furtherance.
