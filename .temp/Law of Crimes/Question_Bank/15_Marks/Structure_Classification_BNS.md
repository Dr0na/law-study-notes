### **STRUCTURE AND CLASSIFICATION OF BHARTIYA NYAYA SANHITA 2023**

#### **Introduction**

The Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 is the **new criminal code of India**, replacing the Indian Penal Code, 1860, which was enacted during British colonial rule. The BNS represents a **historic transformation** of India's criminal justice system, making it more **contemporary, Indian-centric, and aligned with constitutional values**. Effective from July 1, 2024, the BNS retains the fundamental structure of criminal law while introducing significant reforms, removing colonial legacy provisions, and incorporating modern offenses. This marks a shift from colonial criminal jurisprudence to a truly **Bharatiya** (Indian) system of criminal law.

---

### **I. HISTORICAL BACKGROUND**

#### **A. Colonial Era - Indian Penal Code 1860**

**Origins**:
- Drafted by **Lord Macaulay** and Law Commission
- Enacted in **1860** during British rule
- Came into force on **January 1, 1862**
- Based on British criminal law principles

**Characteristics**:
- Colonial mindset and objectives
- Designed to protect British interests
- Some provisions reflecting colonial control
- Served colonial administration needs

**Longevity**:
- Served India for **163 years** (1860-2023)
- One of longest-serving criminal codes
- Underwent numerous amendments over time

---

#### **B. Need for Reform**

**Post-Independence Concerns**:
- Retained colonial character
- Some obsolete provisions
- Need for indigenous, constitutional approach
- Align with modern values and technology

**Key Issues with IPC**:
1. Colonial provisions (sedition as tool of control)
2. Obsolete offenses (adultery provisions struck down)
3. Gaps in modern crimes (cyber crimes, terrorism)
4. Need for speedier justice
5. Victim-centric approach needed

---

#### **C. Transformation to BNS**

**Development**:
- Long-pending demand for reforms
- Parliamentary Committee recommendations
- Public consultations
- Expert committee inputs

**Enactment**:
- **Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita** passed in 2023
- Replaces Indian Penal Code, 1860
- Effective from **July 1, 2024**

**Philosophy**:
- **Bharatiya** (Indian) in character
- Constitutional values-oriented
- Modern, technology-aware
- Victim and justice-centric

---

### **II. STRUCTURE OF BHARTIYA NYAYA SANHITA 2023**

#### **A. Overall Structure**

**Organization**:
- Total **23 Chapters**
- Approximately **358 Sections**
- Systematic classification by subject matter
- Follows logical progression

**Comparison with IPC**:
- IPC: 23 Chapters, 511 Sections
- BNS: 23 Chapters, 358 Sections (streamlined)

---

#### **B. Chapter-Wise Structure**

**PRELIMINARY CHAPTERS**:

**Chapter I**: Introduction (Sections 1-3)
- Title, extent, application
- Definitions and explanations

**Chapter II**: General Explanations (Sections 4-13)
- Definitions applicable throughout
- Interpretation clauses

**Chapter III**: Punishments (Sections 4-13)
- Types of punishments
- Scales of punishment

**Chapter IV**: General Exceptions (Sections 14-44)
- Age-based exceptions (infancy)
- Mental capacity (insanity, intoxication)
- Mistake of fact
- Accident, necessity
- Right of private defense
- Consent, good faith

---

**SUBSTANTIVE OFFENSE CHAPTERS**:

**Chapter V**: Abetment (Sections 45-60)
- Three forms of abetment
- Liability of abettor
- Punishment provisions

**Chapter VI**: Criminal Conspiracy (Section 61)
- Definition and ingredients
- Overt act requirement
- Punishment

**Chapter VII**: Offenses by or Relating to Public Servants (Sections 63-94)

**Chapter VIII**: Offenses Affecting Public Health, Safety, etc. (Sections 95-99)

**Chapter IX**: Offenses Against Human Body (Sections 100-146)
- Culpable homicide and murder
- Death by negligence
- Hurt, assault, kidnapping
- Wrongful restraint

**Chapter X**: Offenses Against Women and Children (Sections 63-86)
- Rape and sexual offenses
- Cruelty, harassment
- Modern offenses (stalking, voyeurism)

**Chapter XI**: Offenses Against Property (Sections 303-334)
- Theft, extortion, robbery
- Criminal breach of trust
- Cheating, mischief, trespass

**Chapter XII**: Offenses Relating to Documents (Sections 335-355)
- Forgery, using forged documents

**Chapter XIII**: Offenses Against Public Tranquility (Sections 189-201)
- Unlawful assembly, rioting, affray

**Chapter XIV**: Offenses Against the State (Sections 147-156)
- Waging war, sedition
- State security offenses

**Chapter XV-XXIII**: Other specialized offenses

Offenses under BNS can be classified on various bases:

#### **B. Based on Severity**

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Felonies/Serious Offenses**:

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Misdemeanors/Minor Offenses**:

#### **C. Based on Cognizability**

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Cognizable Offenses**:
- Police can arrest **without warrant**
- Investigation can start without court order

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Non-Cognizable Offenses**:

#### **D. Based on Bailability**

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Bailable Offenses**:
- Accused has **right to bail**
- Generally minor offenses
- Examples: Simple hurt, theft below certain value

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Non-Bailable Offenses**:
- Bail at **court's discretion**
- Generally serious offenses
- Examples: Murder, rape, dacoity

---

#### **E. Based on Compoundability**

**1Ô∏è‚É£ Compoundable Offenses**:
- Can be **settled** between parties
- With or without court permission
- Examples: Simple hurt, theft (with permission)

**2Ô∏è‚É£ Non-Compoundable Offenses**:
- **Cannot be settled/compromised**
- Public interest involved
- Examples: Murder, rape, offenses against state

#### **F. Based on Object/Subject Matter**

### **Conclusion**

The Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023 represents a **historic transformation** of India's criminal law from a colonial-era code to a truly Indian, modern, and constitutional criminal code. The structure of BNS, organized into 23 chapters covering preliminary matters, general exceptions, and specific offenses, provides a **comprehensive and logical framework** for criminal justice. The classification of offenses based on various criteria - cognizability, bailability, compoundability, and subject matter - ensures appropriate procedural handling and proportionate punishment.

The transition from IPC to BNS removes obsolete provisions, incorporates modern offenses, enhances victim protection, and aligns criminal law with constitutional values and contemporary needs. While retaining the sound principles and tested provisions of IPC, BNS modernizes the law to address 21st-century challenges, making it more accessible, just, and responsive to India's needs. This transformation marks India's journey toward a truly **indigenous and contemporary criminal justice system**.
