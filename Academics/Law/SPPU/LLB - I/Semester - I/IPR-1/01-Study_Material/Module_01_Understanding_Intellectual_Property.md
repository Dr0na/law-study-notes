## üí° 1. Meaning and Theories for Justification of Intellectual Property

### What is Intellectual Property?

Intellectual Property, often abbreviated as IP, refers to unique creations of the human mind. Unlike physical property such as land or buildings, intellectual property is intangible - you cannot touch or see it in a physical sense. However, these intangible creations can sometimes be more valuable than physical assets.

Think of intellectual property as the legal recognition and protection given to products of human intellect. When someone creates something new - whether it's an invention, a book, a piece of music, a brand name, or a design - they deserve recognition and the ability to benefit from their creation. This is where intellectual property rights come into play.

**Key Characteristics of Intellectual Property:**

- **Intangible Nature:** Unlike physical property, IP exists in the form of ideas, expressions, and innovations
- **Legal Protection:** IP is protected by law through various mechanisms like patents, copyrights, and trademarks
- **Economic Value:** IP can be sold, licensed, or used to generate revenue
- **Exclusive Rights:** IP owners have the right to prevent others from using their creation without permission
- **Limited Duration:** Most IP rights are granted for a specific period (except trademarks, which can be renewed indefinitely)

### Why Do We Need Intellectual Property Rights?

Imagine you spend years developing a new medicine that can cure a disease. If anyone could simply copy your formula and sell it without your permission, would you have been motivated to invest all that time and money in research? Probably not. This is precisely why intellectual property rights exist - to encourage innovation and creativity by ensuring that creators can benefit from their work.

### üìö Theories for Justification of Intellectual Property

The protection of intellectual property is justified through several philosophical and economic theories. These theories help us understand why society grants exclusive rights to creators and innovators.

#### **1. Natural Rights Theory**

This theory, rooted in the philosophy of John Locke, suggests that when a person applies their labor and intellect to create something, they have a natural right to own it. Just as you own the fruits you grow in your garden because you planted and nurtured them, you should own the intellectual creations that result from your mental labor.

**Key Principles:**
- Every person has a natural right to the products of their labor
- When you create something through your intellectual efforts, it belongs to you
- This right exists independent of any government or legal system
- Society must recognize and protect these natural rights

**Example:** If you write a novel, you have a natural right to control how it is published and distributed because it is the product of your mental labor.

#### **2. Utilitarian Theory / Incentive Theory**

This theory, associated with philosophers like Jeremy Bentham, focuses on the practical benefits to society. It argues that granting exclusive rights to creators encourages innovation and creativity, which ultimately benefits everyone.

**Key Principles:**
- IP rights create incentives for people to innovate and create
- Without these incentives, there would be less innovation
- The temporary monopoly granted to creators is justified because it leads to long-term social benefits
- The goal is to maximize overall happiness and progress in society

**Example:** Pharmaceutical companies invest billions in developing new drugs. Patent protection ensures they can recover their investment and make a profit, which encourages them to continue research and development.

#### **3. Social Contract Theory**

This theory suggests that intellectual property rights are part of an implicit agreement between creators and society. Creators agree to share their innovations with the public (through disclosure), and in return, society grants them exclusive rights for a limited period.

**Key Principles:**
- IP rights represent a bargain between innovators and society
- Creators disclose their inventions to the public
- In exchange, they receive temporary exclusive rights
- After the protection period expires, the creation enters the public domain for everyone to use

**Example:** When you file a patent, you must fully describe your invention. This disclosure helps advance scientific knowledge. In return, you get 20 years of exclusive rights.

#### **4. Reward Theory**

This theory posits that creators deserve to be rewarded for their contributions to society. The reward comes in the form of exclusive rights that allow them to commercialize their creations.

**Key Principles:**
- Innovation and creativity benefit society
- Creators deserve compensation for their contributions
- IP rights serve as a reward mechanism
- The reward should be proportional to the social benefit created

**Example:** An artist who creates beautiful paintings that enrich culture deserves to profit from selling copies or licensing their work.

#### **5. Personality Theory**

Associated with the philosopher Hegel, this theory suggests that intellectual creations are extensions of the creator's personality. Protecting IP is therefore about protecting the creator's identity and personal expression.

**Key Principles:**
- Creative works embody the creator's personality and spirit
- Controlling one's creations is essential to personal freedom and dignity
- IP rights protect the bond between creator and creation
- This theory particularly supports moral rights in copyright

**Example:** An author should have the right to prevent their book from being altered in ways that misrepresent their ideas or artistic vision.

### The Balance in IP Protection

It's important to understand that intellectual property rights involve a careful balance. While we want to reward and encourage creators, we also don't want to restrict access to knowledge and innovations so much that it hinders progress. This is why:

- IP rights are granted for limited periods (except trademarks)
- There are exceptions and limitations to IP rights (fair use, research exceptions, etc.)
- Some innovations cannot be patented (like mathematical formulas or laws of nature)
- The system aims to benefit both individual creators and society as a whole

## üìú 2. History and Origins of Intellectual Property

### Ancient Origins

The concept of protecting creative works and innovations is not new. Even in ancient times, societies recognized the value of rewarding innovation and creativity.

**Early Examples:**

- **Ancient Greece (500 BCE):** The city of Sybaris granted one-year monopolies to cooks who invented new dishes, encouraging culinary innovation
- **Roman Empire:** Trademarks were used on pottery and other goods to indicate their origin and quality
- **Medieval Europe:** Craftsmen's guilds controlled trade secrets and maintained standards of craftsmanship

### The Birth of Modern Patent Systems

#### **The Venetian Patent Statute (1474)**

The Republic of Venice enacted the first formal patent statute in 1474. This groundbreaking law established many principles that remain relevant today:

- Inventors were granted exclusive rights to their inventions for 10 years
- The invention had to be new and useful
- The patent could be revoked if the invention was not worked (produced) in Venice
- This encouraged inventors to disclose their innovations while protecting their rights

**Significance:** This statute served as a model for patent systems around the world and established the concept of granting temporary monopolies to encourage innovation.

#### **The English Statute of Monopolies (1623)**

In England, the Crown had been granting monopolies arbitrarily, leading to abuse and public resentment. The Statute of Monopolies, enacted in 1623, was a response to these abuses.

**Key Provisions:**
- Declared most monopolies void
- But allowed patents for "the sole working or making of any manner of new manufactures"
- Patents could be granted for up to 14 years
- The invention must be new and the inventor must be the first and true inventor

**Significance:** This statute laid the foundation for modern patent law in English-speaking countries.

### Evolution of Copyright Protection

#### **The Statute of Anne (1710)**

Enacted in England in 1710, the Statute of Anne is considered the first modern copyright law. Before this, copyright was controlled by guilds and royal privileges.

**Key Features:**
- Authors, not printers, were given rights to their works
- Protection lasted for 14 years, renewable for another 14 years if the author was alive
- The law aimed to encourage learning and protect authors

**Significance:** This statute shifted the focus from controlling printing to protecting authors' rights, establishing the principle that authors should benefit from their creative works.

### Development in India

#### **Pre-Independence Era**

During British colonial rule, intellectual property laws were introduced in India to align with British interests and facilitate trade.

- **Patents and Designs Act, 1911:** Applied to India, based on UK law
- **Copyright Act, 1914:** Extended British copyright law to India
- **Trade Marks Act, 1940:** Provided for trademark registration

#### **Post-Independence Development**

After gaining independence in 1947, India began developing its own IP framework:

**Patents:**
- **Patents Act, 1970:** Enacted after extensive debate about balancing innovation incentives with access to essential goods (especially medicines)
- Introduced product patents for most fields but only process patents for pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals
- Amended in 1999, 2002, and 2005 to comply with TRIPS Agreement

**Copyright:**
- **Copyright Act, 1957:** Consolidated and amended copyright law
- Amended multiple times (1983, 1984, 1992, 1994, 1999, 2012) to adapt to changing technologies and international obligations

**Trademarks:**
- **Trade Marks Act, 1999:** Replaced the 1940 Act, modernizing trademark law and aligning with international standards

**Other IP Laws:**
- **Designs Act, 2000:** Replaced the old designs law
- **Geographical Indications of Goods (Registration and Protection) Act, 1999:** Introduced GI protection
- **Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000:** Protected layout designs
- **Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act, 2001:** Balanced plant breeders' rights with farmers' rights

### The Global Evolution

The history of intellectual property has been marked by increasing internationalization:

- **19th Century:** Countries began recognizing the need for international cooperation
- **Late 19th Century:** Paris Convention (1883) and Berne Convention (1886) established international frameworks
- **20th Century:** Creation of WIPO (1967) and adoption of TRIPS Agreement (1995)
- **21st Century:** Continued harmonization and adaptation to digital technologies

## üåç 3. Internationalization of IP Protection

As trade and communication became global, the need for international cooperation in IP protection became evident. An inventor or creator who wanted protection in multiple countries had to navigate different laws, requirements, and procedures in each country. International conventions emerged to address these challenges.

### üìã Paris Convention for Protection of Industrial Property (1883)

The Paris Convention, adopted in 1883, was one of the first major international treaties on intellectual property. It covers patents, trademarks, industrial designs, and other forms of industrial property.

#### **Background and Purpose**

In the 19th century, inventors and businesses faced significant challenges when seeking protection in multiple countries. The Paris Convention aimed to create a more uniform and accessible system for protecting industrial property internationally.

#### **Key Principles of the Paris Convention**

**1. National Treatment**

This is one of the most fundamental principles. It means that each member country must grant the same protection to nationals of other member countries as it grants to its own nationals.

**Practical Example:** If a German inventor applies for a patent in India, the Indian Patent Office must treat the German inventor the same way it treats an Indian inventor. The German inventor receives the same rights, follows the same procedures, and has access to the same legal remedies.

**Why This Matters:** Without national treatment, countries could discriminate against foreign applicants, making it difficult for innovations to spread globally.

**2. Right of Priority**

This is perhaps the most important practical feature of the Paris Convention. It allows an applicant who has filed a patent or trademark application in one member country to file in other member countries within a specific time period and claim the filing date of the first application.

**Priority Periods:**
- **Patents and Utility Models:** 12 months
- **Trademarks and Industrial Designs:** 6 months

**How Priority Works:**

Let's say you invent a new device on January 1, 2024, and file a patent application in India on February 1, 2024. Under the Paris Convention:

- You have until February 1, 2025 (12 months later) to file patent applications in other Paris Convention member countries
- When you file in those countries, your applications will be treated as if they were filed on February 1, 2024 (the date of your Indian application)
- Any publications or patent applications filed by others between February 1, 2024, and the date you file in other countries will not be used against your application

**Why This Is Important:** This gives inventors time to assess the commercial potential of their invention and decide in which countries to seek protection, without losing their priority date.

**3. Independence of Patents**

Patents granted in different countries are independent of each other. This means:

- Grant of a patent in one country does not obligate other countries to grant a patent for the same invention
- Refusal or revocation of a patent in one country does not affect patents in other countries
- Each country examines patent applications according to its own laws and standards

**Example:** A patent granted in the United States can be refused in Japan if the Japanese Patent Office finds that it doesn't meet Japanese patentability requirements, or vice versa.

**4. Protection Against Unfair Competition**

The Convention requires member countries to provide effective protection against unfair competition, particularly:

- False indications of source or origin of goods
- Misleading claims about the nature or characteristics of goods
- Other acts that create confusion or mislead the public

#### **Significance of the Paris Convention**

- **First Major International IP Treaty:** Set the precedent for international IP cooperation
- **Wide Membership:** Over 170 countries are now members
- **Facilitates Global Trade:** Makes it easier for businesses to protect their innovations in multiple countries
- **India's Membership:** India joined the Paris Convention on December 7, 1998

### üìö Berne Convention for Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886)

The Berne Convention, adopted in 1886 in Bern, Switzerland, is the principal international agreement governing copyright. It aims to protect the rights of creators and their works across national borders.

#### **Background**

Before the Berne Convention, authors had little protection for their works abroad. A French author's book could be copied and sold in Germany without permission or compensation. The Berne Convention sought to create an international framework for copyright protection.

#### **Fundamental Principles of the Berne Convention**

**1. National Treatment**

Like the Paris Convention, the Berne Convention requires member countries to grant the same protection to works from other member countries as they grant to their own nationals.

**Example:** An American novel published in India must receive the same copyright protection as an Indian novel.

**2. Automatic Protection**

One of the most revolutionary aspects of the Berne Convention is that copyright protection is automatic - it exists from the moment a work is created and "fixed" in some material form.

**Key Features:**
- No registration is required
- No copyright notice (¬©) is necessary (though it's still advisable)
- No other formalities are required for protection

**What This Means:** When you write a poem or take a photograph, copyright protection exists immediately. You don't need to register it with any office or include a copyright notice for protection to apply.

**3. Independence of Protection**

Protection in the country where the work originates is independent of protection in other Berne Union countries. However, if a work is not protected in its country of origin, other member countries are not obligated to protect it.

**4. Minimum Standards of Protection**

The Convention sets minimum standards for copyright protection:

**Duration:** The Convention requires minimum protection of:
- Life of the author plus 50 years for most works
- 50 years from publication for anonymous works, pseudonymous works, and cinematographic works

**Works Covered:**
- Literary works (novels, poems, plays, etc.)
- Musical works
- Artistic works (paintings, sculptures, photographs)
- Dramatic works
- Cinematographic works
- And other creative works

**Rights Protected:**
- Right of reproduction
- Right of translation
- Right of public performance
- Right of broadcasting
- And other economic rights

**5. Moral Rights**

The Berne Convention introduced the concept of moral rights, which are separate from economic rights:

- **Right of Attribution (Paternity):** The author has the right to be identified as the creator of the work
- **Right of Integrity:** The author has the right to object to any modification, distortion, or other treatment of the work that would harm their honor or reputation

**Important Note:** Moral rights remain with the author even if they sell the economic rights (copyright) to someone else.

**Example:** If you sell your painting to a museum, they cannot modify it or display it in a way that misrepresents your artistic vision without your permission, even though they own the physical painting.

#### **Significance of the Berne Convention**

- **Global Copyright Framework:** Provides a unified international approach to copyright
- **Protects Creators:** Ensures that authors, artists, and composers can benefit from their works internationally
- **Facilitates Cultural Exchange:** Makes it easier for creative works to be disseminated across borders
- **Wide Membership:** Over 180 countries are members
- **India's Membership:** India has been a member since 1928

### üåê The TRIPS Agreement (1995)

The Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS) is an international agreement administered by the World Trade Organization (WTO). It came into effect on January 1, 1995, and represents the most comprehensive multilateral agreement on intellectual property.

#### **Background and Context**

By the late 20th century, intellectual property had become increasingly important in international trade. However, IP protection varied widely among countries, creating challenges for businesses and concerns about counterfeiting and piracy. The TRIPS Agreement was negotiated as part of the Uruguay Round of trade negotiations that led to the creation of the WTO.

#### **Objectives of TRIPS**

The Agreement aims to:
- Reduce distortions and impediments to international trade
- Promote effective and adequate protection of intellectual property rights
- Ensure that enforcement measures don't become barriers to legitimate trade
- Balance the interests of innovators with broader public interests

#### **Key Features of the TRIPS Agreement**

**1. Comprehensive Coverage**

Unlike previous conventions that focused on specific types of IP, TRIPS covers all major categories:
- Copyright and related rights
- Trademarks
- Geographical indications
- Industrial designs
- Patents
- Layout designs of integrated circuits
- Undisclosed information (trade secrets)

**2. Minimum Standards**

TRIPS sets minimum standards of protection that all WTO members must provide. Countries are free to provide more extensive protection, but they cannot provide less.

**For Patents:**
- Patent protection must be available for inventions in all fields of technology
- Protection for at least 20 years from the filing date
- Patent owners must have the exclusive rights to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented invention

**For Copyright:**
- Incorporates most provisions of the Berne Convention
- Protection for computer programs as literary works
- Protection for databases
- Rental rights for computer programs and cinematographic works
- Term of protection: 50 years for most works

**For Trademarks:**
- Protection for service marks as well as marks for goods
- Initial registration and renewals for at least 7 years
- Rights to prevent confusing use by third parties

**3. Enforcement**

A major innovation of TRIPS is its detailed provisions on enforcement:
- Civil and criminal procedures must be available
- Remedies must include injunctions and damages
- Border measures to prevent importation of counterfeit goods
- Effective enforcement without creating barriers to legitimate trade

**4. Flexibilities and Safeguards**

TRIPS includes several flexibilities, particularly for developing countries:

**Compulsory Licensing:** Governments can authorize the use of a patented invention without the patent holder's consent in certain circumstances, such as:
- National emergencies
- Public health crises
- Anti-competitive practices

**Parallel Importation:** Countries can allow imports of genuine goods from other countries (important for access to affordable medicines)

**Exceptions:** Limited exceptions to patent rights for research and experimentation

**5. Transition Periods**

TRIPS provided different implementation timelines:
- Developed countries: 1 year (by January 1, 1996)
- Developing countries: 5 years (by January 1, 2000)
- Least developed countries: Initially 10 years, extended multiple times (currently until 2034 for pharmaceuticals)

**6. Dispute Settlement**

TRIPS disputes can be addressed through the WTO's dispute settlement system, which provides:
- Binding decisions
- Possibility of trade sanctions for non-compliance
- More powerful enforcement than previous IP treaties

#### **Impact on India**

India, as a founding member of the WTO, had to amend its IP laws to comply with TRIPS:

**Patent Law:**
- Amended the Patents Act in 1999, 2002, and 2005
- Introduced product patents for pharmaceuticals (previously only process patents were available)
- Extended patent term to 20 years
- However, retained important safeguards like Section 3(d) to prevent evergreening of patents

**Copyright Law:**
- Extended protection to computer programs
- Strengthened enforcement provisions
- Introduced rental rights

**Other Laws:**
- Updated trademark, designs, and other IP laws
- Introduced new legislation for geographical indications and plant varieties

#### **Criticisms and Debates**

TRIPS has been controversial, particularly regarding:

**Access to Medicines:**
- High patent prices can make medicines unaffordable in developing countries
- The Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health (2001) clarified countries' rights to use flexibilities for public health purposes

**Traditional Knowledge:**
- TRIPS doesn't adequately protect traditional knowledge and biodiversity
- Developing countries have called for reforms to prevent biopiracy

**Development Concerns:**
- Some argue that TRIPS standards are too demanding for developing countries
- Others contend that IP protection is necessary for technology transfer and innovation

#### **The Doha Declaration (2001)**

In response to concerns about access to medicines, the WTO adopted the Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health in 2001:

**Key Points:**
- Affirmed that TRIPS can and should be interpreted to support public health
- Emphasized countries' rights to use flexibilities like compulsory licensing
- Addressed least developed countries' concerns about pharmaceutical patents

**Significance:** The Doha Declaration was crucial during health crises like the HIV/AIDS epidemic and later the COVID-19 pandemic.

## üè¢ 4. International Agencies for Coordination of IP Protection

International cooperation in intellectual property requires coordinating institutions. Two organizations play central roles:

### üåç World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO)

#### **Overview**

WIPO is a specialized agency of the United Nations dedicated to developing a balanced and accessible international intellectual property system. It was established by the WIPO Convention in 1967 and began operations in 1970.

**Headquarters:** Geneva, Switzerland

**Membership:** 193 member states (as of 2024)

#### **History and Background**

WIPO's origins trace back to 1883 when the Paris Convention established an International Bureau to handle administrative tasks. A similar bureau was created for the Berne Convention in 1886. In 1893, these bureaus merged to form the United International Bureaux for the Protection of Intellectual Property (BIRPI). This organization evolved into WIPO in 1967.

#### **WIPO's Mission and Objectives**

WIPO's mission is to:
- Promote the protection of intellectual property worldwide
- Foster creativity and innovation
- Ensure that IP systems reward inventors and creators while serving broader public interests
- Facilitate technology transfer, particularly to developing countries

#### **Key Functions of WIPO**

**1. Setting International Norms**

WIPO administers 26 international treaties on IP, including:
- **Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT):** Simplifies international patent filing
- **Madrid System:** Facilitates international trademark registration
- **Hague System:** Streamlines international design registration
- **Lisbon System:** Protects appellations of origin

**2. Global Protection Systems**

WIPO operates global registration systems:

**PCT (Patent Cooperation Treaty):**
- Allows inventors to file one international patent application
- Search and examination can be done centrally
- Applicant then decides in which countries to seek patents
- Saves time and money compared to filing separately in each country

**Madrid System (Trademarks):**
- File one application to register trademarks in multiple countries
- Manage registration through a single system

**Hague System (Industrial Designs):**
- Register designs in multiple countries through one application

**3. Capacity Building and Technical Assistance**

WIPO helps countries, especially developing nations, to:
- Develop and strengthen IP systems
- Train IP office staff and judges
- Modernize IP infrastructure
- Use IP for economic development

**4. Information and Statistics**

WIPO maintains extensive databases and resources:
- **PATENTSCOPE:** Free database of international patent applications
- **Global Brand Database:** Trademark information from multiple countries
- IP statistics and reports
- Research and economic analysis

**5. Dispute Resolution**

**WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center** provides:
- Domain name dispute resolution
- Alternative dispute resolution for IP conflicts
- Expertise in cross-border IP disputes

**6. Policy Forums**

WIPO provides a forum for:
- Policy debates on emerging IP issues
- Discussions on traditional knowledge and genetic resources
- Addressing challenges posed by new technologies

#### **WIPO's Role in Development**

WIPO emphasizes that IP should contribute to economic, social, and cultural development:

- **Development Agenda:** Adopted in 2007, includes 45 recommendations to ensure IP systems support development
- **Technology and Innovation Support Centers (TISCs):** Help researchers and inventors access technical information
- **IP for Business Programs:** Assist small and medium enterprises in using IP

#### **India and WIPO**

India is a member of WIPO and participates actively:
- Uses the PCT system for international patent filings
- Contributes to policy discussions
- Benefits from capacity-building programs
- Collaborates on traditional knowledge protection

### üèõÔ∏è World Trade Organization (WTO)

#### **Overview**

The World Trade Organization (WTO) is an international organization that regulates international trade. While its primary focus is trade, it plays a crucial role in intellectual property through the TRIPS Agreement.

**Headquarters:** Geneva, Switzerland

**Establishment:** January 1, 1995 (replacing the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade - GATT)

**Membership:** 164 member countries (as of 2024)

#### **WTO's Core Functions**

**1. Trade Liberalization**
- Negotiates reduction of trade barriers
- Promotes free and fair trade

**2. Rules and Agreements**
- Administers trade agreements, including TRIPS
- Provides a framework for trade relations

**3. Dispute Settlement**
- Provides a forum for resolving trade disputes
- Can authorize trade sanctions for non-compliance

**4. Trade Policy Review**
- Monitors members' trade policies
- Promotes transparency

#### **WTO and Intellectual Property: The TRIPS Agreement**

The WTO's role in IP stems from the TRIPS Agreement:

**Administration:**
- The WTO's Council for TRIPS oversees the agreement
- Monitors implementation by member countries
- Provides a forum for IP-related discussions

**Enforcement:**
- WTO's dispute settlement system applies to TRIPS disputes
- Provides binding decisions with potential for trade sanctions
- More powerful enforcement than traditional IP treaties

**Flexibility and Public Health:**
- Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health
- Recognizes countries' rights to protect public health
- Clarifies use of compulsory licenses and parallel imports

#### **Difference Between WIPO and WTO**

While both organizations deal with IP, they have different roles:

| **Aspect** | **WIPO** | **WTO** |
|------------|----------|---------|
| **Focus** | IP promotion and protection | International trade |
| **Nature** | UN specialized agency | Intergovernmental organization |
| **Treaties** | Administers 26 IP treaties | Administers TRIPS Agreement |
| **Enforcement** | No enforcement mechanism | Dispute settlement with sanctions |
| **Membership** | 193 countries | 164 countries |
| **Approach** | Facilitative, assistance-focused | Rules-based, enforcement-focused |

#### **Cooperation Between WIPO and WTO**

Despite their different mandates, WIPO and WTO cooperate:
- WIPO provides technical assistance to implement TRIPS
- Share information and expertise
- Work together on developing countries' capacity building
- Complement each other's roles in the global IP system

## üîë Key Takeaways from Module 01

1. **Intellectual Property** represents creations of the mind that have economic value and deserve legal protection

2. **Theories Justifying IP Protection** include natural rights (rewarding labor), utilitarian (incentivizing innovation), social contract (balancing disclosure and exclusivity), reward (compensating contributions), and personality (protecting identity)

3. **Historical Development** shows IP protection evolved from ancient practices to modern comprehensive systems, with India developing its framework both during colonial times and post-independence

4. **International Conventions** facilitate global IP protection:
   - **Paris Convention (1883):** Industrial property, national treatment, priority rights
   - **Berne Convention (1886):** Copyright, automatic protection, moral rights
   - **TRIPS Agreement (1995):** Comprehensive IP standards linked to trade

5. **International Organizations** coordinate global IP cooperation:
   - **WIPO:** Promotes IP protection, administers treaties, provides global registration systems
   - **WTO:** Enforces TRIPS through trade sanctions, balances IP with trade concerns

6. **The IP System** balances incentives for creators with public access to knowledge and innovation, using mechanisms like limited protection periods, exceptions, and flexibilities
