## üíæ 1. Layout Designs/Topographies Integrated Circuits

### What are Integrated Circuits?

An **integrated circuit (IC)**, also called a **microchip** or **chip**, is a set of electronic circuits on a small flat piece (chip) of semiconductor material, usually silicon. ICs are used in virtually all electronic equipment today - computers, smartphones, appliances, vehicles, etc.

### What is a Layout Design?

The **layout design** (or **topography**) of an integrated circuit refers to the three-dimensional disposition of the elements and interconnections of an integrated circuit, or such a three-dimensional disposition prepared for an integrated circuit intended for manufacture.

**In simpler terms:**
It's the arrangement and interconnection of the transistors, circuits, and other elements on the chip - the "blueprint" or "map" of how the chip is constructed.

**Why Protect Layout Designs?**

- **High Investment:** Developing IC layouts requires significant investment in research, design, and testing
- **Easy Copying:** Once manufactured, chips can be reverse-engineered and copied relatively easily
- **Commercial Value:** Layout designs have significant commercial value
- **Innovation:** Protection encourages continued innovation in semiconductor technology

### International Framework: TRIPS Article 35-38

TRIPS requires members to protect layout designs of integrated circuits.

**Article 35 - Relation to IPIC Treaty:**

Members agree to provide protection to layout designs in accordance with Articles 2-7 (excluding Article 6(3)), Article 12, and Article 16(3) of the **Treaty on Intellectual Property in Respect of Integrated Circuits (IPIC Treaty or Washington Treaty, 1989)**.

**Article 36 - Scope of Protection:**

**Protection applies to:**
- Layout designs that are original (result of creator's intellectual effort)
- Layout designs that are not commonplace among creators and manufacturers

**Rights Conferred:**
- Reproducing the protected layout design (copying)
- Importing, selling, or distributing for commercial purposes a protected layout design or products incorporating it

**Exceptions:**
- Acts done privately for non-commercial purposes
- Acts done for evaluation, analysis, research, or teaching
- Creating an original layout design as a result of evaluation or analysis (reverse engineering for creating a different, original design is allowed)

**Article 37 - Acts Not Requiring Authorization:**

**Exhaustion:** Once a product incorporating the layout design is put on the market by the right holder or with their consent, further dealings with that product are allowed (first sale doctrine).

**Innocent Infringement:** If someone incorporates an unlawfully reproduced layout design into a product without knowing it was unlawful, they can continue to sell existing stock but must pay reasonable royalty for future sales.

**Article 38 - Term of Protection:**

At least **10 years** from:
- The date of filing an application for registration, OR
- The first commercial exploitation anywhere in the world

### Indian Law: Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000

India enacted this Act to comply with TRIPS and protect layout designs.

#### **Key Features**

**Eligibility (Section 2(k), 9):**

A layout design is registrable if it is:
- **Original:** Result of the creator's intellectual effort
- **Not Commonplace:** Not commonplace among creators and manufacturers of IC at the time of creation

**Who Can Apply (Section 10):**

- The creator of the layout design
- Assignee of the creator
- Legal representative

**Procedure for Registration:**

1. **Application:** File with the Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Registry
2. **Examination:** Examiner checks for formalities and eligibility
3. **Registration:** If in order, the layout design is registered

**Rights of Registered Proprietor (Section 15):**

Exclusive right to:
- **Reproduce** the layout design (by incorporation into an IC or otherwise)
- **Import, sell, or distribute** for commercial purposes:
  - The registered layout design
  - An IC incorporating the registered layout design
  - An article incorporating such an IC (insofar as it continues to contain an unlawfully reproduced layout design)

**Exceptions (Section 16):**

The following do NOT infringe:

1. **Private and Non-Commercial Use:** Acts done privately and for non-commercial purposes
2. **Evaluation, Analysis, Research, Teaching:** Use for these purposes
3. **Reverse Engineering:** Creating an original layout design as a result of evaluation or analysis (permitted to promote innovation)
4. **Exhaustion:** Once a product is sold by the proprietor, further dealing with that product doesn't infringe

**Term of Protection (Section 18):**

**10 years** from:
- The date of filing the application for registration, OR
- The date of first commercial exploitation anywhere in the world (whichever is earlier)

**Infringement and Remedies (Section 22):**

**Civil Remedies:**
- Injunction
- Damages
- Accounts of profits
- Delivery up or destruction of infringing material

**Criminal Penalties (Section 24):**
- Imprisonment up to 3 years
- Fine up to ‚Çπ10 lakhs
- Or both

**Note:** Prosecution only after complaint by the proprietor or with sanction of the Central Government.

## üå± 2. Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights

### What are Plant Varieties?

A **plant variety** is a distinct, uniform, and stable population of cultivated plants that is distinguishable by specific characteristics.

**Examples:**
- Different varieties of rice (e.g., Basmati, Sona Masuri, IR-20)
- Different varieties of wheat
- Different varieties of cotton (e.g., Bt Cotton)

### Why Protect Plant Varieties?

- **Encourage Breeding:** Plant breeding requires significant time, effort, and investment. Protection provides incentives
- **Benefit Farmers:** Improved varieties increase agricultural productivity
- **Preserve Biodiversity:** Encourages conservation of plant genetic resources
- **Balance Rights:** Balance between breeders' rights and farmers' rights (especially in developing countries like India)

### International Framework

#### **TRIPS Article 27.3(b)**

Members shall provide for the protection of plant varieties either by:
- **Patents**, OR
- By an **effective sui generis system** (a special system designed specifically for plant varieties), OR
- By a **combination** of both

**"Sui generis" means:** Of its own kind; unique

Most countries have opted for sui generis systems, often based on the UPOV Convention.

#### **UPOV Convention (International Union for the Protection of New Varieties of Plants)**

The UPOV Convention establishes an international system for plant variety protection.

**Established:** 1961 (revised 1972, 1978, 1991)

**Key Features:**

**Plant Breeders' Rights (PBR):**
Rights granted to breeders of new plant varieties.

**Eligibility Criteria (DUS):**
- **Distinct:** Clearly distinguishable from any other known variety
- **Uniform:** Sufficiently uniform in its relevant characteristics
- **Stable:** Remains true to its description after repeated reproduction

**Rights Conferred:**
- Exclusive right to produce, reproduce, sell, export, import the variety
- Others cannot use the variety for commercial purposes without permission

**Term:**
Minimum 20 years for most plants, 25 years for trees and vines.

**Breeder's Exemption:**
Other breeders can use the protected variety for research and breeding new varieties.

**Farmer's Exemption:**
Farmers can save seed from their harvest for replanting on their own land (extent varies by UPOV version).

**Note:** UPOV 1991 is more restrictive on farmers' rights than UPOV 1978.

### Indian Law: Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act, 2001 (PPV&FR Act)

India chose the sui generis route and enacted a unique law that balances breeders' rights with farmers' rights.

#### **Objectives**

- Protect plant breeders' rights (to encourage investment in plant breeding)
- Protect farmers' rights (recognize farmers' contribution, allow seed saving and sharing)
- Promote biodiversity conservation
- Facilitate growth of the seed industry

#### **Types of Varieties Eligible for Registration (Section 15, 16)**

**1. New Varieties:**
Varieties that meet the DUS criteria (Distinct, Uniform, Stable) and are new.

**2. Extant Varieties:**
Varieties that are currently in use, even if they don't meet all novelty requirements. This includes:
- Farmers' varieties (varieties developed by farmers)
- Varieties in the public domain
- Varieties about which there is common knowledge

**Rationale:** Recognizes that varieties already in use have value and should be recorded and protected.

#### **Eligibility Criteria (Section 15)**

**1. Novelty:**
The variety must be new:
- Not sold or disposed of in India for more than one year before the date of application
- Not sold or disposed of outside India for more than specified periods (usually 4-6 years depending on the plant type)

**Relaxed for Extant Varieties.**

**2. Distinctness:**
Clearly distinguishable by at least one essential characteristic from any other variety whose existence is a matter of common knowledge.

**3. Uniformity:**
Subject to the variation expected from the propagation method, sufficiently uniform in its relevant characteristics.

**4. Stability:**
The essential characteristics remain unchanged after repeated propagation or, in case of a particular cycle of propagation, at the end of each cycle.

#### **Farmers' Rights (Sections 39-41)**

**This is a unique and progressive feature of Indian law.**

**1. Right to Save, Use, Sow, Resow, Exchange, Share, or Sell Farm Produce (Section 39(1)(iv)):**

Farmers have the right to save, use, sow, resow, exchange, share seed, or even **sell farm produce** of a registered variety.

**However:** Farmers **cannot sell branded seed** of a registered variety.

**What This Means:**
- Farmer can save seed from their harvest and plant it next year (farmer's exemption)
- Farmer can share or exchange seed with other farmers
- Farmer can sell the harvested crop (farm produce like rice, wheat)
- Farmer **cannot** sell packaged, branded seed for commercial planting by others

**2. Right to Register Farmers' Varieties (Section 39(1)(iii)):**

Farmers or groups of farmers can apply to register farmers' varieties (varieties developed by farmers through selection and preservation over generations).

**3. Right to Compensation (Section 41):**

If a breeder uses farmers' varieties in developing a new variety, the benefit-sharing mechanism ensures farmers receive compensation.

**4. Protection from Innocent Infringement (Section 42):**

Farmers are not liable for infringement if they were not aware and had no reasonable grounds to believe the variety was protected.

#### **Essentially Derived Varieties (EDV) (Section 2(i))**

An **essentially derived variety (EDV)** is a variety that:
- Is predominantly derived from an initial variety (or a variety that itself is derived from the initial variety)
- Is clearly distinguishable from the initial variety
- Conforms (except for differences resulting from derivation) to the initial variety in the expression of essential characteristics

**Purpose:** Prevents "cosmetic breeding" - making minor changes to an existing variety and claiming it as new. EDVs require authorization from the breeder of the initial variety.

#### **Rights Conferred (Section 28)**

Exclusive right to:
- Produce, sell, market, distribute, import, export the variety

**Exceptions:**
- Farmers' rights (as discussed)
- Researchers' rights (use for research and breeding)

#### **Term of Protection (Section 24)**

- **Trees and vines:** 18 years
- **Other crops:** 15 years
- **Extant varieties:** 15 years from the date of notification of that variety by the Central Government

#### **Compulsory Licensing (Section 47)**

If a registered variety is not available to the public at a reasonable price, the Authority may grant a compulsory license to make the variety available.

#### **Benefit Sharing (Section 26)**

When a breeder commercializes a variety that uses farmers' varieties or relies on traditional knowledge, a portion of the benefits must be deposited in the **National Gene Fund** for benefit-sharing with the farming community.

## üîí 3. Protection of Confidential Information & Trade Secrets

### What are Trade Secrets?

**Trade secrets** are confidential business information that provides a competitive advantage. Unlike patents, which require public disclosure, trade secrets remain secret.

**Examples:**
- Coca-Cola formula (famously kept secret for over a century)
- KFC's "11 herbs and spices" recipe
- Google's search algorithm
- Customer lists
- Manufacturing processes
- Business strategies
- Financial information

### Requirements for Trade Secret Protection

**1. Secrecy:**
The information must be secret (not generally known or easily accessible).

**2. Commercial Value:**
The information must have commercial value because it is secret.

**3. Reasonable Steps to Maintain Secrecy:**
The holder must take reasonable steps to keep it secret (NDAs, access controls, security measures).

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Trade Secrets

**Advantages:**

‚úÖ **No Time Limit:** Unlike patents (20 years), trade secrets can last indefinitely as long as secrecy is maintained

‚úÖ **No Registration:** No need to file applications, pay fees, or undergo examination

‚úÖ **No Disclosure:** Unlike patents, you don't have to disclose your invention to the public

‚úÖ **Cost-Effective:** No application or maintenance fees

**Disadvantages:**

‚ùå **Can Be Lost:** If someone independently discovers or reverse-engineers the information, you lose protection

‚ùå **Difficult to Enforce:** If someone steals your trade secret, it can be hard to prove and enforce rights

‚ùå **No Protection Against Independent Discovery:** If two people independently develop the same information, both can use it

‚ùå **Vulnerable to Employee Turnover:** Risk that employees will leave and share information (despite NDAs)

### International Framework: TRIPS Article 39

TRIPS requires members to protect "undisclosed information" (trade secrets).

**Article 39.2 - Protection of Undisclosed Information:**

Natural and legal persons shall have the possibility of preventing information lawfully within their control from being disclosed to, acquired by, or used by others without their consent in a manner contrary to honest commercial practices, provided that such information:

**a) Is Secret:**
Not generally known or readily accessible to persons in circles that normally deal with the kind of information in question.

**b) Has Commercial Value:**
Because it is secret.

**c) Has Been Subject to Reasonable Steps:**
To keep it secret by the person lawfully in control.

**"Contrary to Honest Commercial Practices" includes:**
- Breach of contract
- Breach of confidence
- Inducement to breach
- Acquisition of undisclosed information by third parties who knew, or should have known, the information was obtained through such practices

### Protection in India

India does **not have a specific trade secrets law**. However, trade secrets are protected through various legal mechanisms:

#### **1. Contract Law (Indian Contract Act, 1872)**

**Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs):**
Parties can enter into contracts requiring confidentiality. Breach of such agreements gives rise to contractual remedies.

**Example:** An employer can require employees to sign NDAs preventing disclosure of confidential information.

**Remedies:**
- Injunction (court order to prevent disclosure)
- Damages for breach of contract

#### **2. Law of Equity and Breach of Confidence**

**Common Law Doctrine:**
Courts recognize a duty of confidence where information is shared in confidence.

**Requirements:**
- Information must be confidential
- Information must be communicated in circumstances importing an obligation of confidence
- There must be unauthorized use of that information to the detriment of the party communicating it

**Example:** An employee who learns confidential information during employment has a duty not to disclose it, even absent a written NDA.

**Remedies:**
- Injunction
- Damages for breach of confidence

#### **3. Employment Law**

**Implied Duty of Fidelity:**
Employees have an implied duty not to disclose confidential information obtained during employment.

**Post-Employment Restrictions:**
Employment contracts can include non-compete and non-disclosure clauses (though non-compete clauses are limited under Section 27 of the Indian Contract Act).

#### **4. Intellectual Property Laws**

**Copyright:**
Documents containing trade secrets (manuals, designs, formulas written down) may be protected by copyright.

**Patents:**
If the trade secret qualifies as an invention, it could be patented (though this requires disclosure, which defeats the purpose of keeping it secret).

#### **5. Criminal Law**

**Indian Penal Code Provisions:**

- **Section 405 (Criminal Breach of Trust):** Misappropriating confidential information entrusted to someone
- **Section 408 (Criminal Breach of Trust by Employee):** Specific provision for employees

#### **6. Information Technology Act, 2000**

**Section 43, 66, 72:**
Provisions against unauthorized access to computer systems, data theft, and breach of confidentiality can apply to digital trade secrets.

### Best Practices for Protecting Trade Secrets

**1. Identify Trade Secrets:**
Determine what information is confidential and valuable.

**2. Implement Security Measures:**
- Physical security (locked cabinets, restricted access areas)
- Digital security (passwords, encryption, access controls)
- Limit access on a need-to-know basis

**3. Use Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs):**
With employees, contractors, business partners, anyone accessing confidential information.

**4. Mark Documents as Confidential:**
Clearly label confidential documents.

**5. Train Employees:**
Educate employees about confidentiality obligations.

**6. Exit Procedures:**
When employees leave, ensure they return all confidential materials and remind them of continuing obligations.

**7. Monitor Compliance:**
Regular audits to ensure security measures are followed.

## üåø 4. Protection of Traditional Knowledge and Biodiversity

### What is Traditional Knowledge (TK)?

**Traditional knowledge** refers to knowledge, innovations, and practices of indigenous and local communities that have been developed over generations and are often passed down orally. This includes knowledge about:

- Medicinal plants and their uses (traditional medicine like Ayurveda, Siddha, Unani)
- Agricultural practices
- Environmental management
- Handicrafts and textiles
- Cultural expressions (folklore, music, art)

**Examples:**
- Traditional uses of turmeric (antiseptic, anti-inflammatory)
- Use of neem for various medicinal and agricultural purposes
- Traditional rice cultivation techniques
- Ayurvedic formulations

### What is Biodiversity?

**Biodiversity** (biological diversity) refers to the variety of life on Earth - diversity of species, ecosystems, and genetic variation within species.

**Link to Traditional Knowledge:**
Local communities often possess knowledge about biodiversity - which plants have medicinal properties, how to cultivate crops sustainably, etc.

### Why Protect Traditional Knowledge and Biodiversity?

**1. Prevent Biopiracy:**
"Biopiracy" refers to the appropriation of traditional knowledge and biological resources by others (often foreign entities) without permission or compensation.

**Famous Examples:**
- **Turmeric Patent (US Patent 5,401,504):** University of Mississippi obtained a patent for turmeric's wound-healing properties. India successfully challenged it, arguing this was traditional knowledge known in India for centuries.
- **Neem Patent:** European Patent Office granted a patent for neem's fungicidal properties. India challenged and got it revoked, as neem's uses were traditional knowledge.
- **Basmati Patent:** US company RiceTec obtained a patent claiming a "Basmati" rice variety. After challenges, the patent was significantly narrowed.

**2. Recognition and Compensation:**
Communities that developed and preserved this knowledge deserve recognition and benefit from its commercial exploitation.

**3. Conservation:**
Protecting biodiversity ensures sustainable use and conservation of biological resources.

**4. Cultural Heritage:**
Traditional knowledge is part of cultural identity and heritage.

### Challenges in Protecting Traditional Knowledge

**1. Not Novel:**
TK is often not "new" in the patent sense (it's been used for generations), so it doesn't qualify for patents.

**2. Collectively Held:**
TK is often held collectively by communities, not individuals, making it hard to fit into IP systems designed for individual ownership.

**3. Oral Tradition:**
Much TK is passed down orally, without written documentation, making it hard to prove prior art.

**4. Lack of Documentation:**
Without documentation, it's difficult to prevent others from claiming TK as their own invention.

### International Framework

#### **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992)**

**Key Principles:**

**1. Sovereign Rights:**
States have sovereign rights over their biological resources.

**2. Access and Benefit-Sharing (ABS):**
Access to genetic resources should be subject to **prior informed consent** of the country providing the resources, and there should be **fair and equitable sharing** of benefits arising from their use.

**3. Traditional Knowledge:**
Recognizes the contribution of indigenous and local communities to biodiversity conservation and requires respect for their knowledge and practices.

**India ratified the CBD in 1994.**

#### **Nagoya Protocol (2010)**

An **international agreement** supplementing the CBD, providing a legal framework for:
- Access to genetic resources
- Fair and equitable sharing of benefits arising from their utilization

**Key Elements:**
- Prior informed consent from countries providing genetic resources
- Mutually agreed terms for benefit-sharing
- Compliance measures

**India ratified the Nagoya Protocol in 2012.**

#### **TRIPS and Traditional Knowledge**

**Article 27.3(b):**
Allows exclusion of plants and animals from patentability (recognizing that these may be part of traditional knowledge and biodiversity).

**Article 29.1:**
Requires disclosure of the invention in the patent application. Some argue this should include disclosure of the source of biological materials and traditional knowledge used.

**Debates:**
Developing countries (including India) have been pushing for amendments to TRIPS to require:
- Disclosure of the origin of biological materials and traditional knowledge
- Evidence of prior informed consent and benefit-sharing agreements

### Protection in India

India has taken several measures to protect traditional knowledge and biodiversity:

#### **1. Biological Diversity Act, 2002**

**Objectives:**
- Conservation of biological diversity
- Sustainable use of biological resources
- Fair and equitable sharing of benefits

**Key Provisions:**

**National Biodiversity Authority (NBA):**
Established to implement the Act.

**Prior Approval Required (Section 3, 4):**
- Foreign individuals, companies must obtain approval from NBA before accessing biological resources or associated traditional knowledge for research, commercial use, or bio-survey.
- Indian companies/individuals need to inform State Biodiversity Boards before accessing biological resources for commercial use.

**Benefit-Sharing:**
Beneficiaries of biological resources or traditional knowledge must share benefits with the communities and conservers of the resources.

**Biodiversity Management Committees (BMCs):**
At local level to document and manage biodiversity and traditional knowledge.

**People's Biodiversity Registers (PBRs):**
Documenting local biodiversity and associated traditional knowledge at the community level.

**Penalties:**
Violations can result in imprisonment and fines.

#### **2. Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL)**

**Launched:** 2001 (by Council of Scientific and Industrial Research - CSIR and Ministry of AYUSH)

**Purpose:**
Document traditional knowledge (especially from Ayurveda, Unani, Siddha, Yoga) in a digitized, classified, and structured format to prevent misappropriation through patent systems.

**How It Works:**

- Traditional knowledge from ancient texts is documented in five international languages (English, Japanese, French, German, Spanish) and classified according to international patent classification
- Made available to patent offices worldwide (EPO, USPTO, JPO, etc.) to use as prior art during patent examination
- Prevents granting of patents on inventions that are actually traditional knowledge

**Success:**
Several patent applications based on traditional knowledge have been challenged and rejected using TKDL data.

#### **3. Patents Act Amendments**

**Section 3(p):**
Excludes from patentability: "an invention which, in effect, is traditional knowledge or which is an aggregation or duplication of known properties of traditionally known component or components."

**Section 10(4)(d)(ii):**
Requires disclosure of the source and geographical origin of biological material used in the invention.

**Section 25(1)(k) and (2)(k):**
Grounds for opposition/revocation include failure to disclose or wrongful disclosure of source/geographical origin of biological material.

#### **4. Benefit-Sharing Mechanisms**

**National Biodiversity Fund:**
Benefits arising from commercial use of biological resources and traditional knowledge are deposited in funds managed by NBA/SBBs for benefit-sharing with local communities.

### International Efforts and Debates

**WIPO Intergovernmental Committee (IGC):**
WIPO has an Intergovernmental Committee on Intellectual Property and Genetic Resources, Traditional Knowledge, and Folklore working towards international legal instruments for protection.

**Debates:**
- How to balance TK protection with access for research and innovation?
- How to define and document TK?
- How to ensure benefit-sharing is fair and reaches local communities?

## üîë Key Takeaways from Module 07

1. **Layout Designs of Integrated Circuits:**
   - Protected under Semiconductor Integrated Circuits Layout-Design Act, 2000
   - Must be original and not commonplace
   - Term: 10 years
   - Rights: Reproduction, import, sale, distribution
   - Exceptions: Private use, research, reverse engineering for creating original designs

2. **Plant Varieties (PPV&FR Act, 2001):**
   - Sui generis system balancing breeders' and farmers' rights
   - Eligibility: Distinct, Uniform, Stable (DUS), and new
   - Farmers' rights: Save, use, sow, resow, exchange, share, sell farm produce (but not branded seed)
   - Term: 15 years (crops), 18 years (trees/vines)
   - Benefit-sharing for use of farmers' varieties

3. **Trade Secrets:**
   - No specific Indian law; protected through contracts, equity, employment law, and criminal law
   - Requirements: Secret, commercial value, reasonable steps to maintain secrecy
   - Advantages: Indefinite protection, no disclosure
   - Disadvantages: No protection against independent discovery, difficult to enforce

4. **Traditional Knowledge and Biodiversity:**
   - **Biological Diversity Act, 2002:** Regulates access to biological resources and traditional knowledge; requires prior approval and benefit-sharing
   - **TKDL:** Documents traditional knowledge to prevent biopiracy and wrongful patents
   - **Patents Act:** Excludes traditional knowledge from patentability; requires disclosure of biological material sources
   - **International:** CBD, Nagoya Protocol promote access and benefit-sharing

5. **Common Themes:**
   - These forms of IP address modern challenges (technology, agriculture, biodiversity)
   - Balance between protection and access is crucial
   - India has developed unique frameworks (especially for plant varieties and traditional knowledge) that prioritize community rights
