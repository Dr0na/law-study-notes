## üéØ Introduction

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Moral rights are personal rights that protect the reputation and personal connection of authors to their works. Unlike economic rights, moral rights cannot be transferred and remain with the author even after selling the copyright.

## ‚öñÔ∏è Legal Provision (Section 57)

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Section 57 of the Copyright Act, 1957, recognizes moral rights independent of the author's economic rights.

</div>

---

## üìã 2 Main Moral Rights

---

### Right 1: Right of Paternity (Right of Attribution)

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

The author has the right to:

**1. Claim Authorship:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Claim authorship of the work.
</div>

<br>

**2. Be Identified:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Be identified as the author when the work is used.
</div>

<br>

**3. Prevent False Attribution:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Prevent someone else from claiming to be the author.
</div>

**Practical Implications:**
- Author's name must appear on copies, in credits, in publicity
- Even if copyright is sold, author's name must be acknowledged
- Others cannot claim authorship

**Example:** If you write a screenplay and sell it to a studio, your name must appear in the film's credits. The studio cannot claim it wrote the screenplay.

</div>

---

### Right 2: Right of Integrity

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

The author has the right to:

**1. Prevent Distortion/Mutilation:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Prevent distortion, mutilation, or modification of the work.
</div>

<br>

**2. Object to Prejudicial Treatment:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Object to any treatment that would be prejudicial to their honor or reputation.
</div>

**Practical Implications:**
- Work cannot be altered in ways that harm author's reputation
- Colorization of black-and-white films, editing novels, changes to artworks may violate this
- Alteration must be prejudicial - minor edits may be acceptable

**Example:** If you paint a picture and sell it, the buyer cannot paint over parts or modify it misrepresenting your artistic vision or harming your reputation as an artist.

</div>

---

## ‚è±Ô∏è Duration of Moral Rights

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Moral rights last for the **same duration as economic rights:**
- Life of author + 60 years

After author's death, moral rights can be enforced by:
- Author's legal representatives
- Persons authorized by the author

</div>

---

## üîë Special Characteristics

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

**1. Inalienability:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Cannot be transferred or assigned. Remain with author even if economic rights are sold.
</div>

<br>

**2. Independence:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Exist independently of economic rights. Can be enforced even after selling copyright.
</div>

<br>

**3. Personal Nature:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Personal to the author, protecting reputation and identity connection to work.
</div>

</div>

---

## üåç International Recognition

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Moral rights recognized in:
- **Berne Convention** (Article 6bis)
- **WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty**
- Strong protection in civil law countries (France, Germany)
- India: Section 57 of Copyright Act

</div>

---

## üéØ Conclusion

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Moral rights protect the personal and reputational interests of authors beyond economic considerations. They recognize that creative works embody the author's personality and identity. The two moral rights - attribution (being identified as author) and integrity (preventing harmful modifications) - remain with the author even after selling economic rights. This reflects the principle that creative works are expressions of the author's personality and deserve protection beyond mere economic exploitation.

</div>
