## üìú Legal Provision (Section 2(1)(zg))

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

"Well-known trade mark" means a mark which has become so to the substantial segment of the public which uses such goods or receives such services that the use of such mark in relation to other goods or services would be likely to be taken as indicating a connection in the course of trade or rendering of services between those goods or services and a person using the mark in relation to the first-mentioned goods or services.

</div>

---

## üîë Key Features

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

**1. No Registration Required:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
A mark can be well-known even if not registered in India.
</div>

<br>

**2. Substantial Public Recognition:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Must be known to substantial segment of relevant public (not necessarily general public).
</div>

<br>

**3. Cross-Category Protection:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Protected even for dissimilar goods/services if use would indicate connection or damage repute.
</div>

</div>

---

## ‚≠ê Enhanced Protection (Section 11(2))

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

### Protection Against:

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

**1. Identical or Similar Marks:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Even for goods/services not similar to those covered by well-known mark.
</div>

<br>

**2. Unfair Advantage:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
If use would take unfair advantage of distinctive character or repute.
</div>

<br>

**3. Detriment to Character:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
If use would be detrimental to distinctive character or repute.
</div>

</div>

**Example:** "Ferrari" is well-known for cars. Cannot register "Ferrari" for perfumes even though dissimilar goods - would take unfair advantage of Ferrari's reputation.

</div>

---

## üåç International Framework

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

**Paris Convention (Article 6bis):**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
First international recognition of well-known marks - protection against registration/use of conflicting marks.
</div>

**TRIPS Agreement (Article 16.2-16.3):**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Extended Article 6bis to services (not just goods) - protection against use on dissimilar goods/services if interests likely damaged.
</div>

</div>

---

## üìã 5 Factors to Determine Well-Known Status

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Courts consider:

**1. Extent of Use:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Duration and geographical extent of use.
</div>

<br>

**2. Public Recognition:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Knowledge among relevant section of public.
</div>

<br>

**3. Advertising:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Duration, extent, and geographical area of advertising/promotion.
</div>

<br>

**4. Registrations:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Registrations in India and other countries.
</div>

<br>

**5. Successful Enforcement:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Record of successful enforcement of rights.
</div>

</div>

## üõ°Ô∏è Benefits of Well-Known Status

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

**1. No Registration Needed:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Protection available even without Indian registration.
</div>

<br>

**2. Broader Protection:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Protection extends beyond similar goods/services.
</div>

<br>

**3. Easier Enforcement:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Can prevent confusingly similar marks more easily.
</div>

<br>

**4. Anti-Dilution:**

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">
Protects against dilution of distinctive character and repute.
</div>

</div>

---

## üéØ Conclusion

<div style="margin-left: 20px;">

Well-known marks receive enhanced protection under Indian law, reflecting their significant reputation and goodwill. Even without registration in India, such marks can prevent others from using identical or similar marks, particularly if such use would take unfair advantage or be detrimental to the mark's distinctive character or repute. This protection, rooted in the Paris Convention and strengthened by TRIPS, ensures that marks with substantial public recognition are not exploited by free-riders seeking to benefit from their established reputation.

</div>
