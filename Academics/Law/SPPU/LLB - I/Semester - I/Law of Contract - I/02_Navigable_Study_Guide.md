## 1.1 Overview of Contract Law

### What is a Contract?

A **contract** is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties that creates mutual obligations enforceable by law.

**Key Principle:** 
> "All contracts are agreements, but all agreements are not contracts."

### The Contract Formation Process

```
Agreement â†’ Contract (if legally enforceable) â†’ Valid Contract
```

**Key Distinction:**
- **Agreement** = Promise + Consideration
- **Contract** = Agreement + Legal Enforceability

## 2.2 Offer & Acceptance

### Offer/Proposal (Section 2(a))

**Definition:** Expression of willingness to do or abstain from doing something with the intention of obtaining assent.

#### Characteristics of Valid Offer:
- âœ… Must be definite and certain
- âœ… Must be communicated (Section 3)
- âœ… Must show intention to create legal relations
- âœ… May be express or implied (Section 9)

#### Rules Regarding Offer:

**1. Communication of Offer (Section 3)**
- Offer is complete when it comes to the knowledge of the person to whom it is made

**2. Revocation of Offer (Section 5)**
- Can be revoked before acceptance
- Revocation must be communicated

**3. Lapse of Offer (Section 6)**
Offer lapses by:
- Rejection by offeree
- Counter-offer
- Lapse of time
- Death or insanity of offeror (before acceptance)
- Non-fulfillment of condition precedent
- Revocation

### Acceptance (Section 2(b))

**Definition:** When proposal is accepted, it becomes a promise.

#### Rules of Acceptance:

**1. Must be Absolute (Section 7)**
- Must be unqualified and unconditional
- Must accept all terms of offer

**2. Must be Communicated (Section 4)**
- Acceptance complete:
  - **Postal Rule**: When letter posted (if by post)
  - **Otherwise**: When it comes to knowledge of offeror

**3. Must be in Prescribed Manner (Section 7)**
- If manner prescribed, must follow it
- If no manner prescribed, reasonable manner

**4. Express or Implied (Section 9)**
- **Express**: By words (spoken/written)
- **Implied**: By conduct

### Communication Rules Summary

| Action | When Complete |
|--------|--------------|
| **Offer** | When it comes to knowledge of offeree (Section 3) |
| **Acceptance** | When posted (postal) or communicated (Section 4) |
| **Revocation of Offer** | Before acceptance reaches offeror (Section 5) |
| **Revocation of Acceptance** | Before acceptance reaches offeror (Section 5) |

### Key Sections Reference

- **Section 2(a)**: Definition of proposal
- **Section 2(b)**: Definition of promise/acceptance
- **Section 3**: Communication, acceptance, revocation
- **Section 4**: Communication when complete
- **Section 5**: Revocation of proposals and acceptances
- **Section 6**: Revocation how made
- **Section 7**: Acceptance must be absolute
- **Section 8**: Acceptance by performing conditions
- **Section 9**: Promises, express and implied

## 2.3 Consideration

### Definition (Section 2(d))

**Consideration** = Something in return
- An act (doing something)
- An abstinence (not doing something)
- A promise (to do or not to do)

### Rules of Consideration

#### âœ… Valid Consideration Must:

1. **Move from Promisee**
   - Consideration must be provided by the person to whom promise is made

2. **May be Past, Present, or Future**
   - **Past**: Already done before promise
   - **Present**: Done at time of promise
   - **Future**: To be done after promise

3. **Need Not be Adequate**
   - Law does not require consideration to be equal in value
   - "Adequacy" is for parties to decide

4. **Must be Real, Not Illusory**
   - Must have some value (even if small)
   - Cannot be impossible or illegal

5. **Must be Lawful** (Section 23)
   - Must not be forbidden by law
   - Must not be immoral
   - Must not be against public policy

### Exceptions: Agreement Without Consideration (Section 25)

These agreements are **VALID** even without consideration:

#### 1. Natural Love and Affection (Section 25(1))
- Between parties standing in near relation
- In writing and registered
- Example: Father promises son without consideration

#### 2. Compensation for Past Voluntary Act (Section 25(2))
- Promise to compensate person who has voluntarily done something
- Person must have done it legally
- Example: A saves B's property, B promises reward

#### 3. Promise to Pay Time-Barred Debt (Section 25(3))
- Promise to pay debt barred by limitation
- Must be in writing and signed
- Example: Old debt, promise to pay again

#### 4. Completed Gift
- Gift already given (executed)
- No consideration needed

## 2.4 Capacity to Contract

### Who Can Contract? (Section 11)

A person is **competent to contract** if:
- âœ… Has attained age of majority (18 years)
- âœ… Is of sound mind (Section 12)
- âœ… Is not disqualified by any law

### Detailed Requirements

#### 1. Age of Majority
- **18 years** in India
- Contracts by **minors are VOID** (not voidable)

#### 2. Sound Mind (Section 12)

A person is of **sound mind** if:
- Can understand the contract
- Can form rational judgment about its effect on interests

**Types of Unsound Mind:**
- Lunatic (temporary/permanent)
- Idiot (permanent)
- Drunk/intoxicated (temporary)

#### 3. Not Disqualified by Law

Persons disqualified:
- **Alien enemies** (during war)
- **Convicts** (certain restrictions)
- **Insolvents** (certain restrictions)
- **Corporations** (beyond powers)

## 2.5 Free Consent

### Definition (Section 14)

**Free Consent** = Consent not caused by:
- Coercion (Section 15)
- Undue influence (Section 16)
- Fraud (Section 17)
- Misrepresentation (Section 18)
- Mistake (Sections 20-22)

### Vitiating Factors

#### 1. Coercion (Section 15)

**Definition:** Committing or threatening to commit:
- Any act forbidden by Indian Penal Code
- Unlawful detaining/threatening to detain property

**Effect:** Contract is **VOIDABLE** at option of aggrieved party

**Key Points:**
- Physical or legal threat
- Can be by third party
- Need not be to party, can be to relative

#### 2. Undue Influence (Section 16)

**Definition:** Contract with person in:
- Dominant position
- Uses position to obtain unfair advantage

**Presumed in:**
- Fiduciary relationship (parent-child, doctor-patient, etc.)
- Mental distress
- Old age, illness, mental/body distress

**Effect:** Contract is **VOIDABLE** at option of aggrieved party

#### 3. Fraud (Section 17)

**Definition:** Includes:
- False representation (knowingly/without belief)
- Active concealment of fact
- Promise made without intention to perform
- Any act fitted to deceive
- Any such act/omission as law declares fraudulent

**Essential Elements:**
- Intent to deceive
- Representation must be false
- Other party must have acted on it
- Must have resulted in damage

**Effect:** Contract is **VOIDABLE** at option of aggrieved party

#### 4. Misrepresentation (Section 18)

**Definition:** Includes:
- Unwarranted statement of fact (believed true)
- Breach of duty (gains advantage)
- Innocent mistake

**Key Difference from Fraud:**
- **Fraud**: Intentional deception
- **Misrepresentation**: Innocent false statement

**Effect:** Contract is **VOIDABLE** at option of aggrieved party

#### 5. Mistake (Sections 20-22)

**Bilateral Mistake (Section 20)**
- Both parties under mistake
- Mistake as to matter of fact essential to agreement
- **Effect:** Contract is **VOID**

**Unilateral Mistake (Section 22)**
- One party under mistake
- **Effect:** Contract is **VALID**

**Mistake as to Law (Section 21)**
- Mistake of Indian law = No excuse
- Mistake of foreign law = Treated as mistake of fact

### Comparison Table

| Factor | Section | Intent | Effect |
|--------|---------|--------|--------|
| **Coercion** | 15 | Threat | Voidable |
| **Undue Influence** | 16 | Mental pressure | Voidable |
| **Fraud** | 17 | Intentional | Voidable |
| **Misrepresentation** | 18 | Innocent | Voidable |
| **Bilateral Mistake** | 20 | Both parties | **VOID** |
| **Unilateral Mistake** | 22 | One party | Valid |

## 2.6 Lawful Object

### Requirement (Section 23)

Consideration and object of agreement must be:
- âœ… **Lawful**
- âŒ Not forbidden by law
- âŒ Not immoral
- âŒ Not against public policy
- âŒ Not fraudulent
- âŒ Not causing injury to person/property

### What Makes Object Unlawful?

#### 1. Forbidden by Law
- Any act prohibited by law
- Example: Agreement to commit crime

#### 2. Immoral
- Against moral standards
- Example: Agreement for immoral purpose

#### 3. Against Public Policy
- Harmful to public interest
- Examples:
  - Trading with enemy
  - Interference with administration of justice
  - Restraint of marriage (Section 26)
  - Restraint of trade (Section 27)
  - Restraint of legal proceedings (Section 28)

#### 4. Fraudulent
- Designed to defraud others
- Example: Agreement to defraud creditors

#### 5. Causing Injury
- Injury to person or property
- Example: Agreement to cause harm

### Void Agreements (Sections 24-30)

#### Section 26: Agreement in Restraint of Marriage
- **Void** - Every agreement in restraint of marriage is void
- Exception: Partial restraint may be valid

#### Section 27: Agreement in Restraint of Trade
- **Void** - Every agreement by which one is restrained from exercising lawful profession/trade
- Exceptions:
  - Sale of goodwill
  - Partnership agreements
  - Service agreements (reasonable)

#### Section 28: Agreement in Restraint of Legal Proceedings
- **Void** - Agreement restraining party from enforcing rights
- Exception: Arbitration agreements valid

#### Section 29: Agreements Void for Uncertainty
- **Void** - Agreement terms must be certain
- Must be capable of being made certain

#### Section 30: Agreements by Way of Wager
- **Void** - Wagering agreements are void
- Exception: Horse racing (in some states)

- **Section 24**: Agreements void if considerations unlawful
- **Section 26**: Agreement in restraint of marriage
- **Section 27**: Agreement in restraint of trade
- **Section 28**: Agreement in restraint of legal proceedings

## 2.7 Certainty & Possibility

### Requirement

- âœ… **Certain** - Clear and definite
- âœ… **Possible** - Capable of performance

### Certainty

**Rule:** Agreement must be certain, not vague or ambiguous.

- If terms cannot be determined, agreement is void

**Types of Impossibility:**

## 3.5 Contingent Contracts

### Definition (Section 31)

**Contingent Contract** = Contract to do or not to do something if some event, collateral to such contract, does or does not happen

### Characteristics

- Performance depends on uncertain future event
- Event is collateral (not part of consideration)
- Contract is valid

### Rules (Sections 32-36)

#### Section 32: Enforcement When Event Happens
- Enforceable when event happens
- If event becomes impossible, contract becomes void

#### Section 33: Enforcement When Event Becomes Impossible
- If event becomes impossible, contract becomes void

#### Section 34: Enforcement When Event Becomes Impossible
- Similar to Section 33

#### Section 35: Enforcement When Event Becomes Impossible
- Similar provisions

#### Section 36: Agreement Contingent on Impossible Event
- **Void** - Agreement contingent on impossible event is void

### Key Sections Reference

- **Section 31**: Contingent contract defined
- **Sections 32-36**: Rules regarding contingent contracts

# Part 4: Performance & Breach

## 4.1 Performance of Contracts

### General Provisions (Sections 37-41)

#### Section 37: Obligation of Parties
- Parties must perform or offer to perform
- Performance must be according to terms

#### Section 38: Effect of Refusal to Accept Offer
- If promisee refuses to accept performance, promisor not responsible for non-performance

#### Section 39: Effect of Refusal of Party to Perform
- If one party refuses to perform, other party can:
  - Rescind contract
  - Claim damages

#### Section 40: Person by Whom Promise is to be Performed
- Promisor or agent or legal representative

#### Section 41: Effect of Accepting Performance from Third Person
- If promisee accepts performance from third person, cannot later enforce against promisor

### Time and Place for Performance (Sections 46-50)

#### Section 46: Time for Performance
- If no time specified, reasonable time

#### Section 47: Time and Place for Performance
- Performance at proper time and place

#### Section 48: Application for Performance on Fixed Day
- Must be applied for on fixed day

#### Section 49: Place for Performance
- At place agreed or reasonable place

#### Section 50: Performance in Manner or at Time Prescribed
- Must be in prescribed manner

### Reciprocal Promises (Sections 51-58)

#### Types of Reciprocal Promises:

1. **Simultaneous** (Section 51)
   - Both parties perform simultaneously

2. **Dependent** (Section 52)
   - One promise depends on other

3. **Independent** (Section 52)
   - Promises independent of each other

#### Section 56: Agreement to Do Impossible Act
- Initial impossibility = Void
- Subsequent impossibility = Frustration

### Key Sections Reference

- **Sections 37-41**: General provisions
- **Sections 46-50**: Time and place
- **Sections 51-58**: Reciprocal promises

## 4.2 Breach of Contract

### Definition

**Breach** = Failure to perform contractual obligation

### Types of Breach

#### 1. Actual Breach
- Failure to perform when performance is due
- Can be:
  - At time of performance
  - During performance

#### 2. Anticipatory Breach
- Repudiation before performance is due
- Other party can:
  - Treat as breach immediately
  - Wait for performance date

### Effects of Breach

- Contract comes to end
- Aggrieved party can claim damages
- Can rescind contract
- Can claim specific performance (in some cases)

## 4.3 Remedies for Breach

### Available Remedies

#### 1. Damages (Section 73)

**Definition:** Monetary compensation for loss

**Types:**
- **Ordinary Damages**: Direct and natural loss
- **Special Damages**: Foreseeable special circumstances
- **Remote Damages**: Not recoverable (too remote)

**Rule:** Hadley v. Baxendale
- Damages must be:
  - Arising naturally from breach, OR
  - Reasonably supposed to be in contemplation of parties

**Measure of Damages:**
- Difference between contract price and market price
- Cost of completion
- Loss of profit (if foreseeable)

#### 2. Penalty & Liquidated Damages (Section 74)

**Rule:**
- If penalty stipulated, reasonable compensation
- Not actual penalty amount
- Court determines reasonable compensation

**Key Distinction:**
- **Penalty**: Punishment, excessive
- **Liquidated Damages**: Genuine pre-estimate

#### 3. Quantum Meruit (Section 70)

**Definition:** "As much as he deserves"

**When Available:**
- Work done but contract not completed
- Contract void but work done
- Benefit received without contract

#### 4. Specific Performance

- Court orders performance of contract
- Available when:
  - Damages inadequate
  - Contract for unique goods
  - Land contracts

#### 5. Injunction

- Court order to refrain from doing something
- Negative specific performance

# Part 5: Special Topics

## 5.1 Quasi Contracts

### Definition

**Quasi Contracts** = Obligations resembling contracts but not actual contracts
- Created by law
- Based on principle of unjust enrichment

### Types (Sections 68-72)

#### Section 68: Necessaries Supplied
- Person incapable of contracting
- Necessaries supplied
- Liable to pay reasonable price

#### Section 69: Payment by Interested Person
- Person interested in payment
- Makes payment another bound to pay
- Entitled to reimbursement

#### Section 70: Obligation of Person Enjoying Benefit
- Lawfully does something for another
- Not intending to do gratuitously
- Other person enjoys benefit
- Must compensate

#### Section 71: Finder of Goods
- Takes goods into custody
- Subject to same liability as bailee
- Must take reasonable care

#### Section 72: Payment by Mistake
- Money paid by mistake
- Must repay or return

### Key Sections Reference

- **Section 68**: Necessaries supplied to incompetent person
- **Section 69**: Reimbursement of person paying money
- **Section 70**: Obligation of person enjoying benefit
- **Section 71**: Responsibility of finder of goods
- **Section 72**: Liability of person to whom money is paid

## 5.2 Special Contracts Overview

### Part II: Special Contracts (Sections 124-238)

#### 1. Indemnity & Guarantee (124-147)

**Indemnity (Section 124):**
- Contract to save from loss
- Two parties: Indemnifier, Indemnified

**Guarantee (Section 126):**
- Contract to perform promise or discharge liability
- Three parties: Creditor, Principal Debtor, Surety

#### 2. Bailment & Pledge (148-181)

**Bailment (Section 148):**
- Delivery of goods for specific purpose
- Return or dispose according to directions

**Pledge (Section 172):**
- Bailment of goods as security
- For payment of debt or performance of promise

#### 3. Agency (182-238)

**Agency (Section 182):**
- Person employed to act for another
- Agent acts on behalf of principal

## 6.2 Case Law Summary

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)**
- **Facts**: Company offered reward, woman used product, got flu
- **Principle**: Offer can be made to the world (unilateral contract)
- **Relevance**: Acceptance by conduct, unilateral contracts

#### 2. **Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)**
- **Facts**: Mill shaft delayed, mill closed, claimed lost profits
- **Principle**: Damages must be foreseeable
- **Rule**: 
  - Arising naturally, OR
  - In contemplation of parties
- **Relevance**: Section 73 - Remoteness of damages

#### 3. **Nash v. Inman (1908)**
- **Facts**: Minor bought clothes, refused to pay
- **Principle**: Minor's contract is void
- **Relevance**: Section 11 - Capacity to contract

#### 4. **Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt (1913)**
- **Facts**: Employee found missing boy, employer had offered reward
- **Principle**: Acceptance must be communicated
- **Relevance**: Section 4 - Communication of acceptance

#### 5. **Balfour v. Balfour (1919)**
- **Facts**: Husband promised wife allowance, separated
- **Principle**: Domestic agreements lack intention to create legal relations
- **Relevance**: Agreement vs Contract distinction

## 6.4 Exam Strategy

### Question Types & How to Answer

#### 1. "Is this a valid contract?"

**Approach:**
1. Check all 7 essential elements systematically
2. Use the checklist:
   - âœ… Offer & Acceptance?
   - âœ… Consideration?
   - âœ… Competent parties?
   - âœ… Free consent?
   - âœ… Lawful object?
   - âœ… Not expressly void?
   - âœ… Certainty?
3. State which elements are present/absent
4. Conclude: Valid or Invalid (and why)

**Example Structure:**
```
For a contract to be valid under Section 10, it must satisfy:
1. Offer & Acceptance: [Analyze]
2. Consideration: [Analyze]
3. Competent Parties: [Analyze]
4. Free Consent: [Analyze]
5. Lawful Object: [Analyze]
6. Not Expressly Void: [Analyze]
7. Certainty: [Analyze]

Conclusion: [Valid/Invalid because...]
```

#### 2. "Distinguish between X and Y"

**Approach:**
1. Define both concepts
2. List key differences (use table if helpful)
3. Give examples
4. Reference relevant sections

**Example:**
```
VOID vs VOIDABLE

Definition:
- Void (2g): Agreement not enforceable
- Voidable (2i): Contract valid until avoided

Key Differences:
1. Effect: Void = No effect; Voidable = Valid until avoided
2. Enforceability: Void = Cannot enforce; Voidable = Can enforce
3. Examples: Void = Bilateral mistake; Voidable = Fraud

Sections: 2(g) and 2(i)
```

#### 3. "Explain with examples"

**Approach:**
1. Define the concept
2. Explain the rule/law
3. Give relevant example
4. Reference section number

#### 4. "What are the remedies?"

**Approach:**
1. Identify the breach
2. List available remedies (Section 73-75)
3. Explain each remedy
4. Apply to facts
5. Reference case law if relevant

### Time Management

- **Short Questions (5 marks)**: 5-7 minutes
- **Medium Questions (10 marks)**: 12-15 minutes
- **Long Questions (15-20 marks)**: 20-25 minutes

### Answer Writing Tips

1. **Start with definition** (if applicable)
2. **Reference section numbers** (shows knowledge)
3. **Use examples** (demonstrates understanding)
4. **Apply to facts** (shows application)
5. **Conclude clearly** (summarize answer)
