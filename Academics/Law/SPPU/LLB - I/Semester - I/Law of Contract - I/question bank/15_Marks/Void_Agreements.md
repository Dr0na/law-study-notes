# VOID AGREEMENTS – 15 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Question 1: Discuss the various types of void agreements under the Indian Contract Act, 1872. Distinguish between void, voidable, and illegal agreements.

### MODEL ANSWER:

#### Introduction
The Indian Contract Act, 1872 recognizes certain agreements as void from the very beginning (void ab initio), meaning they have no legal effect and cannot be enforced by law. These agreements lack one or more essential elements of a valid contract. The Act specifically lists various categories of agreements that are void under different sections.

#### Definition of Void Agreement - Section 2(g)

**Section 2(g) states:**
"An agreement not enforceable by law is said to be void."

**Characteristics:**
1. Not enforceable from the beginning
2. Creates no legal rights or obligations
3. Cannot be ratified
4. Collateral agreements may be valid

#### Types of Void Agreements

### 1. Agreement by Person Incompetent to Contract [Section 11]

**Who are incompetent:**
- Minors
- Persons of unsound mind
- Persons disqualified by law

**Case Law: Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)**
Minor's agreements are void ab initio.

### 2. Agreement Without Consideration [Section 25]

**General Rule:** Agreement without consideration is void.

**Exceptions:**
1. Natural love and affection (written and registered)
2. Compensation for past voluntary services
3. Promise to pay time-barred debt (written)

**Case Law: Durga Prasad v. Baldeo (1880)**
Work done at desire of third party, not promisor - no consideration, void.

### 3. Agreement with Unlawful Object or Consideration [Sections 23 & 24]

Agreements that are:
- Forbidden by law
- Defeating provisions of law
- Fraudulent
- Injurious to person or property
- Immoral
- Opposed to public policy

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas (1959)**
Agreement to sell beyond legally permitted quantity - void.

### 4. Agreement in Restraint of Marriage [Section 26]

**Section 26 states:**
"Every agreement in restraint of the marriage of any person, other than a minor, is void."

**Exception:** Agreements regarding marriage to particular person or class may be upheld if reasonable.

**Rationale:**
- Marriage is social institution
- Freedom of choice in marriage is important
- Public policy favors marriage

**Case Law: Lowe v. Peers (1768)**
Mutual promise not to marry anyone held void.

**Illustration:**
A promises B ₹10 lakhs if B does not marry during his lifetime.
**Result:** Void agreement.

**Valid Restriction:**
A promises B ₹5 lakhs if B does not marry C (specific person). May be valid.

### 5. Agreement in Restraint of Trade [Section 27]

**Section 27:** "Every agreement by which any one is restrained from exercising a lawful profession, trade or business of any kind, is to that extent void."

**Exceptions:**
1. Sale of goodwill
2. Partnership (during continuance)
3. Partnership (after dissolution, with limits)

**Case Law: Niranjan Shankar v. Century Spinning (1967)**
Negative covenant restraining employment even after service termination - void.

### 6. Agreement in Restraint of Legal Proceedings [Section 28]

**Section 28:** "Every agreement, by which any party thereto is restricted absolutely from enforcing his rights under or in respect of any contract, by the usual legal proceedings in the ordinary tribunals, or which limits the time within which he may thus enforce his rights, is void to that extent."

**What is Void:**
- Absolute restriction on right to sue
- Shortening limitation period

**What is Valid:**
- Arbitration agreements
- Extending limitation period
- Reference to arbitration for specified disputes

**Case Law: Nath v. Radha Kishan (1941)**
Agreement ousting jurisdiction of courts for all future disputes - void.

**Proviso:** Arbitration agreements not affected.

**Illustration (a) from Section 28:**
A and B enter agreement that A shall not sue B for any dispute. Void.

**Illustration (b) from Section 28:**
A and B agree to refer future disputes to arbitration. Valid.

### 7. Agreements the Meaning of Which is Uncertain [Section 29]

**Section 29:** "Agreements, the meaning of which is not certain, or capable of being made certain, are void."

**Rationale:**
Courts cannot enforce vague agreements.

**Test:**
Can the meaning be made certain from agreement itself or by reference to external standards?

**Case Law: Scammel v. Ouston (1941)**
Agreement to buy van "on hire-purchase terms" - void for uncertainty as no specific terms mentioned.

**Case Law: Guthing v. Lynn (1831)**
Promise to pay more money if horse proves "lucky" - void for uncertainty.

**Illustration (a) from Section 29:**
A agrees to sell B "a hundred tonnes of oil." There is nothing to show what kind of oil was intended. Agreement void.

**Illustration (b) from Section 29:**
A agrees to sell B 1,000 "maunds" of rice at a certain price. Nothing to show what kind of maund is intended. If there is usage of trade which makes meaning certain, agreement is valid.

**When Agreement is NOT Uncertain:**

**1. Trade Usage:**
If trade usage makes meaning certain.

**2. Reference to External Standard:**
"Market price," "prevailing rate" - capable of being ascertained.

**3. Reasonable Interpretation:**
Courts may give reasonable meaning if possible.

**Case Law: Hillas & Co. v. Arcos Ltd. (1932)**
Agreement for "fair specification" of timber - not void as capable of being made certain by reference to trade practice.

### 8. Agreements by Way of Wager [Section 30]

**Section 30:** "Agreements by way of wager are void; and no suit shall be brought for recovering anything alleged to be won on any wager, or entrusted to any person to abide by the result of any game or other uncertain event on which any wager is made."

**Exception:** Certain horse racing bets permitted under State laws.

**Definition of Wager:**
A wager is a contract of mutual chance where parties have opposite views regarding uncertain event, and one wins what the other loses.

**Essentials of Wagering Agreement:**

**1. Promise to Give Money or Money's Worth**
Stake must be money or something of value.

**2. Uncertain Event**
Event must be uncertain in outcome.

**3. Mutual Chance of Gain or Loss**
Each party stands to win or lose.

**4. No Other Interest**
Parties have no other interest in event except stake.

**5. Opposite Views**
Parties must have contrary views about event outcome.

**6. Event Not Under Control**
Event outcome not in control of parties.

**Case Law: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)**
Not a wager - company had commercial interest, not mere speculation.

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas Maiya (1959)**
Distinction between speculation and wager explained.

**Illustrations:**

**Example 1:**
A and B bet ₹10,000 on outcome of cricket match. A bets India wins, B bets Pakistan wins.
**Result:** Wagering agreement, void.

**Example 2:**
A agrees to insure B's house against fire. Premium paid.
**Result:** Not wager - insurance contracts valid as insurable interest exists.

**Example 3:**
A and B bet on whether it will rain tomorrow.
**Result:** Wager, void.

**Difference Between Wager and Insurance:**

| **Wager** | **Insurance** |
|-----------|---------------|
| No insurable interest | Insurable interest necessary |
| Based on chance | Based on risk management |
| Both parties speculate | One party seeks security |
| Void | Valid |
| Uncertain event with no interest | Protection against loss |

**Difference Between Wager and Game of Skill:**

**Wager:** Based on chance, void.

**Game of Skill:** Based on skill, may be valid.

**Case Law: State of Bombay v. RMD Chamarbaugwala (1957)**
Crossword competition based substantially on skill, not wager.

### 9. Agreement Contingent on Impossible Events [Section 36]

**Section 36:** "Contingent agreements to do or not to do anything, if an impossible event happens, are void, whether the impossibility of the event is known to the parties or not."

**Illustration from Section 36:**
A agrees to pay B ₹10,000 if B will marry A's daughter C. C was dead at time of agreement. Agreement void.

### 10. Agreements to Do Impossible Acts [Section 56]

**Section 56:** "An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void."

**Types of Impossibility:**

**1. Physical Impossibility:**
Inherently impossible.

**Example:** Agreement to discover treasure by magic - void.

**2. Legal Impossibility:**
Impossible in law.

**Example:** Agreement to transfer non-transferable right.

**3. Impossibility of Performance:**
See doctrine of frustration.

**Illustration from Section 56:**
A agrees with B to discover treasure by magic. Agreement void.

#### Distinction: Void, Voidable, and Illegal Agreements

**VOID AGREEMENT**

**Definition:** Agreement without legal effect from beginning.

**Characteristics:**
1. Not enforceable by law
2. Void ab initio
3. Creates no rights or obligations
4. Cannot be performed
5. Cannot be ratified

**Effect on Collateral Transactions:**
Collateral agreements may be valid.

**Restitution:**
Generally possible (Section 65).

**Examples:**
- Agreement with minor
- Agreement without consideration
- Agreement in restraint of marriage
- Wagering agreement

**VOIDABLE AGREEMENT/CONTRACT**

**Definition:** Agreement enforceable at option of one party but not at option of other.

**Characteristics:**
1. Valid until rescinded
2. Voidable at option of aggrieved party
3. Creates rights and obligations
4. Can be performed if not avoided
5. Can be ratified

**Causes:**
- Coercion (Section 15)
- Undue influence (Section 16)
- Fraud (Section 17)
- Misrepresentation (Section 18)

**Effect on Collateral Transactions:**
Not affected if made before rescission.

**Restitution:**
Party rescinding must restore benefits (Section 64).

**Examples:**
- Contract induced by fraud
- Contract by coercion
- Contract by undue influence

**Bars to Rescission (Section 19A):**
1. Restitution impossible
2. Third party rights
3. Affirmation
4. Undue delay

**ILLEGAL AGREEMENT**

**Definition:** Agreement forbidden by law or contrary to public policy.

**Characteristics:**
1. Not enforceable by law
2. Void ab initio
3. Criminal/immoral in nature
4. Cannot be performed
5. Cannot be ratified

**Effect on Collateral Transactions:**
Collateral agreements also void and tainted by illegality.

**Restitution:**
Generally not possible (ex turpi causa non oritur actio).

**Examples:**
- Agreement to commit crime
- Agreement to defraud
- Agreement for immoral purpose
- Agreement forbidden by statute

#### Comparison Table

| **Aspect** | **Void** | **Voidable** | **Illegal** |
|------------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Enforceability** | Not enforceable | Enforceable until rescinded | Not enforceable |
| **From When** | Ab initio | After rescission | Ab initio |
| **Nature** | Lacks essential element | Consent vitiated | Unlawful object |
| **Ratification** | Not possible | Possible | Not possible |
| **Collateral** | May be valid | Valid | Also void |
| **Restitution** | Generally yes | Yes (Section 64) | Generally no |
| **Example** | Minor's contract | Contract by fraud | Agreement to commit crime |
| **Parties** | Neither can enforce | One party can rescind | Neither can enforce |

#### Important Case Laws

**1. Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)**
Minor's agreement void ab initio, cannot be ratified.

**2. Balfour v. Batten (1957)**
Illegal agreements taint collateral transactions.

**3. Chinnaya v. Ramayya (1882)**
Agreement without consideration from promisee but from third party - valid in India.

**4. Raffles v. Wichelhaus (1864)**
Agreement uncertain due to mutual mistake - void.

**5. Trimble v. Hill (1879)**
Voidable contract can be ratified by aggrieved party after cause ceases.

#### Consequences of Void Agreements

**1. No Legal Remedy:**
Courts will not enforce.

**2. Money Paid or Goods Delivered:**
May be recovered under Section 65 (subject to exceptions).

**3. Collateral Transactions:**
Generally not affected (unless agreement is illegal).

**4. No Estoppel:**
Party cannot be estopped from pleading that agreement is void.

**5. No Damages:**
No remedy in damages for breach.

#### Practical Illustrations

**Illustration 1: Void**
A, aged 17, agrees to buy B's car for ₹5 lakhs.
**Result:** Void. A is minor.

**Illustration 2: Voidable**
A threatens to harm B's family unless B sells house. B agrees.
**Result:** Voidable at B's option due to coercion.

**Illustration 3: Illegal**
A agrees to pay B ₹10 lakhs to eliminate A's business competitor.
**Result:** Illegal. Agreement to commit crime.

**Illustration 4: Void for Uncertainty**
A agrees to buy B's "good furniture."
**Result:** Void for uncertainty unless capable of being made certain.

**Illustration 5: Wager**
A and B bet ₹50,000 on election results.
**Result:** Void. Wagering agreement.

#### Conclusion
The Indian Contract Act provides a comprehensive framework for identifying void agreements, distinguishing them from voidable and illegal agreements. Void agreements lack legal enforceability from the outset due to absence of essential elements such as competent parties, lawful consideration, lawful object, or certainty of terms. While void and voidable agreements differ in their enforceability and potential for ratification, illegal agreements go further by tainting associated transactions and barring restitution. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for contract drafting and enforcement, as they determine not only whether a contract can be enforced, but also the remedies available to parties and the effect on related transactions. The Act's provisions on void agreements protect public policy, individual rights, and ensure that only genuine, lawful, and certain agreements receive the sanction of law.

## Question 2: "Agreements by way of wager are void." Explain the meaning and essentials of wagering agreements under Section 30 of the Indian Contract Act. Distinguish between wagers, insurance contracts, and speculative transactions.

### MODEL ANSWER:

#### Introduction
Wagering agreements are declared void under Section 30 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. These agreements, based on uncertain events where parties take opposite positions purely for gain or loss, are contrary to public policy. While gambling and betting are discouraged, the Act draws important distinctions between wagers and legitimate commercial transactions involving risk, such as insurance and speculation.

#### Section 30 - Wagering Agreements

**Section 30 states:**
"Agreements by way of wager are void; and no suit shall be brought for recovering anything alleged to be won on any wager, or entrusted to any person to abide by the result of any game or other uncertain event on which any wager is made."

**Proviso:** "This section shall not be deemed to render unlawful a subscription or contribution, or agreement to subscribe or contribute, made or entered into for or toward any plate, prize, or sum of money, of the value or amount of five hundred rupees or upwards, to be awarded to the winner or winners of any horse-race."

#### Definition of Wager

**Not Defined in Act:**
The Indian Contract Act does not define "wager."

**Judicial Definition:**

**Hawkins J. in Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1892):**
"A wagering contract is one by which two persons, professing to hold opposite views touching the issue of a future uncertain event, mutually agree that, dependent upon the determination of that event, one shall win from the other, and that other shall pay or hand over to him, a sum of money or other stake; neither of the contracting parties having any other interest in that contract than the sum or stake he will so win or lose, there being no other real consideration for the making of such contract by either of the parties."

**Simple Definition:**
An agreement between two parties where each promises to pay money or give something of value to the other based on outcome of an uncertain future event, and each party stands to either win or lose.

#### Essentials of Wagering Agreement

**1. Promise to Pay Money or Money's Worth**

Must involve transfer of money or valuable thing from loser to winner.

**Example:** 
- Money bet on cricket match
- Property staked on card game
- Prize for winner of bet

**2. Uncertain Future Event**

- Event must be uncertain in outcome
- Neither party knows result in advance
- Element of chance involved

**Examples of Uncertain Events:**
- Sports match result
- Whether it will rain
- Election outcome
- Share price movement

**3. Opposite or Contrary Views**

- Parties must hold opposite views about event outcome
- One says event will happen, other says it won't
- Or both predict different outcomes

**Example:**
A says India will win, B says Pakistan will win (opposite views).

**4. Mutual Chance of Gain or Loss**

- Each party stands either to win or lose
- Win/loss is reciprocal
- One party's gain is other's loss

**Not a Wager:**
A promises to pay B if India wins. B promises nothing.
(Only A risks loss; not mutual)

**5. No Other Interest in Event**

**Most Important Element:**
- Parties have no interest in event except winning/losing stake
- No commercial, business, or personal interest in event itself
- Merely speculating on outcome

**Example of NO OTHER INTEREST:**
A and B bet on whether X will marry Y. Neither A nor B is related to X or Y, no other interest except stake.

**Example of OTHER INTEREST (Not Wager):**
A insures his own house - A has insurable interest, not mere speculation.

**6. No Intention to Perform**

- Neither party intends the event to happen differently
- Both simply waiting for predetermined event outcome
- No control over event

**7. Event Not Under Control of Parties**

Event outcome must be independent of parties' will or control.

**Example:**
Betting on cricket match result - parties cannot control outcome (assuming no match-fixing!).

#### Important Case Laws

**1. Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)**
Facts: Company advertised reward for anyone using their product as directed and still getting flu.
Held: Not a wager. Company had commercial interest in promoting product, not mere speculation. Unilateral offer for consideration.

**2. Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas Maiya (1959) - Supreme Court**
Facts: Forward contract for sale of gur (jaggery) in future at specified price.
Held: Not a wager. Commercial transaction with intention to perform contract. Parties had business interest in actual delivery.

**Distinction between Speculation and Wager:**
- Speculation: Genuine commercial transaction, parties intend performance
- Wager: No intention of performance, purely betting on event

**3. State of Bombay v. RMD Chamarbaugwala (1957)**
Facts: Crossword competition with prizes. Entry fee required.
Held: Not a wager. Crossword solving requires skill. Competition based substantially on skill is not wagering.

**Principle:** Games of skill are not wagers; games of chance are wagers.

**4. Dr. KR Lakshmanan v. State of Tamil Nadu (1996)**
Facts: Horse racing with betting.
Held: Horse racing is game of skill, not chance. Betting on horse races permissible under Section 30 exception.

**5. Dayabhai Tribhobandas v. Life Insurance Corporation (1971)**
Facts: Insurance policy challenged as wager.
Held: Not wager. Insurance requires insurable interest. Legitimate risk management tool.

#### What is NOT a Wager

**1. Insurance Contracts**
- Insurable interest exists
- Risk management, not speculation
- Valid contracts

**2. Games of Skill**
- Substantially dependent on skill
- Not mere chance
- Valid (e.g., chess tournaments, knowledge competitions)

**3. Speculative Business Transactions**
- Genuine business purpose
- Intention to perform
- Commercial interest beyond stake
- Valid (e.g., futures contracts, forward contracts with intention to deliver)

**4. Competitions and Prizes**
- Based on skill or knowledge
- Not pure chance
- Valid

**5. Share Market Transactions**
- Genuine investment
- Business purpose
- Even if speculative, not wagers if intention to perform

#### Effects of Wagering Agreement

**1. Void and Unenforceable**
No legal action can be brought to recover winnings.

**2. Money Paid Under Wager**
Generally cannot be recovered if voluntarily paid.

**Rationale:** Ex turpi causa (from a dishonorable cause, no action arises).

**3. Collateral Transactions**

**General Rule:** Collateral transactions to wagering agreement are not void unless they are also illegal.

**Case Law: Thacker v. Hardy (1878) - English**
Facts: Money lent specifically for gambling. Lender sued to recover.
Held: Loan recoverable. Collateral transaction to wager, not void unless lender participated in wagering.

**Indian Position:** Similar principle but courts scrutinize closely.

**4. Stakeholder**

Money entrusted to stakeholder to abide by wager result cannot be recovered by either party (Section 30 expressly prohibits).

**Case Law: Read v. Anderson (1884)**
Money deposited with stakeholder for wager cannot be recovered.

**Exception:** Before event occurs, party may withdraw stake if stakeholder hasn't paid winner yet.

**5. Securities for Wagers**

Loan advanced for wagering purpose is void and unrecoverable.

**6. Agreements Collateral to Wagers**

**Not Void:**
- Loan not specifically for wager
- Agency to invest (if agent has discretion)

**Void:**
- Commission for arranging wager
- Lending specifically for gambling

#### Distinction: Wager vs. Insurance Contract

| **Basis** | **Wager** | **Insurance** |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Insurable Interest** | No insurable interest | Insurable interest required |
| **Purpose** | Speculation/gambling | Risk management/protection |
| **Number of Parties** | Two parties with opposite views | Insured seeks protection, insurer provides |
| **Interest in Subject** | No interest except stake | Direct interest in subject matter |
| **Loss** | One's gain is other's loss (zero-sum) | Insured compensated for actual loss |
| **Legality** | Void (Section 30) | Valid and enforceable |
| **Example** | A and B bet on whether X's house burns | A insures his own house against fire |
| **Measure of Payment** | Fixed stake agreed beforehand | Actual loss (up to policy limit) |
| **Relationship to Event** | Indifferent to event except winning | Prefer event not to occur (loss prevention) |

**Key Distinguishing Factor: Insurable Interest**

**Insurable Interest** means person has legitimate interest in preservation of subject matter.

**Case Law: Dayabhai Tribhobandas v. LIC (1971)**
Insurance valid because insurable interest exists. Not a wager.

**Case Law: Castellain v. Preston (1883)**
Insurance is contract of indemnity, not speculation.

#### Distinction: Wager vs. Speculation (Legitimate Business)

| **Basis** | **Wager** | **Speculation** |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Intention** | No intention to perform | Intention to perform contract |
| **Business Interest** | No business interest | Genuine business interest |
| **Delivery** | No delivery contemplated | Delivery intended (even if not always done) |
| **Purpose** | Pure betting | Commercial transaction |
| **Legality** | Void | Valid |
| **Example** | Betting on cotton prices | Futures contract for cotton delivery |
| **Market** | Illegal betting market | Recognized commodity exchange |
| **Regulation** | Prohibited | Regulated by SEBI, etc. |

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh (1959)**
Forward contracts with intention to perform are valid speculative transactions, not wagers.

**Important Note:**
Even if parties later settle by paying difference (without actual delivery), contract may still be valid speculation if original intention was to perform.

#### Distinction: Game of Skill vs. Game of Chance

**Game of Chance:**
- Outcome depends on luck/chance
- No or minimal skill involved
- Amounts to wagering
- **Examples:** Dice, roulette, lottery

**Game of Skill:**
- Outcome depends substantially on skill
- Not wagering
- Competitions valid
- **Examples:** Chess, bridge, crossword, horse racing (selecting winner requires knowledge)

**Test:**
Does success depend substantially on superior knowledge, judgment, skill, or attention of player?

**Case Law: RMD Chamarbaugwala (1957)**
Crossword competition primarily skill-based, not wager.

**Case Law: State of Andhra Pradesh v. K Satyanarayana (1968)**
Rummy is game of skill, not chance.

#### Share Market and Commodity Market Transactions

**Question:** Are stock market transactions wagers?

**Answer:** Generally NO, if:
1. Transactions on recognized stock exchange
2. Intention to perform (delivery or settlement)
3. Genuine investment or hedging purpose
4. Regulated by SEBI

**Speculation vs. Wager:**
- **Speculation:** Taking risk in hope of gain based on market analysis - Valid
- **Wager:** Pure betting without business purpose - Void

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh (1959)**
Speculative forward contracts with intention to perform are not wagers.

**Derivative Contracts:**
Options, futures, forwards - valid if on recognized exchange with intention to perform or settle according to market mechanism.

#### Horse Racing Exception - Proviso to Section 30

**Exception:**
Horse racing bets for prize money of ₹500 or more are exempted from Section 30.

**Rationale:**
- Horse racing considered game of skill
- Requires knowledge of horses, jockeys, conditions
- Sport with commercial aspects

**Case Law: Dr. KR Lakshmanan (1996)**
Horse racing is game of skill. Betting on skill-based activity permissible.

**State Laws:**
Many states have specific laws regulating or prohibiting betting and gambling.

#### Practical Examples

**Example 1: Wager**
A and B bet ₹10,000 on whether England will win Ashes series. Neither has any interest except the stake.
**Result:** Wagering agreement, void.

**Example 2: Insurance (Not Wager)**
A insures his car for ₹10 lakhs against accident.
**Result:** Valid insurance, not wager. A has insurable interest in his car.

**Example 3: Speculation (Not Wager)**
A agrees to buy 100 tonnes of wheat from B in 3 months at ₹30,000 per tonne.
**Result:** If intention to perform (deliver wheat), valid speculative transaction, not wager.

**Example 4: Game of Skill (Not Wager)**
Chess tournament with ₹1 lakh prize for winner. Entry fee ₹1,000.
**Result:** Game of skill, not wager. Valid.

**Example 5: Wager on Event**
A and B bet on whether PM will visit USA in 2026. ₹50,000 stake.
**Result:** Wagering agreement, void.

**Example 6: Share Trading (Not Wager)**
A buys 100 shares of Reliance on stock exchange hoping price will rise.
**Result:** Valid investment/speculation, not wager. Commercial purpose.

#### Conclusion
Section 30 of the Indian Contract Act declares wagering agreements void as a matter of public policy, recognizing that gambling is harmful to society and individuals. A wagering agreement is characterized by mutual promises based on uncertain events where parties have no interest beyond the stake itself, with one's gain being the other's loss. However, the law carefully distinguishes wagers from legitimate commercial activities involving risk, such as insurance contracts (which require insurable interest), speculative business transactions (which involve genuine commercial purpose and intention to perform), and games of skill (which reward merit rather than chance). Understanding these distinctions is essential for determining the validity of contracts in modern commerce, where risk-taking, hedging, and speculation are integral to business operations. The exception for horse racing reflects the recognition of skill-based activities, though they remain subject to state regulations on gambling and betting.
