# CAPACITY TO CONTRACT – 5 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Q1: Who are competent to contract under Section 11?

**Section 11:**
"Every person is competent to contract who is—
(a) of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject; and
(b) of sound mind; and
(c) is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject."

**Three Classes Incompetent:**
1. **Minors:** Below 18 years (21 if guardian appointed)
2. **Persons of unsound mind:** Idiots, lunatics, intoxicated persons
3. **Persons disqualified by law:** Alien enemies, convicts, insolvents, corporations (ultra vires)

**Consequence:** Agreements with incompetent persons are void.

## Q2: Explain the position of minor's agreements with reference to Mohori Bibee case.

**Case Law: Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903) - Privy Council**

**Facts:**
- Minor mortgaged property to money lender
- Lender knew he was minor
- Minor's mother sued to set aside mortgage

**Held:**
1. Minor's agreement is **VOID AB INITIO** (not voidable)
2. Section 11 makes minors incompetent
3. Contract void from beginning
4. Minor need not return money obtained
5. Minor's property cannot be made liable
6. No estoppel against minor even if he committed fraud

**Significance:** Leading authority establishing minor's agreements are absolutely void in India.

## Q3: What are necessaries? Explain Section 68.

**Section 68:** "If a person, incapable of entering into a contract, or any one whom he is legally bound to support, is supplied by another person with necessaries suited to his condition in life, the person who has furnished such supplies is entitled to be reimbursed from the property of such incapable person."

**Necessaries:**
Goods/services essential for person's existence and suitable to his station in life.

**Include:**
- Food, clothing, shelter
- Education, medical treatment
- Marriage expenses (in India)
- Legal expenses for protection

**Exclude:**
- Luxury items
- Goods beyond person's status
- Items already adequately supplied

**Important Points:**
1. Only property liable, not person
2. Reasonable price, not contracted price
3. Must be suitable to condition

**Case Law: Nash v. Inman (1908)**
11 fancy waistcoats to student - not necessaries (already supplied). Cannot recover.

**Case Law: Chapple v. Cooper (1844)**
Funeral expenses - necessaries. Estate liable.

## Q4: State the test for soundness of mind under Section 12.

**Section 12:**
"A person is said to be of sound mind for the purpose of making a contract, if, at the time when he makes it, he is capable of understanding it and of forming a rational judgment as to its effect upon his interests."

**Three Requirements:**
1. **Capacity to understand:** Must understand nature and effect
2. **Rational judgment:** Ability to form reasonable judgment about consequences
3. **At time of contract:** Soundness required when making contract

**Lucid Intervals:**
"A person who is usually of unsound mind, but occasionally of sound mind, may make a contract when he is of sound mind."

**Burden of Proof:** On person alleging unsoundness.

**Effect:** Contract with unsound person is void.

## Q5: Explain the rule regarding minor's liability for necessaries.

**Section 68 Application:**
Minor's **property** (not person) liable for necessaries supplied.

**Conditions:**
1. Person must be minor (or unsound, or disqualified)
2. Necessaries supplied
3. Suited to minor's condition in life
4. Actually supplied (not merely contracted)

**Important Distinctions:**
- **Personal liability:** None - Minor cannot be sued personally
- **Property liability:** Minor's property can be made liable
- **Amount:** Reasonable price, not necessarily contracted price

**Rationale:** Protects minors from exploitation while ensuring they can obtain essential goods/services.

**Case Law: Nash v. Inman (1908)**
Tailor supplied waistcoats to minor. Not necessaries as adequately supplied. Cannot recover from minor's estate.

**Case Law: Chapple v. Cooper (1844)**
Widow (minor) ordered husband's funeral. Funeral is necessary. Estate liable.

**Example:**
- A supplies food to minor B. Food is necessary.
- A entitled to reasonable price from B's property.
- A cannot sue B personally.
- B's property liable for reimbursement.

## Q6: Can a minor's agreement be ratified on attaining majority?

**General Rule:** No. Minor's agreement cannot be ratified on attaining majority.

**Reason:**
- Minor's agreement is VOID AB INITIO (void from beginning)
- What is void remains void forever
- Ratification applies to voidable contracts, not void

**Case Law: Suraj Narain Dube v. Sukhu Aheer (1929)**
Minor executed promissory note. After majority, executed fresh note for same debt. Held: Fresh promise without consideration and not enforceable.

**For Fresh Contract:**
After attaining majority, fresh contract with new consideration can be made.

**Comparison:**
- **Void contract:** Cannot be ratified
- **Voidable contract:** Can be ratified by aggrieved party

## Q7: Explain the position of persons disqualified by law to contract.

**Section 11(c):** Persons disqualified by law cannot contract.

**Categories:**

**1. Alien Enemies:**
- During war: Contracts void
- Existing contracts suspended
- After war: Can contract freely

**2. Foreign Sovereigns & Ambassadors:**
- Can contract
- Cannot be sued without government consent
- Diplomatic immunity

**3. Corporations:**
- Can contract within memorandum powers
- Ultra vires contracts void
- Cannot be ratified

**4. Insolvents:**
- Can contract
- Property vests in official receiver
- Limited capacity regarding property

**5. Convicts:**
- During imprisonment: Civil rights suspended
- After release: Rights restored

**6. Married Women:**
- Currently: Full capacity (old restrictions removed)

**Effect:** Contracts by disqualified persons are void.

## Q8: Can a minor be a partner or an agent?

**Minor as Partner:**
**Partnership Act, Section 30:**
- Minor CANNOT be partner in firm
- Minor CAN be admitted to benefits of partnership

**Position:**
- With consent of all partners
- Admitted to share of profits
- Not personally liable for firm's debts
- Minor's share liable (not beyond)
- Can sue partners for accounts
- Can opt out or become partner on attaining majority (within 6 months)

**Minor as Agent:**
**Section 184:** Minor can act as agent.

**Position:**
- Can bind principal
- Not personally liable to principal
- Not personally liable to third parties
- Principal liable for minor's acts within authority

**Rationale:** Agency doesn't create personal obligations on agent, so minor can act as agent.

**Example:**
- A (aged 16) appointed as agent by P to sell goods.
- A can bind P in sale to third party T.
- P liable to T.
- A not personally liable even if acts improperly (but P has liability).
