# OFFER AND ACCEPTANCE – 5 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Q1: Define offer and acceptance. What are cross offers?

**Offer (Section 2(a)):**
When one person signifies to another his willingness to do or abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining assent, he makes a proposal/offer.

**Acceptance (Section 2(b)):**
When person to whom proposal made signifies assent, proposal is accepted. A proposal when accepted becomes a promise.

**Cross Offers:**
When two parties make identical offers to each other in ignorance of each other's offer, and offers cross in mail.

**Case Law: Tinn v. Hoffmann (1873)**
Identical cross offers do not constitute acceptance. No contract formed.

**Reason:** Neither party accepting other's offer; both making offers.

## Q2: Explain the communication of offer and acceptance under Section 4.

**Section 4 - When Communication Complete:**

**1. Communication of Proposal:**
- Against proposer: When put into transmission
- Against offeree: When comes to knowledge

**2. Communication of Acceptance:**
- Against acceptor: When put into transmission
- Against proposer: When comes to knowledge

**Example:**
Day 1: A posts offer (complete against A)
Day 3: B receives (complete against B)
Day 4: B posts acceptance (complete against B and A)
Day 6: A receives acceptance

**Contract formed on Day 4** when acceptance posted (Postal Rule).

**Instantaneous Communication:**
For phone, fax, email - complete only when received, not sent.

## Q3: State the rules of valid acceptance.

**Rules of Valid Acceptance:**

1. **Absolute and unqualified** (Section 7) - No modifications
2. **Communicated to offeror** - Silence not acceptance
3. **By offeree only** - Third party cannot accept
4. **Before offer lapses** - Within time or reasonable time
5. **In prescribed manner** - As specified or reasonably
6. **Must succeed offer** - Acceptance before offer invalid

**Case Law: Hyde v. Wrench (1840)**
Counter-offer destroys original offer.

**Case Law: Felthouse v. Bindley (1862)**
Silence cannot be acceptance.

## Q4: Distinguish between offer and invitation to offer.

| **Offer** | **Invitation to Offer** |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| Definite proposal | Invites others to make offers |
| Acceptance creates contract | No contract until offer made |
| Shows intent to be bound | Preliminary negotiation |
| Example: Advertisement promising reward | Example: Display of goods in shop |

**Case Laws:**
- **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball:** Reward advertisement is offer
- **Pharmaceutical Society v. Boots:** Display in shop is invitation to offer
- **Harris v. Nickerson:** Auction advertisement is invitation to offer

## Q5: What is a general offer? Can it be accepted by anyone?

**General Offer:**
Offer made to world at large, not specific person(s).

**Acceptance:**
Anyone with knowledge of offer can accept by performing conditions.

**Case Law: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)**
- Company advertised ₤100 reward for anyone using smoke ball as directed and still getting flu
- Mrs. Carlill used it, got flu, claimed reward
- Held: Valid general offer. Accepted by performing conditions. No prior communication of acceptance needed.

**Requirements:**
1. Knowledge of offer essential
2. Must perform conditions
3. Act must be in response to offer

## Q6: When can an offer be revoked? Explain Section 5.

**Section 5 - Revocation:**

**Proposal can be revoked:**
Anytime before communication of acceptance is complete as against proposer, but not afterwards.

**Acceptance can be revoked:**
Anytime before communication of acceptance is complete as against acceptor, but not afterwards.

**Case Law: Byrne v. Van Tienhoven (1880)**
- Offer posted Oct 1
- Revocation posted Oct 8
- Acceptance sent Oct 11 (contract formed)
- Revocation received Oct 20
- Held: Valid contract. Revocation came after acceptance was complete.

**Other Modes of Lapse:**
- Time expiry
- Death/insanity (if known)
- Non-fulfillment of condition precedent
- Counter-offer
- Rejection

## Q7: Explain the doctrine of "postal acceptance" or "postal rule."

**Postal Rule:**
When acceptance sent by post, contract complete when acceptance posted, not when received.

**Case Law: Adams v. Lindsell (1818)**
Contract complete when acceptance posted, even if letter lost or delayed.

**Application:**
- Acceptance complete against both parties when posted
- Offeror bound from moment of posting
- Applies even if letter lost in post

**Does NOT Apply To:**
- Instantaneous communication (phone, fax, telex, email)
- When offeror specifies actual communication required

**Case Law: Entores v. Miles Far East (1955)**
Telex acceptance - complete when and where received, not sent.

**Revocation:**
Acceptance cannot be revoked once posted (complete against acceptor).

## Q8: What is a standing offer?

**Standing Offer (Continuing Offer):**
Offer remaining open for acceptance over period of time or for series of transactions.

**Legal Position:**
- Not a contract until accepted
- Each acceptance creates separate contract
- Can be revoked for future transactions
- Already accepted orders remain binding

**Case Law: Great Northern Railway v. Witham (1873)**
- W made standing offer to supply items for one year as required
- Railway placed several orders
- W refused later orders
- Held: Each order creates separate contract. W liable for accepted orders but can refuse future orders.

**Example:**
Tender to supply 1,000 tonnes coal per month for one year.
- Standing offer (not contract)
- Each monthly order: acceptance creating contract
- Supplier can withdraw for future months
- Bound for already accepted orders
