# FREE CONSENT – 5 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Q1: What is free consent? State the factors vitiating free consent.

**Section 13 - Consent:**
"Two or more persons are said to consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense."

**Section 14 - Free Consent:**
"Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by—
1. Coercion (Section 15)
2. Undue influence (Section 16)
3. Fraud (Section 17)
4. Misrepresentation (Section 18)
5. Mistake (Sections 20, 21, 22)"

**Effect:** Consent vitiated by coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation makes contract **voidable** at aggrieved party's option (Section 19).

---

## Q2: Define coercion under Section 15 with examples.

**Section 15:**
"Coercion is the committing, or threatening to commit, any act forbidden by the Indian Penal Code, or the unlawful detaining, or threatening to detain, any property, to the prejudice of any person whatever, with the intention of causing any person to enter into an agreement."

**Essentials:**
1. Commission or threat of act forbidden by IPC
2. Unlawful detention or threat to detain property
3. Intention to cause agreement
4. Against any person (not limited to contracting party)

**Examples:**
- Threat to file false criminal case
- Threat to harm family member
- Unlawful detention of goods
- Physical violence

**Case Law: Ranganayakamma v. Alwar Setti (1889)**
Money lender detained borrower's son. Father signed note. Coercion established.

**Effect:** Contract voidable at option of coerced party (Section 19).

---

## Q3: Explain undue influence under Section 16.

**Section 16(1):**
Contract induced by "undue influence" where relations between parties such that one in position to dominate will of other and uses position to obtain unfair advantage.

**Essential Elements:**
1. Position to dominate will
2. Use of that position
3. Unfair advantage obtained

**Section 16(2) - When Presumed:**
Person deemed in position to dominate where:
(a) He holds real/apparent authority or stands in fiduciary relation; or
(b) He makes contract with person whose mental capacity affected by age, illness, or distress

**Relationships Where Presumed:**
- Parent-child
- Guardian-ward
- Doctor-patient
- Lawyer-client
- Spiritual advisor-devotee
- Trustee-beneficiary

**Section 16(3) - Burden of Proof:**
When person in dominant position makes unconscionable transaction, burden shifts to him to prove no undue influence.

**Case Law: Inche Noriah v. Shaik Allie Bin Omar (1929)**
Old lady gifted property to nephew managing affairs. No independent advice. Gift set aside.

**Effect:** Contract voidable (Section 19).

## Q4: Distinguish between coercion and undue influence.

| **Coercion** | **Undue Influence** |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Physical/criminal force | Moral/mental pressure |
| Criminal threats | Domination of will |
| No relationship required | Usually relationship involved |
| Against any person | Against party to contract |
| Precisely defined (Section 15) | Broadly defined (Section 16) |
| Involves IPC violations | No criminal element |
| Burden on alleging party | May be presumed; burden shifts |
| Example: Threat to kill | Example: Doctor pressuring patient |

**Case Laws:**
- **Coercion:** Ranganayakamma - Detention of son
- **Undue Influence:** Inche Noriah - Old lady's gift to nephew

**Common Feature:** Both make contract voidable at option of aggrieved party (Section 19).

---

## Q5: What is fraud under Section 17? Give examples.

**Section 17 - Fraud:**
Fraud means and includes any of following acts by party or with his connivance/agent:
1. Suggestion of fact which is not true by one who doesn't believe it true
2. Active concealment of fact by one having knowledge/belief
3. Promise without intention to perform
4. Any other act fitted to deceive
5. Any such act or omission as law specially declares fraudulent

**Essentials:**
1. False representation of fact
2. Knowledge of falsity or belief in untruth
3. Intention to deceive
4. Inducement to act
5. Other party actually deceived
6. Damage/injury suffered

**Examples:**
- Selling fake branded goods as genuine
- Concealing material defects in property
- False statement about property ownership
- Misrepresenting financial position

**Effect:** Contract voidable at option of aggrieved party. Party can also claim damages (Section 19).

**Exception:** If party had means to discover truth with ordinary diligence, may not claim fraud.

## Q6: Distinguish between fraud and misrepresentation.

| **Fraud (Section 17)** | **Misrepresentation (Section 18)** |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| False statement knowingly | False statement innocently |
| Intent to deceive | No intent to deceive |
| Knowledge of falsity | Belief in truth |
| Moral wrong | No moral wrong |
| Remedy: Rescission + Damages | Remedy: Rescission only |
| Example: Deliberately selling fake as genuine | Example: Honestly believing fake is genuine and selling |

**Common Feature:** Both make contract voidable (Section 19).

**Section 19:** Party can:
- Insist contract be performed, or
- Rescind contract
- Fraud: Can also claim damages
- Misrepresentation: Only rescission, no damages

---

## Q7: Explain the effect of coercion, fraud, and misrepresentation on contracts (Section 19).

**Section 19:**
"When consent to an agreement is caused by coercion, fraud or misrepresentation, the agreement is a contract voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused."

**Contract is Voidable:**
- Not void, but voidable
- Choice lies with aggrieved party
- Can rescind or affirm

**Party Can:**
1. **Rescind contract** - Avoid it entirely
2. **Affirm contract** - Insist on performance
3. **Claim damages** - For fraud only (not for coercion/misrepresentation unless special circumstances)

**Bars to Rescission (Section 19A):**
1. **Restitution impossible:** Parties cannot be restored
2. **Third party rights:** Bona fide purchasers for value
3. **Affirmation:** Party has affirmed after cause ceased
4. **Undue delay:** Unreasonable delay in rescinding

**Restitution (Section 64):**
On rescission, party must restore benefits received.

## Q8: What is mistake under the Indian Contract Act? Explain mistake of fact.

**Types of Mistake:**
1. **Mistake of Fact** (Sections 20, 21, 22)
2. **Mistake of Law** (Section 21)

**Mistake of Fact (Section 20):**
Agreement void if both parties mistaken as to matter of fact essential to agreement.

**Types:**

**1. Bilateral Mistake (Section 20):**
- Both parties under same mistake
- About fact essential to agreement
- Contract void

**Example:** A agrees to sell B a specific painting believing it exists. Both unaware painting destroyed. Void.

**Case Law: Raffles v. Wichelhaus (1864)**
Two ships named "Peerless." Buyer meant one, seller meant other. No consensus. Void.

**2. Unilateral Mistake (Section 22):**
- Only one party mistaken
- Generally does NOT vitiate consent
- Contract valid unless mistake relates to identity of party or nature of contract

**Mistake as to Identity (Section 22):**
Contract voidable if mistake as to identity of person contracted with.

**Section 21 - Mistake of Law:**
Contract NOT voidable for mistake of law.
- Ignorance of law is no excuse
- Everyone presumed to know law

**Exception:** Mistake of private rights may be relieved.
