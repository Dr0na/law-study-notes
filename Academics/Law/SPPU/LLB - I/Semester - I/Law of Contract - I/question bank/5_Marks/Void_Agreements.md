# VOID AGREEMENTS – 5 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Q1: What is a void agreement? Give examples.

**Section 2(g):**
"An agreement not enforceable by law is said to be void."

**Characteristics:**
- Not enforceable from beginning (ab initio)
- Creates no legal rights or obligations
- Cannot be ratified
- Neither party can sue

**Examples:**
1. Agreement with minor (Section 11)
2. Agreement without consideration (Section 25)
3. Agreement in restraint of marriage (Section 26)
4. Agreement in restraint of trade (Section 27)
5. Agreement in restraint of legal proceedings (Section 28)
6. Agreement with uncertain meaning (Section 29)
7. Wagering agreement (Section 30)
8. Agreement contingent on impossible event (Section 36)
9. Agreement with unlawful object/consideration (Sections 23, 24)

**Case Law: Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)**
Minor's agreement void ab initio.

## Q2: Define wagering agreement and state its essentials.

**Wagering Agreement (Section 30):**
"Agreements by way of wager are void."

**Definition:**
Agreement between two persons with opposite views about uncertain event, where dependent on event's determination, one wins what other loses, having no other interest except stake.

**Essentials:**
1. **Mutual promises** - Both parties must promise
2. **Uncertain future event** - Outcome unknown
3. **Opposite views** - Contrary opinions about event
4. **No other interest** - Interest only in stake (most important)
5. **No control** - Event independent of parties
6. **Money/money's worth** - Valuable stake

**Effect:** Void (Section 30).

**Exception:** Horse racing bets for prize ₹500+ exempted.

## Q3: Distinguish between void and voidable agreements.

| **Void Agreement** | **Voidable Contract** |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| Not enforceable from beginning | Enforceable until rescinded |
| Void ab initio | Valid initially |
| Neither party can enforce | One party can rescind |
| Cannot be ratified | Can be ratified |
| No legal effect | Creates obligations until avoided |
| Collateral agreements may be valid | Collateral not affected |
| Example: Minor's contract | Example: Contract by fraud |

**Section 2(g):** Defines void agreement.
**Section 2(i):** Defines voidable contract.

**Key Difference:** Void has no legal effect. Voidable gives choice to aggrieved party.

## Q4: Explain wagering agreements vs. insurance contracts.

| **Wager** | **Insurance** |
|-----------|---------------|
| No insurable interest | Insurable interest required |
| Pure speculation | Risk management |
| Both speculating | One seeks protection |
| One's gain = other's loss | Compensation for actual loss |
| Void (Section 30) | Valid contract |
| No genuine interest | Interest in subject matter |
| Example: Bet on whether house burns | Example: Insurance of own house |

**Case Law: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball (1893)**
Reward offer not wager - company had commercial interest.

**Case Law: Dayabhai Tribhobandas v. LIC (1971)**
Life insurance not wager - insurable interest distinguishes it.

**Key Distinction:** Insurable interest.

## Q5: Explain Section 29 - Agreements with uncertain meaning.

**Section 29:**
"Agreements, the meaning of which is not certain, or capable of being made certain, are void."

**Principle:** Courts cannot enforce vague agreements.

**Test:**
Can meaning be determined from agreement or external standards?

**Void for Uncertainty:**
**Case Law: Guthing v. Lynn (1831)**
Promise to pay more if horse proves "lucky" - void for uncertainty.

**Case Law: Scammel v. Ouston (1941)**
"On hire-purchase terms" without specifying - void for uncertainty.

**Not Uncertain:**
**Case Law: Hillas v. Arcos (1932)**
"Fair specification" of timber - not void, can be determined by trade practice.

**Valid if:**
1. Trade usage makes meaning certain
2. Reference to external standard possible
3. Reasonable interpretation available

**Example:**
- "Market price" - valid (can be ascertained)
- "Good quality" - may be void (too vague)

## Q6: Explain Section 30 - Wagering Agreements with its exception.

**Section 30:**
"Agreements by way of wager are void; and no suit shall be brought for recovering anything alleged to be won on any wager, or entrusted to any person to abide by the result of any game or other uncertain event on which any wager is made."

**Effect:**
1. Wagers void
2. Cannot sue to recover winnings
3. Money with stakeholder cannot be recovered

**Exception (Proviso):**
"Horse-race" exception - Bets on horse races for prize money ₹500+ exempted.

**Rationale:** Horse racing considered game of skill.

**Case Law: Dr. KR Lakshmanan v. State of Tamil Nadu (1996)**
Horse racing is game of skill. Betting on skill-based activity permissible.

**State Laws:** Many states have specific gambling laws.

## Q7: Distinguish between wager and speculation.

| **Wager** | **Speculation** |
|-----------|-----------------|
| No business interest | Genuine commercial purpose |
| No intention to perform | Intention to perform contract |
| Pure betting | Business transaction |
| No delivery contemplated | Delivery intended |
| Void | Valid |
| Example: Bet on cotton prices | Example: Futures contract for delivery |

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas (1959)**
Forward contracts with intention to perform are valid speculation, not wagers.

**Key Factors:**
1. **Intention:** Perform vs. settle difference
2. **Interest:** Business vs. gambling
3. **Purpose:** Commerce vs. speculation
4. **Legality:** Valid vs. void

**Share Trading:** On recognized exchanges with intention to perform/settle - valid speculation.

## Q8: What are games of skill vs. games of chance?

**Game of Chance:**
- Outcome depends on luck/chance
- No/minimal skill required
- Amounts to wagering
- **Examples:** Dice, roulette, lottery
- **Void**

**Game of Skill:**
- Outcome depends substantially on skill
- Knowledge, judgment, attention important
- Not wagering
- **Examples:** Chess, bridge, horse racing
- **Valid** - Competitions allowed

**Test:** Does success depend substantially on superior knowledge, judgment, skill?

**Case Law: State of Bombay v. RMD Chamarbaugwala (1957)**
Crossword competition requires skill - not wager. Valid.

**Case Law: State of Andhra Pradesh v. K Satyanarayana (1968)**
Rummy is game of skill - not chance.

**Case Law: Dr. KR Lakshmanan (1996)**
Horse racing is game of skill. Selecting winner requires knowledge of horses, jockeys, conditions.

**Implication:** Competitions/prizes for skill-based activities valid. Gambling on chance-based activities void.
