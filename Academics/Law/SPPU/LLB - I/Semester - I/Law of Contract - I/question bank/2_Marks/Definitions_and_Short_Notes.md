# DEFINITIONS AND SHORT NOTES – 2 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Definitions

### 1. Contract (Section 2(h))
An agreement enforceable by law is a contract.

### 2. Agreement (Section 2(e))
Every promise and every set of promises, forming consideration for each other, is an agreement.

### 3. Proposal/Offer (Section 2(a))
When one person signifies willingness to do or abstain from doing anything to obtain assent of another, he makes a proposal.

### 4. Acceptance (Section 2(b))
When person to whom proposal made signifies assent, proposal is accepted.

### 5. Promise (Section 2(b))
A proposal when accepted becomes a promise.

### 6. Promisor and Promisee (Section 2(c))
Person making promise is promisor. Person accepting is promisee.

### 7. Consideration (Section 2(d))
When at promisor's desire, promisee or any other person does/abstains from doing something, such act/abstinence/promise is consideration.

### 8. Void Agreement (Section 2(g))
An agreement not enforceable by law is void.

### 9. Voidable Contract (Section 2(i))
Agreement enforceable at option of one party but not other is voidable contract.

### 10. Void Contract (Section 2(j))
A contract which ceases to be enforceable by law becomes void when it ceases to be enforceable.

### 11. Consent (Section 13)
Two or more persons consent when they agree upon same thing in same sense.

### 12. Free Consent (Section 14)
Consent free when not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake.

### 13. Coercion (Section 15)
Committing or threatening to commit any act forbidden by IPC, or unlawful detention of property, to induce agreement.

### 14. Undue Influence (Section 16)
Using position to dominate will of another and obtain unfair advantage.

### 15. Fraud (Section 17)
False representation of fact knowingly made with intent to deceive.

### 16. Misrepresentation (Section 18)
False representation innocently made without intent to deceive.

### 17. Contingent Contract (Section 31)
Contract to do/not do something if some event collateral to contract does/doesn't happen.

## Short Notes (2 Marks Each)

### 1. Cross Offers
Identical offers made by two parties to each other in ignorance of each other's offer, crossing in mail. Do not constitute acceptance. No contract formed. *Case: Tinn v. Hoffmann (1873)*

### 2. Standing Offer
Offer remaining open for acceptance over period or for series of transactions. Not contract until accepted. Each acceptance creates separate contract. *Case: Great Northern Railway v. Witham (1873)*

### 3. Postal Rule
When acceptance sent by post, contract complete when acceptance posted, not when received. Applies even if letter lost. *Case: Adams v. Lindsell (1818)*

### 4. Invitation to Offer
Invitation to others to make offers. Not an offer itself. Display of goods, advertisements, auctions are invitations. *Case: Pharmaceutical Society v. Boots (1953)*

### 5. General Offer
Offer made to world at large. Anyone with knowledge can accept by performing conditions. *Case: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball (1893)*

### 6. Past Consideration
Consideration already given before promise made. Valid in India (Section 2(d)). Generally invalid in England.

### 7. Executed Consideration (Present)
Consideration given simultaneously with promise. Example: Cash sale.

### 8. Executory Consideration (Future)
Promise to be performed in future. Most common in commercial contracts.

### 9. Forbearance
Refraining from doing something one has right to do. Valid consideration. Example: Forbearance to sue.

### 10. Stranger to Consideration
Person who gives no consideration. Can sue in India if beneficiary (Section 2(d) allows consideration from any person). *Case: Chinnaya v. Ramayya (1882)*

### 11. Minor
Person below 18 years (21 if guardian appointed under Indian Majority Act). Incompetent to contract (Section 11). Agreements void ab initio. *Case: Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)*

### 12. Necessaries (Section 68)
Goods/services essential for life and suited to person's condition. Minor's property (not person) liable for necessaries supplied.

### 13. Lucid Interval
Period when usually insane person is of sound mind. Can contract during lucid interval (Section 12).

### 14. Consensus ad Idem
Meeting of minds. Parties must agree on same thing in same sense for valid consent (Section 13).

### 15. Estoppel
Principle that person cannot deny what he previously stated/represented. Generally no estoppel against minor even if fraud.

### 16. Burden of Proof (Undue Influence)
In certain relationships (parent-child, doctor-patient), burden shifts to dominant party to prove no undue influence if transaction unconscionable (Section 16(3)).

### 17. Bars to Rescission (Section 19A)
Cannot rescind contract if: (1) Restitution impossible, (2) Third party rights acquired, (3) Contract affirmed, (4) Undue delay.

### 18. Wagering Agreement (Section 30)
Agreement where parties with opposite views bet on uncertain event having no interest except stake. Void.

### 19. Insurable Interest
Legal interest in preservation of subject matter. Distinguishes insurance from wager. Essential for valid insurance.

### 20. Game of Skill vs. Chance
**Skill:** Outcome depends substantially on skill (valid - chess, horse racing). **Chance:** Depends on luck (void - dice, roulette).

### 21. Public Policy
Agreements harmful to public welfare are void though not expressly forbidden. Courts cautious in invoking as "unruly horse."

### 22. Ultra Vires
Acts beyond company's memorandum powers. Such contracts void and cannot be ratified. *Case: Ashbury Railway v. Riche (1875)*

### 23. Champerty and Maintenance
Supporting another's litigation for share in proceeds. Generally opposed to public policy.

### 24. Severability
Separating illegal part from legal part of contract. Legal part may be enforced if severable without altering nature.

### 25. Ex Turpi Causa
"No action arises from immoral cause." Money paid under illegal agreement generally cannot be recovered.

### 26. Quantum Meruit
"As much as earned." Claim for reasonable remuneration for work done when contract cannot be enforced. *Case: Planché v. Colburn (1831)*

### 27. Restitution
Restoration of benefits received. On rescission, parties must restore what obtained (Sections 64, 65).

### 28. Time-Barred Debt
Debt barred by limitation period. Written promise to pay revives it without fresh consideration (Section 25(3)).

### 29. Novation
Substitution of new contract for old one with consent of all parties. Old contract discharged.

### 30. Rescission
Cancellation of contract. Makes contract void. Available when consent vitiated or breach occurs.

### 31. Ratification
Confirmation of previously voidable act making it binding. Only voidable contracts can be ratified, not void.

### 32. Agency (Section 185)
No consideration necessary to create agency relationship. Exception to "no consideration, no contract" rule.

### 33. Bailee's Responsibility
Finder of goods becomes bailee (Section 71). Must take care, not use goods, return to owner.

### 34. Lien
Right to retain property until dues paid. Finder has lien for expenses (Section 168).

### 35. Doctrine of Privity
Only parties to contract can sue. But in India, stranger to consideration can sue if beneficiary.

### 36. Impossible Event (Section 36)
Agreement contingent on impossible event is void, whether parties knew of impossibility or not.

### 37. Collateral Event
Event incidental to main contract. In contingent contracts, performance depends on collateral event (Section 31).

### 38. Indemnity
Contract where one party promises to save other from loss. Contingent contract.

### 39. Guarantee
Contract to perform promise or discharge liability if third person defaults. Contingent on default.

### 40. Unjust Enrichment
Principle that no one should enrich at another's expense without justification. Basis of quasi contracts.

## Important Maxims

1. **Ex nudo pacto non oritur actio** - No action arises from naked contract (no consideration, no contract)

2. **Nemo debet locupletari ex alterius jactura** - No one should enrich unjustly at another's expense (quasi contracts)

3. **Ex turpi causa non oritur actio** - No action arises from immoral cause (illegal agreements)

4. **Consensus ad idem** - Meeting of minds (essential for consent)

5. **Ignorantia juris non excusat** - Ignorance of law is no excuse

6. **Caveat emptor** - Let the buyer beware

## Remember - Essential Elements of Valid Contract

**Section 10:** All agreements are contracts if made by:
1. Free consent of parties
2. Competent to contract
3. For lawful consideration
4. With lawful object
5. Not expressly declared void
