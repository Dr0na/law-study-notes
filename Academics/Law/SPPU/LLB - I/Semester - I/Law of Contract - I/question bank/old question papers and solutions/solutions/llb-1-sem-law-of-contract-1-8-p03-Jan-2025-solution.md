### Q1) Define proposal. Explain the essential elements of a valid proposal.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of proposal under Section 2(a)
- Essential elements of valid proposal (8 elements)
- Communication requirements
- Distinction from invitation to treat
- Case laws illustrating principles
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
A proposal is defined under **Section 2(a)** of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 as "when one person signifies to another his willingness to do or to abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining the assent of that other to such act or abstinence."

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 2(a)**: Definition of proposal/offer
- **Section 3**: Communication, acceptance and revocation of proposals
- **Section 4**: Communication when complete
- **Section 5**: Revocation of proposals and acceptances
- **Section 6**: Revocation how made
- **Section 9**: Promises, express and implied

---

A proposal is the first step in the formation of a contract. It is an expression of willingness by one party (offeror) to another party (offeree) to do or abstain from doing something, with the intention of obtaining the other party's consent.

**Essential Elements of a Valid Proposal:**

**1. Expression of Willingness**
- The proposal must express willingness to do or abstain from doing something
- Mere statement of intention is not a proposal
- Must be definite and certain

**2. Made to Another Person**
- Proposal must be communicated to a specific person or persons
- Can be made to a specific person, group, or the world at large (as in Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.)

**3. With a View to Obtaining Assent**
- The offeror must intend to obtain the assent of the offeree
- Must show intention to create legal relations
- Social or domestic agreements may lack this intention

**4. Definite and Certain Terms**
- Terms must be clear, definite, and certain
- Vague or ambiguous proposals are invalid
- Must include all essential terms (price, quantity, time, etc.)

**5. Communication**
- Proposal must be communicated to the offeree (Section 3)
- Communication is complete when it comes to the knowledge of the person to whom it is made (Section 4)
- Uncommunicated proposal has no legal effect

**6. Legal Object**
- The act or abstinence proposed must be lawful
- Must not be forbidden by law, immoral, or against public policy

**7. Not a Mere Invitation to Treat**
- Must be distinguished from invitation to treat
- Display of goods, advertisements, tenders are usually invitations to treat
- Proposal creates legal obligation upon acceptance

**8. Must Not Be Revoked Before Acceptance**
- Proposal can be revoked before acceptance (Section 5)
- Once accepted, it becomes a promise and cannot be revoked

---

**Case Laws:**
- **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)**: Established that an offer can be made to the world at large. The company's advertisement was held to be a valid proposal, and acceptance by conduct (using the product) was sufficient.
- **Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt (1913)**: Established that a proposal must be communicated. The employee found the missing nephew without knowing about the reward offer, so there was no valid acceptance.
- **Harvey v. Facey (1893)**: Distinguished between offer and invitation to treat. A statement of price is not an offer but an invitation to treat.

---

**Conclusion:**
A valid proposal is fundamental to contract formation. The definition under Section 2(a) ensures that only serious, communicated intentions create legal obligations. The essential elements protect parties from vague or uncommunicated offers while facilitating commercial transactions. The distinction between offer and invitation to treat prevents unintended contractual obligations. The provision effectively balances the need for certainty with commercial flexibility.

### Q2) "All contracts are agreements but all agreements are not contract". Explain.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definitions of agreement and contract
- Why all contracts are agreements
- Why all agreements are not contracts
- Conditions for agreement to become contract (Section 10)
- Types of agreements that are not contracts
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
- **Agreement (Section 2(e))**: "Every promise and every set of promises, forming the consideration for each other, is an agreement."
- **Contract (Section 2(h))**: "An agreement enforceable by law is a contract."
- **Promise (Section 2(b))**: "When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent thereto, the proposal is said to be accepted. A proposal, when accepted, becomes a promise."

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 2(e)**: Definition of agreement
- **Section 2(h)**: Definition of contract
- **Section 2(b)**: Definition of promise
- **Section 10**: What agreements are contracts
- **Sections 11-30**: Conditions for valid contract and void agreements

---

**Relationship Between Agreement and Contract:**

The statement "All contracts are agreements but all agreements are not contracts" highlights the hierarchical relationship between these concepts.

**Why All Contracts are Agreements:**

1. **Contract = Agreement + Enforceability**
   - A contract is essentially an agreement that is enforceable by law
   - Every contract must first be an agreement
   - Agreement is the foundation of a contract

2. **Formation Process:**
   - Agreement is formed when: Proposal (Section 2(a)) + Acceptance (Section 2(b)) = Promise
   - Set of promises = Agreement (Section 2(e))
   - Agreement + Enforceability = Contract (Section 2(h))

3. **Legal Basis:**
   - Section 2(h) defines contract as "an agreement enforceable by law"
   - This clearly shows contract is a subset of agreement

**Why All Agreements are Not Contracts:**

An agreement becomes a contract only when it satisfies all conditions laid down in Section 10:

**1. Competent Parties (Section 11)**
- Parties must be of age of majority
- Of sound mind
- Not disqualified by law
- **Example**: Agreement with minor is void (not a contract)

**2. Free Consent (Section 14)**
- Consent must not be caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake
- **Example**: Agreement obtained by fraud is voidable (may not be a contract)

**3. Lawful Consideration (Section 2(d), 23)**
- Must have lawful consideration
- **Example**: Agreement without consideration is void (Section 25)

**4. Lawful Object (Section 23)**
- Object must not be forbidden by law, immoral, or against public policy
- **Example**: Agreement to commit crime is void

**5. Not Expressly Declared Void (Sections 24-30)**
- Must not fall under categories of void agreements
- **Example**: Agreement in restraint of marriage (Section 26) is void

**6. Certainty and Possibility (Section 29, 56)**
- Terms must be certain
- Performance must be possible
- **Example**: Agreement with uncertain terms is void

**Types of Agreements That Are Not Contracts:**

1. **Void Agreements (Section 2(g))**: Agreements not enforceable by law
   - Agreements with minors
   - Agreements without consideration (with exceptions)
   - Agreements with unlawful object
   - Agreements expressly declared void (Sections 24-30)

2. **Voidable Agreements (Section 2(i))**: Agreements enforceable at option of one party
   - Agreements without free consent
   - May or may not become contracts

3. **Unenforceable Agreements**: Valid but cannot be enforced due to technical defects
   - Agreements without proper stamping
   - Agreements barred by limitation

4. **Social/Domestic Agreements**: Lack intention to create legal relations
   - **Example**: Balfour v. Balfour - husband's promise to wife

---

**Case Laws:**
- **Balfour v. Balfour (1919)**: Established that domestic agreements between spouses lack intention to create legal relations, hence are agreements but not contracts.
- **Nash v. Inman (1908)**: Minor's agreement is void, hence not a contract.
- **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.**: Valid offer and acceptance created agreement, which became a contract when all conditions were satisfied.

**Conclusion:**
The statement correctly reflects the legal position. An agreement is a broader concept encompassing all promises with consideration, while a contract is a narrower concept requiring additional conditions. This distinction protects parties by ensuring only legally sound agreements become enforceable contracts. Section 10 provides a comprehensive framework to determine when an agreement becomes a contract. The provision effectively balances freedom of contract with legal safeguards, ensuring fairness and legality in contractual relationships.

### Q3) State the remedies available to the aggrieved person in case of breach of contract.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of breach of contract
- Remedy 1: Rescission of contract
- Remedy 2: Damages/Compensation (ordinary, special, liquidated)
- Remedy 3: Specific Performance
- Remedy 4: Injunction (temporary, perpetual)
- Remedy 5: Quantum Meruit
- Remedy 6: Suit for Account
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Breach of Contract**: When a party to a contract fails to perform their contractual obligations, either by non-performance, defective performance, or anticipatory breach, it constitutes a breach of contract.

**Remedies**: Legal means available to the aggrieved party to obtain relief for the loss suffered due to breach of contract.

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 73**: Compensation for loss or damage caused by breach of contract
- **Section 74**: Compensation for breach of contract where penalty is stipulated
- **Section 75**: Party rightfully rescinding contract entitled to compensation
- **Sections 51-67**: Performance of contracts
- **Specific Relief Act, 1963**: Sections 9-27 (Recovery of possession, specific performance, injunctions)

---

When a contract is breached, the aggrieved party has several remedies available under law. These remedies aim to place the aggrieved party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed.

**1. Rescission of Contract (Section 64, 75)**

**Meaning**: Cancellation of the contract, releasing both parties from their obligations.

**When Available**:
- When contract is voidable (Section 19)
- When contract is breached
- When contract becomes impossible to perform

**Effect**:
- Both parties restored to original position
- Aggrieved party entitled to compensation (Section 75)
- Must restore benefits received (Section 64)

**2. Damages/Compensation (Sections 73, 74)**

**Types of Damages:**

**a) Ordinary Damages (Section 73)**
- Compensation for loss naturally arising from breach
- Must be foreseeable (Hadley v. Baxendale rule)
- Includes:
  - Direct losses
  - Loss of profits
  - Expenses incurred

**b) Special Damages**
- Damages for special circumstances
- Must be communicated to defaulting party at time of contract

**c) Exemplary/Punitive Damages**
- Awarded to punish wrongdoer
- Rare in contract law

**d) Nominal Damages**
- Small amount when breach occurred but no actual loss

**e) Liquidated Damages (Section 74)**
- Pre-determined amount in contract
- Court can reduce if excessive
- Distinction from penalty

**3. Specific Performance (Specific Relief Act, 1963)**

**Meaning**: Court orders defaulting party to perform exactly what was promised.

**When Available**:
- When damages are inadequate remedy
- When subject matter is unique (land, rare goods)
- When monetary compensation insufficient

**When Not Available**:
- Personal service contracts
- Contracts requiring continuous supervision
- When damages are adequate

**4. Injunction (Specific Relief Act, 1963)**

**Meaning**: Court order restraining a party from doing a particular act.

**Types**:
- **Temporary/Interim Injunction**: During pendency of suit
- **Perpetual Injunction**: Final order after trial
- **Mandatory Injunction**: Order to do something
- **Prohibitory Injunction**: Order not to do something

**When Available**:
- To prevent breach of negative covenant
- To prevent irreparable harm
- When damages inadequate

**5. Quantum Meruit**

**Meaning**: "As much as he deserves" - reasonable compensation for work done.

**When Available**:
- When contract is discharged by breach
- When contract becomes void
- When work is done but contract incomplete
- When contract is divisible

**6. Suit for Account**

**When Available**:
- In contracts involving accounts
- When party has to render accounts
- Partnership and agency contracts

**Case Laws:**
- **Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)**: Established rule of remoteness of damages. Damages recoverable only if:
  - Arising naturally from breach, OR
  - Reasonably supposed to be in contemplation of parties
- **State of Kerala v. United Shippers and Dredgers Ltd.**: Explained distinction between liquidated damages and penalty under Section 74.
- **Chand Rani v. Kamal Rani**: Established principles of specific performance for sale of immovable property.

---

**Conclusion:**
The Indian Contract Act and Specific Relief Act provide comprehensive remedies for breach of contract. These remedies ensure that aggrieved parties are adequately compensated and wrongdoers are held accountable. The principle of placing the aggrieved party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed is effectively achieved through these remedies. The distinction between different types of damages and the availability of specific performance and injunctions provide flexibility to courts in awarding appropriate relief. The provisions effectively balance the interests of both parties while ensuring justice and fairness.

### Q4) Define 'Contingent Contract'. What are the rules as to the enforcement of Contingent contract provided under Indian Contract Act, 1872.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of contingent contract (Section 31)
- Rule 1: Contingent on event happening (Section 32)
- Rule 2: Contingent on event not happening (Section 33)
- Rule 3: When event deemed impossible (Section 34)
- Rule 4: Contingent on impossible event (Section 35)
- Rule 5: Agreement contingent on impossible event (Section 36)
- Distinction from wagering agreement
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
A **Contingent Contract** is defined under **Section 31** as "a contract to do or not to do something, if some event, collateral to such contract, does or does not happen."

**Key Elements:**
- Performance depends on future uncertain event
- Event is collateral (incidental) to contract
- Event may or may not happen

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 31**: Definition of contingent contract
- **Section 32**: Enforcement of contracts contingent on an event happening
- **Section 33**: Enforcement of contracts contingent on an event not happening
- **Section 34**: When event on which contract is contingent to be deemed impossible
- **Section 35**: Contracts contingent on impossible event
- **Section 36**: Agreement contingent on impossible event void

---

**Distinction from Wagering Agreement:**
- Contingent contract: Event is collateral, parties have interest in subject matter
- Wagering agreement: Event is sole determining factor, parties have no interest except winning/losing

**Rules for Enforcement of Contingent Contracts:**

**1. Rule 1: Contingent on Event Happening (Section 32)**

**Provision**: "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event happens, cannot be enforced by law unless and until that event has happened. If the event becomes impossible, such contract becomes void."

**Explanation**:
- Contract cannot be enforced until event happens
- If event becomes impossible, contract becomes void
- Performance is suspended until event occurs

**Example**: A promises to pay B ₹10,000 if B's ship returns safely. Contract enforceable only when ship returns. If ship sinks, contract becomes void.

**2. Rule 2: Contingent on Event Not Happening (Section 33)**

**Provision**: "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event does not happen, can be enforced when the happening of that event becomes impossible, and not before."

**Explanation**:
- Contract enforceable when event becomes impossible
- Cannot be enforced before event becomes impossible
- Once event becomes impossible, contract can be enforced immediately

**Example**: A promises to pay B ₹10,000 if a certain ship does not return. Contract enforceable when ship sinks (becomes impossible to return).

**3. Rule 3: When Event Deemed Impossible (Section 34)**

**Provision**: "If the future event on which a contract is contingent is the way in which a person will act at an unspecified time, the event shall be considered to become impossible when such person does anything which renders it impossible that he should so act within any definite time, or otherwise than under further contingencies."

**Explanation**:
- When event depends on future conduct of person
- Event becomes impossible when person acts in way making the event impossible
- Contract becomes void at that point

**Example**: A promises to pay B if B marries C. If C dies, event becomes impossible, contract becomes void.

**4. Rule 4: Contingent on Impossible Event (Section 35)**

**Provision**: "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an impossible event happens, are void, whether the impossibility of the event is known or not to the parties to the agreement at the time when it is made."

**Explanation**:
- Contracts contingent on impossible events are void ab initio
- Applies whether parties knew of impossibility or not
- No distinction between known and unknown impossibility

**Example**: A promises to pay B if two parallel lines meet. This is impossible, so contract is void.

**5. Rule 5: Agreement Contingent on Impossible Event (Section 36)**

**Provision**: "Contingent agreements to do or not to do anything, if an impossible event does not happen, are enforceable to the same extent as if such event had not been contemplated."

**Explanation**:
- If contract is contingent on impossible event NOT happening
- Contract is enforceable as if event was never contemplated
- Since impossible event cannot happen, contract can be enforced immediately

**Example**: A promises to pay B if the sun does not rise tomorrow. Since this is impossible, contract is enforceable immediately.

**Key Principles:**

1. **Suspension of Performance**: Performance suspended until contingency occurs or becomes impossible
2. **Void on Impossibility**: Contract becomes void if contingent event becomes impossible
3. **No Enforcement Before Contingency**: Cannot enforce before event happens (or becomes impossible)
4. **Immediate Enforcement**: When event becomes impossible, contract can be enforced (or becomes void)

**Case Laws:**
- **Frost v. Knight**: Established that in contingent contracts, performance is suspended until contingency occurs.
- **Taylor v. Caldwell**: Related to impossibility of performance, relevant for understanding when contingent events become impossible.

---

**Conclusion:**
Contingent contracts provide flexibility in commercial transactions where performance depends on uncertain future events. The rules under Sections 32-36 ensure certainty and fairness by clearly defining when such contracts can be enforced. The distinction between events happening and not happening, and the treatment of impossible events, provides comprehensive coverage. The provisions effectively balance the need for flexibility with legal certainty, ensuring parties understand their rights and obligations. The rules prevent enforcement of contracts based on impossible contingencies while allowing valid contingent contracts to be enforced at the appropriate time.

### Q5) "Minor's agreement is void" explain it with exceptions.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Legal position: Minor's agreement is void (Section 11)
- Characteristics of void agreement with minor
- Exception 1: Contracts for necessaries (Section 68)
- Exception 2: Beneficial contracts
- Exception 3: Contracts where minor is promisee
- Exception 4: Insurance contracts
- Exception 5: Negotiable instruments
- Exception 6: Partnership - admission to benefits
- Important principles (no estoppel, restitution, no ratification)
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Minor**: A person who has not attained the age of majority. Under Indian law, a person attains majority at 18 years (Indian Majority Act, 1875).

**Section 11** states: "Every person is competent to contract who is of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject, and who is of sound mind, and is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject."

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 11**: Who are competent to contract
- **Section 10**: Essential elements of valid contract (includes competent parties)
- **Section 68**: Necessaries supplied to person incapable of contracting
- **Indian Majority Act, 1875**: Defines age of majority

---

**Legal Position: "Minor's Agreement is Void"**

**Principle**: Under Section 11, a minor is not competent to contract. Therefore, any agreement entered into by a minor is void ab initio (void from the beginning).

**Characteristics of Void Agreement with Minor:**

1. **No Legal Effect**: Agreement has no legal effect
2. **Cannot be Enforced**: Cannot be enforced by either party
3. **No Estoppel**: Minor cannot be estopped from pleading minority
4. **Restitution**: Minor must return benefits if still in possession
5. **No Ratification**: Minor cannot ratify agreement after attaining majority

**Why Minor's Agreement is Void:**

1. **Lack of Capacity**: Minor lacks legal capacity to understand consequences
2. **Protection**: Law protects minors from exploitation
3. **Public Policy**: Prevents unfair advantage being taken of minors
4. **No Binding Obligation**: Minor cannot be bound by contractual obligations

**Exceptions to the Rule:**

**1. Contracts for Necessaries (Section 68)**

**Provision**: "If a person, incapable of entering into a contract, or any one whom he is legally bound to support, is supplied by another person with necessaries suited to his condition in life, the person who has furnished such supplies is entitled to be reimbursed from the property of such incapable person."

**Meaning**: 
- Minor can be supplied with necessaries
- Supplier can recover reasonable price from minor's property
- Not a contract, but quasi-contractual obligation

**What are Necessaries**:
- Food, clothing, shelter
- Education, medical care
- Articles reasonably necessary for minor's station in life
- Must be suited to minor's condition

**2. Beneficial Contracts**

**Principle**: Contracts that are beneficial to minor are valid.

**Examples**:
- **Contracts of Apprenticeship**: Valid if beneficial
- **Contracts of Service**: Valid if beneficial
- **Contracts for Education**: Valid
- **Partnership**: Minor can be admitted to benefits (cannot be partner)

**3. Contracts Where Minor is Promisor (No Liability) but Can be Promisee**

- Minor can receive benefits
- Minor cannot be compelled to perform
- Can enforce contract against other party

**4. Insurance Contracts**

- Minor can take life insurance policy
- Can be beneficiary
- Premiums paid by minor may be recoverable

**5. Negotiable Instruments**

- Minor can be payee or endorsee
- Cannot be liable as drawer, acceptor, or endorser
- Can receive payment

**6. Partnership - Admission to Benefits (Section 30, Indian Partnership Act)**

- Minor can be admitted to benefits of partnership
- Cannot be full partner
- Liability limited to share in partnership property
- Can opt out within reasonable time after attaining majority

**Important Principles:**

**1. No Estoppel Against Minor**
- Minor can always plead minority
- Even if minor misrepresented age
- Even if minor received benefits

**2. Restitution of Benefits**
- Minor must return property if still in possession
- Cannot be compelled to return if already consumed
- Principle: Restore status quo if possible

**3. No Ratification**
- Agreement cannot be ratified after majority
- Must enter into fresh contract
- Old agreement remains void

**4. Minor as Agent**
- Minor can act as agent
- Principal bound by minor's acts
- Minor not personally liable

**Case Laws:**
- **Nash v. Inman (1908)**: Established that minor's contract is void. Minor bought clothes, later refused to pay. Court held contract void, minor not liable.
- **Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)**: Minor mortgaged property, later sought to avoid contract. Court held contract void, minor not bound, but lender could recover money if minor still had it.
- **Leslie v. Sheill**: Established no estoppel against minor. Minor cannot be estopped from pleading minority even if misrepresented age.

---

**Conclusion:**
The rule that minor's agreement is void is fundamental to protecting minors from exploitation. While the general rule is strict, the exceptions ensure that minors can receive necessaries and benefits essential for their welfare. The distinction between void contracts and quasi-contractual obligations for necessaries provides necessary flexibility. The principle of no estoppel ensures consistent protection. However, the rule may sometimes work harshly against parties dealing with minors in good faith. The provisions effectively balance protection of minors with practical commercial needs, ensuring minors are not exploited while allowing necessary transactions for their benefit.

### Q6) Define consideration. Explain essentials of valid consideration.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of consideration (Section 2(d))
- Essential 1: Must move at desire of promisor
- Essential 2: May move from promisee or third party
- Essential 3: May be past, present, or future
- Essential 4: Need not be adequate
- Essential 5: Must be real, not illusory
- Essential 6: Must be lawful (Section 23)
- Essential 7: Must not be existing duty
- Essential 8: Must be certain
- Essential 9: Must be possible of performance
- Types of consideration
- Exceptions (Section 25)
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Consideration** is defined under **Section 2(d)** as "when, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing, something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise."

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 2(d)**: Definition of consideration
- **Section 23**: What considerations and objects are lawful
- **Section 25**: Agreement without consideration, void - exceptions
- **Section 2(f)**: Void agreement

---

Consideration is the price of a promise. It is something of value given in exchange for a promise, making the promise legally enforceable.

**Essentials of Valid Consideration:**

**1. Must Move at the Desire of the Promisor**

- Consideration must be given at the request/desire of promisor
- Voluntary acts without request are not consideration
- Must be requested by promisor

**Example**: A saves B's life. B later promises reward. A's act was not at B's desire, so no consideration. But if B had offered reward before, then A's act would be consideration.

**2. May Move from Promisee or Any Other Person**

- Consideration can be provided by promisee
- Can also be provided by third party (Section 2(d) allows this)
- Indian law differs from English law on this point

**Example**: A promises B to pay C ₹1000 if C does work for A. C's work is consideration, even though C is not promisee.

**3. May be Past, Present, or Future**

**a) Past Consideration:**
- Act already performed before promise
- Valid in India (unlike English law)
- Must be at desire of promisor

**Example**: A had previously done service for B. B later promises to pay. Past service is valid consideration.

**b) Present/Executed Consideration:**
- Act done simultaneously with promise
- Both happen at same time

**Example**: Cash sale - payment and delivery happen together.

**c) Future/Executory Consideration:**
- Promise to do something in future
- To be performed after promise

**Example**: A promises to deliver goods next month, B promises to pay on delivery.

**4. Need Not be Adequate**

- Law does not require consideration to be equal in value
- Parties are free to determine adequacy
- Court will not inquire into adequacy
- Must have some value, even if small

**Example**: A sells car worth ₹5 lakh for ₹1 lakh. Consideration is valid, though inadequate.

**5. Must be Real, Not Illusory**

- Must have some value
- Cannot be impossible or meaningless
- Must be capable of performance
- Cannot be something already done without request

**Example**: Promise to do impossible act is not valid consideration.

**6. Must be Lawful (Section 23)**

Consideration must not be:
- **Forbidden by law**: Acts prohibited by law
- **Immoral**: Against morality
- **Against public policy**: Harmful to public interest
- **Fraudulent**: Involving fraud
- **Injurious**: Causing injury to person or property

**Example**: Promise to pay for committing crime - consideration unlawful, contract void.

**7. Must Not be Something Which Promisor is Already Bound to Do**

- Performance of existing legal duty is not consideration
- Must be something more than existing obligation
- Performance of contractual duty to third party can be consideration

**Example**: Police officer cannot claim reward for doing duty. But doing more than duty can be consideration.

**8. Must be Certain**

- Terms must be clear and certain
- Vague or uncertain consideration invalid
- Must be capable of being ascertained

**Example**: "Reasonable payment" may be uncertain, but can be made certain by context.

**9. Must be Possible of Performance**

- Consideration must be capable of being performed
- Impossible acts cannot be consideration
- Must be physically and legally possible

**Example**: Promise to bring dead person to life - impossible, not valid consideration.

**Types of Consideration:**

1. **Executed**: Already performed
2. **Executory**: To be performed
3. **Past**: Performed before promise
4. **Present**: Performed simultaneously

**Exceptions to Consideration (Section 25):**

1. Natural love and affection (written and registered)
2. Compensation for past voluntary act
3. Promise to pay time-barred debt (written and signed)
4. Completed gift

**Case Laws:**
- **Kedarnath v. Gorie Mohammad**: Established that consideration need not be adequate. Court will not inquire into adequacy.
- **Chinnaya v. Ramayya**: Established that consideration can move from third party. A promised B to pay C if C transferred property to A. C's transfer was valid consideration.
- **Durga Prasad v. Baldeo**: Established that past consideration is valid in India if done at desire of promisor.

---

**Conclusion:**
Consideration is essential for contract validity, ensuring contracts are not gratuitous. The essentials ensure that consideration is real, lawful, and meaningful. The flexibility allowing past, present, and future consideration, and consideration from third parties, facilitates commercial transactions. The rule that adequacy is not required respects party autonomy while the requirement of lawfulness protects public interest. The provisions effectively balance freedom of contract with legal safeguards, ensuring only valid and enforceable promises create contractual obligations.

### Q7) Discuss in brief Agreements expressly declared to be void under Indian Contract Act, 1872.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of void agreement (Section 2(g))
- Agreement 1: By way of wager (Sections 24, 30)
- Agreement 2: Without consideration (Section 25)
- Agreement 3: In restraint of marriage (Section 26)
- Agreement 4: In restraint of trade (Section 27)
- Agreement 5: In restraint of legal proceedings (Section 28)
- Agreement 6: Void for uncertainty (Section 29)
- Agreement 7: Contingent on impossible event (Section 36)
- Agreement 8: With unlawful object (Section 23)
- Agreement 9: With minor (Section 11)
- Agreement 10: Without free consent (Sections 19, 19A)
- Legal consequences
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Void Agreement (Section 2(g))**: "An agreement not enforceable by law is said to be void."

**Expressly Declared Void**: Agreements specifically declared void by the Act under Sections 24-30, regardless of other factors.

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 2(g)**: Definition of void agreement
- **Section 24**: Agreements by way of wager, void

- **Section 26**: Agreement in restraint of marriage, void

- **Section 29**: Agreements void for uncertainty
- **Section 30**: Agreements by way of wager, void

---

The Indian Contract Act expressly declares certain types of agreements as void to protect public interest, morality, and individual freedom. These agreements are void ab initio (from the beginning) and cannot be enforced.

**1. Agreements by Way of Wager (Sections 24, 30)**

**Section 24**: "Every agreement by way of wager is void; and no suit shall be brought for recovering anything alleged to be won on any wager, or entrusted to any person to abide by the result of any game or other uncertain event on which any wager is made."

**Section 30**: Reiterates that agreements by way of wager are void.

**Characteristics**:
- Uncertain event determines win/loss
- Parties have no interest except winning/losing
- One gains, other loses based on event

**Example**: Betting on horse race, sports events (pure wagers)

**Exception**: Prize competitions, insurance contracts, stock exchange transactions

**2. Agreements Without Consideration (Section 25)**

**Provision**: "An agreement made without consideration is void, unless..."

**Exceptions** (when valid):
- Natural love and affection (written, registered)
- Compensation for past voluntary act
- Promise to pay time-barred debt (written, signed)
- Completed gift

**Example**: A promises B ₹10,000 without any consideration - void (unless exception applies)

**3. Agreements in Restraint of Marriage (Section 26)**

**Provision**: "Every agreement in restraint of the marriage of any person, other than a minor, is void."

**Rationale**: Marriage is fundamental right, cannot be restricted

**Example**: A promises B ₹50,000 if B does not marry - void

**Exception**: Restraint on minor's marriage may be valid in certain circumstances

**4. Agreements in Restraint of Trade (Section 27)**

**Provision**: "Every agreement by which any one is restrained from exercising a lawful profession, trade or business of any kind, is to that extent void."

**Rationale**: Freedom to carry on trade/business is fundamental

**Example**: A agrees not to practice law in Mumbai for 5 years - void

**Exceptions**:
- Sale of goodwill with reasonable restraint
- Partnership agreements with reasonable restrictions
- Service contracts with reasonable restrictions (after employment ends)

**5. Agreements in Restraint of Legal Proceedings (Section 28)**

**Provision**: "Every agreement, by which any party thereto is restricted absolutely from enforcing his rights under or in respect of any contract, by the usual legal proceedings in the ordinary tribunals, or which limits the time within which he may thus enforce his rights, is void to that extent."

**Rationale**: Right to access justice cannot be restricted

**Types Void**:
- Absolute restriction on legal proceedings
- Limiting time for enforcement (if less than limitation period)

**Example**: Agreement not to sue at all - void

**Exception**: Arbitration agreements are valid

**6. Agreements Void for Uncertainty (Section 29)**

**Provision**: "Agreements, the meaning of which is not certain, or capable of being made certain, are void."

**Requirements**:
- Terms must be clear and certain
- Must be capable of being ascertained
- Vague or ambiguous terms make agreement void

**Example**: A agrees to sell "some goods" at "reasonable price" - void for uncertainty

**Can be Made Certain**: If terms can be determined by reference to external standard, may be valid

**7. Agreements Contingent on Impossible Event (Section 36)**

**Provision**: "Contingent agreements to do or not to do anything, if an impossible event happens, are void, whether the impossibility of the event is known or not to the parties to the agreement at the time when it is made."

**Example**: Agreement contingent on impossible event - void

**8. Agreements with Unlawful Object (Section 23)**

**Provision**: "The consideration or object of an agreement is lawful, unless... it is forbidden by law; or is of such a nature that, if permitted, it would defeat the provisions of any law; or is fraudulent; or involves or implies injury to the person or property of another; or the Court regards it as immoral, or opposed to public policy."

**Example**: Agreement to commit crime, immoral acts - void

**9. Agreements with Minor (Section 11)**

Minor's agreement is void (discussed in detail in Q5)

**10. Agreements Without Free Consent (Sections 19, 19A)**

Agreements without free consent are voidable, but if consent is completely absent, may be void

**Legal Consequences of Void Agreements:**

1. **No Legal Effect**: Cannot be enforced
2. **No Obligations**: Parties not bound
3. **Collateral Transactions**: May affect related agreements
4. **Restitution**: Benefits received may need to be returned
5. **No Damages**: Cannot claim damages for breach

**Case Laws:**
- **Nash v. Inman**: Minor's agreement is void
- **Madhub Chunder v. Rajcoomar Doss**: Established principles of agreements in restraint of trade
- **Taylor v. Caldwell**: Related to impossibility making contract void

---

**Conclusion:**
The express declaration of certain agreements as void protects fundamental rights, public policy, and morality. These provisions ensure that contracts do not restrict individual freedoms, promote illegal activities, or create uncertainty. The exceptions provided (like reasonable restraints in trade, arbitration agreements) show the law's flexibility. The provisions effectively balance freedom of contract with necessary restrictions, ensuring contracts serve legitimate purposes while protecting public interest and individual rights. The comprehensive coverage of void agreements provides clarity and prevents exploitation.

### Q8) Define free consent. When the consent is said to be caused by fraud.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition of free consent (Section 14)
- Importance of free consent
- Definition of fraud (Section 17)
- Elements of fraud (5 types)
- Essential elements for fraud (intent, party/agent, inducement, damage)
- When mere silence is fraud (exceptions)
- Consequences of fraud (voidable, rescission, damages, affirmation)
- Distinction: Fraud vs Misrepresentation
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Free Consent (Section 14)**: "Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by:
(1) coercion, as defined in section 15, or
(2) undue influence, as defined in section 16, or
(3) fraud, as defined in section 17, or
(4) misrepresentation, as defined in section 18, or
(5) mistake, subject to the provisions of sections 20, 21 and 22."

**Fraud (Section 17)**: "Fraud means and includes any of the following acts committed by a party to a contract, or with his connivance, or by his agent, with intent to deceive another party thereto or his agent, or to induce him to enter into the contract:
(1) the suggestion, as a fact, of that which is not true, by one who does not believe it to be true;
(2) the active concealment of a fact by one having knowledge or belief of the fact;
(3) a promise made without any intention of performing it;
(4) any other act fitted to deceive;
(5) any such act or omission as the law specially declares to be fraudulent."

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 14**: Definition of free consent
- **Section 17**: Definition of fraud
- **Section 19**: Voidability of agreements without free consent
- **Section 19A**: Power to set aside contract induced by undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation
- **Section 10**: Free consent is essential element of valid contract

---

**Free Consent - Importance:**

Free consent is essential for a valid contract under Section 10. Without free consent, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the aggrieved party under Section 19.

**When Consent is Caused by Fraud:**

**Elements of Fraud (Section 17):**

**1. Suggestion of False Fact**

- Making false statement as fact
- Statement must be false
- Person making it must know it is false
- Must be made to deceive

**Example**: Seller says car has run only 10,000 km when it has run 50,000 km.

**2. Active Concealment of Fact**

- Intentionally hiding material fact
- Must have knowledge of fact
- Must be material to contract
- Mere silence is not fraud (with exceptions)

**Example**: Seller conceals that car was in accident and repaired.

**3. Promise Without Intention to Perform**

- Making promise with no intention to fulfill
- At time of making promise, no intention exists
- Must be proved by circumstances

**Example**: A promises to deliver goods knowing he cannot obtain them.

**4. Any Act Fitted to Deceive**

- Any act designed to deceive
- Broad category covering various deceptive practices
- Includes acts not covered by above categories

**Example**: Using fake documents, impersonation, etc.

**5. Act Declared Fraudulent by Law**

- Acts specifically declared fraudulent by law
- Includes acts under other statutes
- Comprehensive coverage

**Essential Elements for Fraud:**

**1. Intent to Deceive (Mens Rea)**
- Must have intention to deceive
- Key difference from misrepresentation
- Must be deliberate

**2. Must be by Party to Contract or Agent**
- Fraud by party to contract
- Or by agent with connivance
- Third party fraud may not affect contract

**3. Must Induce Other Party to Enter Contract**
- Fraud must be reason for entering contract
- Must be material
- If party would have contracted anyway, may not be fraud

**4. Must Cause Damage**
- Generally, fraud must cause damage
- But under Indian law, damage not always necessary
- Contract voidable even without actual damage

**When Mere Silence is Fraud:**

**Exceptions (when silence is fraud):**

1. **Duty to Speak**: When there is duty to disclose
   - Fiduciary relationships
   - Insurance contracts (utmost good faith)
   - Contracts uberrimae fidei

2. **Silence is Equivalent to Speech**: When silence itself is deceptive
   - Half-truths
   - Partial disclosure creating false impression

3. **Change of Circumstances**: When circumstances change after representation
   - Must disclose change
   - Failure to disclose is fraud

**Consequences of Fraud:**

**1. Contract is Voidable (Section 19)**
- Aggrieved party can avoid contract
- Can affirm contract if chooses
- Must be done within reasonable time

**2. Right to Rescind**
- Can cancel contract
- Must restore benefits received (Section 64)
- Entitled to compensation (Section 75)

**3. Right to Affirm**
- Can choose to keep contract
- Can claim damages
- Cannot both rescind and claim damages

**4. Damages**
- Can claim damages for loss
- Under Section 75
- Must prove actual loss

**5. Cannot Rescind if:**
- Cannot restore benefits
- Third party has acquired rights
- Affirmed contract with knowledge
- Lapse of reasonable time

**Distinction: Fraud vs Misrepresentation**

| Aspect | Fraud | Misrepresentation |
|--------|-------|-------------------|
| **Intent** | Intent to deceive | No intent to deceive |
| **Knowledge** | Knows statement is false | Believes statement is true |
| **Remedy** | Can rescind + damages | Can rescind OR damages |
| **Section** | Section 17 | Section 18 |

**Case Laws:**
- **Derry v. Peek (1889)**: Established that fraud requires actual intent to deceive. Mere carelessness is not fraud.
- **R. v. Kylsant**: Established that half-truths can constitute fraud. Partial disclosure creating false impression is fraud.
- **Babulal v. Rameshwar**: Established that active concealment of material fact is fraud.
- **Peek v. Gurney**: Established that fraud must induce contract. If party would have contracted anyway, may not be fraud.

---

**Conclusion:**
Free consent is fundamental to contract validity. Fraud vitiates consent by deceiving a party into entering a contract they would not have entered otherwise. The comprehensive definition under Section 17 covers various forms of deception, ensuring protection against fraudulent practices. The requirement of intent distinguishes fraud from innocent misrepresentation, providing appropriate remedies. The provisions effectively protect parties from deception while maintaining commercial certainty. The flexibility in remedies (rescission or damages) allows aggrieved parties to choose appropriate relief. The law strikes a balance between protecting victims of fraud and maintaining stability in commercial transactions.

### Q9) Write a detail note on Rescission of contract.

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Definition and nature of rescission
- When rescission is available (voidable contracts, breach, failure of consideration, mutual agreement)
- How rescission is effected (by notice, by suit, by conduct)
- Consequences (restoration of benefits, compensation, no further performance, restitution)
- Limitations (cannot restore, third party rights, affirmation, lapse of time, performance impossible, partial performance)
- Rescission vs other remedies
- Types of rescission (common law, equity, mutual agreement)
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**
**Rescission** means the cancellation or annulment of a contract, which releases both parties from their contractual obligations and restores them to their original position as if the contract had never been made.

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**
- **Section 19**: Voidability of agreements without free consent
- **Section 19A**: Power to set aside contract induced by undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation
- **Section 27**: Rescission in case of voidable contract
- **Section 28**: Rescission in case of agreement voidable on ground of failure of consideration
- **Section 35**: Rescission where contract becomes void
- **Section 64**: Effect of rescission of voidable contract
- **Section 75**: Compensation for rescission
- **Specific Relief Act, 1963**: Sections 27-30 (Rescission of contracts)

---

**Meaning and Nature:**

Rescission is a remedy available to an aggrieved party to cancel a contract and be restored to the position they were in before the contract was made. It is available for voidable contracts and certain other circumstances.

**When Rescission is Available:**

**1. Voidable Contracts (Section 19)**

Rescission available when contract is voidable due to:
- Lack of free consent (coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation)
- Mistake (in certain cases)
- Failure of consideration

**2. Breach of Contract**

- When one party commits fundamental breach
- When contract cannot be performed
- When performance becomes impossible

**3. Failure of Consideration (Section 28)**

- When consideration fails
- When party fails to perform their part
- When contract becomes impossible to perform

**4. Mutual Agreement**

- Parties can mutually agree to rescind
- Must be by fresh agreement
- Requires consideration (unless both parties are executors)

**How Rescission is Effected:**

**1. By Notice (Section 66)**

- Aggrieved party can rescind by giving notice
- Notice must be clear and unambiguous
- Communication must be complete

**2. By Suit (Specific Relief Act)**

- Can approach court for rescission
- Court can order rescission
- Court can impose conditions

**3. By Conduct**

- Can rescind by conduct showing intention
- Must be clear and unequivocal
- Must be communicated

**Consequences of Rescission:**

**1. Restoration of Benefits (Section 64)**

**Provision**: "When a person at whose option a contract is voidable rescinds it, the other party thereto need not perform any promise therein contained in which he is the promisor. The party rescinding a voidable contract shall, if he has received any benefit thereunder from another party to such contract, restore such benefit, so far as may be, to the person from whom it was received."

**Effect**:
- Both parties released from obligations
- Benefits received must be restored
- Must return property if still in possession
- Cannot restore if already consumed

**2. Compensation (Section 75)**

**Provision**: "A person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage which he has sustained through the non-fulfillment of the contract."

**Right**: Aggrieved party entitled to compensation for loss

**3. No Further Performance**

- Neither party bound to perform
- Contract is cancelled
- Future obligations extinguished

**4. Restitution in Integrum**

- Both parties restored to original position
- As far as possible
- Must restore what was received

**Limitations on Right to Rescind:**

**1. Cannot Restore Benefits**

- If cannot restore what was received
- If property consumed or destroyed
- Cannot rescind if restoration impossible

**2. Third Party Rights**

- If third party acquired rights in good faith
- For value and without notice
- Cannot rescind against third party

**3. Affirmation**

- If contract affirmed with knowledge
- After discovering grounds for rescission
- Cannot later rescind

**4. Lapse of Time**

- Must rescind within reasonable time
- Delay may bar rescission
- Must act promptly

**5. Performance Impossible to Restore**

- If performance cannot be undone
- If parties cannot be restored
- Rescission may not be possible

**6. Partial Performance**

- If contract partially performed
- May affect right to rescind
- Depends on circumstances

**Rescission vs Other Remedies:**

**1. Rescission vs Damages**
- Rescission: Cancels contract, restores parties
- Damages: Keeps contract, compensates loss
- Cannot have both (generally)

**2. Rescission vs Specific Performance**
- Rescission: Cancels contract
- Specific Performance: Enforces contract
- Opposite remedies

**3. Rescission vs Refusal to Perform**
- Rescission: Cancels contract
- Refusal: May be breach
- Different consequences

**Types of Rescission:**

**1. Rescission at Common Law**
- By notice or conduct
- Self-help remedy
- Immediate effect

**2. Rescission in Equity**
- By court order
- Court can impose conditions
- More flexible

**3. Rescission by Mutual Agreement**
- Both parties agree
- Fresh contract
- Requires consideration

**Case Laws:**
- **Abram Steamship Co. v. Westville Shipping Co.**: Established that rescission must be communicated clearly.
- **Car and Universal Finance Co. v. Caldwell**: Established that rescission can be by conduct, but must be clear.
- **Leaf v. International Galleries**: Established that delay in rescission may bar the right.

---

**Conclusion:**
Rescission is an important remedy allowing parties to escape from voidable or breached contracts. The requirement of restoration ensures fairness, preventing unjust enrichment. The limitations protect third parties and prevent abuse. The provisions effectively balance the right to rescind with practical considerations. Rescission provides a clean break, allowing parties to move on from problematic contracts. However, the limitations ensure that rescission is not used unfairly or to the detriment of innocent third parties. The remedy effectively serves its purpose of providing relief while maintaining commercial stability.

**Anticipatory Breach** occurs when a party to a contract, before the time for performance arrives, either expressly or by conduct, shows intention not to perform the contract.

---

**Meaning:**
Anticipatory breach happens when a party repudiates the contract before the time for performance. The breach is "anticipated" because it occurs before performance is due.

**1. Express Repudiation**
- Party clearly states they will not perform
- Written or oral communication
- Unconditional refusal

**2. Implied Repudiation (By Conduct)**
- Conduct showing intention not to perform
- Making performance impossible
- Acts inconsistent with contract

**Example**: A contracts to sell house to B. Before performance date, A sells to C.

**1. Treat Contract as Repudiated (Section 39)**

- Need not prove actual breach

- Can wait for performance date

- Alternative option

**4. Affirm Contract**
- Can ignore repudiation
- Can insist on performance
- Can wait for performance date

2. **Before Performance Due**: Must be before time for performance
3. **Unconditional**: Refusal must be unconditional
4. **Material**: Must relate to material part of contract

**Case Laws:**
- **Hochster v. De La Tour**: Established that anticipatory breach allows immediate action. Party need not wait for performance date.
- **Frost v. Knight**: Established that anticipatory breach gives immediate right to sue for damages.

---

**Conclusion:**
Anticipatory breach allows aggrieved party to take immediate action without waiting for performance date. This prevents further loss and provides prompt remedy. The provision effectively protects parties from repudiation while maintaining flexibility in remedies.
