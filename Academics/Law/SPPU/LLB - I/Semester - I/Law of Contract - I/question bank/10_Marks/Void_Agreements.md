# VOID AGREEMENTS – 10 MARKS QUESTIONS

## Question 1: Distinguish between void, voidable, and illegal agreements with examples.

### MODEL ANSWER:

#### Definitions

**Void Agreement (Section 2(g)):**
"An agreement not enforceable by law is said to be void."

**Voidable Contract (Section 2(i)):**
"An agreement which is enforceable by law at the option of one or more of the parties thereto, but not at the option of the other or others, is a voidable contract."

**Illegal Agreement:**
Agreement forbidden by law or contrary to public policy; void and tainted.

#### Detailed Distinctions

### 1. Enforceability

**Void:**
- Not enforceable by law
- Never enforceable
- No legal effect

**Voidable:**
- Enforceable until rescinded
- Valid until aggrieved party avoids it
- Has legal effect until rescission

**Illegal:**
- Not enforceable
- Prohibited by law
- Criminal or highly immoral

### 2. When Void/Voidable

**Void:**
- Void from beginning (ab initio)
- Never creates obligations

**Voidable:**
- Valid initially
- Becomes void only when rescinded
- Choice lies with aggrieved party

**Illegal:**
- Void from beginning
- Tainted from inception

### 3. Ratification

**Void:**
- Cannot be ratified
- Remains void forever

**Voidable:**
- Can be ratified by aggrieved party
- Affirmation makes it binding

**Illegal:**
- Cannot be ratified
- Perpetually unenforceable

### 4. Collateral Transactions

**Void:**
- Collateral agreements may be valid
- Not tainted

**Voidable:**
- Collateral agreements not affected
- Only main contract voidable

**Illegal:**
- Collateral agreements also void
- Illegality taints connected transactions

### 5. Restitution

**Void:**
- Generally possible under Section 65
- Restoration of benefits

**Voidable:**
- Possible under Section 64
- On rescission, restoration required

**Illegal:**
- Generally not possible
- Ex turpi causa (no action from wrong)

### 6. Examples

**Void:**
- Agreement with minor (Section 11)
- Agreement without consideration (Section 25)
- Agreement in restraint of marriage (Section 26)
- Wagering agreement (Section 30)

**Voidable:**
- Contract by coercion (Section 15)
- Contract by undue influence (Section 16)
- Contract by fraud (Section 17)
- Contract by misrepresentation (Section 18)

**Illegal:**
- Agreement to commit crime
- Agreement for immoral purpose
- Agreement to defraud third parties
- Trading with enemy during war

#### Comparison Table

| **Aspect** | **Void** | **Voidable** | **Illegal** |
|------------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Enforceability** | Never | Until rescinded | Never |
| **From when** | Ab initio | After rescission | Ab initio |
| **Ratification** | Not possible | Possible | Not possible |
| **Collateral** | May be valid | Valid | Also void |
| **Restitution** | Section 65 | Section 64 | Generally no |
| **Choice** | Neither party | Aggrieved party | Neither party |

#### Important Case Laws

**Void:**
**Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)**
Minor's agreement void ab initio. Cannot be enforced.

**Voidable:**
**Ranganayakamma v. Alwar Setti (1889)**
Contract by coercion - voidable at option of coerced party.

**Illegal:**
**Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas (1959)**
Contract forbidden by law - illegal and void.

#### Practical Implications

**Void Agreement:**
- No legal remedy
- Cannot sue for performance
- Section 65 may allow restoration

**Voidable Contract:**
- Aggrieved party can choose
- Can affirm or rescind
- Time limit applies
- Bars to rescission (Section 19A)

**Illegal Agreement:**
- No court assistance
- Connected transactions void
- Money paid not recoverable
- May involve criminal liability

#### Conclusion
Understanding differences between void, voidable, and illegal agreements is crucial for determining enforceability and remedies. Void agreements lack essential elements, voidable contracts have vitiated consent but offer choice to aggrieved party, and illegal agreements violate law or public policy and taint all connected transactions.

## Question 2: What are wagering agreements? Explain the essentials and legal position under Section 30.

### MODEL ANSWER:

#### Section 30 - Wagering Agreements

"Agreements by way of wager are void; and no suit shall be brought for recovering anything alleged to be won on any wager, or entrusted to any person to abide by the result of any game or other uncertain event on which any wager is made."

**Exception:** Horse racing bets for prize money of ₹500 or more exempted.

#### Definition of Wager

**Judicial Definition (Hawkins J. in Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball):**
A wagering contract is one by which two persons, with opposite views about uncertain event, mutually agree that dependent on event's determination, one shall win from other a sum of money or stake, neither having any other interest in contract except the stake.

**Simple Definition:**
Agreement where parties bet on uncertain event, one party's gain being exactly other's loss.

#### Essentials of Wagering Agreement

**1. Mutual Promises**
- Both parties must promise to pay
- Reciprocal obligations
- One's gain is other's loss

**2. Uncertain Future Event**
- Event outcome must be uncertain
- Neither party knows result beforehand
- Element of chance

**3. Opposite Views**
- Parties must have contrary views about event
- One says it will happen, other says it won't

**4. No Other Interest**
- **Most Important Element**
- Parties have no interest except winning stake
- No commercial, business, or personal interest in event

**5. No Control Over Event**
- Event must be independent of parties' will
- Neither party can influence outcome

**6. Money or Money's Worth**
- Stake must be valuable consideration
- Usually money

#### What is NOT a Wager

**1. Insurance Contracts**
- Insurable interest exists
- Risk management tool
- Valid contracts

**Case Law: Dayabhai Tribhobandas v. LIC (1971)**
Life insurance not wager - insurable interest distinguishes it.

**2. Games of Skill**
- Success depends on skill
- Not mere chance
- Valid competitions

**Case Law: State of Bombay v. RMD Chamarbaugwala (1957)**
Crossword competition based on skill - not wager.

**3. Speculative Transactions**
- Genuine business purpose
- Intention to perform
- Commercial interest beyond stake

**Case Law: Gherulal Parakh (1959)**
Forward contracts with intention to perform are valid speculation, not wager.

**4. Share Market Transactions**
- On recognized exchange
- Intention to perform or settle
- Regulated by SEBI

#### Effect of Wagering Agreement

**1. Void and Unenforceable**
No legal action to recover winnings.

**2. Collateral Transactions**
Generally not void unless participant in wager.

**3. Stakeholder**
Money with stakeholder cannot be recovered (Section 30 expressly prohibits).

**4. Voluntary Payment**
If paid voluntarily, generally cannot recover.

#### Horse Racing Exception

**Proviso to Section 30:** Bets on horse races for prizes of ₹500+ exempted.

**Rationale:** Horse racing considered game of skill.

**Case Law: Dr. KR Lakshmanan (1996)**
Horse racing is game of skill. Betting permissible under exception.

#### Wager vs. Insurance

| **Wager** | **Insurance** |
|-----------|---------------|
| No insurable interest | Insurable interest required |
| Pure speculation | Risk management |
| Zero-sum game | Indemnity for loss |
| Void | Valid |

#### Wager vs. Speculation

| **Wager** | **Speculation** |
|-----------|-----------------|
| No business interest | Genuine commercial purpose |
| No intention to perform | Intention to perform |
| Pure betting | Business transaction |
| Void | Valid |

#### Conclusion
Wagering agreements are void under Section 30 as matter of public policy. They are characterized by mutual promises based on uncertain events where parties have no interest beyond stake. Law distinguishes wagers from legitimate commercial activities like insurance and speculation, protecting valid business transactions while prohibiting gambling.
