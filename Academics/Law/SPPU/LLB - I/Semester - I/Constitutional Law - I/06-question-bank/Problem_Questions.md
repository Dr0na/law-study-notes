## ‚öñÔ∏è **ARTICLE 14 PROBLEMS**

### Problem 1: Classification Challenge

**Question:**

State of Gujarat passed a law:
1. Only persons above 30 years eligible for Civil Judge posts
2. 80% seats reserved for Gujarat domicile candidates
3. Women judges to retire at 55, men at 58
4. Direct recruits and promotees in same cadre, but promotees' seniority fixed below all direct recruits

**Advise on constitutional validity of each provision under Article 14.**

---

**Answer:**

**PROVISION 1: Age 30 for Civil Judge**

**ISSUE:** Whether age qualification violates Art. 14?

**RULE:**
- Art. 14 permits reasonable classification (I-R test)
- Qualifications can be prescribed (Shiv Ram Sharma)
- Must have rational nexus with post

**APPLICATION:**
- Civil Judge position requires maturity, judgment
- Age 30 has rational nexus with requirements
- Intelligible differentia exists (above 30 vs below 30)
- Not arbitrary

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely VALID**

---

**PROVISION 2: 80% domicile reservation**

**ISSUE:** Whether domicile-based reservation violates Arts. 14 and 16?

**RULE:**
- Art. 16(3): Parliament may prescribe residence requirement
- For **specified classes** of posts
- A.V.S. Narasimha Rao case
- Can be for whole State, not part of State

**APPLICATION:**
- 80% is excessive
- State law, not Parliamentary law (Art. 16(3) requires Parliament)
- Violates Art. 16(2) (residence as ground)
- No authority under Art. 16(3)

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - Violates Arts. 14 and 16

---

**PROVISION 3: Different retirement age**

**ISSUE:** Gender-based discrimination in retirement?

**RULE:**
- Art. 16(2) prohibits discrimination on ground of "sex"
- Must have rational nexus
- Air India v. Nergesh Meerza

**APPLICATION:**
- Differentiation solely on sex
- No rational nexus with efficiency
- Women equally capable after 55
- Arbitrary discrimination

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - Violates Art. 16(2)

---

**PROVISION 4: Seniority of promotees below direct recruits**

**ISSUE:** Whether fixed seniority violates Art. 14?

**RULE:**
- Seniority fixation must be objective and fair
- Not arbitrary (Paliwal case)
- If same cadre, unequal treatment needs justification

**APPLICATION:**
- Both in same cadre
- Promotees always below direct recruits
- Ignores performance, experience
- May be arbitrary

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely INVALID** if no rational basis shown

## üìú **ARTICLE 16(4) PROBLEMS**

### Problem 2: Reservation Validity

**Question:**

State of Karnataka issued order:
1. 60% posts in State services reserved (30% OBC, 22.5% SC/ST, 7.5% ST)
2. No creamy layer exclusion
3. Reservation in promotions up to highest posts
4. Carry forward for 5 years (can accumulate to 75%)

**Examine validity under Article 16(4) with case law.**

---

**Answer:**

**ISSUE 1: 60% Total Reservation**

**RULE:**
- Balaji v. Mysore: Reservation should be < 50%
- Indra Sawhney: 50% rule reaffirmed
- Extraordinary circumstances exception

**APPLICATION:**
- 60% exceeds 50% rule
- No extraordinary circumstances shown
- Violates Balaji and Indra Sawhney

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** (exceeds 50%)

---

**ISSUE 2: No Creamy Layer**

**RULE:**
- Indra Sawhney, 1993: Creamy layer must be excluded
- Applies to OBC only (not SC/ST per Mandal judgment)

**APPLICATION:**
- Order has 30% OBC
- No creamy layer exclusion mentioned
- Violates Indra Sawhney mandate

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** for OBC quota (must exclude creamy layer)

---

**ISSUE 3: Promotions to Highest Posts**

**RULE:**
- Rangachari (1962): Promotion reservations valid
- But Indra Sawhney: No reservation in promotions to higher posts
- Later amendments: Arts. 16(4A), (4B) added

**APPLICATION:**
- Promotions to highest posts questioned
- Constitutional amendments now allow
- But efficiency consideration required

**CONCLUSION:** May be challenged; depends on current constitutional position post-amendments

---

**ISSUE 4: Carry Forward (75%)**

**RULE:**
- Devadasan: > 50% in single year invalid
- A.B.S.K. Sangh: For SC/ST different approach (66% upheld)
- But general rule: 50% ceiling in any year

**APPLICATION:**
- 75% clearly exceeds 50%
- Even for SC/ST, questionable
- Violates Balaji principle

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** (exceeds 50% in single year)

## üó£Ô∏è **ARTICLE 19 PROBLEMS**

### Problem 3: Speech Restriction

**Question:**

Government passed law prohibiting:
1. Criticism of government schemes on social media
2. Publishing "fake news" (not defined)
3. Using "offensive language" online
4. Ban on strikes by government employees

**Examine validity under Article 19.**

---

**Answer:**

**PROVISION 1: Ban on criticism**

**ISSUE:** Violates Art. 19(1)(a)?

**RULE:**
- Art. 19(1)(a) guarantees freedom of speech
- Includes right to criticize government
- Restrictions under Art. 19(2) (8 grounds)
- Must be reasonable

**APPLICATION:**
- Complete ban on criticism arbitrary
- Not covered under Art. 19(2) grounds
- Undermines democracy
- Shreya Singhal principle applies

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - Violates Art. 19(1)(a)

---

**PROVISION 2: "Fake news" ban**

**ISSUE:** Vague terminology?

**RULE:**
- Restrictions must be clear and specific
- Shreya Singhal: Section 66A struck down for vagueness
- Terms like "annoying," "inconvenient" too vague

**APPLICATION:**
- "Fake news" not defined
- Susceptible to arbitrary interpretation
- Chilling effect on free speech
- Similar to Section 66A

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - Too vague (Shreya Singhal principle)

---

**PROVISION 3: "Offensive language"**

**ISSUE:** Over-broad restriction?

**RULE:**
- Restrictions must be narrowly tailored
- "Offensive" too subjective
- Proportionality required

**APPLICATION:**
- "Offensive" can mean anything
- No clear standards
- Over-broad
- Arbitrary enforcement possible

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - Over-broad and vague

---

**PROVISION 4: Ban on strikes**

**ISSUE:** Right to strike under Art. 19(1)(c)?

**RULE:**
- Right to form association protected
- Right to strike NOT fundamental right
- Strike is mode of action
- Can be regulated

**APPLICATION:**
- Complete ban may be excessive
- But regulation permissible
- Government employees' strike affects public service

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely VALID** if reasonable conditions (right to strike not FR)

## üîê **ARTICLE 21 PROBLEMS**

### Problem 4: Life and Liberty

**Question:**

Police arrested Ramesh without warrant at 8 PM. Took to station, interrogated whole night. Did not allow him to call anyone. Did not inform grounds. Next day at 10 PM (26 hours later) produced before magistrate who remanded to custody. In custody, beaten by police. After 10 days, released without charges.

**Advise Ramesh on:**
1. Violations of his rights
2. Remedies available

---

**Answer:**

**VIOLATIONS:**

**1. Article 22(1) - Information**
- Not informed of grounds
- Mandatory under Art. 22(1)
- Shobharam case

**2. Article 22(2) - Lawyer**
- Not allowed to call anyone/lawyer
- Right from day of arrest
- Joginder Kumar case

**3. Article 22(2) - Production**
- Produced after 26 hours
- Exceeds 24-hour limit
- Gunpati case

**4. Article 21 - Custodial Violence**
- Beaten in custody
- Violates right to life with dignity
- D.K. Basu case

**5. Article 21 - Illegal Detention**
- Detained 10 days
- Released without charges
- Detention illegal

---

**REMEDIES:**

**IMMEDIATE:**

1. **Habeas Corpus** (if still detained)
   - Under Art. 32 or 226
   - For immediate release

**SUBSEQUENT:**

2. **Compensation**
   - For illegal detention
   - Rudul Shah precedent (‚Çπ35,000 for 14 years)
   - For 10 days proportionate amount

3. **Compensation for Torture**
   - For custodial violence
   - Under Art. 21
   - D.K. Basu guidelines violated

4. **Criminal Prosecution**
   - Against police officers
   - For torture, illegal detention
   - Under IPC and other laws

**CONCLUSION:**
Multiple serious violations. Ramesh entitled to release (if detained), compensation, and prosecution of erring officers.

## üö´ **EXPLOITATION PROBLEMS**

### Problem 5: Forced Labour

**Question:**

Construction company employed workers on a site:
1. Paid ‚Çπ100 per day (minimum wage ‚Çπ178)
2. Children aged 10-12 working on site
3. Some workers brought from other state, kept in camps, not allowed to leave
4. No safety equipment provided

**Examine violations and remedies.**

---

**Answer:**

**VIOLATION 1: Below Minimum Wage**

**LAW:** Article 23 + Minimum Wages Act

**APPLICATION:**
- Wages below minimum = forced labour
- Sanjit Roy v. Rajasthan
- Clear violation

**CONCLUSION:** Violates Art. 23

---

**VIOLATION 2: Child Labour**

**LAW:** Article 24 + Child Labour Act

**APPLICATION:**
- Children 10-12 years (below 14)
- Construction = hazardous employment
- Prohibited under Art. 24
- M.C. Mehta principle (construction hazardous)
- Asiad case (enforceable against everyone)

**CONCLUSION:** Violates Art. 24

---

**VIOLATION 3: Bonded Labour**

**LAW:** Article 23 + Bonded Labour (Abolition) Act, 1976

**APPLICATION:**
- Workers not allowed to leave
- Kept in camps
- Elements of bondage
- Bandhua Mukti Morcha principle

**CONCLUSION:** Violates Art. 23 (bonded labour system)

---

**VIOLATION 4: Unsafe Conditions**

**LAW:** Article 21 (right to health and safety)

**APPLICATION:**
- No safety equipment
- Hazardous work
- Violates right to life with dignity

**CONCLUSION:** Violates Art. 21

---

**REMEDIES:**

**1. Public Interest Litigation**
- Any person can file
- Under Art. 32 or 226
- For enforcement of Arts. 21, 23, 24

**2. Writ of Mandamus**
- Direct authorities to:
  - Pay minimum wages
  - Remove children
  - Release bonded workers
  - Ensure safety

**3. Criminal Prosecution**
- Under Child Labour Act
- Under Bonded Labour Act
- Under Minimum Wages Act

**4. Compensation**
- To affected workers
- For violation of FRs

**5. Rehabilitation**
- For released bonded workers
- For children (education)
- State's duty

## üîÑ **PREVENTIVE DETENTION PROBLEMS**

### Problem 6: PD Validity

**Question:**

X detained under NSA on June 1. Grounds communicated on June 5. X submitted representation on June 10. Government considered representation on July 15 (45 days later). Sent to Advisory Board on July 20. Board constituted on Aug 15, submitted report on October 5.

**Examine validity at each stage.**

---

**Answer:**

**STAGE 1: Communication of Grounds (June 5)**

**LAW:** Art. 22(5) - "as soon as may be"

**APPLICATION:**
- 4-day delay
- May be reasonable depending on circumstances
- Generally valid

**CONCLUSION:** Likely valid

---

**STAGE 2: Representation (June 10)**

**LAW:** Art. 22(5) - Right to make representation

**APPLICATION:**
- X made representation
- Right satisfied

**CONCLUSION:** Complied

---

**STAGE 3: Government Consideration (July 15)**

**LAW:** 
- Government must consider representation before sending to Board
- Independent of Board reference

**APPLICATION:**
- Considered after 35 days
- Delay may affect effectiveness
- But considered before sending to Board (July 20)

**CONCLUSION:** Procedurally complied (but delay questionable)

---

**STAGE 4: Advisory Board (Aug 15 - Oct 5)**

**LAW:**
- Art. 22(4): Board must report within 3 months of detention
- 3 months from June 1 = September 1

**APPLICATION:**
- Board constituted Aug 15 (75 days)
- Report submitted Oct 5 (126 days)
- **Exceeds 3-month period**

**CONCLUSION:** **DETENTION ILLEGAL** after September 1

**Board report submitted after 3 months = Invalid**

---

**FINAL CONCLUSION:**

**Detention valid till August 31 (3 months)**

**After September 1: ILLEGAL**

**Remedy:** **Habeas Corpus** for immediate release + compensation

## üéì **RESERVATION PROBLEMS**

### Problem 7: Multiple Classifications

**Question:**

University admissions:
1. 15% reserved for SC
2. 7.5% for ST  
3. 27% for OBC (including affluent members)
4. 50% for women in all categories
5. 5% for children of faculty

**Total = 104.5%**

**Examine validity.**

---

**Answer:**

**ISSUE 1: Total Reservation (49.5% + 50% women)**

**RULE:**
- Balaji: Reservation < 50%
- Indra Sawhney: 50% rule
- Separate women's quota may be overlapping

**APPLICATION:**
- If women's quota separate: Total exceeds 100% (invalid)
- If women's quota within other categories: 49.5% (valid)
- Unclear from question

**CONCLUSION:** Need clarity on women's quota calculation

---

**ISSUE 2: OBC without Creamy Layer**

**RULE:**
- Indra Sawhney: Creamy layer must be excluded
- Mandatory for OBC

**APPLICATION:**
- Order includes affluent members
- No creamy layer exclusion
- Direct violation of Indra Sawhney

**CONCLUSION:** **OBC quota INVALID** (must exclude creamy layer)

---

**ISSUE 3: Faculty Children (5%)**

**RULE:**
- Reservation only for backward classes (Art. 16(4))
- Art. 15(4), (5) for education
- Faculty children not backward class

**APPLICATION:**
- Creates nepotism
- No constitutional authority
- Not backward class
- Arbitrary

**CONCLUSION:** **INVALID** - No constitutional authority

---

**OVERALL:**
- SC/ST quota: Valid (15% + 7.5% = 22.5%)
- OBC: Invalid (no creamy layer)
- Women: Depends on calculation
- Faculty children: Invalid

## üîê **ARTICLE 22 PROBLEMS**

### Problem 8: Arrest Procedure

**Question:**

Ram arrested at airport by CBI without warrant. Taken to CBI office. Not informed why arrested. After 20 hours, CBI officer realized wrong person arrested. Released without being produced before magistrate.

**What rights violated? Can he claim compensation?**

---

**Answer:**

**VIOLATIONS:**

**1. No Information (Art. 22(1))**
- Must be informed "as soon as may be"
- Not informed at all
- Clear violation

**2. No Production (Art. 22(2))**
- Must be produced within 24 hours
- Released after 20 hours without production
- Even if released, production mandatory

**3. Wrong Arrest (Art. 21)**
- Arrested wrong person
- Negligence/lack of diligence
- Violates right to liberty

---

**COMPENSATION:**

**YES, entitled to compensation**

**Rule:**
- Rudul Shah precedent
- Compensation for illegal detention
- Even short duration covered

**Application:**
- 20 hours illegal detention
- Wrongful arrest
- Mental trauma, reputation loss

**Amount:**
- Proportionate to duration and circumstances
- Lesser than Rudul Shah (‚Çπ35,000 for 14 years)
- But significant for wrongful arrest

**Remedy:**
- File writ petition under Art. 32 or 226
- Seek compensation
- CBI liable

**CONCLUSION:** Multiple violations; entitled to compensation

## ‚öñÔ∏è **RELIGION PROBLEMS**

### Problem 10: Religious Practice

**Question:**

Hindu temple management passed resolution:
1. Non-Hindus not allowed entry
2. Women of menstruating age (10-50) not allowed
3. Temple priests must be Brahmins only
4. State government wanted to take over temple management

**Examine under Articles 25, 26.**

---

**Answer:**

**ISSUE 1: Non-Hindus Entry**

**RULE:**
- Art. 25: Freedom of religion
- Art. 26: Manage affairs
- But subject to public order, morality, health

**APPLICATION:**
- Religious denomination can restrict
- If essential to that religion
- Hindu temples traditionally for Hindus
- Not against Arts. 25, 26

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely valid** if essential practice

---

**ISSUE 2: Women Entry**

**RULE:**
- Art. 15(1): No discrimination on sex
- Art. 25(2)(b): Social reform
- Art. 26: Religious affairs
- Art. 17: Dignity

**APPLICATION:**
- Restriction based on sex
- May not be essential practice
- Social reform possible
- Recent SC judgments favor entry
- Balances Art. 26 with Arts. 15, 17

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely INVALID** (post-Sabarimala jurisprudence)

---

**ISSUE 3: Brahmin Priests Only**

**RULE:**
- Art. 26(b): Manage affairs in matters of religion
- Art. 15(1): No discrimination on caste

**APPLICATION:**
- Appointment of priests = religious matter
- Denomination can decide
- If essential to that denomination
- Art. 26 protects

**CONCLUSION:** **Likely VALID** if essential to that temple tradition

---

**ISSUE 4: State Takeover**

**RULE:**
- Art. 26(d): Administer property per law
- Durgah Committee case
- Property administration = secular
- Religious practices protected

**APPLICATION:**
- State can regulate property administration
- But cannot interfere with religious practices
- Must leave religious autonomy

**CONCLUSION:** **State can regulate finances** but not religious practices
