#### **Q1. Article 21 - Evolution**

**Question:** Trace the evolution of the judicial interpretation of Article 21 from A.K. Gopalan to Maneka Gandhi. Discuss the expanded scope of Article 21 in the post-Maneka Gandhi era.

**Model Answer Structure:**

```
INTRODUCTION (2 marks)
- Text of Art. 21
- Most expansive FR
- "Heart of FRs" with Art. 14

PART I: A.K. GOPALAN ERA (3 marks)
1. A.K. Gopalan v. Madras, AIR 1950
   - Facts briefly
   - Narrow interpretation
   - Personal liberty = physical restraint only
   - Arts. 19, 21, 22 mutually exclusive
   - Any procedure sufficient (no due process)

2. Problems with Gopalan view
   - Too restrictive
   - Did not protect adequately
   - Formal approach

PART II: MANEKA GANDHI REVOLUTION (4 marks)
1. Maneka Gandhi v. UOI, AIR 1978
   - Facts: Passport impounded
   - Five key principles
     (a) Arts. 14,19,21 interconnected
     (b) Procedure must be reasonable, fair, just
     (c) Law must meet Art. 19 requirements
     (d) Procedure must answer Art. 14
     (e) Court may strike down law

2. Due Process introduced
   - Though word "due" absent
   - Substantive due process
   - Procedural due process

PART III: POST-MANEKA EXPANSION (5 marks)
1. Expanded Rights (LEPSH-VE)
   - Livelihood (Olga Tellis)
   - Education (Unnikrishnan)
   - Privacy
   - Speedy trial (Antulay)
   - Health
   - Sexual harassment (Vishaka)
   - Environment (M.C. Mehta)

2. Criminal Justice Improvements
   - Fair trial
   - Legal aid (Khatri v. Bihar)
   - No custodial violence (D.K. Basu)
   - Compensation (Rudul Shah)
   - No handcuffing (Citizens for Democracy)

3. Prison Reforms
   - Sunil Batra cases
   - Prisoners' rights
   - Charles Sobraj

CONCLUSION (1 mark)
- Art. 21 march continues
- Concept of dignified life inexhaustible
- Most significant FR in current times
```

**Must Cite Cases:**
- A.K. Gopalan (1950)
- Maneka Gandhi (1978)
- Olga Tellis

#### **Q2. Article 21 - Due Process**

**Question:** "Maneka Gandhi case introduced due process in India." Explain with reference to the relationship between Articles 14, 19 and 21.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Gopalan view (no due process)
- Word "due" absent from Art. 21
- Constituent Assembly deliberately omitted

MANEKA GANDHI FACTS
- Passport impounded
- No hearing given
- Reasons not disclosed

MANEKA GANDHI HOLDINGS

1. INTERCONNECTION
   - Arts. 14, 19, 21 NOT mutually exclusive
   - Must be read together
   - Nexus established

2. DUE PROCESS INTRODUCED
   a) Procedural Due Process
      - Procedure must be fair
      - Natural justice required
      - Hearing must be given

   b) Substantive Due Process
      - Law must be reasonable
      - Not arbitrary
      - Must meet Art. 14, 19 tests

3. EXPANDED JUDICIAL REVIEW
   - Court can examine law itself
   - Not just procedure
   - Reasonableness of both

IMPLICATIONS
1. Gopalan practically overruled
2. Greater protection to liberty
3. Judicial activism enabled
4. Expansion of Art. 21 scope

CONCLUSION
- Due process through backdoor
- Powerful tool for rights protection
```

#### **Q3. Article 22 - Arrest and Detention**

**Question:** Discuss the safeguards available to arrested persons under Article 22. Distinguish between arrest and preventive detention.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Importance of liberty protection
- Art. 22 provides minimum safeguards
- Art. 21 also applies

PART I: SAFEGUARDS FOR ARRESTED (4 marks)
Four safeguards under Art. 22(1) & (2):

1. Information about grounds
   - As soon as may be
   - Must be intelligible
   - Shobharam case

2. Right to consult lawyer
   - Legal practitioner of choice
   - Starts from day of arrest
   - Private consultation

3. Production before magistrate
   - Within 24 hours
   - Excluding journey time
   - Gunpati v. Nafisul Hasan

4. No detention beyond 24 hours
   - Without magistrate's authority

NOT APPLICABLE TO:
- Enemy aliens
- Preventive detention

PART II: PREVENTIVE DETENTION (5 marks)

1. Definition
   - Detention without trial
   - To prevent future act
   - Subjective satisfaction

2. Constitutional Provisions
   - Entry 9, List I (Parliament exclusive)
   - Entry 3, List III (Concurrent)

3. Rights of Detenu
   - Art. 22(5): Communication of grounds
   - Right to make representation
   - Art. 22(4): Advisory Board (3 months)

4. Laws: NSA, COFEPOSA, PBMSECA

PART III: DISTINCTION (3 marks)

Table format:
Conviction vs Preventive Detention
- Past act vs Future act
- Trial vs No trial
- Proof vs Subjective satisfaction
- Punishment vs Prevention

CONCLUSION
- Balance between liberty and security
- Safeguards essential
- Judicial review limited in PD
```

#### **SN2. Speedy Trial**

**Answer:**

**Constitutional Basis:** Article 21

**Abdul Rehman Antulay v. R.S. Naik:**

**Principles:**
1. Right to speedy trial is part of Art. 21
2. No time limit fixed (depends on case)
3. Obligation to proceed with reasonable promptitude
4. Covers all stages: investigation, trial, appeal, revision

**Implications:**
- Long pendency violates Art. 21
- Under-trials languishing in jail
- Unreasonable delay can lead to dismissal
- Release if detention exceeds maximum sentence

**Example:** Currency notes recovered 1981, charge-sheet 2007 - case quashed (18 years delay)

#### **SN3. Custodial Violence**

**Answer:**

**D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal:**

**Problem:**
- Police arrests without recording
- Torture to extract information
- Sometimes results in death

**SC Observations:**
> "Custodial violence strikes a blow at Rule of Law"

**Guidelines issued:**
1. Memorandum of arrest
2. Inform friend/relative
3. Medical examination
4. No denial of legal advice
5. Interrogation in presence of lawyer
6. Maintain arrest records

**Principle:** Art. 21 protects even in custody

**Other cases:**
- Sheela Barse (women in lock-up)
- Citizens for Democracy (no handcuffing)

#### **SN4. Preventive Detention**

**Answer:**

**Definition:** Detention without trial to prevent future act

**Different from Conviction:**
- No trial, no proof required
- Based on subjective satisfaction
- Purpose: Prevention, not punishment

**Constitutional Provisions:**
- Entry 9, List I (exclusive for defence, foreign affairs, security)
- Entry 3, List III (concurrent for state security, public order, supplies)

**Safeguards:**
- Art. 22(5): Communication of grounds, representation
- Art. 22(4): Advisory Board within 3 months

**Laws:** NSA 1980, COFEPOSA 1974, PBMSECA 1980

**Judicial Review:** Limited - mala fides, non-existent grounds, procedural violations

#### **Q1. Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India, AIR 1978**

**Comprehensive Analysis:**

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi, journalist
- Passport impounded under S. 10(3)(c) Passports Act
- Regional Passport Officer ordered impounding
- "In interests of general public"
- No reasons given
- No hearing provided

**Issues:**
1. Does Art. 21 apply to passport impounding?
2. What is "procedure established by law"?
3. Relationship between Arts. 14, 19, 21?
4. Is natural justice required?

**Arguments:**
- For petitioner: Art. 21 violated, no due process
- For respondent: S. 10(3)(c) valid procedure

**Supreme Court Held:**

**1. On Procedure (Most Important):**
> "Procedure must be reasonable, fair and just, not arbitrary or fanciful"

**2. On Interconnection:**
> Arts. 14, 19, 21 are NOT mutually exclusive
- Must be read together
- Procedure under Art. 21 must also satisfy Art. 19
- Must also satisfy Art. 14 (not arbitrary)

**3. On Natural Justice:**
> Principles of natural justice integral to Art. 14

**4. On Gopalan:**
> Practically overruled
- New approach adopted
- Expansive interpretation

**5. On Judicial Review:**
> Court can examine both procedure and substance of law

**Significance:**
1. Revolutionary case - changed constitutional law landscape
2. Due process introduced (though word "due" absent)
3. Greater protection to personal liberty
4. Enabled expansion of Art. 21
5. Most cited case in Indian constitutional law
6. Foundation for all subsequent Art. 21 expansion

**Aftermath:**
- Series of cases expanding Art. 21
- Multiple rights implied
- Judicial activism in personal liberty

#### **Q2. Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation**

**Facts:**
- Pavement dwellers in Bombay
- BMC wanted to evict them
- Claimed dwelling on pavements illegal
- Dwellers resisted - said right to life

**Issue:** Does right to life include right to livelihood?

**Arguments:**
- Petitioners: No livelihood without dwelling place
- BMC: No right to dwell on public property

**Held:**

**On Right to Livelihood:**
> Right to livelihood is born out of right to life

**Reasoning:**
- No person can live without means of living
- Means of living = Means of livelihood
- If deprived of livelihood, deprived of life
- Art. 21 includes livelihood

**On Eviction:**
- Eviction permissible BUT
- Must follow procedure
- Must give reasonable notice
- Must provide alternative

**Principle:** Right to life ‚â† mere animal existence

**Impact:**
- Landmark on socio-economic rights
- Expanded Art. 21 significantly
- Street vendors, hawkers protection

#### **Problem 1: Arrest Procedure**

**Question:**

Ram was arrested by police at 10 AM on Monday without warrant. Police took him to station and interrogated. He asked to call his lawyer but was refused. He was produced before magistrate at 2 PM on Tuesday (28 hours after arrest). Magistrate remanded him to judicial custody.

**Advise Ram on remedies available.**

**Answer:**

**ISSUES:**
1. Information about grounds?
2. Right to lawyer denied?
3. Production beyond 24 hours?

**LAW APPLICABLE:** Article 22(1) & (2)

**ANALYSIS:**

**Issue 1: Information**
- Art. 22(1) mandates information "as soon as may be"
- Must inform grounds of arrest
- If not informed - **violation**

**Issue 2: Lawyer**
- Art. 22(2) guarantees right to consult lawyer
- Right from day of arrest
- Private consultation
- Refusal is **violation**

**Issue 3: Production**
- Art. 22(2) mandates production within 24 hours
- Excluding journey time
- Here: 28 hours (assuming minimal journey time)
- **Violation** of Art. 22(2)

**CASES:**
- Shobharam (information mandatory)
- Joginder Kumar (arrest must be justified)
- Gunpati v. Nafisul Hasan (24 hours mandatory)

**REMEDIES:**

1. **Habeas Corpus** (immediate)
   - Under Art. 32 (SC) or Art. 226 (HC)
   - For release from illegal detention

2. **Compensation** (later)
   - For violation of Art. 22
   - Rudul Shah precedent

3. **Prosecution**
   - Police officers for violation
   - Under relevant provisions

**CONCLUSION:**
Multiple violations of Art. 22. Ram entitled to immediate release and compensation.

#### **Problem 2: Preventive Detention**

**Question:**

Shyam detained under NSA on grounds of "acting prejudicial to public order." Grounds communicated in English which Shyam doesn't understand. He requested Hindi translation but not provided. Advisory Board constituted 100 days after detention.

**Advise on validity of detention.**

**Answer:**

**ISSUES:**
1. Language of grounds?
2. Advisory Board timing?

**LAW:** Articles 22(4) and 22(5)

**ISSUE 1: Language of Grounds**

**Law:**
- Art. 22(5) requires communication of grounds
- Must be in language detenu understands
- Purpose: Enable effective representation

**Application:**
- Grounds in English
- Shyam doesn't understand
- Requested Hindi - denied
- **Cannot make effective representation**

**Case:** Similar case - SC quashed detention when grounds not in language known to detenu

**Conclusion:** **Detention invalid** due to this violation alone

**ISSUE 2: Advisory Board**

**Law:**
- Art. 22(4) requires Advisory Board within 3 months
- If not, detention illegal

**Application:**
- 100 days = Over 3 months
- Clear violation

**Conclusion:** **Detention illegal** after 90 days

**FINAL ADVICE:**
Detention **doubly invalid**:
1. Grounds not in understood language
2. Advisory Board delayed beyond 3 months

**Remedy:** Habeas Corpus for immediate release

#### **Q1. Article 23 - Evolution**

**Question:** Discuss the constitutional provisions relating to prohibition of forced labour. Examine the judicial interpretation of Article 23 before and after 1982.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION (1 mark)
- Text of Art. 23
- Three prohibitions: TBF
- Horizontal application

CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISION (2 marks)
1. Article 23(1)
   - Traffic in human beings
   - Begar
   - Other forms of forced labour
   - Contravention is offence

2. Article 23(2)
   - Exception: Compulsory service for public purpose
   - No discrimination

PHASE I: DORMANT PERIOD (1950-1982) (2 marks)
- Rarely invoked
- Narrow interpretation
- Limited impact
- Few cases

PHASE II: POST-1982 EXPANSION (6 marks)

1. Asiad Case, 1982 (Watershed)
   - Construction workers for Asian Games
   - Below minimum wages
   - SC held: Every form of forced labour prohibited

2. New Principle
   - Wages below minimum = Forced labour
   - Makes no difference if remunerated or not

3. Key Cases
   a) Sanjit Roy v. Rajasthan
      - Famine relief work
      - Wages below minimum
      - Invalid under Art. 23

   b) Salal Hydro-Project
      - Labour laws not implemented
      - Violations of minimum wages
      - Inter-State Migrant Workers Act

4. Bonded Labour
   - Bandhua Mukti Morcha (PIL)
   - Link with Art. 21
   - Life with dignity
   - Release + Rehabilitation

   - Neeraja Choudhary
   - Emphasis on rehabilitation

5. Trafficking
   - Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956
   - Vishal Jeet (child prostitution)
   - Gaurav Jain (rehabilitation)

LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK (2 marks)
- Bonded Labour (Abolition) Act, 1976
- Minimum Wages Act
- Equal Remuneration Act, 1976
- Inter-State Migrant Workers Act, 1979

CONCLUSION (2 marks)
- Dramatic change post-1982
- From dormant to potent
- PIL instrumental
- Implementation challenges remain
```
