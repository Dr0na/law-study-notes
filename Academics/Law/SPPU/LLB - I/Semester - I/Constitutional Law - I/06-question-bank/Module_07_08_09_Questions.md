#### **Q1. Freedom of Religion**

**Question:** Discuss the constitutional provisions relating to freedom of religion in India. Explain the concept of "essential religious practices" with the help of case law.

**Model Answer Structure:**

```
INTRODUCTION (2 marks)
- India is secular state
- Arts. 25-28 guarantee religious freedom
- All persons (not just citizens)

PART I: ARTICLE 25 - INDIVIDUAL FREEDOM (4 marks)
1. Text of Art. 25(1)
2. Three Ps: Profess, Practice, Propagate
   - Profess: Declare belief
   - Practice: Perform rituals
   - Propagate: Spread (not forced conversion)
3. Subject to: Public order, morality, health
4. Art. 25(2): Social reform

PART II: ESSENTIAL RELIGIOUS PRACTICES (5 marks)
1. Definition
   - Commissioner HRE v. Lakshmindra, AIR 1954
   - Religion consists of essential and non-essential practices
   - Only essential practices protected

2. Test
   - Is practice essential and integral to religion?
   - Can religion exist without it?
   - If NO to second question = Not essential

3. Case Law
   a) Ratilal Panachand (1954)
      - Polygamy not essential to Hindu religion
      - Can be prohibited

   b) M. Ismail Faruqui (1994)
      - Mosque not essential for namaz
      - Muslims can pray anywhere
      - State acquisition of disputed land valid

   c) Sardar Syedna (1962)
      - Excommunication essential to Bohra community
      - Protected under Art. 26

PART III: ARTICLE 26 - DENOMINATIONAL RIGHTS (3 marks)
1. Text
2. Four rights (EMOA)
3. Durgah Committee case
   - Property administration secular
   - Can be regulated

CONCLUSION (1 mark)
- Balance between religious freedom and reform
- State can regulate secular aspects
- Essential practices protected

CASES TO CITE:
- Commissioner HRE v. Lakshmindra (doctrine)
- Ratilal Panachand (polygamy)
- M. Ismail Faruqui (mosque)
- Sardar Syedna (excommunication)
```

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES**

#### **SN1. Article 26**

**Answer:**

**Text:** Religious denominations' rights (subject to public order, morality, health)

**Four Rights - EMOA:**
1. **Establish** institutions for religious/charitable purposes
2. **Manage** own affairs in matters of religion
3. **Own** and acquire property
4. **Administer** property in accordance with law

**Religious Denomination:**
- Collection of individuals
- Common beliefs/doctrines
- Common organization
- Distinctive name

**Key Case: Durgah Committee, Ajmer (1961)**
- Management of property is secular activity
- State can regulate administration
- But not religious practices

**Shirur Mutt case:** Balance between regulation and freedom

#### **SN2. Essential Religious Practices Doctrine**

**Answer:**

**Origin:** Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Lakshmindra, AIR 1954

**Principle:** 
- Religion has essential and non-essential practices
- Only **essential** practices protected under Art. 25
- Non-essential can be regulated/prohibited

**Test:**
- Is practice essential and integral to religion?
- Can religion exist without it?

**Applications:**
- ‚ùå Polygamy not essential (Ratilal Panachand)
- ‚ùå Mosque not essential (Ismail Faruqui)
- ‚úÖ Excommunication essential for Bohras (Sardar Syedna)
- ‚ùå Various social practices not essential (can be reformed)

**Significance:** Enables social reform while protecting religion

#### **Q1. Article 32**

**Question:** "Article 32 is the soul of the Constitution." Explain the significance of Article 32 and discuss the powers of the Supreme Court under it.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Dr. Ambedkar's quote
- Art. 32 is itself a FR
- Right to have rights enforced

NATURE AND SIGNIFICANCE
1. Fundamental Right itself
2. Guaranteed right to approach SC
3. Basic feature (Minerva Mills 1980)
4. Only for FRs
5. Cannot be taken away (even by amendment)

POWERS UNDER ARTICLE 32(2)
1. Issue directions
2. Issue orders
3. Issue writs (five types)

THE FIVE WRITS (CHAMP)

1. Certiorari
   - Quash order of lower court/tribunal
   - After decision
   - Grounds: Excess/lack of jurisdiction, natural justice

2. Habeas Corpus
   - Produce the body
   - Illegal detention
   - Most important for personal liberty
   - Rudul Shah: Compensation awarded

3. Quo Warranto
   - Question authority to hold public office
   - Public offices only
   - University of Mysore case

4. Mandamus
   - Command to perform public duty
   - Against public authorities
   - Duty must be clear and enforceable

5. Prohibition
   - Prevent lower court from acting beyond jurisdiction
   - Before decision
   - Preventive in nature

BASIC FEATURE
- Minerva Mills v. UOI, 1980
- Art. 32 cannot be taken away
- Judicial review essential
- Basic structure of Constitution

DURING EMERGENCY
- Can be suspended under Art. 359
- But Arts. 20 and 21 cannot be suspended (44th Amendment)
- A.D.M. Jabalpur case (1976) - infamous

CONCLUSION
- Most important provision for rights protection
- Without remedy, rights meaningless
- Truly soul of Constitution
```

#### **Q2. Public Interest Litigation**

**Question:** Discuss the concept and development of Public Interest Litigation in India. Examine its role in enforcement of Fundamental Rights.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Revolutionary concept
- Democratization of justice
- Access to justice for all

TRADITIONAL RULE
- Locus standi required
- Only aggrieved person could file
- Personal injury necessary

DEVELOPMENT OF PIL

1. S.P. Gupta v. Union of India, 1982
   - Judges Transfer Case
   - Justice Bhagwati liberalized locus standi
   - Any public-spirited person can file
   - If public injury or public duty involved

2. Rationale
   - Poor and disadvantaged cannot approach court
   - Social action groups can file
   - Even letter to SC sufficient
   - Democratize judicial process

WHO CAN FILE
- Any citizen
- Social action group
- NGO
- Advocate
- Even anonymously (if genuine)

WHEN PIL CAN BE FILED
1. FR violation of poor
2. Public duty not performed
3. Public injury
4. Victims unable to approach

LANDMARK PIL CASES

1. Bandhua Mukti Morcha
   - Bonded labour
   - Release and rehabilitation

2. M.C. Mehta (Multiple)
   - Oleum gas leak
   - Ganga pollution
   - Vehicular pollution
   - Taj Mahal protection
   - Sivakasi child labour

3. Sheela Barse
   - Women in lock-ups
   - Children in jails

4. Vishaka v. Rajasthan
   - Sexual harassment
   - Guidelines issued
   - Judicial legislation

5. Olga Tellis
   - Pavement dwellers
   - Right to livelihood

LIMITATIONS
- Not for private interest
- Not for publicity
- Not for political vendetta
- Court can dismiss frivolous PIL
- Costs can be imposed (Ashok Kumar Pandey)

CONCLUSION
- Significant contribution to justice
- Made FRs effective for poor
- Continuing importance
- Need to prevent misuse
```

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES**

#### **SN1. Five Writs (CHAMP)**

**Answer:**

**Mnemonic: CHAMP**

**1. Certiorari (C):**
- Quash order of lower court/tribunal
- After decision (curative)
- Grounds: Jurisdiction, natural justice

**2. Habeas Corpus (H):**
- Produce the body
- Release from illegal detention
- Most important for liberty
- Against public and private persons

**3. (Quo) Warranto (A):**
- By what authority
- Question authority to hold public office
- Only for public offices
- University of Mysore case

**4. Mandamus (M):**
- We command
- Command to perform public duty
- Against public authorities
- Duty must be clear

**5. Prohibition (P):**
- Forbid
- Prevent lower court from excess
- Before decision (preventive)
- Not against private persons

**Article 32:** SC can issue (only for FRs)
**Article 226:** HC can issue (FRs + other rights)

---

#### **SN2. Article 32 vs Article 226**

**Answer:**

**Table format:**

| Aspect | Art. 32 (SC) | Art. 226 (HC) |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Nature | FR itself | Power of HC |
| Scope | Only FRs | FRs + other rights |
| Territorial | All India | HC jurisdiction |
| Suspend | Yes (Emergency) | No |
| Refuse | Cannot (if FR violated) | Can (discretionary) |
| Alternative remedy | No bar | May refuse |

**Choice of Forum:**
- Petitioner can choose
- Cannot approach both simultaneously
- Romesh Thappar case

**When to approach SC:**
- National importance
- FR violation
- Quick remedy

**When to approach HC:**
- Convenience
- Local matter
- Fact investigation

#### **Q1. Directive Principles**

**Question:** Discuss the nature and importance of Directive Principles of State Policy. Examine the relationship between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION (1 mark)
- Part IV (Arts. 36-51)
- Borrowed from Ireland
- Unique feature

NATURE (3 marks)
1. Article 37: Non-justiciable
2. Fundamental in governance
3. State's duty to apply
4. Guidelines for policy
5. Socio-economic rights

Why non-justiciable?
- Resource constraints
- Policy matters
- Progressive implementation
- Flexibility needed

CLASSIFICATION (3 marks)
Three categories (SGL):

1. Socialistic
   - Arts. 38, 39, 41, 42, 43, 47
   - Social and economic justice

2. Gandhian
   - Arts. 40, 43, 46, 47, 48
   - Village economy, cattle protection

3. Liberal-Intellectual
   - Arts. 44, 45, 49, 50, 51
   - Uniform Civil Code, separation of judiciary

IMPORTANT DPs (2 marks)
- Art. 39: Most important (six clauses)
- Art. 39A: Free legal aid
- Art. 40: Panchayats
- Art. 44: Uniform Civil Code (not implemented)
- Art. 45: Early childhood care
- Art. 46: SC/ST welfare
- Art. 48A: Environment

RELATIONSHIP WITH FRs (5 marks)

1. Early View: Champakam Dorairajan (1951)
   - FRs prevail over DPs
   - In conflict, FRs win

2. Harmonious Construction Need
   - Both important
   - Should be read together

3. Minerva Mills v. UOI (1980)
   - **Most important case**
   - FRs and DPs COMPLEMENTARY
   - Not contradictory
   - Balance between them is **basic structure**
   - Constitution founded on bedrock of balance
   - Neither can claim supremacy

4. Article 31C
   - Laws for Arts. 39(b), (c)
   - Cannot be challenged under Arts. 14, 19
   - Gives limited primacy to DPs

5. Unnikrishnan Case (1993)
   - Read FRs with DPs
   - Together provide comprehensive social order
   - DPs can enrich FRs

IMPLEMENTATION (1 mark)
- Legislative implementation
- Many Acts enacted
- Judicial interpretation
- Progressive realization

CONCLUSION (1 mark)
- DPs essential for welfare state
- Complementary to FRs
- Together create balance
- Ongoing implementation
```

#### **SN1. Uniform Civil Code (Article 44)**

**Answer:**

**Text:** "State to secure for citizens a uniform civil code throughout territory of India"

**Current Position:**
- Different personal laws for different religions
- Hindu law, Muslim law, Christian law, etc.
- Art. 44 NOT implemented

**Arguments For:**
- Gender justice (especially for women)
- National integration
- Equality before law
- Secularism

**Arguments Against:**
- Religious freedom
- Cultural diversity
- Minority apprehensions
- Political sensitivity

**Goa Exception:** Goa has uniform civil code (retained from Portuguese law)

**Way Forward:**
- Gradual approach
- Voluntary common civil code
- Reform personal laws first
- Build consensus

#### **SN2. Article 39 - Certain Principles**

**Answer:**

**Most important Directive Principle**

**Six Clauses (LWWCH):**

(a) **Livelihood:** Adequate means for men and women

(b) **Wealth:** Material resources subserve common good

(c) **Concentration:** No concentration of wealth

(d) **Women:** Equal pay for equal work

(e) **Children:** Health and strength not abused

(f) **Healthy:** Children opportunities in freedom and dignity

**Implementation:**
- Equal Remuneration Act, 1976 (for clause d)
- Child Labour Act, 1986 (for clauses e, f)

**Article 31C:** Laws for clauses (b) and (c) protected from challenge under Arts. 14, 19

**Significance:** Socio-economic justice foundation
