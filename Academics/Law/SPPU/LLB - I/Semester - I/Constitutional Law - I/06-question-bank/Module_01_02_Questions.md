#### **Q1. Making of Indian Constitution**

**Question:** Discuss the making of the Indian Constitution with reference to the role of Constituent Assembly.

**Model Answer Structure:**

```
INTRODUCTION (1 mark)
- India's Constitution adopted Nov 26, 1949
- Came into force Jan 26, 1950
- Product of Constituent Assembly

FORMATION OF CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY (3 marks)
1. Cabinet Mission Plan (1946)
   - Proposed Constituent Assembly
   - Indirect election by Provincial Legislatures
   
2. Composition
   - Total: 389 members (later 299 after partition)
   - British India: 296, Princely States: 93
   
3. Key Personalities
   - President: Dr. Rajendra Prasad
   - Chairman, Drafting Committee: Dr. B.R. Ambedkar
   - Constitutional Advisor: B.N. Rau
   - Drafting Committee: 7 members

4. First Meeting: December 9, 1946

PROCESS OF FRAMING (4 marks)
1. Objectives Resolution (Nehru, Dec 13, 1946)
2. Drafting Committee formed (Aug 29, 1947)
3. B.N. Rau prepared initial draft
4. First reading: Nov 4, 1948
5. Second reading: Nov 15, 1948 - Oct 17, 1949
   - Clause by clause discussion
   - 7,600 amendments considered
   - ~2,000 incorporated
6. Third reading: Nov 14-26, 1949
7. Adopted: November 26, 1949
8. Signed by 284 members

STATISTICS (2 marks)
- Time: 2 years, 11 months, 18 days
- Sittings: 165 days
- Cost: ‚Çπ64 lakh
- Articles: Originally 395
- Schedules: Originally 8

ISSUES BEFORE ASSEMBLY (3 marks)
1. Federal or Unitary
2. Rights to be included
3. Minorities protection
4. Language policy
5. Center-State relations
6. Result: Quasi-federal structure

CONCLUSION (2 marks)
- Democratic process
- Comprehensive discussions
- Representatives of all sections
- Vision for new India
```

#### **Q2. Salient Features**

**Question:** Explain the salient features of the Indian Constitution.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Lengthiest written Constitution
- Unique features
- Federal structure with unitary spirit

FEATURES (Explain each - 2 marks each)

1. Lengthiest Written Constitution
   - 470+ Articles, 12 Schedules
   - Originally 395 Articles, 8 Schedules
   - Detailed provisions
   - Comparison with USA (7 Articles)

2. Parliamentary System
   - Borrowed from Britain
   - PM and Council responsible to Lok Sabha
   - Nominal head (President)
   - Real head (Prime Minister)

3. Quasi-Federal Structure
   - Federal features (5 points)
   - Unitary features (5 points)
   - "Federation with strong unitary bias"

4. Fundamental Rights (Part III)
   - Articles 12-35
   - Justiciable
   - Borrowed from USA
   - Seven categories

5. Directive Principles (Part IV)
   - Articles 36-51
   - Non-justiciable
   - Borrowed from Ireland
   - Guidelines for State

6. Secular State
   - Added 1976 to Preamble
   - No state religion
   - Equal respect to all

7. Independent Judiciary
   - Single integrated system
   - Judicial review
   - Fixed tenure
   - Difficult removal

8. Single Citizenship
   - Unlike USA (dual)
   - Only Indian citizenship
   - Promotes unity

9. Universal Adult Franchise
   - All citizens 18+ can vote
   - Originally 21 (changed 1989)
   - One person one vote

10. Emergency Provisions
    - Three types (352, 356, 360)
    - Centralize power temporarily

CONCLUSION
- Unique blend
- Borrowed from many sources
- Suited to Indian conditions
```

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES (5-7 Marks)**

#### **SN1. Dr. Ambedkar's Warnings**

**Answer:**

**Context:** Final speech in Constituent Assembly (Nov 25, 1949)

**Three Warnings:**

**1. Social Democracy Needed:**
> "Political democracy cannot last unless there lies at base of it social democracy."

**Meaning:** Formal equality not enough; social and economic equality essential.

**2. Life of Contradictions:**
> "We are going to enter into life of contradictions."

**Contradictions:**
- Political: One person, one vote, one value
- Social/Economic: Denial of this principle
- Deep inequalities continue

**Warning:** Must resolve quickly or democracy will be blown up.

**3. Avoid Grammar of Anarchy:**
> "However good Constitution, if implementers not good, will prove bad."

**Emphasized:**
- Constitution not everything
- Avoid hero worship, Bhakti in politics
- Use constitutional methods only

**Relevance:** Warnings still valid today

#### **SN2. Constitutionalism**

**Answer:**

**Definition:** Limited government under law

**Elements:**
1. Rule of Law
2. Separation of Powers
3. Judicial Review
4. FR Protection
5. Democratic accountability

**Distinction from Constitution:**
- Constitution = Document
- Constitutionalism = Philosophy

**Example:** USSR had Constitution but not constitutionalism

**In India:**
- Arts. 14, 21 embody Rule of Law
- Separation of powers (parliamentary system)
- Judicial review (Arts. 13, 32, 226)
- FRs (Part III)

**Significance:** Essence of limited government

#### **Q1. Citizenship**

**Question:** Discuss the constitutional and statutory provisions relating to acquisition and loss of citizenship in India.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION (1 mark)
- Single citizenship (Arts. 5-11)
- Citizenship Act, 1955
- No dual citizenship

CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS (4 marks)
At Commencement (Jan 26, 1950):

1. Citizens by Domicile (Art. 5)
   - Domicile in India + any one condition
   - Born in India OR
   - Parent born in India OR
   - Resident 5+ years

2. Citizens by Migration (Art. 6)
   - From Pakistan
   - Before/after July 19, 1948
   - Different conditions

3. Citizens by Registration (Art. 8)
   - Overseas Indians
   - Register with diplomatic representative

CITIZENSHIP ACT, 1955 (6 marks)

ACQUISITION (BDRNI):
1. Birth (S. 3)
   - Born in India
   - Exceptions: Diplomatic immunity, enemy alien

2. Descent (S. 4)
   - Born outside India
   - Parent Indian citizen

3. Registration (S. 5)
   - Categories: Indian origin, married to Indian, minors, Commonwealth

4. Naturalisation (S. 6)
   - Non-Commonwealth foreigners
   - Conditions: 12 months resident, 4 years in 7 years, etc.

5. Incorporation (S. 7)
   - Territory becomes part of India

LOSS OF CITIZENSHIP (RCD) (3 marks):
1. Renunciation (S. 8)
   - Voluntary declaration

2. Cessation (S. 9)
   - Acquiring foreign citizenship
   - S. Nalini Srikaran case (Central Govt determines)

3. Deprivation (S. 10)
   - Five grounds: Fraud, disloyal, enemy trade, sentence, 7 years abroad
   - Committee of Inquiry

CONCLUSION (1 mark)
- Comprehensive framework
- Single citizenship promotes unity
```

#### **Q2. Basic Structure Doctrine**

**Question:** Explain the Basic Structure Doctrine with reference to the evolution of judicial thinking on constitutional amendments.

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Kesavananda Bharati, 1973
- Most important constitutional doctrine
- Parliament's amending power limited

EVOLUTION (6 marks)

Phase 1: Shankari Prasad (1951)
- Amendment not "law" under Art. 13
- Parliament can amend FRs
- No limitation

Phase 2: Sajjan Singh (1965)
- Reaffirmed Shankari Prasad
- But suggested possible revisiting

Phase 3: Golak Nath (1967)
- Overruled Shankari Prasad
- Amendment IS "law" under Art. 13
- Parliament CANNOT amend FRs
- Prospective operation

Phase 4: Kesavananda Bharati (1973)
- Overruled Golak Nath
- Amendment NOT "law" under Art. 13
- BUT cannot alter BASIC STRUCTURE
- Balanced approach

BASIC STRUCTURE DOCTRINE (4 marks)

Principle:
- Parliament can amend
- But cannot destroy essential features

Basic Features (SURFS-JRP):
1. Supremacy of Constitution
2. Unity and integrity
3. Rule of Law
4. Federal character
5. Secular character
6. Judicial review
7. Republican democracy
8. Parliamentary system
9. Separation of powers
10. Independence of judiciary
11. Fundamental Rights (especially 14, 19, 21)

IMPLICATIONS (3 marks)
1. Constitutional amendments can be struck down
2. Judicial review of amendments
3. Core of Constitution protected
4. Balance of power

SUBSEQUENT DEVELOPMENTS
- Minerva Mills (1980): Reaffirmed
- Waman Rao (1981): Prospective application
- I.R. Coelho (2007): Ninth Schedule laws

CONCLUSION (2 marks)
- Greatest contribution of judiciary
- Protects Constitution from whimsical amendments
- Balance between flexibility and stability
```

#### **Q3. Article 12**

**Question:** Explain the definition of "State" under Article 12. Discuss the test laid down for determining "other authorities."

**Model Answer:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Art. 12 defines "State" for Part III
- Comprehensive definition
- Includes "other authorities"

TEXT OF ARTICLE 12
[Quote full text]

COMPONENTS (5 marks)
1. Government and Parliament of India
2. Government and Legislature of each State
3. All local authorities
   - Municipalities
   - Panchayats
   - Port trusts
4. Other authorities

"OTHER AUTHORITIES" (8 marks)

Problem: What is "other authority"?

TEST: Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib, AIR 1981

Four Factors (SPFM):
1. Statutory creation
2. Pervasive State control
3. Public Function
4. Financial assistance from State

Holistic Approach:
- Consider totality of facts
- Not all factors necessary
- Preponderance of factors

Entities Held to be "State":
- LIC (statutory corporation)
- ONGC (government company)
- Statutory universities
- Airport Authority (Ramana Shetty)
- Regional Engineering College (Ajay Hasia)

Entities NOT "State":
- Private companies
- Societies (generally)
- Unless state-controlled

SIGNIFICANCE (2 marks)
- Expands scope of Art. 12
- More entities accountable
- Better FR protection
- Prevents evasion

CONCLUSION
- Wide interpretation
- Purposive approach
- Covers instrumentalities of State
```

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES (5-7 Marks)**

#### **SN1. Single Citizenship**

**Answer:**

**Contrast with USA:**
- USA: Dual citizenship (Federal + State)
- India: Single citizenship only

**Constitutional Provision:**
- Articles 5-11
- Only Indian citizenship
- No separate state citizenship

**Reasons:**
1. Promote national unity
2. Prevent discrimination
3. India as one nation (not loose federation)
4. Historical context (partition)

**Implications:**
- No distinction between citizens of different states
- Same rights throughout India
- Art. 16(2): No discrimination on "residence"
- Art. 16(3): Parliament can prescribe residence for specified posts

**Significance:** Promotes emotional integration

---

#### **SN2. Domicile**

**Answer:**

**Definition:** Permanent home - place where person intends to reside permanently

**Essential Elements:**
1. Residence in country
2. Intention to make it permanent home

**Case: Central Bank v. Ram Narain**
- Intention to reside forever is essential

**Rules:**
- Minor: Takes father's domicile
- Married woman: Takes husband's domicile (traditionally)

**Constitutional Context:**
- Art. 5: Citizens by domicile at commencement
- Domicile in India + one of three conditions
- Birth, or parent born, or resident 5 years

---

#### **SN3. Overseas Citizenship**

**Answer:**

**Introduced:** 2003 (Ss. 7A, 7B of Citizenship Act)

**Expanded:** 2005 to all countries (except Pakistan, Bangladesh)

**Who Can Get:**
- Persons of Indian origin
- From specified countries

**Benefits:**
- Multiple entry visa
- Exemption from registration with police
- Parity with NRIs in economic, financial, educational fields

**NOT Full Citizenship:**
- Cannot vote
- Cannot hold constitutional posts
- Not all FR protections

**Cancellation:** Under S. 7D by Central Government

---

#### **SN4. Doctrine of Eclipse**

**Answer:**

**Applicable to:** Pre-Constitution laws only

**Principle:** 
- Pre-Constitution law inconsistent with FR not dead
- Only eclipsed (overshadowed)
- Remains dormant
- Can become operative if Constitution amended

**Bhikaji v. State of M.P.:**
- Pre-Constitution law violating Art. 19(1)(g)
- Eclipsed when Constitution came into force
- Can revive if Art. 19(1)(g) amended

**Post-Constitution laws:**
- Not eclipsed
- Void ab initio (from beginning)

**Rationale:** Pre-Constitution law not made with knowledge of FRs

---

#### **SN5. Basic Structure**

**Answer:**

**Case:** Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, AIR 1973

**Principle:** Parliament can amend Constitution but cannot alter or destroy basic structure

**Basic Features (SURFS-JRP):**
1. Supremacy of Constitution
2. Unity and integrity
3. Rule of Law
4. Federal character
5. Secular character
6. Judicial review
7. Republican democracy
8. Parliamentary system

**Other features:**
- Independence of judiciary
- Separation of powers
- Fundamental Rights
- Balance between FRs and DPs

**Implication:** Even constitutional amendments can be struck down

**Significance:** Greatest contribution of Indian judiciary

**Evolution:** Shankari Prasad ‚Üí Golak Nath ‚Üí Kesavananda

#### **Q1. Why November 26 and January 26?**

**Answer:**

**November 26, 1949:**
- Constitution adopted and signed
- Drafting process completed
- 284 members signed

**January 26, 1950:**
- Constitution came into force
- Chosen to commemorate "Purna Swaraj Day"
- Congress declared complete independence on Jan 26, 1930
- Historic significance
- India became Republic (earlier Dominion)

**Partial commencement:** Some provisions came into force Nov 26, 1949 (citizenship, elections, etc.)

---

#### **Q2. Federal vs Unitary Features**

**Answer:**

**Federal Features:**
1. Written Constitution
2. Division of powers (three lists)
3. Independent Judiciary
4. Bicameralism
5. Rigid Constitution (Art. 368)

**Unitary Features:**
1. Strong Centre (residuary powers)
2. Single Constitution
3. Single citizenship
4. Emergency provisions
5. Integrated judiciary
6. Appointment of Governors by Centre

**Conclusion:** **Quasi-federal** - Federal structure, Unitary spirit

### üìï **SECTION D: CASE-BASED QUESTIONS**

#### **Q1. Kesavananda Bharati Case**

**Facts:**
- Challenge to land reform laws
- Questioned 24th, 25th, 29th Amendments
- Fundamental Rights vs Property vs State's power

**Issues:**
1. Can Parliament amend FRs?
2. Is there any limitation?

**Held:**

1. Amendment NOT "law" under Art. 13
   - Overruled Golak Nath
   - Parliament CAN amend

2. **But Cannot Destroy Basic Structure**
   - New doctrine propounded
   - Essential features protected
   - Basic features listed

3. Arts. 31A, 31B, 31C: Upheld
   - But within basic structure limits

4. Judicial Review Essential
   - Cannot be taken away
   - Basic feature

**Composition:** 13-judge bench (largest constitutional bench)

**Opinions:** 11 separate opinions (9 agreed on basic structure)

**Significance:**
- Most important constitutional case
- Balanced approach
- Flexibility + Stability
- Protects Constitution from destruction
