#### **Q1. Article 14 - Equality Before Law**

**Question:** Explain the concepts of "Equality before law" and "Equal protection of laws" enshrined in Article 14. Discuss the doctrine of reasonable classification with the help of decided cases.

**Model Answer Structure:**

```
INTRODUCTION (2 marks)
- Article 14 text
- Cornerstone of Indian democracy
- Basic feature of Constitution

MAIN BODY (10 marks)
1. Two Concepts
   - Equality before law (negative)
   - Equal protection (positive)
   - Sri Srinivasa Theatre case

2. Reasonable Classification
   - Two-fold test
     (a) Intelligible Differentia
     (b) Rational Nexus
   - Examples: Geographical (Balsara, Kishan Singh)
              Procedural (Deoman Upadhyaya)
              Single entity (Charanjit Lal)
              Disaster-specific (Charan Lal Sahu)

3. Burden of Proof
   - General rule (G.K. Krishnan)
   - Exception (Dalmia v. Tendolkar)

CONCLUSION (3 marks)
- Art. 14 as guardian against arbitrariness
- Expands to strike at unreasonableness
- Current relevance
```

**Key Cases to Cite:**
- Kesavananda Bharati (basic feature)
- E.P. Royappa (arbitrariness)
- Maneka Gandhi (interconnection with 19, 21)
- Bachan Singh (rule of law)
- State of Bombay v. Balsara
- Kishan Singh v. Rajasthan
- Charanjit Lal v. Union

#### **Q2. Article 14 - Arbitrariness**

**Question:** "Article 14 strikes at arbitrariness." Discuss with the help of case law.

**Model Answer Structure:**

```
INTRODUCTION
- Traditional view: Art. 14 only about classification
- Modern view: Anti-arbitrariness principle

EVOLUTION
1. E.P. Royappa v. Tamil Nadu (1974)
   - Arbitrary = negation of equality
   - Abuse of power hit by Art. 14

2. Maneka Gandhi (1978)
   - Natural justice part of Art. 14
   - Order without hearing = arbitrary

3. Bachan Singh (1982)
   - Rule of law excludes arbitrariness
   - Art. 14 = guarantee against arbitrariness

APPLICATIONS
1. Administrative Discretion
   - Air India v. Nergesh Meerza (unguided discretion)
   - Re Kerala Education Bill (valid if policy deducible)

2. Administrative Action
   - Ramana Dayaram Shetty (airport tender)
   - Vellore Educational Trust (discriminatory refusal)

3. Cut-off Dates
   - D.S. Nakara (pension scheme)

CONCLUSION
- Art. 14 evolved from formal equality to substantive equality
- Powerful tool against executive arbitrariness
```

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES (5-7 Marks)**

#### **SN1. Doctrine of Eclipse**

**Answer:**

**Bhikaji v. State of M.P.:**

Pre-Constitution law inconsistent with FRs is **eclipsed**, not dead.

**Principle:**
- Law remains dormant
- Becomes operative if Constitution amended to remove inconsistency
- Applies only to pre-Constitution laws
- Post-Constitution laws void ab initio

**Example:** Pre-Constitution law violating Art. 19(1)(g) was eclipsed.

---

#### **SN2. Doctrine of Severability**

**Answer:**

If part of law unconstitutional, only that part void if severable.

**Test:**
1. Can invalid part be separated?
2. Does remainder form complete code?
3. Was remainder intended to operate independently?

**Case: A.K. Gopalan v. Madras**

Court can sever invalid portion if remainder can stand independently.

**Purpose:** Save valid portions of law.

---

#### **SN3. Basic Structure Doctrine**

**Answer:**

**Case: Kesavananda Bharati v. Kerala, AIR 1973**

**Principle:** Parliament can amend Constitution BUT cannot alter basic structure.

**Basic Features (Illustrative):**
1. Supremacy of Constitution
2. Republican & Democratic form
3. Secular character
4. Federal character
5. Separation of powers
6. Judicial review
7. Fundamental Rights (especially 14, 19, 21)
8. Rule of Law
9. Independence of Judiciary

**Effect:** Even constitutional amendments can be struck down.

**Evolution:**
- Shankari Prasad (1951) - Amendment not law
- Golak Nath (1967) - Amendment is law (overruled)
- Kesavananda (1973) - Amendment not law BUT basic structure protected

---

#### **SN4. Creamy Layer**

**Answer:**

**Case: Indra Sawhney v. UOI, 1993**

**Principle:** Socially advanced members of backward class must be excluded from reservation benefits.

**Rationale:**
- Reservation for truly disadvantaged
- Benefits should not be monopolized by advanced members
- More appropriately serves Art. 16(4) purpose

**Application:** Applicable to OBCs, not SC/ST (as per Mandal judgment)

**Identification:** Government to frame criteria based on economic, social parameters.

---

#### **SN5. Compassionate Appointment**

**Answer:**

**V. Sivamurthy v. AP, 2008**

**Nature:**
- NOT a right
- Exception to rule of equality
- Violates Art. 14 principle but justified

**Purpose:** Save bereaved family from sudden financial crisis due to death of sole breadwinner.

**Conditions:**
- Must follow rules/guidelines
- **Vacancy must be available**
- Cannot be made without vacancy

**Case: Indian Bank v. K. Usha:** If vacancy exists, appoint soon; improper to delay for years.

### üìô **SECTION C: CASE-BASED QUESTIONS**

#### **Q1. Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India, AIR 1978**

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi's passport impounded
- No hearing given
- Reasons not disclosed

**Issues:**
1. Whether Art. 21 applies?
2. What is "procedure established by law"?
3. Relationship between Arts. 14, 19, 21?

**Held:**

1. **Arts. 14, 19, 21 NOT mutually exclusive**
   - Nexus established
   - Must be read together

2. **"Procedure" must be reasonable, fair, just**
   - Due process introduced (though word "due" absent)
   - Not any procedure, but fair procedure

3. **Natural justice integral to Art. 21**
   - Order without hearing = arbitrary
   - Violates Art. 14

**Significance:**
- Practically overruled Gopalan
- Revolutionized interpretation of Art. 21
- Expanded scope of judicial review

---

#### **Q2. E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu, AIR 1974**

**Facts:**
- Royappa was Chief Secretary
- Transferred as Deputy Chairman of a Board
- Alleged arbitrary and mala fide

**Held:**

**New principle enunciated:**
> "Any action that is arbitrary must necessarily involve negation of equality."

**Expanded Art. 14:**
- From formal equality to substantive equality
- Strikes at arbitrariness
- Abuse of power hit by Art. 14

**Significance:** Landmark in evolution of Art. 14 from classification doctrine to anti-arbitrariness principle.

---

#### **Q3. Indra Sawhney v. Union of India, AIR 1993 (Mandal Commission Case)**

**Background:**
- Mandal Commission (1979) - investigate OBC conditions
- Recommendation: 27% reservation for OBCs
- V.P. Singh Govt (1990) - accepted via office memorandum
- Widespread disturbances

**Issues:**
1. Whether OBC reservation valid?
2. What is "backward class"?
3. Can exceed 50%?
4. Creamy layer exclusion?

**Held:**

1. **27% reservation for OBC valid**
2. **Creamy layer must be excluded**
3. **50% rule reaffirmed** (except extraordinary situations)
4. **Caste one factor, not sole factor**
5. **No reservation in promotions** to higher posts
6. **Carry forward** with 50% ceiling in a year

**Significance:**
- Most important reservation case
- Creamy layer principle
- Balance between equality and affirmative action

### üìï **SECTION D: PROBLEM QUESTIONS**

#### **Problem 1: Classification**

**Question:**

State of Maharashtra enacted a law providing that:
1. No person below 40 years can be appointed as Vice Chancellor
2. Private colleges to reserve 70% seats for backward classes
3. Women doctors in government service to retire at 55, men at 58

**Advise on constitutional validity of each provision.**

**Answer:**

**Provision 1:** Age limit for VC

**Likely VALID**
- Qualifications can be prescribed (Shiv Ram Sharma)
- Age 40 for VC position has rational nexus
- Experience and maturity required for administrative post
- Reasonable classification

**Cases:** State of J&K v. Shiv Ram Sharma; Mohan Kumar Singhania

---

**Provision 2:** 70% reservation

**INVALID**
- Exceeds 50% rule (Balaji)
- **"Reservation should be less than 50%"** - Supreme Court
- No extraordinary circumstances shown
- Fraud on Constitution

**Cases:** Balaji v. Mysore; Indra Sawhney

---

**Provision 3:** Different retirement age for women

**INVALID**
- Discrimination on ground of "sex"
- Prohibited under Art. 16(2)
- No rational nexus with efficiency
- Women can continue beyond 55 as well as men

**Exception:** Art. 15(3) permits special provisions for women, but detrimental classification not allowed under guise of protection.

**Cases:** Air India v. Nergesh Meerza

---

#### **Problem 2: State Action**

**Question:**

Regional Engineering College, Srinagar, is a society registered under Societies Registration Act, 1860. The President of India is the Visitor. It receives substantial grants from State. In admissions, it:
1. Reserved 15% seats for children of faculty
2. Gave preference to children of politicians
3. Manipulated interview marks

**Can its actions be challenged under Art. 14?**

**Answer:**

**YES - College is "State" under Art. 12**

**Test: Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib**
1. ‚úì Statutory creation (Societies Act)
2. ‚úì Deep state control (President as Visitor)
3. ‚úì Public function (education)
4. ‚úì Financial assistance (substantial grants)

**Each action challenged:**

**Action 1:** 15% for faculty children
- **Likely INVALID**
- Creates nepotism
- No rational nexus with merit
- Denies equal opportunity to others

**Action 2:** Preference to politicians' children
- **CLEARLY INVALID**
- Blatant discrimination
- Arbitrary
- No intelligible differentia

**Action 3:** Manipulated interview marks
- **INVALID**
- Ajay Hasia case similar facts
- Violates Art. 14
- Arbitrary and discriminatory

**Remedy:** Writ under Art. 32 or 226.

```

   - Restrictions: 19(3)
```

#### **Q2. Freedom of Speech and Expression**

**Question:** Critically examine the scope of freedom of speech and expression guaranteed under Article 19(1)(a) and discuss the reasonable restrictions that can be imposed on it.

**Answer:**

**INTRODUCTION:**
- Most important freedom
- Democracy depends on free speech
- Foundation of all liberties

**SCOPE:**

1. **Positive Content:**
   - Freedom of press implied
   - Right to know (implied)
   - Commercial speech protected
   - Right to broadcast
   - Right to advertise

2. **Negative Content:**
   - Right NOT to speak
   - Right to remain silent

3. **Forms:**
   - Oral, written
   - Print, electronic
   - Symbolic speech
   - Peaceful protest

**REASONABLE RESTRICTIONS - Art. 19(2):**

Eight grounds:
1. Sovereignty and integrity of India
2. Security of State
3. Friendly relations with foreign states
4. Public order
5. Decency or morality
6. Contempt of court
7. Defamation
8. Incitement to offence

**TEST OF REASONABLENESS:**
- Must be reasonable (not just any restriction)
- Proportionality
- Not excessive
- Courts to decide

**LANDMARK CASES:**

1. **Romesh Thappar (1950)**
   - Security of State ‚â† Public order
   - Narrow interpretation of restrictions

2. **Shreya Singhal (2015)**
   - Section 66A IT Act struck down
   - Vague, over-broad
   - Chilling effect on speech

3. **Bennett Coleman (1972)**
   - Newsprint restriction violates 19(1)(a)
   - Freedom of press part of speech

**CONCLUSION:**
- Balance between freedom and order
- Democracy requires maximum freedom
- Restrictions only in grave circumstances

### üìó **SECTION B: SHORT NOTES**

#### **SN1. Freedom of Press**

**Not expressly guaranteed but implied in Art. 19(1)(a)**

**Romesh Thappar case:** Freedom of press part of freedom of speech.

**Scope:**
- Print what they like
- Subject to Art. 19(2) restrictions
- Prior restraint generally not allowed

**Cases:**
- Bennett Coleman - newsprint control invalid
- Indian Express - interference with circulation invalid

---

#### **SN2. Right to Property (Present Position)**

**History:**
- Originally: Art. 19(1)(f) + Art. 31
- 44th Amendment, 1978: Deleted as FR
- Now: Art. 300-A (constitutional right, not FR)

**Art. 300-A:** No person deprived of property except by authority of law.

**Difference:**
- Not enforceable under Art. 32
- No restrictions on State's power
- Just procedure required, not reasonable procedure

**Special Provisions:**
- Art. 31-A: Estates, tenancy laws
- Art. 31-B: Ninth Schedule
- Art. 31-C: Laws for Arts. 39(b), (c)

---

#### **SN3. Reasonable Restrictions**

**Test:**
1. **Procedurally reasonable:** Made by competent authority, proper procedure
2. **Substantively reasonable:** Not excessive, proportionate, necessary

**Grounds must be in respective clauses** (Art. 19(2) to 19(6))

**Courts decide reasonableness**

**Examples:**
- **Valid:** Prohibition on hate speech
- **Invalid:** Section 66A IT Act (Shreya Singhal)
