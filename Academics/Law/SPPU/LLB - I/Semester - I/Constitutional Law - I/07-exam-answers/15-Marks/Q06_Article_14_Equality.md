# 15-MARK ANSWER

## Q. Discuss the Concept of Equality under Article 14.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Constitutional Foundation of Equality

**Article 14** of the Constitution of India is the foundational provision of the **Right to Equality** guaranteed under **Part III** (Articles 14-18). It provides:

> **"The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India."**

Equality is described as the "magnificent cornerstone of Indian democracy" and forms one of the basic features of the Constitution. The doctrine of equality before law is a necessary corollary of the **Rule of Law**, which pervades the entire Constitution.

### 1.2 Importance of Article 14

The Supreme Court has declared **equality as a basic feature** of the Constitution in **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)**. This means that even a constitutional amendment offending the principle of equality can be declared invalid. The Preamble to the Constitution emphasizes equality of status and opportunity as one of the core objectives.

### 1.3 Scope of Article 14

Article 14 contains two expressions:
1. **"Equality before the law"** (Negative concept - British origin)
2. **"Equal protection of the laws"** (Positive concept - American origin)

Together, these ensure that:
- No person or class of persons is privileged above the law
- All persons in similar circumstances are treated alike
- The law applies equally to all without discrimination
- The State must take affirmative steps to ensure substantive equality

---

## II. TWO CONCEPTS IN ARTICLE 14

### 2.1 Equality Before the Law (Negative Concept)

#### Meaning

"Equality before the law" is a negative concept borrowed from the British **Rule of Law** doctrine formulated by Professor A.V. Dicey. It ensures:
1. **Absence of special privileges:** No person, whatever be his rank or condition, is above the law
2. **Subjection to ordinary law:** All persons are equally subject to the ordinary law of the land
3. **Equal amenability:** All persons are amenable to the jurisdiction of ordinary tribunals

**In essence:** There shall be no privileged class or person who is exempt from the law or immune from the jurisdiction of ordinary courts.

#### Exceptions to Equality Before Law

While Article 14 guarantees equality before law, the Constitution itself recognizes certain exceptions:

**1. President and Governors (Article 361):**
- President and Governors immune from criminal proceedings during term of office
- Not answerable to any court for exercise of official duties
- Cannot be arrested or imprisoned during term

**Justification:** To enable them to discharge constitutional duties without fear of legal proceedings

**2. Foreign Diplomats:**
- Diplomatic immunity under international law
- Cannot be sued in Indian courts
- Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations, 1961

**3. Members of Parliament and State Legislatures:**
- Freedom of speech in legislature (Article 105 and 194)
- No civil or criminal proceedings for anything said in legislature
- But this is limited to parliamentary proceedings

**4. Judges:**
- Protection under Article 121 (Parliament cannot discuss conduct of judges)
- Immunity for judicial acts

**These exceptions are reasonable and necessary for effective functioning of organs of State.**

---

### 2.2 Equal Protection of the Laws (Positive Concept)

#### Meaning

"Equal protection of the laws" is a positive concept borrowed from the American Constitution (14th Amendment). It requires:
1. **Equality of treatment in equal circumstances**
2. **Like should be treated alike**
3. **State must not discriminate between persons similarly situated**
4. **Affirmative State action** to ensure substantive equality

**Important:** Equal protection does not mean identical treatment to all. It permits **reasonable classification** based on intelligible differentia having rational relation to the object sought to be achieved.

#### Equality vs Uniformity

**Equal protection â‰  Identical treatment**

As Justice Frankfurter observed:
> "The Constitution does not require things which are different in fact to be treated in law as though they were the same."

The law must recognize that:
- People differ in capacity, needs, circumstances
- Unequal treatment may be necessary to achieve substantive equality
- Treating unequals equally is as violative as treating equals unequally

**Example:** Special provisions for women, children, SCs, STs, OBCs are not violations of equality but affirmative action to achieve real equality.

## III. REASONABLE CLASSIFICATION DOCTRINE

### 3.1 Principle

**Article 14 forbids class legislation; it does not forbid reasonable classification.**

The Legislature can classify persons, objects, and transactions for the purpose of achieving specific ends, provided the classification is reasonable and not arbitrary.

### 3.2 Two-Fold Test for Valid Classification

The Supreme Court has evolved a **two-fold test** to determine whether a classification is reasonable:

#### Test 1: Intelligible Differentia

**The classification must be founded on an intelligible differentia which distinguishes persons or things that are grouped together from others left out of the group.**

**Meaning:**
- Basis of classification must be real and substantial
- Not arbitrary, artificial, or evasive
- Must be based on qualities or characteristics peculiar to the class
- Mere numerical classification not sufficient

**Examples of Valid Differentia:**
- Age (minors vs adults)
- Sex (women vs men for protection)
- Nature of employment (hazardous vs non-hazardous)
- Size of landholding (big landlords vs small farmers)
- Income level (for taxation)
- Nature of business (trade vs profession)

**Examples of Invalid Differentia:**
- Arbitrary selection of individuals
- Classification based on irrelevant factors
- Discrimination without any basis

---

#### Test 2: Rational Nexus

**The differentia adopted as the basis of classification must have a rational or reasonable nexus (connection) with the object sought to be achieved by the statute in question.**

**Meaning:**
- Classification must have reasonable relation to object of law
- Object must be legitimate
- Classification must be necessary to achieve object
- Not merely convenient but truly necessary

**Example:**
If law's object is to prevent adulteration of food, classification between food items and non-food items has rational nexus with object. But classification between food sold on Mondays and food sold on other days has no rational nexus.

#### Both Tests Must Be Satisfied

A classification is valid only if it satisfies **both tests**. If either test fails, the classification is unreasonable and violates Article 14.

---

### 3.3 Burden of Proof

**General Rule:** When a person challenges a law on ground of violation of Article 14, the **burden is on him** to prove that:
1. Classification is arbitrary
2. Lacks intelligible differentia
3. Has no rational nexus with object

**Initial Presumption:** Every law is presumed to be constitutional unless proven otherwise.

**Case Law:** State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (AIR 1952 SC 75) - Burden on challenger to show discrimination.

**Exception:** If discrimination is **writ large** on the face of legislation, burden shifts to State to justify.

**Case Law:** Dalmia v. Tendolkar (AIR 1958 SC 538) - If discrimination apparent, State must justify.

## IV. ARTICLE 14 STRIKES AT ARBITRARINESS

### 4.1 Evolution of Principle

Initially, Article 14 was understood to prohibit only **discriminatory treatment** (treating equals unequally).

**Evolution through Judicial Interpretation:**

In **E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (AIR 1974 SC 555)**, the Supreme Court expanded the scope of Article 14 to include **prohibition of arbitrariness**.

**Justice P.N. Bhagwati held:**
> "Equality is a dynamic concept with many aspects and dimensions and it cannot be 'cribbed, cabined and confined' within traditional and doctrinaire limits... From a positivistic point of view, equality is antithetic to arbitrariness. In fact, equality and arbitrariness are sworn enemies; one belongs to the rule of law in a republic while the other, to the whim and caprice of an absolute monarch. Where an act is arbitrary, it is implicit in it that it is unequal both according to political logic and constitutional law and is therefore violative of Article 14."

**Principle Established:**
**Article 14 strikes at arbitrariness in State action** and ensures fairness and equality of treatment.

**Significance:**
- Widened scope of Article 14
- Not limited to discrimination
- Any arbitrary State action violates Article 14
- Brought administrative action within Article 14

---

### 4.2 Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (AIR 1978 SC 597)

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi's passport was impounded without giving her an opportunity of hearing
- She challenged the action under Articles 14, 19, and 21

**Held:**

**On Article 14:**
- Procedure must not be arbitrary, unfair, or unreasonable
- Reasonableness is essence of Article 14
- Articles 14, 19, and 21 form a **"Golden Triangle"**
- They are not mutually exclusive but overlapping and supplementary

**Principle:**
Any law or action that is arbitrary, unfair, or unreasonable violates Article 14, even if it does not involve discrimination.

**Impact:**
- Article 14 became a powerful tool against arbitrary executive action
- Administrative discretion must be exercised reasonably
- Established interconnection between Articles 14, 19, and 21

## V. LANDMARK JUDICIAL PRONOUNCEMENTS

### 5.1 State of Bombay v. F.N. Balsara (AIR 1951 SC 318)

**Facts:**
- Bombay Prohibition Act prohibited manufacture, sale, and possession of alcohol
- Exempted foreigners and tourists visiting India for short period
- Challenged as discriminatory

**Issue:**
Is exemption to foreigners and tourists a reasonable classification?

**Held:**
- **Classification valid**
- Foreigners and tourists are distinct class from permanent residents
- Reasonable to assume they are habituated to alcohol in their countries
- Object of prohibition is to wean Indians from drinking habit
- Classification has intelligible differentia and rational nexus with object

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Classification based on nationality or residence can be valid if it has rational relation to object of legislation.

**Significance:**
- Early case establishing reasonable classification doctrine
- Showed wide legislative power to classify

---

### 5.2 Chiranjit Lal Chowdhuri v. Union of India (AIR 1951 SC 41)

**Facts:**
- Sholapur Spinning and Weaving Company was taken over by Central Government
- Order challenged as discriminatory - why only this company?

**Issue:**
Does selective application to one company violate Article 14?

**Held:**
- **No violation of Article 14**
- Legislature can select areas, localities, or industries for special treatment
- If classification is reasonable, selection is valid
- Even if law applies to single individual, valid if class is identifiable

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Legislature can classify on basis of geographical area or nature of business. Application to single person or entity does not make law discriminatory if class is identifiable.

---

### 5.3 D.S. Nakara v. Union of India (AIR 1983 SC 130)

**Facts:**
- Government issued circular liberalizing pension rules for those retiring after specific date
- Those who had retired before that date not given benefit
- Challenged as discriminatory

**Issue:**
Does date-based classification violate Article 14?

**Held:**
- **Classification invalid**
- All pensioners form one class
- Date of retirement is not intelligible differentia
- No rational nexus between date and liberalization of pension
- Arbitrary classification

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Classification based on arbitrary dates without rational nexus to object is unconstitutional.

**Significance:**
- Showed that even date-based classification must satisfy twin test
- Courts will scrutinize basis of classification carefully

---

### 5.4 State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (AIR 1952 SC 75)

**Facts:**
- West Bengal Special Courts Act empowered government to transfer any case to special courts
- Special courts had summary procedure, limited appeal rights
- Challenged as arbitrary classification

**Issue:**
Does law conferring unguided discretion on government violate Article 14?

**Held:**
- **Act valid** (by majority)
- Legislature can vest discretion in government
- Mere conferment of discretion does not violate Article 14
- Classification can be made by executive based on guidelines

**Dissent (Justice Fazl Ali):**
- Unguided discretion is arbitrary
- Leads to discrimination
- Violates Article 14

**Overruled:** Substantially by later judgments which held that excessive delegation and unguided discretion violate Article 14.

**Significance:**
- Early case upholding executive discretion
- Later jurisprudence moved away from this position
- Now, unguided discretion is considered arbitrary

---

### 5.5 Budhan Choudhry v. State of Bihar (AIR 1955 SC 191)

**Facts:**
- Bihar Land Reforms Act exempted certain lands from ceiling provisions
- Challenged as discriminatory

**Issue:**
Can State exempt certain categories from general law?

**Held:**
- **Exemptions valid if reasonable**
- State can provide exemptions based on reasonable classification
- Must satisfy twin test

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Exemptions from general law are permissible if based on reasonable classification.

---

### 5.6 Ramakrishna Dalmia v. Justice S.R. Tendolkar (AIR 1958 SC 538)

**Facts:**
- Commission of Inquiry Act provisions applied
- Challenged as discriminatory

**Held:**
If discrimination is **writ large** on face of legislation, burden shifts to State to justify.

**Principle:**
Normally, challenger must prove discrimination. But if discrimination is apparent, State must justify.

---

### 5.7 State of Kerala v. N.M. Thomas (AIR 1976 SC 490)

**Facts:**
- Reservation for SCs/STs in promotions
- Exempted from passing departmental tests
- Challenged as violating equality

**Held:**
- **Reservation valid**
- Article 14 permits compensatory discrimination
- Special provisions for disadvantaged not violation of equality
- **Substantive equality** vs formal equality

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Equality does not mean treating unequals equally
2. Compensatory discrimination to achieve real equality is constitutional
3. Article 14 prohibits hostile discrimination, not protective discrimination

**Significance:**
- Recognized concept of affirmative action
- Substantive equality as goal
- Special provisions for disadvantaged upheld

## VI. ARTICLE 14 AND ADMINISTRATIVE ACTION

### 6.1 Scope

Initially, Article 14 was understood to apply only to legislative action. Through judicial interpretation, it now applies to:
1. **Legislative action** (laws made by Parliament and State Legislatures)
2. **Executive action** (administrative orders, circulars, notifications)
3. **Quasi-judicial action** (decisions by tribunals and authorities)

**Case Law:** E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974) - Article 14 applies to administrative action.

### 6.2 Principles Governing Administrative Action

**1. No Arbitrary Discretion:**
- Administrative authorities must act reasonably
- Discretion must be exercised according to rules of reason and justice
- Cannot be arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical

**2. Consistent Policy:**
- State must follow consistent policy
- Cannot discriminate between similarly situated persons
- Change in policy must be justified

**3. Reasoned Decisions:**
- Administrative orders must be based on reasons
- Cannot be based on irrelevant considerations

**4. Procedural Fairness:**
- Natural justice must be followed
- Reasonable opportunity of hearing

## VII. EXCEPTIONS AND SPECIAL PROVISIONS

### 7.1 Constitutional Exceptions

The Constitution itself provides for special provisions that may appear to violate Article 14 but are constitutionally valid:

**1. Article 15(3):** Special provisions for women and children
**2. Article 15(4):** Special provisions for SCs, STs, and other backward classes
**3. Article 15(5):** Reservations in educational institutions
**4. Article 16(4):** Reservation in appointments
**5. Article 16(4A):** Reservation in promotions for SCs/STs
**6. Article 16(4B):** Consequential seniority for reserved candidates
**7. Article 46:** Promotion of educational and economic interests of SCs, STs

**These are not violations of Article 14 but constitutional recognition of need for affirmative action to achieve substantive equality.**

---

### 7.2 Affirmative Action and Reservations

#### Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (AIR 1993 SC 477) - "Mandal Commission Case"

**Facts:**
- Government implemented 27% reservation for OBCs in Central services
- Total reservation: 49.5% (22.5% SC/ST + 27% OBC)
- Challenged as violating equality

**Issues:**
1. Does reservation violate Article 14?
2. What are limits on reservation?

**Held:**

**On Article 14 and Equality:**
- **Equality in Preamble is substantive, not merely formal**
- Permits affirmative action for disadvantaged
- Reservation is not violation of equality but means to achieve equality

**Key Principles Laid Down:**

**1. 50% Ceiling:**
- Reservation cannot exceed 50% as a rule
- Extraordinary circumstances may justify exceeding 50%

**2. Creamy Layer:**
- Socially advanced persons from backward classes must be excluded
- Creamy layer concept applies to OBCs (not SC/STs)

**3. No Reservation in Promotions:**
- Article 16(4) does not permit reservation in promotions
- Only in appointments
- **[Note: Later overruled by constitutional amendments and M. Nagaraj case]**

**4. Backwardness:**
- Only social and educational backwardness, not economic alone
- Caste can be criterion but not sole criterion

**5. Efficiency:**
- Reservation must not affect efficiency of administration
- Balance between social justice and efficiency

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Equality permits affirmative action
2. Reservation is tool to achieve substantive equality
3. But must be reasonable with limits
4. 50% ceiling, creamy layer exclusion, efficiency consideration

**Significance:**
- Most comprehensive judgment on reservations
- Defined limits and principles
- Balance between equality and affirmative action
- Guidelines for implementation

---

## VIII. DISTINCTION: ARTICLE 14 AND ARTICLE 16

While both Article 14 and Article 16 deal with equality, there are distinctions:

| Basis | Article 14 | Article 16 |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Scope** | General equality clause | Equality in public employment |
| **Applicability** | All persons (citizens + non-citizens) | Only citizens |
| **Area** | All fields of State action | Only matters of public employment |
| **Nature** | General provision | Special provision |
| **Relationship** | Genus | Species |

**Article 16 is application of Article 14 to specific field of public employment.**

**Case Law:** E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu - Article 16 is facet of Article 14; both prohibit arbitrariness.

## IX. CONTEMPORARY RELEVANCE

### 9.1 Article 14 in Modern Context

**1. Economic Regulation:**
- Government policies affecting different economic sectors
- Must satisfy reasonableness test
- Cannot be arbitrary

**2. Taxation:**
- Different tax rates for different income groups
- Must be based on intelligible differentia
- Progressive taxation valid

**3. Criminal Law:**
- Different punishments for different offenses
- Classification based on nature and gravity of offense

**4. Social Welfare:**
- Special programs for disadvantaged groups
- Compensatory discrimination valid

**5. Digital Governance:**
- Algorithmic decision-making must not be discriminatory
- Transparency and fairness in automated systems

### 9.2 Challenges

**1. Implementing Affirmative Action:**
- Balancing merit and social justice
- Determining backwardness
- Creamy layer identification

**2. Emerging Inequalities:**
- Economic inequality
- Digital divide
- Access to healthcare and education

**3. Globalization:**
- Different treatment to domestic and foreign entities
- Must justify on reasonable grounds

## X. CONCLUSION

**Article 14** is the bedrock of Indian democracy and the **"magna carta of equality."** It embodies the principle that all persons are equal before the law and entitled to equal protection of laws. Through seven decades of judicial interpretation, Article 14 has evolved from a simple anti-discrimination provision to a powerful tool ensuring fairness, reasonableness, and justice in all State action.

The **reasonable classification doctrine**, with its twin tests of intelligible differentia and rational nexus, provides the framework for balancing the need for differentiation with the principle of equality. It recognizes that **treating unequals equally is as violative of equality as treating equals unequally**.

The expansion of Article 14 to strike at **arbitrariness** in State action (**E.P. Royappa**, **Maneka Gandhi**) has made it a potent weapon against unfair, unreasonable, and capricious governmental action. The **"Golden Triangle"** of Articles 14, 19, and 21 ensures comprehensive protection of rights and liberties.

The Supreme Court's judgment in **Indra Sawhney (Mandal Commission case)** clarified that equality in the Indian Constitution is **substantive, not merely formal**. It permits and indeed requires affirmative action to bring about real equality for historically disadvantaged groups. However, such affirmative action must be reasonable, with limits like the 50% ceiling and creamy layer exclusion.

Article 14 continues to be relevant in contemporary times, addressing new challenges like:
- Digital discrimination
- Algorithmic bias
- Economic inequalities
- Access to resources

As Justice Matthew observed in **Budhan Choudhry case**:
> "The principle of equality before the law requires that persons or things similarly situated should be similarly treated."

**Article 14 remains the guardian of equality, ensuring that the promise of "equality of status and opportunity" enshrined in the Preamble is not merely a constitutional aspiration but a living reality for all persons in India.**
