# 15-MARK ANSWER

## Q. Discuss Freedom under Article 19 with Reasonable Restrictions.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Constitutional Framework of Freedom

**Article 19** of the Constitution of India is the cornerstone of individual liberty and forms part of the Fundamental Rights guaranteed under **Part III** of the Constitution. It embodies six freedoms that are essential for the dignity of individuals and the functioning of a democratic society. These freedoms represent the positive aspect of liberty - not merely freedom from State interference, but the affirmative grant of rights that citizens can exercise.

### 1.2 Importance of Article 19

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar recognized Article 19 as crucial for democracy. Freedom of speech and expression, in particular, was considered **"the first condition of liberty."** These freedoms are essential for:
- Democratic governance
- Individual self-development
- Social progress
- Economic advancement
- Political participation
- Cultural expression

### 1.3 Scope of Discussion

This answer discusses:
1. Six freedoms guaranteed under Article 19(1)
2. Corresponding reasonable restrictions under Article 19(2) to (6)
3. Doctrine of reasonable restrictions
4. Judicial interpretation through landmark cases
5. Balance between liberty and social order

---

## II. SIX FREEDOMS UNDER ARTICLE 19(1)

Article 19(1) originally guaranteed **seven freedoms**, but after the **44th Constitutional Amendment (1978)**, freedom to acquire, hold, and dispose of property [Article 19(1)(f)] was deleted. Currently, **six freedoms** are guaranteed:

### 2.1 Freedom of Speech and Expression - Article 19(1)(a)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to freedom of speech and expression."

**Scope:**
Freedom of speech and expression includes:
- Right to express one's views through words (spoken or written)
- Right to propagate one's views
- Freedom of press (implied)
- Right to information
- Commercial speech (advertising with limitations)
- Right to silence (negative aspect)
- Symbolic expression

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(2):**

The State can impose reasonable restrictions on this freedom in the interest of:
1. **Sovereignty and integrity of India**
2. **Security of the State**
3. **Friendly relations with foreign States** (added by 1st Amendment, 1951)
4. **Public order** (added by 1st Amendment, 1951)
5. **Decency or morality**
6. **Contempt of Court**
7. **Defamation**
8. **Incitement to an offence**

---

### 2.2 Freedom of Assembly - Article 19(1)(b)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to assemble peaceably and without arms."

**Scope:**
- Right to hold public meetings
- Right to organize demonstrations and processions
- Right to protest peacefully
- **Conditions:** Assembly must be peaceful and without arms

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(3):**

The State can impose reasonable restrictions in the interest of:
1. **Sovereignty and integrity of India**
2. **Public order**

**Note:** Restrictions renumbered as Article 19(4) after deletion of 19(1)(f)

---

### 2.3 Freedom to Form Associations or Unions - Article 19(1)(c)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to form associations or unions."

**Scope:**
- Right to form associations, societies, clubs
- Right to form trade unions
- Right to form political parties
- Right to form companies
- Includes right not to form or join associations (negative aspect)

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(4):**

The State can impose reasonable restrictions in the interest of:
1. **Sovereignty and integrity of India**
2. **Public order**
3. **Morality**

---

### 2.4 Freedom of Movement - Article 19(1)(d)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to move freely throughout the territory of India."

**Scope:**
- Right to move from one part of India to another
- Internal movement within country
- Not absolute - subject to reasonable restrictions

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(5):**

The State can impose reasonable restrictions in the interest of:
1. **General public**
2. **Protection of interests of any Scheduled Tribe**

**Examples of Restrictions:**
- Entry restrictions in tribal areas
- Permit requirements for certain areas
- Movement restrictions during emergency
- Curfew in public interest

---

### 2.5 Freedom of Residence and Settlement - Article 19(1)(e)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to reside and settle in any part of the territory of India."

**Scope:**
- Right to reside anywhere in India
- Right to settle permanently in any part of India
- Complements freedom of movement

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(5):**

Same as Article 19(1)(d):
1. **General public**
2. **Protection of interests of any Scheduled Tribe**

**Examples:**
- Restrictions on settlement in certain hill areas
- Protection of tribal culture and land

---

### 2.6 Freedom of Profession, Occupation, Trade, and Business - Article 19(1)(g)

**Text:** "All citizens shall have the right to practise any profession, or to carry on any occupation, trade or business."

**Scope:**
- Right to choose any profession
- Right to carry on any occupation
- Right to carry on any trade or business
- Freedom of enterprise

**Reasonable Restrictions - Article 19(6):**

The State can impose reasonable restrictions in the interest of:
1. **General public**

**Additionally, State can make laws:**
- Prescribing professional or technical qualifications
- Carrying on any trade, business, or service by State or corporations owned or controlled by State (complete or partial monopoly)

**Examples of Restrictions:**
- Professional qualifications (e.g., medical degree for doctors)
- License requirements (e.g., for arms, liquor, drugs)
- Regulations for public health and safety
- Monopoly by State in certain sectors

## III. DOCTRINE OF REASONABLE RESTRICTIONS

### 3.1 Meaning and Purpose

**Freedoms under Article 19(1) are not absolute.** They are subject to reasonable restrictions. The Constitution itself recognizes that absolute freedom would lead to anarchy and conflict with others' rights and public interest.

**Purpose of Restrictions:**
- Prevent abuse of freedom
- Protect public interest
- Maintain social order
- Balance individual liberty with collective good
- Protect rights of others

### 3.2 Constitutional Limitations

**Only the "State" can impose restrictions:**
- Restrictions must be imposed by law (not executive action)
- Must be authorized by Constitution
- Must fall within specified grounds mentioned in Article 19(2) to (6)

**"Reasonable" Restriction:**
- Not defined in Constitution
- Left to judicial interpretation
- Must be determined by courts in each case

### 3.3 Test of Reasonableness

**Two-Fold Test (Evolved by Courts):**

**1. Proximate and Rational Relation:**
- Restriction must have proximate and rational relation to object sought to be achieved
- Cannot be arbitrary or excessive
- Must be necessary, not merely convenient

**2. Proportionality:**
- Restriction must not be disproportionate to object
- Must not go beyond what is necessary
- Least restrictive alternative should be chosen

**Burden of Proof:**
- State must justify reasonableness of restriction
- Courts will examine each restriction independently

## IV. LANDMARK JUDICIAL PRONOUNCEMENTS

### 4.1 Romesh Thapar v. State of Madras (AIR 1950 SC 124)

**Facts:**
- State of Madras banned entry and circulation of English journal "Cross Road"
- Order passed under Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949
- Ground: Prejudicial to public safety and maintenance of public order
- Challenged as violating Article 19(1)(a)

**Issue:**
Can State Government ban publication in the interest of "public safety"?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Restriction invalid** as it was beyond grounds specified in Article 19(2)
- Original Article 19(2) allowed restrictions only in the interest of "security of State" or "public order"
- "Public safety" was broader than "security of State"
- Freedom of speech and expression includes **freedom of press**

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Freedom of press is implied in freedom of speech and expression
2. Restrictions must be within grounds specified in Article 19(2)
3. Cannot impose restrictions on grounds not mentioned in Constitution

**Significance:**
- Recognized freedom of press as fundamental right
- Led to First Amendment (1951) to widen grounds of restrictions

---

### 4.2 Sakal Papers (P) Ltd. v. Union of India (AIR 1962 SC 305)

**Facts:**
- Newspaper owners challenged Daily Newspapers (Price and Page) Order, 1960
- Order fixed relation between price of newspaper and number of pages
- Also restricted advertising space
- Objective: Prevent monopoly, help small newspapers

**Issue:**
Does regulation of advertising space violate Article 19(1)(a)?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Regulation invalid** as it violated freedom of speech and expression
- Circulation of newspaper depends on advertising revenue
- Reducing advertisement space directly affects circulation
- Thus violates Article 19(1)(a)
- Restriction not reasonable

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Commercial speech (advertising) is part of freedom of speech and expression, though subject to greater restrictions than political speech.

**Significance:**
- Commercial speech recognized but not with same protection as political speech
- Economic regulation affecting freedom of speech must satisfy test of reasonableness

---

### 4.3 Bennett Coleman & Co. v. Union of India (AIR 1973 SC 106)

**Facts:**
- Challenged Newsprint Control Order restricting newsprint allocation to newspapers
- Order fixed pages based on past circulation
- Argued to violate Article 19(1)(a) and Article 19(1)(g)

**Issue:**
Does restriction on newsprint violate freedom of press?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Restriction invalid**
- Right to freedom of press includes right to publish without pre-censorship
- Economic control becoming content control is impermissible
- Circulation restricted means views restricted
- Thus violates Article 19(1)(a)

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Indirect restriction through economic control is as violative as direct restriction if it affects content and circulation.

**Significance:**
- Affirmed robust protection for press freedom
- Economic regulation affecting press must be narrowly tailored

---

### 4.4 Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (AIR 1978 SC 597)

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi's passport impounded without hearing
- Challenged under Article 14, 19, and 21
- Government argued Article 21 excludes Article 19

**Issue:**
What is relationship between Article 19 and Article 21?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Articles 14, 19, and 21 form "Golden Triangle"**
- They are not mutually exclusive but supplementary
- Procedure under Article 21 must be fair, just, and reasonable
- Must satisfy Article 14 (not arbitrary) and Article 19 (reasonable restrictions)
- Any law depriving liberty must satisfy all three articles

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Articles 14, 19, and 21 are interconnected and must be read together
2. Procedure established by law (Article 21) must be just, fair, and reasonable
3. Law must satisfy Article 19 reasonableness test

**Significance:**
- Landmark case changing constitutional interpretation
- Procedure must now be substantively fair, not just legally valid
- Expanded scope of judicial review

---

### 4.5 Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (AIR 2015 SC 1523)

**Facts:**
- Challenged Section 66A of Information Technology Act, 2000
- Section criminalized sending "offensive" messages through communication services
- Terms "grossly offensive," "menacing character," "causing annoyance" were vague
- Alleged "chilling effect" on free speech

**Issue:**
Does Section 66A violate Article 19(1)(a)?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Section 66A struck down as unconstitutional**
- Terms used in section are vague and overbroad
- No clear procedure
- Creates "chilling effect" on freedom of speech
- Not a reasonable restriction

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Restriction on speech must be narrowly drawn
2. Vague and overbroad language violates Article 19(1)(a)
3. Discussion, advocacy, and incitement to be distinguished
4. Only speech that incites imminent lawless action can be restricted

**Significance:**
- Protected online free speech
- Set standards for regulating internet speech
- Affirmed that same protections apply to digital medium
- Vagueness and overbreadth make law unconstitutional

---

### 4.6 State of Bombay v. R.M.D. Chamarbaugwala (AIR 1957 SC 699)

**Facts:**
- State of Bombay granted exclusive privilege to organize lottery to specific entity
- Other lottery operators challenged under Article 19(1)(g)
- State argued lottery is not "business" and even if it is, monopoly permitted under Article 19(6)

**Issue:**
1. Is lottery a "business" under Article 19(1)(g)?
2. Can State grant monopoly?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Monopoly grant invalid**
- Lottery is a business
- State can carry on business itself (monopoly by State)
- But State cannot grant monopoly to private entity
- Article 19(6) allows State monopoly, not private monopoly

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Article 19(6) permits State to carry on trade or business to exclusion of citizens, but does not permit State to grant monopoly to private parties.

**Significance:**
- Defined scope of Article 19(6)
- State can nationalize but cannot grant exclusive privilege to private entity

---

### 4.7 Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (AIR 1987 SC 748)

**Facts:**
- Three children expelled from school for not singing national anthem
- They were Jehovah's Witnesses (religious belief against singing)
- They stood respectfully but did not sing
- Expelled under Kerala Education Rules

**Issue:**
Does compulsion to sing national anthem violate Article 19(1)(a)?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**
- **Expulsion invalid**
- Freedom of speech includes right not to speak (negative aspect)
- Standing respectfully shows respect for anthem
- Compulsion to sing violates freedom of conscience and expression
- No law making singing compulsory

**Ratio Decidendi:**
Freedom of speech includes right to silence and right not to speak. Compulsion to express particular view violates Article 19(1)(a).

**Significance:**
- Recognized negative aspect of freedom of speech
- Balanced national interest with individual conscience
- Respect can be shown without compelled speech

## V. PRINCIPLES GOVERNING REASONABLE RESTRICTIONS

### 5.1 Established Principles

**1. Restrictions Must Be Reasonable:**
- Not arbitrary or excessive
- Proportionate to object
- Necessary for achieving object

**Case:** State of Madras v. V.G. Row (AIR 1952 SC 196) - Reasonableness to be determined objectively by courts.

**2. Specified Grounds Only:**
- Restrictions must fall within grounds mentioned in Article 19(2) to (6)
- Cannot impose restrictions on grounds not specified

**Case:** Romesh Thapar - Public safety not specified, hence restriction invalid.

**3. Procedure Established by Law:**
- Restrictions must be by law, not executive order
- Law must be valid and in force

**4. Content-Neutral:**
- Restriction should be content-neutral as far as possible
- Viewpoint-based discrimination impermissible

**5. Least Restrictive Alternative:**
- If object can be achieved by less restrictive means, that must be preferred
- Proportionality principle

**6. No Excessive Delegation:**
- Legislature cannot delegate essential legislative function
- Administrative authorities cannot be given unguided discretion

### 5.2 Overbreadth and Vagueness

**Overbreadth:**
- Law is overbroad if it restricts substantially more speech than necessary
- Example: Section 66A in Shreya Singhal case

**Vagueness:**
- Law is vague if ordinary person cannot understand what is prohibited
- Example: "Grossly offensive" in Section 66A

**Test:**
A person of ordinary intelligence must be able to understand what is prohibited. If not, law is void for vagueness.

## VI. BALANCING LIBERTY AND SOCIAL ORDER

### 6.1 Constitutional Philosophy

The Constitution balances:
- **Individual Liberty:** Freedom is rule, restriction is exception
- **Social Order:** Individual liberty cannot destroy social fabric
- **Public Interest:** Common good sometimes requires limitations on individual freedom

**Justice Holmes' Metaphor:**
> "The most stringent protection of free speech would not protect a man in falsely shouting fire in a theatre and causing a panic."

### 6.2 Three-Tier Test (American Jurisprudence, Applied in India)

**1. Content-Based Restrictions (Strict Scrutiny):**
- Restrictions based on content of speech
- Must pass strict scrutiny
- Must be narrowly tailored to serve compelling government interest

**2. Content-Neutral Restrictions (Intermediate Scrutiny):**
- Restrictions on time, place, manner of speech
- Must serve important government interest
- Must leave open alternative channels

**3. Commercial Speech (Rational Basis):**
- Subject to greater regulation
- Must serve legitimate government interest

### 6.3 Clear and Present Danger Test

Adopted from American jurisprudence:
- Speech can be restricted only if it creates clear and present danger of substantive evil
- Mere tendency to cause public disorder is not enough
- Danger must be imminent and serious

**Applied in:** Shreya Singhal case - Discussion, advocacy, and incitement distinguished.

## VII. CONTEMPORARY CHALLENGES

### 7.1 Digital Age Issues

**1. Social Media Regulation:**
- Fake news and misinformation
- Hate speech online
- Balance between free speech and regulation

**2. Intermediary Liability:**
- IT Rules, 2021
- Content moderation
- Safe harbor provisions

**3. Internet Shutdowns:**
- Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020) - Internet restrictions must be proportionate and time-bound
- Indefinite suspensions violate Article 19(1)(a)

### 7.2 National Security vs Freedom

**1. Sedition Law (Section 124A IPC):**
- Challenged as colonial-era law
- Courts have narrowed interpretation
- Requires violence or incitement to violence

**2. UAPA and Anti-Terror Laws:**
- Preventive detention provisions
- Restrictions on speech supporting terrorism
- Balance between security and liberty

**3. Official Secrets Act:**
- Government information and transparency
- Right to information vs official secrecy

## VIII. CONCLUSION

**Article 19** is the bulwark of Indian democracy, guaranteeing six essential freedoms to all citizens. These freedoms are fundamental for individual dignity, democratic governance, and social progress. However, recognizing that absolute freedom leads to anarchy, the Constitution permits **reasonable restrictions** on these freedoms in public interest.

The key lies in the word **"reasonable"** - restrictions must be proportionate, necessary, and narrowly tailored to achieve legitimate government objectives. Courts play the crucial role of testing the reasonableness of restrictions and ensuring that the government does not transgress constitutional limits.

Through landmark judgments from **Romesh Thapar** to **Shreya Singhal**, the Supreme Court has evolved sophisticated jurisprudence balancing liberty and order. The Court has recognized:
- Freedom of press as essential for democracy
- Interconnection between Articles 14, 19, and 21
- Need for narrow tailoring of restrictions
- Protection against vague and overbroad laws
- Negative aspect of freedom (right to remain silent)

In the digital age, new challenges emerge - social media regulation, fake news, internet shutdowns, surveillance. The constitutional framework remains robust, but constant judicial vigilance is needed to ensure that:
- **Freedom remains the rule, restriction the exception**
- **Restrictions are truly reasonable and proportionate**
- **Democratic values are protected while maintaining public order**

As the Supreme Court observed in **Shreya Singhal**:
> "Liberty and freedom of expression are the pillars on which a democratic society stands. Without them, the edifice will crumble."

**Article 19, with its doctrine of reasonable restrictions, continues to be the guardian of Indian democracy and individual liberty.**
