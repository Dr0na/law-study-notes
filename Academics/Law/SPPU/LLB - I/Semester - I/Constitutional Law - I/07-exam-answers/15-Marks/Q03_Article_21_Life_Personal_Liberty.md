# 15-MARK ANSWER

## Q.4) Explain the concept of right to life and Personal liberty under the Indian Constitution.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Constitutional Provision

**Article 21** of the Constitution of India is the most expansive and dynamic Fundamental Right, often described as the "heart of Fundamental Rights." It provides:

> **"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."**

This seemingly simple provision has been transformed through progressive judicial interpretation into a reservoir of multiple substantive rights, making it the most litigated and judicially developed provision in the Constitution.

### 1.2 Scope and Applicability

**"Any person"** - Article 21 applies to:
- **All persons** - citizens and non-citizens alike
- Within the territory of India
- Both natural and juristic persons (in certain contexts)

**Two Components:**
1. **Right to Life**
2. **Right to Personal Liberty**

### 1.3 Significance

Article 21 is significant because:
1. **Universal protection** - Not limited to citizens
2. **Expansive interpretation** - Includes numerous derived rights
3. **Golden Triangle** - Forms core with Articles 14 and 19
4. **Judicial creativity** - Most judicially developed right
5. **Social transformation** - Used for protecting marginalized sections

---

## II. EVOLUTION OF ARTICLE 21 - TWO DISTINCT ERAS

### 2.1 Pre-Maneka Gandhi Era: Narrow Interpretation (1950-1978)

#### A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (AIR 1950 SC 27)

**Facts:**
- A.K. Gopalan, a communist leader, was detained under Preventive Detention Act, 1950
- Challenged detention as violating Articles 13, 19, 21, and 22
- Argued that "procedure established by law" must be just, fair, and reasonable

**Issues:**
1. What is meaning of "procedure established by law"?
2. Must the procedure be just and fair?
3. What is relationship between Article 19 and Article 21?

**Judgment:**

**Held (Majority View):**

**1. On "Procedure Established by Law":**
- Means procedure prescribed by law made by competent legislature
- Law need not be just, fair, or reasonable
- If procedure prescribed by valid law is followed, deprivation is constitutional
- **Indian Constitution adopted "procedure established by law," not "due process of law" (American concept)**

**2. On Relationship between Articles 19 and 21:**
- Articles 19 and 21 are **mutually exclusive**
- Article 21 is complete code for deprivation of life and liberty
- Article 19 does not apply when Article 21 applies
- Preventive detention laws only need to satisfy Article 21, not Article 19

**3. On Preventive Detention:**
- Preventive Detention Act is valid
- Satisfies Article 21
- Detention upheld

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. "Procedure established by law" means any procedure prescribed by valid law
2. Law need not be substantively just or fair, only procedurally valid
3. Articles 19 and 21 are mutually exclusive

**Impact:**
- **Narrow interpretation** adopted
- Limited protection under Article 21
- Executive had wide powers
- Personal liberty could be taken away easily if procedure followed
- No requirement of substantive fairness

**Dissent (Justice Fazal Ali):**
- Argued for broader interpretation
- Procedure should be fair and reasonable
- But majority did not accept

**Criticism:**
- Made Article 21 weak
- Allowed arbitrary laws as long as procedurally valid
- Did not protect substantive liberty

---

### 2.2 Maneka Gandhi Era: Paradigm Shift to Expansive Interpretation (1978 onwards)

#### Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (AIR 1978 SC 597) - LANDMARK JUDGMENT

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi, journalist and politician, had valid passport
- On 2 July 1977, Regional Passport Officer impounded her passport
- Under Section 10(3)(c) of Passports Act: "In the interest of general public"
- **No reasons communicated**
- **No opportunity of hearing given**
- Challenged under Articles 14, 19, and 21

**Issues:**
1. What is scope of "procedure established by law" under Article 21?
2. Must procedure be fair, just, and reasonable?
3. What is relationship between Articles 14, 19, and 21?
4. Is right to travel abroad a Fundamental Right?

**Judgment:**

**HELD:**

**1. Revolutionary Interpretation of "Procedure Established by Law":**

Justice Bhagwati (writing for majority):
> **"The principle of reasonableness which legally as well as philosophically is an essential element of equality or non-arbitrariness is projected by Article 14 and it must characterise the procedure contemplated by Article 21."**

**Key Principles:**

- "Procedure established by law" must be **right, just, and fair**
- Not sufficient that law is validly made by competent legislature
- Procedure must also be **reasonable** (Article 14 test)
- **Partially overruled A.K. Gopalan**

**2. Golden Triangle Doctrine Established:**

> **"Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not mutually exclusive. They form a 'Golden Triangle' - they overlap and are supplementary to each other."**

**Meaning:**
- Any law depriving life or liberty must satisfy:
  - **Article 14:** Not arbitrary, reasonable
  - **Article 19:** Restrictions reasonable (if applicable)
  - **Article 21:** Procedure fair, just, reasonable

- All three articles must be read together
- Complementary, not exclusive

**3. "Due Process" Introduced (Through Interpretive Route):**

- Though Constitution says "procedure established by law" (not "due process")
- By requiring procedure to be fair, just, and reasonable, Court introduced essence of American "due process"
- Substantive and procedural due process

**4. Principles of Natural Justice Applied:**

For procedure to be fair:
- **Audi alteram partem** - Right to be heard
- **Nemo judex in causa sua** - No bias
- Reasons must be communicated
- Opportunity to challenge decision
- Proportionality

**5. Right to Travel Abroad:**

- Held that right to go abroad is part of personal liberty under Article 21
- Passport impoundment deprived personal liberty
- Since no hearing given, procedure unfair
- Violated Article 21

**Ratio Decidendi:**

1. **Procedure in Article 21 must be just, fair, and reasonable**
2. **Articles 14, 19, and 21 are interconnected** (Golden Triangle)
3. **Any law affecting liberty must satisfy all three Articles**
4. **Natural justice is constitutional requirement**
5. **Right to go abroad is part of personal liberty**

**Significance:**

This is arguably the **most important constitutional judgment after Kesavananda Bharati**:

1. **Transformed Article 21** from procedural safeguard to substantive right
2. **Empowered courts** to examine fairness of law, not just its validity
3. **Foundation for expansive interpretation** of Article 21
4. **Introduced due process** without amending Constitution
5. **Balanced individual liberty with State power**
6. **Set stage for judicial activism** in protecting rights

## III. MEANING OF "LIFE" UNDER ARTICLE 21

### 3.1 Beyond Mere Animal Existence

**Initial Understanding:** "Life" meant physical existence - protection from death.

**Expanded Interpretation:** "Life" means much more than mere animal existence.

#### Francis Coralie Mullin v. Administrator, Union Territory of Delhi (AIR 1981 SC 746)

**Facts:**
- Francis Coralie Mullin, a foreign national, detained in Tihar Jail
- Challenged inhuman conditions of detention
- Solitary confinement, no facilities

**Held:**

Justice Bhagwati:
> **"The right to life includes the right to live with human dignity and all that goes along with it, namely, the bare necessaries of life such as adequate nutrition, clothing and shelter over the head and facilities for reading, writing and expressing oneself in diverse forms, freely moving about and commingling with fellow human beings."**

**Principle Established:**
- **Life = Life with human dignity**
- Not just survival, but dignified existence
- Includes basic necessities and facilities for self-development

**Impact:**
- Redefined "life" expansively
- Foundation for reading multiple rights into Article 21

---

### 3.2 Right to Livelihood

#### Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (AIR 1985 SC 3)

**Facts:**
- Pavement dwellers in Mumbai faced eviction
- Municipal Corporation sought to demolish their hutments
- Challenged as violating Article 21
- Argued: Eviction would deprive livelihood; livelihood essential for life

**Issue:**
Is right to livelihood part of right to life under Article 21?

**Held:**

**Right to livelihood is integral part of right to life:**
> "No person can live without the means of living, that is, the means of livelihood. If the right to livelihood is not treated as a part of the constitutional right to life, the easiest way of depriving a person of his right to life would be to deprive him of his means of livelihood to the point of abrogation."

**But Not Absolute:**
- Right to livelihood can be curtailed by "procedure established by law"
- Must satisfy Article 21 requirements (fair, just, reasonable)
- State can regulate in public interest

**Orders:**
- Directed corporation to follow proper procedure before eviction
- Cannot evict arbitrarily
- Must provide notice and hearing

**Significance:**
- Expanded "life" to include economic rights
- Social welfare dimension of Article 21
- Protection to most vulnerable sections

---

### 3.3 Right to Shelter

#### Chameli Singh v. State of UP (AIR 1996 SC 1051)

**Held:**
- **Right to shelter is part of Article 21**
- Right to life includes right to food, water, decent environment, and reasonable accommodation
- Basic necessities for survival

---

### 3.4 Right to Food

#### People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL) v. Union of India (2001) - "Right to Food Case"

**Facts:**
- Despite food stocks in warehouses, people dying of starvation
- Inefficient Public Distribution System
- PIL filed for enforcement of food security

**Held:**
- **Right to food flows from Article 21**
- State has obligation to prevent starvation
- Court issued comprehensive directions for food security programs

**Orders:**
- Mid-day meal scheme in schools mandatory
- Anganwadi services
- PDS reforms
- Food security measures

**Continuing Case:**
- Court continues to monitor through commissioners
- Regular reports on implementation

---

## IV. MEANING OF "PERSONAL LIBERTY" UNDER ARTICLE 21

### 4.1 Expansive Meaning

**Personal Liberty** is not defined in Constitution. Courts have given it widest possible interpretation.

#### Kharak Singh v. State of UP (AIR 1963 SC 1295)

Justice Subba Rao (in dissent, later accepted):
> "Personal liberty is used in Article 21 as a compendious term to include within itself all the varieties of rights which go to make up the personal liberties of man."

**Includes:**
- Freedom from physical restraint
- Freedom of movement
- Privacy
- Dignity
- All freedoms that constitute personal liberty

---

### 4.2 Right to Privacy

#### Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (AIR 2017 SC 4161) - 9-Judge Bench

**Facts:**
- Challenge to Aadhaar scheme
- Collection and storage of biometric data
- Question: Is privacy a Fundamental Right?

**Held:**

**Privacy is intrinsic to right to life and personal liberty under Article 21**

**Overruled:**
- M.P. Sharma v. Satish Chandra (8-judge bench) - Had held privacy not FR
- Kharak Singh (6-judge bench) - Majority had denied privacy as FR

**Three Dimensions of Privacy:**

1. **Informational Privacy** - Control over personal data
2. **Bodily Privacy** - Autonomy over one's body
3. **Decisional Privacy** - Freedom to make personal choices

**Not Absolute - Three-Fold Test:**

Privacy can be restricted if:
1. **Legality** - By law (not arbitrary)
2. **Legitimate Goal** - Serves legitimate State aim
3. **Proportionality** - Restriction proportionate to need

**Significance:**
- Landmark judgment recognizing privacy as Fundamental Right
- Settled decades-old controversy
- Foundation for data protection legislation
- Relevant in digital age

---

## V. EXPANDED INTERPRETATION - DERIVED RIGHTS UNDER ARTICLE 21

Post-Maneka Gandhi, Supreme Court has read numerous rights into Article 21. These are now recognized as part of right to life and personal liberty:

### 5.1 Right to Speedy Trial

#### Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar (AIR 1979 SC 1369)

**Facts:**
- Thousands of undertrials in Bihar jails
- Many detained for periods exceeding maximum sentence for alleged offense
- Most were poor, illiterate, unaware of rights
- Supreme Court took suo motu cognizance

**Held:**

**Right to speedy trial is Fundamental Right under Article 21:**
- Unreasonable delay in trial violates Article 21
- Prolonged detention without trial is deprivation of personal liberty
- Cannot continue indefinitely

**Speedy trial includes:**
- Trial within reasonable time
- No unnecessary adjournments
- Expeditious disposal
- Right to bail if trial delayed

**Orders:**
- Immediate release of undertrials detained beyond maximum sentence
- Legal aid at State expense
- Speedy disposal of pending cases

**Significance:**
- Reformed criminal justice system
- Protected undertrial prisoners
- Made legal aid system functional

---

### 5.2 Right to Free Legal Aid

#### M.H. Hoskot v. State of Maharashtra (AIR 1978 SC 1548)

**Facts:**
- Convict unable to afford lawyer for filing appeal
- Requested free legal aid but denied

**Held:**

**Right to free legal aid is Fundamental Right:**
- Flows from Articles 21 and 39A
- Access to justice is essence of Article 21
- Poor cannot be denied justice due to poverty
- State must provide legal aid free of cost

**Article 39A (DPSP):**
"State shall secure that operation of legal system promotes justice on basis of equal opportunity and shall provide free legal aid by suitable legislation or schemes."

**Integration:**
- DPSP (Article 39A) read into FR (Article 21)
- Made legal aid justiciable
- State's duty, not charity

---

### 5.3 Right to Health and Medical Care

#### Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v. State of West Bengal (AIR 1996 SC 2426)

**Facts:**
- Railway employee injured in accident near Howrah
- Denied emergency treatment by multiple government hospitals
- Suffered permanent disability due to delayed treatment

**Held:**

**Right to health is Fundamental Right under Article 21:**
- Preservation of life is paramount duty of State
- Government hospitals cannot refuse emergency treatment
- Failure to provide timely medical care violates Article 21

**State's Obligations:**
- Provide adequate medical facilities
- Emergency treatment without insisting on payment
- Compensation for medical negligence

**Ordered compensation** to victim for State's failure.

**Significance:**
- Healthcare made Fundamental Right
- Government hospitals accountable
- Right to emergency treatment non-negotiable

---

### 5.4 Right to Clean and Healthy Environment

#### Multiple M.C. Mehta Cases

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak Case) (AIR 1987 SC 1086):**

**Facts:**
- Shriram Foods & Fertilizers plant in Delhi leaked oleum gas
- Several people killed and injured

**Held:**
- **Absolute Liability Doctrine** evolved
- Enterprise engaged in hazardous activity absolutely liable
- No exceptions (Act of God, Third Party)
- Compensation correlated with magnitude of enterprise

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Taj Mahal Case) (1997):**
- Pollution threatening Taj Mahal
- Ordered closure/relocation of polluting industries
- Taj Trapezium Zone created

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Vehicular Pollution Case) (1998):**
- Air pollution in Delhi
- Ordered:
  - Introduction of CNG buses
  - Phasing out of old commercial vehicles
  - Catalytic converters mandatory
  - Lead-free petrol

**Principle Established:**
> **"Right to life includes right to live in pollution-free environment. Right to wholesome environment is integral to Article 21."**

**Significance:**
- Environmental jurisprudence developed
- Court can issue continuing mandamus
- Implementation monitored
- Proactive judicial role

---

### 5.5 Right to Education

#### Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka (AIR 1992 SC 1858)

**Facts:**
- Private medical college demanded huge capitation fee
- Student unable to pay challenged as violating right to education

**Held:**
- **Right to education flows from Article 21**
- Charging capitation fee denies right to education
- Violates right to life
- State cannot deny education based on ability to pay

#### Unnikrishnan v. State of AP (AIR 1993 SC 2178)

**Held:**
- Right to free education up to 14 years is Fundamental Right
- Beyond 14 years, subject to State's limits

**Result:**
Led to **86th Constitutional Amendment (2002)** inserting **Article 21A:**
> "The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years."

**Right to Education Act, 2009** enacted to implement Article 21A.

---

### 5.6 Right Against Custodial Violence

#### D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (AIR 1997 SC 610)

**Facts:**
- Custodial deaths and torture rampant in police stations
- PIL filed by D.K. Basu, Executive Chairman of Legal Aid Services
- Sought guidelines to prevent custodial violence

**Held:**

**Custodial violence violates Article 21:**
- Right to life includes right to live with human dignity
- Custodial torture is anti-thesis of Article 21
- Police must follow strict guidelines

**11 Guidelines Laid Down (D.K. Basu Guidelines):**

**During Arrest:**
1. Personnel conducting arrest must bear accurate, visible identification
2. Arrest memo prepared, witnessed by member of family/respectable person
3. Person arrested has right to inform friend/relative about arrest and place of detention
4. Entry in diary at place of detention showing time of arrest, name of informant

**During Interrogation:**
5. Person arrested must be informed of right to consult lawyer
6. Interrogation conducted in place with CCTV or visible to public
7. Medical examination within 48 hours of detention
8. Copies of documents to arrested person

**Remedies:**
9. Non-compliance renders officials liable for departmental action and contempt
10. Compensation for violation
11. These are constitutional requirements, not just guidelines

**Significance:**
- Most comprehensive guidelines against custodial violence
- Made police accountable
- Reduced custodial deaths significantly
- Enforceable through contempt proceedings

---

### 5.7 Right to Reputation

#### Umesh Kumar v. State of Andhra Pradesh (AIR 2013 SC 1671)

**Held:**
- Right to reputation is part of Article 21
- Defamation affects dignity and honor
- But right to reputation must be balanced with freedom of speech (Article 19(1)(a))

---

### 5.8 Right Against Torture

#### Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa (AIR 1993 SC 1960)

**Held:**
- Right not to be tortured is part of Article 21
- Custodial death attracts compensation
- Awarded compensation to mother whose son died in police custody

---

### 5.9 Right to Die with Dignity (Passive Euthanasia)

#### Common Cause v. Union of India (AIR 2018 SC 1665)

**Facts:**
- NGO Common Cause filed PIL
- Issue: Can terminally ill person refuse life support?
- Question of passive euthanasia and living will

**Held:**

**Right to die with dignity is part of Article 21:**
- Right to life includes right to live with dignity
- Also includes right to die with dignity
- Passive euthanasia permitted with safeguards
- Living will recognized

**Guidelines:**
- Living will must be executed before two witnesses
- Preserved with approved authorities
- Medical board to verify condition
- Safeguards to prevent abuse

**Distinction:**
- **Passive euthanasia** (withholding treatment) - ALLOWED
- **Active euthanasia** (actively causing death) - NOT ALLOWED

**Significance:**
- Recognized personal autonomy
- Dignity in life and death
- Balanced right to life with quality of life

---

## VI. MEANING OF "PERSONAL LIBERTY"

### Widest Amplitude

**Personal liberty** under Article 21 has been given the widest possible interpretation.

**Includes:**
1. Freedom from physical restraint (arrest, detention)
2. Freedom of movement
3. Privacy of person
4. Freedom to travel
5. Freedom to live with dignity
6. Freedom from torture
7. All freedoms that constitute liberty of person

**Not Limited To:**
- Just freedom from arrest or detention
- Physical liberty alone

**Extends To:**
- Mental freedom
- Privacy
- Dignity
- Autonomy

---

## VII. ARTICLES 14, 19, AND 21 - THE GOLDEN TRIANGLE

### Interconnection Established in Maneka Gandhi

**Before Maneka Gandhi:**
- Articles 14, 19, and 21 considered separate
- Mutually exclusive

**After Maneka Gandhi:**
- **Form "Golden Triangle"**
- Overlapping and supplementary
- Must be read together

### How They Interrelate

**Article 14:** General equality clause
- No arbitrary action
- Reasonableness essential

**Article 19:** Specific freedoms
- Six freedoms guaranteed
- Subject to reasonable restrictions

**Article 21:** Life and personal liberty
- Procedure must be fair, just, reasonable
- Must satisfy Articles 14 and 19

**Practical Application:**

Any law depriving life or liberty must:
1. **Not be arbitrary** (Article 14)
2. **Have reasonable restrictions** (Article 19)
3. **Follow fair procedure** (Article 21)

**Example:**
Preventive detention law must:
- Have objective criteria (not arbitrary) - Article 14
- Be reasonable restriction on freedom of movement (Article 19(1)(d))
- Provide procedural safeguards (Article 21 and 22)

**Significance:**
- Comprehensive protection
- No loopholes
- Substantive and procedural justice

---

## VIII. ARTICLE 21 AND ARTICLE 22

### Complementary Provisions

**Article 22** provides specific safeguards against arrest and detention:

**Article 22(1) and (2) - Protection Against Arrest:**
1. Right to be informed of grounds of arrest
2. Right to consult and be defended by lawyer
3. Production before magistrate within 24 hours

**Article 22(3) to (7) - Preventive Detention:**
Detailed safeguards including:
- Advisory Board review within 3 months
- Right to make representation
- Maximum detention period

**Relationship:**
- Article 22 is **special provision** for arrest and detention
- Article 21 is **general provision**
- Both must be satisfied
- Article 22 supplements Article 21

---

## IX. LIMITATIONS AND EXCEPTIONS

### Article 21 is Not Absolute

**1. Procedure Established by Law:**
- Person CAN be deprived of life or liberty
- If proper procedure followed
- Procedure must be fair, just, reasonable

**2. Death Penalty:**
- Constitution permits death penalty
- But only in **"rarest of rare"** cases
- Must satisfy due process
- **Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)** - Rarest of rare doctrine

**3. Preventive Detention:**
- Article 22 permits preventive detention
- With strict safeguards
- For national security, public order

**4. During Emergency:**
- Article 21 enforcement can be suspended
- But **44th Amendment (1978)** restricted this
- Even during emergency, Article 21 cannot be completely suspended

**5. Life Imprisonment:**
- Valid deprivation of liberty
- If conviction follows due process

## X. PROCEDURE ESTABLISHED BY LAW - POST MANEKA GANDHI

### Requirements for Valid Procedure

**Procedure must be:**

**1. Prescribed by Law:**
- Must be enacted by competent legislature
- Not executive fiat

**2. Just:**
- Morally right
- Not oppressive

**3. Fair:**
- Reasonable opportunity of being heard
- Unbiased decision-maker
- Reasons communicated

**4. Reasonable:**
- Not arbitrary
- Proportionate to object
- Necessary, not merely convenient

**5. Satisfy Article 14:**
- Not discriminatory
- Not arbitrary

**6. Satisfy Article 19:**
- If freedom under Article 19 affected, restrictions must be reasonable

## XII. CONCLUSION

**Article 21** represents the finest example of judicial creativity and constitutional evolution. From the narrow, restrictive interpretation in **A.K. Gopalan (1950)** to the expansive, life-affirming vision in **Maneka Gandhi (1978)** and subsequent cases, Article 21 has been transformed into the most powerful and dynamic Fundamental Right.

**The journey of Article 21 shows:**

**From Procedure to Substance:**
- Initially: Procedural safeguard (just follow the law)
- Now: Substantive right (law must be fair, just, reasonable)

**From Survival to Dignity:**
- Initially: Life = mere existence
- Now: Life = life with human dignity

**From Negative to Positive:**
- Initially: State shall not deprive without procedure
- Now: State has positive duty to ensure dignified life

**The "Golden Triangle" doctrine** (Articles 14, 19, 21) ensures that any law affecting life or liberty must pass the triple test of:
- Non-arbitrariness (Article 14)
- Reasonableness (Article 19)
- Fairness (Article 21)

**Through landmark judgments:**
- **Maneka Gandhi** - Transformed interpretation
- **Francis Coralie** - Life with dignity
- **Olga Tellis** - Livelihood is life
- **Hussainara Khatoon** - Speedy trial
- **D.K. Basu** - Protection against custodial violence
- **M.C. Mehta** - Environmental rights
- **Puttaswamy** - Privacy as fundamental right
- **Common Cause** - Death with dignity

The Supreme Court has expanded Article 21 to include:
- Livelihood, shelter, food, clean environment
- Speedy trial, legal aid, fair trial
- Health, education
- Privacy
- Die with dignity
- And many more...

**Article 21 continues to evolve**, responding to emerging challenges and ensuring that the constitutional promise of life and liberty is not merely formal but substantive and meaningful for every person in India.

As **Justice Bhagwati** beautifully articulated in **Francis Coralie Mullin case**:
> "The right to life enshrined in Article 21 cannot be restricted to mere animal existence. It means something much more than just physical survival... The right to life includes the right to live with human dignity and all that goes along with it."

**Article 21 is not just a provision - it is a vision of constitutional democracy where every person lives with dignity, freedom, and full development of personality.**
