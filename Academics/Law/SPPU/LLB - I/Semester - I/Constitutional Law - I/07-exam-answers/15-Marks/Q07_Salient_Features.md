# 15-MARK ANSWER

## Q.1) Explain the salient features of the Indian Constitution.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

The **Constitution of India**, adopted on **November 26, 1949** and enforced on **January 26, 1950**, is the supreme law of the land. It is a comprehensive document that establishes the framework for political principles, structure of government, procedures, powers, and duties of government institutions, and sets out Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles, and duties of citizens.

The Indian Constitution is **unique** in many respects. While it borrowed provisions from various constitutions worldwide, it adapted them to Indian conditions, creating a distinctive constitutional framework. It is the **longest written constitution in the world**, currently containing **448 Articles, 12 Schedules, 25 Parts, and 5 Appendices**.

The salient features reflect India's historical experience, geographical vastness, cultural diversity, and aspirations for a just and democratic society.

---

## II. SALIENT FEATURES OF THE INDIAN CONSTITUTION

### FEATURE 1: Lengthiest Written Constitution in the World

**Original (1950):**
- 395 Articles
- 8 Schedules
- 22 Parts

**Current (2024):**
- 448 Articles
- 12 Schedules
- 25 Parts
- 5 Appendices

**Reasons for Length:**

**A. Geographical Factors:**
- Vast territory
- Large population
- Continental dimensions

**B. Diversity:**
- Multiple religions (Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, Jainism)
- Numerous languages (22 in Eighth Schedule)
- Diverse cultures and customs
- Needed detailed provisions for accommodation

**C. Historical Factors:**
- Experience of British rule
- Government of India Act, 1935 incorporated
- Partition necessitated clear provisions
- Colonial legacy of detailed administration

**D. Single Constitution:**
- Unlike USA (federal Constitution + state constitutions)
- India has one Constitution for Union and States
- Includes matters that could be in ordinary laws

**E. Borrowed Provisions:**
- Incorporated best practices from various constitutions
- Detailed provisions from multiple sources

**Dr. Ambedkar's Defense:**
> "Nobody holds any patent rights in constitutional ideas."

---

### FEATURE 2: Drawn from Various Sources

The Indian Constitution borrowed provisions from multiple sources but adapted them to Indian conditions:

| Source | Provisions Borrowed |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Britain** | Parliamentary system, Rule of law, Legislative procedure, Bicameral Parliament |
| **USA** | Fundamental Rights, Judicial review, Independence of judiciary, Preamble language |
| **Ireland** | Directive Principles of State Policy, Nomination to Rajya Sabha |
| **Canada** | Federation with strong center, Distribution of powers, Residuary powers with center |
| **Australia** | Concurrent List, Freedom of trade and commerce |
| **Germany (Weimar)** | Suspension of Fundamental Rights during emergency |
| **USSR (Soviet)** | Fundamental Duties, Ideals of justice in Preamble |
| **France** | Principles of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity in Preamble |
| **South Africa** | Amendment procedure, Election of Rajya Sabha members |
| **Japan** | Procedure established by law (Article 21) |
| **Govt of India Act, 1935** | Federal scheme, Office of Governor, Judiciary, Public Service Commissions, Emergency provisions |

**Uniqueness:** Not a mere copy - provisions adapted and modified to suit Indian genius and needs.

---

### FEATURE 3: Federal System with Unitary Bias (Quasi-Federal)

**K.C. Wheare's Description:** "Quasi-federal"  
**Granville Austin's Description:** "Cooperative federalism"

**Federal Features:**

**A. Written Constitution**
**B. Division of Powers:**
- Union List (97 subjects)
- State List (66 subjects)
- Concurrent List (47 subjects)

**C. Independent Judiciary:**
- Supreme Court
- Judicial review power

**D. Bicameral Legislature:**
- Lok Sabha (people's house)
- Rajya Sabha (states' house)

**E. Rigid Constitution:**
- Special majority for amendments
- Some provisions require state ratification

**Unitary Features:**

**A. Strong Center:**
- Residuary powers with Union (Article 248)
- More subjects in Union List
- Center can legislate on State subjects in national interest (Article 249)

**B. Single Citizenship:**
- Only Indian citizenship
- No state citizenship (unlike USA)

**C. Integrated Judiciary:**
- Single system for Union and States
- Not separate like USA

**D. Governor Appointed:**
- By President (not elected)
- Acts as agent of center

**E. Emergency Provisions:**
- During emergency, federation becomes unitary
- Center's control increases

**F. All India Services:**
- IAS, IPS, IFS
- Serve both Union and States

**G. Parliament's Power:**
- Can form new states
- Alter boundaries
- Article 3

**H. Financial Dependence:**
- States depend on Union for funds
- Grants-in-aid

**Conclusion:** Federal in structure, Unitary in spirit.

**During Normal Times:** Federal  
**During Emergency:** Unitary

---

### FEATURE 4: Parliamentary Form of Government

**India adopted British Parliamentary system**, not American Presidential system.

**Features:**

**A. Nominal Executive:**
- **President:** Constitutional head
- Nominal powers
- Acts on advice of Council of Ministers
- Like British Queen/King

**B. Real Executive:**
- **Prime Minister:** Head of government
- Real power holder
- Leader of majority party in Lok Sabha

**C. Collective Responsibility:**
- Council of Ministers collectively responsible to Lok Sabha (Article 75(3))
- Vote of no confidence can remove government

**D. Membership of Legislature:**
- Ministers must be members of Parliament (Article 75(5))
- If not, must become member within 6 months
- Accountable in Parliament

**E. Leadership of Prime Minister:**
- Primus inter pares (first among equals)
- Leads Council of Ministers
- Coordination and decision-making

**F. Dissolution:**
- Lok Sabha can be dissolved
- Fresh elections

**Advantages:**
- Harmonious relations between executive and legislature
- Responsible government
- Flexible and responsive

---

### FEATURE 5: Fundamental Rights (Articles 12-35)

**Six Categories:**

**1. Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)**
**2. Right to Freedom (Articles 19-22)**
**3. Right against Exploitation (Articles 23-24)**
**4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25-28)**
**5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29-30)**
**6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Articles 32-35)**

**Features:**
- **Justiciable** - Enforceable through courts
- Against State (primarily)
- Subject to reasonable restrictions
- Core of Constitution

**Article 32:** Right to move Supreme Court - "Heart and soul" (Dr. Ambedkar)

**Note:** Right to Property (Article 31) deleted in 1978 by 44th Amendment; now legal right under Article 300A, not Fundamental Right.

---

### FEATURE 6: Directive Principles of State Policy (Articles 36-51)

**Nature:**
- Non-justiciable (not enforceable in courts)
- Guidelines for State policy
- Positive duties of State

**Article 37:** Fundamental in governance though not enforceable.

**Classification:**

**A. Socialist Principles:**
- Social and economic justice
- Articles 38, 39, 41-43

**B. Gandhian Principles:**
- Village Panchayats, cottage industries, prohibition
- Articles 40, 43, 46-48

**C. Liberal-Intellectual Principles:**
- UCC, education, environment, international peace
- Articles 44, 45, 48A, 49-51

**Relationship with FR:**
- FR and DPSP complementary
- Harmony between both is **basic feature** (Minerva Mills, 1980)

---

### FEATURE 7: Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

**Added by:** 42nd Amendment (1976)

**Nature:**
- Non-justiciable
- Moral obligations
- Civic responsibilities

**11 Fundamental Duties:**
1. Respect Constitution, national flag, anthem
2. Cherish noble ideals of freedom struggle
3. Protect sovereignty, unity, integrity
4. Defend country
5. Promote harmony and brotherhood
6. Preserve composite culture
7. Protect environment
8. Develop scientific temper and humanism
9. Safeguard public property
10. Strive for excellence
11. Provide opportunities for education to children (added by 86th Amendment, 2002)

**Purpose:** Balance rights with duties

---

### FEATURE 8: Independent Judiciary

**Features:**

**A. Separation from Executive:**
- Judges appointed independently
- Executive cannot interfere

**B. Security of Tenure:**
- Cannot be removed easily
- Removal only by impeachment

**C. Fixed Salaries:**
- Cannot be reduced
- Financial security

**D. Power of Judicial Review:**
- Can declare laws unconstitutional (Article 13)
- Strike down executive actions
- Protect Fundamental Rights

**E. Single Integrated System:**
- Supreme Court (apex)
- High Courts (states)
- Subordinate courts
- All function as one system

**F. Contempt Power:**
- Can punish for contempt
- Maintain authority and dignity

**Significance:**
- Guardian of Constitution
- Protector of Fundamental Rights
- Interpreter of Constitution

---

### FEATURE 9: Secular State

**"Secular" added to Preamble:** 42nd Amendment (1976)

**But India was secular from inception.**

**Meaning:**
- **No state religion** (unlike Pakistan - Islamic Republic)
- **Equal treatment of all religions**
- **State maintains equal distance** from all religions

**Constitutional Provisions:**
- Articles 25-28: Freedom of religion
- Article 15: No discrimination based on religion
- Article 16: No discrimination in public employment
- Article 325: No separate electorate based on religion

**Indian Secularism Unique:**
- Not complete separation (like Western secularism)
- Equal distance with power to reform
- State can intervene in religious matters for social welfare
- Example: Abolition of untouchability (Article 17)

**Case Law:** **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** - Secularism is **basic feature**.

---

### FEATURE 10: Single Citizenship (Article 5-11)

**India follows single citizenship:**
- Only **Indian citizenship**
- No separate state citizenship

**Contrast:** USA has dual citizenship (Federal + State)

**Advantages:**
1. **Promotes national unity**
2. **No domicile-based discrimination**
3. **Uniform rights throughout India**
4. **Sense of belonging to one nation**

**Article 19(1)(e):** Right to reside anywhere in India

**Article 9:** Dual citizenship **prohibited** - Acquiring foreign citizenship terminates Indian citizenship

---

### FEATURE 11: Universal Adult Suffrage (Article 326)

> "Elections to the House of the People and to the Legislative Assembly of every State shall be on the basis of adult suffrage."

**Features:**
- Every citizen above **18 years** can vote (originally 21, reduced by 61st Amendment, 1989)
- **"One person, one vote, one value"**
- No discrimination based on religion, race, caste, sex, or wealth

**Significance:**
- Political equality
- Democratic participation
- Empowerment of masses
- Bold decision for newly independent country with high illiteracy

---

### FEATURE 12: Blend of Rigidity and Flexibility

**Rigid Constitution:** Difficult to amend (USA)  
**Flexible Constitution:** Easy to amend (Britain - no written constitution)

**Indian Constitution: Both**

**Article 368:** Three types of amendments

**Type 1: Simple Majority (Flexible)**
- Like ordinary legislation
- Example: Formation of new states, delimitation

**Type 2: Special Majority (Moderately Rigid)**
- 2/3rd majority of members present and voting
- PLUS absolute majority of total membership
- Example: Fundamental Rights, DPSP, language provisions

**Type 3: Special Majority + State Ratification (Rigid)**
- Special majority in Parliament
- PLUS ratification by 50% of states
- Example: Election of President, distribution of legislative powers

**Significance:**
- **Flexible:** Can adapt to changing needs
- **Rigid:** Core features protected from hasty changes
- Balance between stability and change

---

### FEATURE 13: Synthesis of Parliamentary Sovereignty and Judicial Supremacy

**British System:** Parliamentary sovereignty
- Parliament is supreme
- No judicial review

**American System:** Judicial supremacy  
- Supreme Court can strike down laws
- Strong judicial review

**Indian System: Balance**

**Parliament:**
- Wide legislative powers
- Can amend Constitution (except basic structure)

**Judiciary:**
- Can declare laws unconstitutional
- Judicial review power
- Protects Fundamental Rights
- Interprets Constitution

**Result:**
- Neither absolute parliamentary sovereignty
- Nor complete judicial supremacy
- **Checks and balances**

**Basic Structure Doctrine:** Parliament cannot destroy Constitution's core - Judicial review ensures this.

---

### FEATURE 14: Emergency Provisions (Articles 352, 356, 360)

**Three Types:**

**1. National Emergency (Article 352):**
- War, external aggression, armed rebellion
- Proclaimed by President
- Fundamental Rights suspended (except Article 21 after 44th Amendment)

**2. State Emergency (Article 356):**
- Constitutional breakdown in state
- President's Rule imposed
- State Legislature suspended

**3. Financial Emergency (Article 360):**
- Financial stability threatened
- Federal financial relations disturbed

**Effect:**
- Federal structure becomes unitary
- Center's control increases
- State autonomy reduced

**Criticism:** Misused during 1975-77 Emergency under Indira Gandhi

**Safeguards:** 44th Amendment (1978) added safeguards to prevent misuse

---

### FEATURE 15: Independent Election Commission (Article 324)

**Election Commission:**
- Conducts elections to Parliament and State Legislatures
- Independent of government
- Chief Election Commissioner - Secured tenure

**Ensures:**
- Free and fair elections
- Democratic governance
- People's will reflected

---

### FEATURE 16: Other Notable Features

**A. Official Language (Article 343):**
- Hindi in Devanagari script
- English continues (Official Languages Act, 1963)

**B. Special Provisions:**
- For certain states (J&K - Article 370 abrogated 2019, NE states - Article 371)
- For SCs, STs (Articles 15(4), 16(4), 330, 332)
- For Anglo-Indians (Articles 331, 333 - expired 2020)

**C. Three-tier Government:**
- Union, State, Local (Panchayats and Municipalities)
- 73rd and 74th Amendments (1992) - Constitutional status to local bodies

**D. Secular Personal Laws (Goal):**
- Article 44 (DPSP): Uniform Civil Code
- Currently, different personal laws for different religions
- Goal not yet achieved

## III. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

### Borrowed Yet Unique

**While India borrowed from many constitutions:**

**From Britain:**
- Parliamentary system
- Rule of law
- Legislative procedure

**But Made Indian:**
- Combined with republican system (not monarchy)
- Federal structure (Britain is unitary)
- Written Constitution (Britain's is unwritten)

**From USA:**
- Fundamental Rights
- Judicial review

**But Modified:**
- Added Directive Principles
- Limited property right
- Reasonable restrictions on rights

**From Ireland:**
- Directive Principles

**But Expanded:**
- More comprehensive DPSP
- Integrated with Fundamental Rights

**Result:** **Unique Indian Constitution** - not a copy but creative adaptation.

## IV. BASIC STRUCTURE FEATURES

**Supreme Court has declared certain features as "basic structure"** (cannot be amended):

1. Supremacy of Constitution
2. Sovereign, democratic, republican government
3. Secular character
4. Federal character
5. Separation of powers
6. Judicial review
7. Rule of law
8. Harmony between FR and DPSP
9. Parliamentary system
10. Independence of judiciary

**Case Law:** **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)**, **Minerva Mills (1980)**

---

## V. LIVING CONSTITUTION

**Dynamic Nature:**
- Constitution evolves through:
  - Amendments (105 amendments till 2024)
  - Judicial interpretation
  - Conventions and practices

**Examples of Evolution:**
- Article 21 expanded (Maneka Gandhi, 1978)
- Basic Structure Doctrine (Kesavananda Bharati, 1973)
- PIL developed through judicial creativity
- Right to privacy recognized (Puttaswamy, 2017)

---

## VI. CRITICISM

**1. Too Lengthy:**
- Includes matters that could be in ordinary laws
- Cumbersome

**2. Borrowed Heavily:**
- Called "bag of borrowings"
- But justified as learning from global experience

**3. Over-Centralization:**
- Strong center threatens federal balance
- States have limited autonomy

**4. Complexity:**
- Difficult for common person to understand
- Legal expertise needed

**5. Frequent Amendments:**
- 105 amendments in 74 years
- Some argue too many, others say shows adaptability

---

## VII. STRENGTHS

**1. Comprehensive:**
- Covers all aspects of governance
- Detailed provisions

**2. Flexible Yet Stable:**
- Can be amended but core protected
- Responds to changing needs

**3. Protects Rights:**
- Comprehensive Fundamental Rights
- Enforceable through courts

**4. Democratic:**
- Universal adult suffrage
- Periodic elections
- Accountable government

**5. Accommodates Diversity:**
- Special provisions for different groups
- Federal structure
- Multiple languages, religions

**6. Vision for Future:**
- DPSP provide goals
- Progressive and transformative

---

## VIII. CONCLUSION

The **salient features of the Indian Constitution** reflect the wisdom, foresight, and vision of its framers. It is:

**1. Lengthiest written constitution** - Comprehensive and detailed  
**2. Borrowed yet unique** - Global best practices adapted to India  
**3. Federal with unitary bias** - Balance between unity and diversity  
**4. Parliamentary system** - Responsive and accountable government  
**5. Fundamental Rights** - Protection of individual liberties  
**6. Directive Principles** - Vision for socio-economic justice  
**7. Fundamental Duties** - Balance of rights and responsibilities  
**8. Independent judiciary** - Guardian of Constitution and rights  
**9. Secular state** - Equal respect for all religions  
**10. Single citizenship** - National unity  
**11. Adult suffrage** - Political equality  
**12. Rigid and flexible** - Stability with adaptability  
**13. Emergency provisions** - Crisis management  

The Constitution successfully accommodates India's vast diversity while maintaining unity. It balances:
- Individual liberty with social welfare (FR + DPSP)
- Center's strength with states' autonomy (Quasi-federalism)
- Flexibility with stability (Amendment procedures)
- Rights with duties (FR + Fundamental Duties)

As Dr. Ambedkar stated:
> "The Constitution is not a mere lawyer's document, it is a vehicle of Life, and its spirit is always the spirit of Age."

The Indian Constitution has stood the test of time, enabling India to remain a vibrant democracy for over seven decades. Its salient features continue to guide India's journey towards the constitutional goals of **justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity** enshrined in the Preamble.

**It is truly a "living Constitution"** - adapting through amendments and judicial interpretation while protecting its core values through the Basic Structure Doctrine.
