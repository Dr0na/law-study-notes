# 10-MARK ANSWER

## Q. Protection of Right to Life and Personal Liberty with landmark cases.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

**Article 21** of the Constitution of India is the most dynamic and expansive Fundamental Right. It provides:

> **"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."**

Originally conceived as a simple procedural safeguard, Article 21 has been transformed through judicial interpretation into a repository of numerous substantive rights. It forms part of the **"Golden Triangle"** of Articles 14, 19, and 21, which together constitute the core of Fundamental Rights in India.

**Applicability:** Article 21 protects **"any person"** - citizens and non-citizens alike - within the territory of India.

---

## II. EVOLUTION OF ARTICLE 21: FROM NARROW TO EXPANSIVE INTERPRETATION

### 2.1 Pre-Maneka Gandhi Era - Narrow Interpretation

#### A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (AIR 1950 SC 27)

**Facts:**
- A.K. Gopalan detained under Preventive Detention Act
- Challenged detention as violating Articles 19, 21, and 22
- Argued "procedure established by law" must be just and fair

**Held:**
- **Narrow interpretation of Article 21 adopted**
- "Procedure established by law" means any law validly enacted by competent legislature
- Law need not be fair, just, or reasonable
- Article 21 does not incorporate "due process" doctrine
- Articles 19 and 21 are mutually exclusive

**Principle:**
If procedure prescribed by valid law is followed, person can be deprived of life or liberty even if procedure is unfair or unreasonable.

**Impact:**
- Limited protection under Article 21
- Procedural safeguard only
- Executive had wide powers

---

### 2.2 Maneka Gandhi Era - Paradigm Shift

#### Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (AIR 1978 SC 597)

**Facts:**
- Maneka Gandhi's passport impounded by Government under Section 10(3)(c) of Passports Act
- No opportunity of hearing given
- No reasons communicated
- Challenged as violating Articles 14, 19, and 21

**Issues:**
1. What is scope of "procedure established by law"?
2. Must procedure be fair and reasonable?
3. What is relationship between Articles 14, 19, and 21?

**Judgment:**

**Held:**

**1. Overruled A.K. Gopalan Partially:**
- "Procedure established by law" in Article 21 must be just, fair, and reasonable
- Not sufficient that law is validly enacted
- Procedure must also satisfy test of reasonableness under Article 14

**2. Golden Triangle Doctrine:**
> **"Articles 14, 19, and 21 form a 'Golden Triangle' - they are not mutually exclusive but overlapping and supplementary."**

- Any law depriving life or liberty must satisfy:
  - Article 14: Not arbitrary, reasonable
  - Article 19: Restrictions reasonable
  - Article 21: Procedure fair and just

**3. "Due Process" Introduced (Through Back Door):**
- Though Constitution says "procedure established by law" (not "due process")
- By requiring procedure to be fair, Court introduced essence of due process
- Brought Indian jurisprudence closer to American "due process"

**4. Principles Laid Down:**
- Right to be heard before adverse action
- Reasons must be communicated
- Opportunity to challenge decision
- Procedural fairness is constitutional requirement

**Ratio Decidendi:**
1. Procedure in Article 21 must be fair, just, and reasonable
2. Articles 14, 19, and 21 interconnected and complementary
3. Any law affecting personal liberty must pass test of all three articles

**Significance:**
- **Landmark judgment** transforming Article 21
- Shifted from procedure to substance
- Empowered courts to examine fairness of law
- Foundation for expansive interpretation

---

## III. EXPANDED INTERPRETATION - RIGHTS UNDER ARTICLE 21

Post-Maneka Gandhi, Supreme Court has read numerous rights into Article 21. **"Life" does not mean mere animal existence but life with human dignity.**

### 3.1 Right to Live with Human Dignity

#### Francis Coralie Mullin v. Union Territory of Delhi (AIR 1981 SC 746)

**Facts:**
- Foreign national detained in Tihar Jail
- Challenged inhuman conditions in jail

**Held:**
> **"The right to life includes the right to live with human dignity and all that goes along with it, namely, the bare necessaries of life such as adequate nutrition, clothing and shelter and facilities for reading, writing and expressing oneself in diverse forms, freely moving about and mixing and commingling with fellow human beings."**

**Principle:** Article 21 includes right to live with dignity, not mere survival.

---

### 3.2 Right to Livelihood

#### Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (AIR 1985 SC 3)

**Facts:**
- Pavement dwellers in Mumbai faced eviction
- Argued that eviction would deprive them of livelihood
- Livelihood depends on proximity to work places

**Held:**
- **Right to livelihood is part of right to life**
- No person can live without means of livelihood
- Depriving livelihood = depriving life
- But not absolute right - can be curtailed by procedure established by law

**Principle:** Right to livelihood is integral part of right to life under Article 21.

---

### 3.3 Right to Speedy Trial

#### Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar (AIR 1979 SC 1369)

**Facts:**
- Thousands of undertrials in Bihar jails
- Many detained for periods longer than maximum sentence for alleged offense
- PIL filed highlighting their plight

**Held:**
- **Right to speedy trial is Fundamental Right** under Article 21
- Unreasonable delay in trial violates Article 21
- Prolonged detention without trial is deprivation of personal liberty
- State must provide free legal aid

**Orders:**
- Release undertrials detained beyond maximum sentence
- Legal aid at State expense
- Speedy disposal of cases

**Principle:** Speedy trial is essential ingredient of reasonable, fair, and just procedure under Article 21.

---

### 3.4 Right to Free Legal Aid

#### M.H. Hoskot v. State of Maharashtra (AIR 1978 SC 1548)

**Facts:**
- Convict unable to afford lawyer for appeal
- Requested free legal aid

**Held:**
- **Right to free legal aid is Fundamental Right** under Articles 21 and 39A
- Access to justice is essence of Article 21
- Poor cannot be denied justice due to poverty
- State must provide legal aid free of cost

**Principle:** Legal aid is part of "procedure established by law" under Article 21.

---

### 3.5 Right to Health and Medical Care

#### Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v. State of West Bengal (AIR 1996 SC 2426)

**Facts:**
- Accident victim denied emergency treatment by multiple government hospitals
- Suffered permanent disability due to delayed treatment

**Held:**
- **Right to health is Fundamental Right** under Article 21
- State has constitutional obligation to provide medical facilities
- Failure to provide timely treatment violates Article 21
- Ordered compensation for medical negligence

**Principle:** Right to emergency medical treatment is part of Article 21; State cannot refuse emergency care.

---

### 3.6 Right to Clean Environment

#### M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Multiple Cases)

**Oleum Gas Leak Case (AIR 1987 SC 1086):**
- Evolved doctrine of **"Absolute Liability"** for hazardous industries
- Enterprise engaged in hazardous activity absolutely liable for harm

**Taj Mahal Case:**
- Industries near Taj causing pollution
- Ordered closure/relocation of polluting industries

**Vehicular Pollution Case:**
- Ordered CNG for public transport in Delhi
- Phasing out of old vehicles

**Principle Established:**
> **Right to healthy environment is part of Article 21. Right to life includes right to pollution-free air and water.**

---

### 3.7 Right to Privacy

#### Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (AIR 2017 SC 4161) - 9-Judge Bench

**Facts:**
- Challenge to Aadhaar scheme
- Question: Is privacy a Fundamental Right?

**Held:**
- **Right to privacy is intrinsic to right to life and personal liberty under Article 21**
- Privacy is essential for human dignity
- Includes informational privacy, bodily privacy, decisional privacy
- Not absolute - subject to reasonable restrictions

**Test for Invasion of Privacy:**
1. Invasion must be by law (legality)
2. Legitimate State aim (legitimate goal)
3. Proportionate to need (proportionality)

**Significance:**
- Landmark judgment recognizing privacy as Fundamental Right
- Overruled earlier 8-judge bench in M.P. Sharma and 6-judge bench in Kharak Singh
- Foundation for data protection laws

---

### 3.8 Right to Education

#### Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka (AIR 1992 SC 1858)

**Held:**
- Right to education flows from Article 21
- State cannot deny education based on ability to pay capitation fee

#### Unnikrishnan v. State of AP (AIR 1993 SC 2178)

**Held:**
- Right to free education up to 14 years (now enshrined as Article 21A)
- Beyond 14 years, subject to State's economic capacity

**Result:** Led to 86th Amendment inserting **Article 21A - Right to Education** as explicit Fundamental Right.

---

### 3.9 Right to Shelter

#### Chameli Singh v. State of UP (AIR 1996 SC 1051)

**Held:**
- Right to shelter is part of Article 21
- Right to life includes right to food, water, decent environment, and reasonable accommodation

---

### 3.10 Right to Die with Dignity (Passive Euthanasia)

#### Common Cause v. Union of India (AIR 2018 SC 1665)

**Held:**
- **Right to die with dignity is part of Article 21**
- Living will recognized
- Passive euthanasia permitted with safeguards
- Guidelines laid down to prevent abuse

**Principle:** Right to life includes right to die with dignity when life becomes unbearable due to terminal illness.

---

## IV. PROTECTION AGAINST ARREST AND DETENTION

### Article 22: Complementary to Article 21

**Article 22** provides procedural safeguards against arrest and detention.

#### D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (AIR 1997 SC 610)

**Facts:**
- Custodial deaths and torture common
- PIL filed for guidelines

**Held:**
- **Guidelines for Arrest and Detention laid down:**
  1. Arrest memo with reasons
  2. Right to inform friend/relative
  3. Right to be examined by doctor
  4. Interrogation in place with CCTVs
  5. Prohibition of torture
  6. Compensation for custodial violence

**Principle:** Custodial violence violates Article 21; State must ensure safeguards.

---

## V. LIMITATIONS ON ARTICLE 21

**Article 21 is not absolute.** Person can be deprived of life or liberty if:

1. **By Procedure Established by Law:**
   - Must be valid law
   - Procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable
   - Must satisfy Articles 14 and 19

2. **Death Penalty:**
   - Constitution permits death penalty
   - But only in "rarest of rare" cases
   - Must satisfy due process

3. **Emergency:**
   - During National Emergency, enforcement of Article 21 can be suspended (but 44th Amendment restricted this)

4. **Preventive Detention:**
   - Article 22 permits preventive detention
   - But with safeguards

---

## VI. CONCLUSION

**Article 21** has emerged as the most dynamic and powerful Fundamental Right in the Indian Constitution. From the narrow interpretation in **A.K. Gopalan (1950)** to the expansive vision in **Maneka Gandhi (1978)** and subsequent cases, Article 21 has been transformed into a reservoir of substantive rights.

The Supreme Court's creative interpretation has read numerous rights into Article 21:
- Right to livelihood, speedy trial, legal aid
- Right to health, education, shelter
- Right to clean environment
- Right to privacy
- Right to die with dignity

**"Life" under Article 21 does not mean mere animal existence but life with human dignity, encompassing all facets that make life worth living.**

The **Golden Triangle doctrine** (Articles 14, 19, 21) ensures that any law affecting life or liberty must pass the test of reasonableness, fairness, and justice. This has shifted Indian jurisprudence from procedural protection to substantive justice.

As Justice Bhagwati observed in **Francis Coralie case**:
> "The right to life enshrined in Article 21 cannot be restricted to mere animal existence. It means something much more than just physical survival."

**Article 21 continues to evolve, responding to emerging challenges and ensuring that the constitutional promise of life and liberty remains meaningful and relevant for every person in India.**
