# 10-MARK ANSWER

## Q.9) Explain the provisions relating to freedom of Religion under the Indian Constitution.

---

## I. INTRODUCTION

**Freedom of Religion** is a Fundamental Right guaranteed under **Articles 25 to 28** of the Constitution of India. India is a **secular state** where the State maintains equal distance from all religions without favoring or discriminating against any.

The Constitution guarantees:
- Individual freedom of conscience and religion (Article 25)
- Collective freedom of religious denominations (Article 26)
- Freedom from taxation for any particular religion (Article 27)
- Freedom from religious instruction in State institutions (Article 28)

**"Secular" added to Preamble** by 42nd Amendment (1976), though India was secular from inception.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994):** Secularism is **basic feature** of Constitution.

---

## II. ARTICLE 25: FREEDOM OF CONSCIENCE AND RELIGION (Individual Rights)

### 2.1 Provision

**Article 25(1):**
> "Subject to public order, morality and health and to the other provisions of this Part, all persons are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and free profession, practice and propagation of religion."

**Applicability:** **"All persons"** - citizens and non-citizens

**Three Components:**

**1. Freedom of Conscience:**
- Inner freedom to believe or not believe
- Freedom to follow any religion or no religion
- Right to be atheist or agnostic

**2. Freedom to Profess:**
- Right to declare one's religious beliefs openly
- Profess faith publicly

**3. Freedom to Practice and Propagate:**
- **Practice:** Perform religious rituals, worship, observe religious practices
- **Propagate:** Spread and disseminate religious ideas, convert others

**Note:** "Propagate" does NOT include forcible conversion.

---

### 2.2 Restrictions on Article 25 (Clause 1)

Freedom under Article 25 is **subject to:**

**A. Public Order:**
- Religious practices disturbing public peace can be restricted
- Example: Processions blocking traffic

**B. Morality:**
- Practices against morality can be prohibited
- Example: Human sacrifice, devadasi system

**C. Health:**
- Practices harmful to health can be restricted
- Example: Handling venomous snakes in rituals without precautions

**D. Other Provisions of Part III:**
- Subject to other Fundamental Rights
- Example: Article 17 (abolition of untouchability) overrides religious practices

---

### 2.3 State's Power to Regulate (Article 25(2))

**Article 25(2) empowers State to:**

**Clause (a):** Regulate or restrict any **economic, financial, commercial, or secular activity** associated with religious practice

**Examples:**
- Managing temple properties
- Regulating temple administration
- Controlling religious endowments

**Clause (b):** Provide for **social welfare and reform**

**Specifically mentioned:**
- Opening Hindu religious institutions to all classes and sections of Hindus

**Examples:**
- Abolishing untouchability
- Allowing all Hindus to enter temples
- Reform of religious practices

**Case Law:** **Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras v. Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar (AIR 1954 SC 282)**
- State can regulate secular aspects of religion
- Cannot interfere with essential religious practices

---

## III. ARTICLE 26: FREEDOM OF RELIGIOUS DENOMINATIONS (Collective Rights)

### 3.1 Provision

**Article 26:**
> "Subject to public order, morality and health, every religious denomination or any section thereof shall have the rightâ€”
> (a) to establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes;
> (b) to manage its own affairs in matters of religion;
> (c) to own and acquire movable and immovable property; and
> (d) to administer such property in accordance with law."

**Applicability:** Religious denominations (collective bodies)

**Four Rights:**

**1. Establish Institutions (26(a)):**
- Set up religious and charitable institutions
- Temples, churches, mosques, charities

**2. Manage Affairs (26(b)):**
- Manage religious affairs independently
- Appoint priests, conduct ceremonies
- Essential religious practices

**3. Own Property (26(c)):**
- Acquire and own property
- For religious purposes

**4. Administer Property (26(d)):**
- Manage property
- **"In accordance with law"** - State can regulate

---

### 3.2 Essential Religious Practices Test

**Question:** What practices are protected under Articles 25 and 26?

**Answer:** Only **essential religious practices** protected.

**Test:** Whether practice is essential to that religion?
- Must be integral to religion
- Not merely customary or traditional
- Core of religious belief

**Case Law:** **Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Sri Lakshmindra (1954)**
- Established "essential religious practices" test
- State can reform non-essential practices
- Cannot interfere with essential practices

**Examples:**

**Essential (Protected):**
- Namaz for Muslims
- Baptism for Christians
- Wearing turban for Sikhs

**Non-Essential (Can be Regulated):**
- Temple management
- Economic activities
- Social practices like untouchability

---

## IV. ARTICLE 27: FREEDOM FROM TAXATION FOR RELIGION

**Article 27:**
> "No person shall be compelled to pay any taxes, the proceeds of which are specifically appropriated in payment of expenses for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or religious denomination."

**Meaning:**
- Cannot levy tax specifically for promoting particular religion
- Protects secular character
- Individual not forced to support religion he doesn't believe in

**But:**
- General taxes can be used for all religions equally
- Example: Government can provide electricity, water to all religious places

---

## V. ARTICLE 28: FREEDOM FROM RELIGIOUS INSTRUCTION

**Article 28:**

**Clause (1):** No religious instruction in educational institutions wholly maintained by State funds

**Clause (2):** Institutions administered by State but established under endowment/trust requiring religious instruction - not applicable

**Clause (3):** Recognized or aided institutions (minority or otherwise) - no compulsion to attend religious instruction

**Purpose:**
- Secular education in State institutions
- No compulsion even in aided institutions
- Protects students' freedom of conscience

---

## VI. CONCEPT OF SECULARISM IN INDIAN CONSTITUTION

### Indian Secularism Unique

| Western Secularism | Indian Secularism |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| Separation of State and religion | Equal distance from all religions |
| State completely neutral | State can intervene for reform |
| No state support to any religion | State can support all equally |
| Privatization of religion | Public role of religion acknowledged |

**Features of Indian Secularism:**

**1. No State Religion:**
- Unlike Pakistan (Islamic Republic)
- Unlike UK (Church of England)

**2. Equal Treatment:**
- State respects all religions equally
- No preference or discrimination

**3. Freedom of Religion:**
- Individual and collective freedom
- Articles 25-28

**4. Positive Secularism:**
- State can reform religious practices
- Social reform permitted
- Example: Abolition of untouchability (Article 17)

**Case Law:** **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)**
> "Secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution. The State has no religion. It treats all religions equally."

---

## VII. IMPORTANT CASE LAWS

### Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Sri Lakshmindra (AIR 1954 SC 282)

**Held:**
- Essential religious practices test
- State can regulate secular aspects
- Cannot interfere with essential practices

### The Durgah Committee, Ajmer v. Syed Hussain Ali (AIR 1961 SC 1402)

**Held:**
- State can regulate administration of religious institutions
- Regulation of property and management is secular activity

### Rev. Stanislaus v. State of Madhya Pradesh (AIR 1977 SC 908)

**Held:**
- Propagation in Article 25 means right to transmit or spread one's religion by exposition
- Does not include right to convert another person
- Anti-conversion laws valid if they prohibit only forcible conversion

### Ismail Faruqui v. Union of India (AIR 1994 SC 605) - Ayodhya Case

**Held:**
- Mosque not essential for offering namaz
- Can be offered anywhere
- Acquisition of religious property for secular purpose not unconstitutional

---

## VIII. LIMITATIONS AND BALANCE

**Freedom of religion is subject to:**
1. Public order, morality, health
2. Other Fundamental Rights (Article 17 overrides)
3. State's power of social reform (Article 25(2)(b))
4. Regulation of secular activities (Article 25(2)(a))
5. Law (administration of property - Article 26(d))

**Balance Achieved:**
- Individual freedom of religion protected
- State can reform for social welfare
- Cannot interfere with essential practices
- Secular activities can be regulated

---

## IX. CONCLUSION

**Freedom of Religion** under **Articles 25 to 28** is a comprehensive guarantee ensuring both individual and collective religious freedom in India's secular democratic framework. 

**Article 25** protects individual freedom of conscience, profession, practice, and propagation of religion, while **Article 26** protects collective rights of religious denominations to manage their affairs. **Articles 27 and 28** ensure that State does not use taxation or education for promoting particular religion.

**Indian secularism is unique** - not complete separation but equal distance from all religions with power to reform for social welfare. The State:
- Has no religion of its own
- Respects all religions equally
- Can intervene for social reform
- Cannot interfere with essential religious practices

Through the **essential religious practices test**, courts have balanced religious freedom with social reform, permitting State to regulate non-essential aspects while protecting the core of religious beliefs.

As held in **S.R. Bommai (1994)**, **secularism is a basic feature** of the Constitution, ensuring that India remains a land where all religions coexist peacefully with equal freedom and equal respect.

**Freedom of religion ensures that in India, every person is free to follow the religion of their choice, practice their faith, and propagate their beliefs - subject only to reasonable restrictions for maintaining public order, morality, health, and social reform.**
