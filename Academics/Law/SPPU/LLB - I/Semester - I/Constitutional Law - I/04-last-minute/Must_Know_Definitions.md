## üìú **FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS**

### 1. Constitution

**Definition:** 
> A Constitution is the supreme fundamental law of a country that establishes the structure, powers and functions of government organs, and defines the relationship between the State and citizens.

**Key aspects:** Written/Unwritten, Rigid/Flexible, Federal/Unitary

---

### 2. Constitutionalism

**Definition:**
> Constitutionalism is the philosophy of limited government under law, where governmental power is restricted by constitutional provisions and rule of law prevails.

**Elements:** Rule of Law, Separation of Powers, Judicial Review, FR protection

---

### 3. Fundamental Rights

**Definition:**
> Fundamental Rights are basic human rights guaranteed by Part III of the Constitution (Articles 12-35), which are justiciable and enforceable through courts against the State.

**Nature:** Justiciable, not absolute, subject to reasonable restrictions

---

### 4. Directive Principles

**Definition:**
> Directive Principles of State Policy are non-justiciable guidelines for State policy laid down in Part IV (Articles 36-51), which are fundamental in governance but not enforceable in courts.

**Purpose:** Socio-economic democracy, welfare state

---

### 5. Fundamental Duties

**Definition:**
> Fundamental Duties under Article 51A are eleven moral and civic obligations of citizens added by 42nd Amendment (1976), which are non-justiciable but can be indirectly enforced.

**Count:** 10 (1976) + 1 (2002) = 11 total

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è **KEY LEGAL TERMS**

### 6. State (Article 12)

**Definition:**
> "State" for Part III includes Government and Parliament of India, State Governments and Legislatures, all local authorities, and other authorities within territory or under control of Government of India.

**Test for "other authority":** Ajay Hasia (statutory creation, state control, public function, financial assistance)

---

### 7. Law (Article 13)

**Definition:**
> "Law" under Article 13 includes any ordinance, order, bye-law, rule, regulation, notification, custom or usage having force of law in India.

**Effect:** Laws inconsistent with FRs are void to extent of inconsistency

---

### 8. Person

**Definition:**
> "Person" includes natural persons (human beings) and in some contexts may include legal persons (companies, corporations), though companies generally don't have all FRs.

**Scope:** Varies by Article (some say "person," others say "citizen")

---

### 9. Citizen

**Definition:**
> A citizen is a person who owes permanent allegiance to a State and is entitled to its protection and to full political and civil rights.

**India:** Single citizenship only, no state citizenship

---

### 10. Domicile

**Definition:**
> Domicile means permanent home - the country which is considered by law to be a person's permanent home. Requires residence + intention to make it permanent home.

**Case:** Central Bank v. Ram Narain (intention essential)

---

## üéØ **SPECIFIC RIGHTS DEFINITIONS**

### 11. Equality Before Law

**Definition:**
> Equality before law is a negative concept meaning no person is above law, there are no special privileges for anyone, and all are equally subject to ordinary law of the land.

**Source:** Dicey's Rule of Law

---

### 12. Equal Protection of Laws

**Definition:**
> Equal protection of laws is a positive concept meaning equality of treatment in equal circumstances - like should be treated alike without discrimination.

**Source:** American Constitution

---

### 13. Reasonable Classification

**Definition:**
> Reasonable classification is permissible differentiation for purposes of legislation based on intelligible differentia having rational nexus with object sought to be achieved.

**Test:** I-R (Intelligible Differentia + Rational Nexus)

---

### 14. Class Legislation

**Definition:**
> Class legislation is arbitrary discrimination without reasonable basis, which is prohibited under Article 14 as it treats similarly situated persons unequally.

**Distinction:** Classification (valid) vs Class legislation (invalid)

---

### 15. Arbitrariness

**Definition:**
> Arbitrariness means action taken without any rational or reasonable basis, which is whimsical, capricious or unreasonable.

**Principle:** E.P. Royappa - "Arbitrary action = negation of equality"

---

### 16. Backward Class

**Definition:**
> Backward class under Article 16(4) means socially and educationally backward classes of citizens who are not adequately represented in State services.

**Includes:** SC, ST, OBC

---

### 17. Creamy Layer

**Definition:**
> Creamy layer refers to the socially and economically advanced sections within a backward class who must be excluded from reservation benefits so that truly disadvantaged get the benefit.

**Case:** Indra Sawhney, 1993 (applies to OBC only)

---

### 18. Basic Structure

**Definition:**
> Basic Structure is the fundamental framework and essential features of the Constitution which cannot be destroyed even by constitutional amendment, as established in Kesavananda Bharati (1973).

**Examples:** Supremacy of Constitution, Rule of Law, Judicial Review, Secularism, Federal character

---

## üó£Ô∏è **FREEDOM-RELATED TERMS**

### 19. Freedom of Speech and Expression

**Definition:**
> Freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) means the right to express one's views, opinions and convictions freely by word of mouth, writing, printing, picture or any other mode.

**Includes:** Freedom of press (implied), right to know, commercial speech

---

### 20. Reasonable Restrictions

**Definition:**
> Reasonable restrictions are limitations imposed on fundamental freedoms which are not arbitrary or excessive but are fair, just and necessary in a democratic society for specified purposes.

**Test:** Procedural + Substantive reasonableness

---

### 21. Prior Restraint

**Definition:**
> Prior restraint means censorship before publication or expression, which generally violates freedom of speech as it prevents expression before it occurs.

**General rule:** Not allowed (except films, emergency)

---

### 22. Personal Liberty

**Definition:**
> Personal liberty under Article 21 means rights relating to life, personal freedom and dignity of a person, which has been interpreted expansively to include numerous implied rights.

**Evolution:** Gopalan (narrow) ‚Üí Maneka (broad)

---

### 23. Due Process of Law

**Definition:**
> Due process of law is the principle that procedure for deprivation of life or liberty must be reasonable, fair and just, and the law itself must be reasonable and not arbitrary.

**India:** Introduced by Maneka Gandhi, 1978 (though word "due" absent in Art. 21)

---

### 24. Procedure Established by Law

**Definition:**
> Procedure established by law means the procedure prescribed by statute or law made by Legislature. After Maneka Gandhi, this procedure must also be reasonable, fair and just.

**Pre-Maneka:** Any procedure sufficient
**Post-Maneka:** Must be reasonable

---

## üîê **ARREST & DETENTION TERMS**

### 25. Arrest

**Definition:**
> Arrest is the act of depriving a person of liberty by legal authority for alleged commission of an offence or for other lawful grounds.

**Art. 22(1) & (2):** Four safeguards (ICLP)

---

### 26. Preventive Detention

**Definition:**
> Preventive detention is detention of a person without trial and conviction by court, based on subjective satisfaction of executive authority, to prevent him from committing an undesirable future act.

**Distinguished from:** Conviction (which is for past act with trial)

---

### 27. Detenu

**Definition:**
> A detenu is a person who is detained under a preventive detention law without trial.

**Rights:** Art. 22(4) and (5) - grounds communication, representation, Advisory Board

---

### 28. Advisory Board

**Definition:**
> Advisory Board under Article 22(4) is a board consisting of persons qualified to be judges, which examines cases of preventive detention beyond 3 months and reports whether there is sufficient cause for detention.

**Composition:** Persons qualified to be High Court judges

---

## üö´ **EXPLOITATION TERMS**

### 29. Traffic in Human Beings

**Definition:**
> Traffic in human beings, commonly known as slavery, means buying and selling of human beings as if they are chattels, which is absolutely prohibited under Article 23(1).

**Includes:** Trafficking for prostitution

---

### 30. Begar

**Definition:**
> Begar means compulsory work without any payment, which is prohibited under Article 23(1).

**Principle:** Right not to be compelled to work without wages

---

### 31. Forced Labour

**Definition:**
> Forced labour means work or service extracted from a person by force or compulsion, which after 1982 includes even paid work where wages are below minimum wages.

**Evolution:** 1982 Asiad case expanded meaning

---

### 32. Bonded Labour

**Definition:**
> Bonded labour is a system where one person is bonded to provide labour to another for years until a debt is supposedly repaid, which is a form of forced labour prohibited under Article 23.

**Act:** Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976

---

### 33. Child Labour

**Definition:**
> Child labour under Article 24 means employment of children below 14 years of age in factories, mines or any hazardous employment, which is constitutionally prohibited.

**Scope:** Partial prohibition (only specified employments)

---

## ‚úùÔ∏è **RELIGION TERMS**

### 34. Secularism

**Definition:**
> Secularism under Indian Constitution means a state that has no state religion, treats all religions equally with equal respect, and maintains principled distance from all religions while allowing positive engagement for reform.

**Added:** 42nd Amendment, 1976 (to Preamble)

---

### 35. Religion

**Definition:**
> Religion under Articles 25-26 consists of essential and integral beliefs, doctrines and practices fundamental to a faith, including both individual conscience and collective denominational affairs.

**Two aspects:** Essential practices (protected) + Non-essential (can be regulated)

---

### 36. Religious Denomination

**Definition:**
> A religious denomination is a collection of individuals with a common system of beliefs or doctrines, a common organization, and designated by a distinctive name.

**Rights:** Article 26 (EMOA - Establish, Manage, Own, Administer)

---

### 37. Essential Religious Practice

**Definition:**
> Essential religious practice is a practice which is essential and integral to the religion such that the religion cannot exist without it, as distinguished from non-essential practices which can be regulated by State.

**Test:** Commissioner HRE v. Lakshmindra, 1954

---

### 38. Minority

**Definition:**
> A minority under Articles 29-30 is a community numerically inferior to rest of population in a State (not India), whether based on religion or language, which has right to conserve culture and establish educational institutions.

**Determination:** At State level, not national level

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è **REMEDY TERMS**

### 39. Writ

**Definition:**
> A writ is a formal written order issued by Supreme Court under Article 32 or High Court under Article 226 to enforce fundamental rights or legal rights.

**Types:** CHAMP (5 writs)

---

### 40. Habeas Corpus

**Definition:**
> Habeas Corpus (Latin: "produce the body") is a writ issued to secure release of a person from illegal or unlawful detention by commanding the detaining authority to produce the person before court.

**Most important:** For personal liberty

---

### 41. Mandamus

**Definition:**
> Mandamus (Latin: "we command") is a writ issued commanding a public authority to perform a public duty which it has failed or refused to perform.

**Requirements:** Legal right, legal duty, public duty

---

### 42. Certiorari

**Definition:**
> Certiorari (Latin: "to be certified") is a writ issued to quash an order already made by an inferior court or tribunal on grounds of excess/lack of jurisdiction or violation of natural justice.

**Nature:** Curative (after decision)

---

### 43. Prohibition

**Definition:**
> Prohibition (Latin: "to forbid") is a writ issued to prevent an inferior court or tribunal from acting beyond its jurisdiction or against principles of natural justice.

**Nature:** Preventive (before decision)

---

### 44. Quo Warranto

**Definition:**
> Quo Warranto (Latin: "by what authority") is a writ issued to inquire into the legality of claim of a person to a public office and to oust him if he is holding office unlawfully.

**Scope:** Only public offices (substantive, not temporary)

---

### 45. Public Interest Litigation

**Definition:**
> Public Interest Litigation is litigation undertaken for public interest or for enforcement of rights of poor and disadvantaged sections who cannot approach court themselves, with liberalized locus standi allowing any public-spirited person to file.

**Pioneer case:** S.P. Gupta v. Union of India, 1982

---

### 46. Locus Standi

**Definition:**
> Locus standi means the legal right or capacity to bring an action or appear in court, traditionally requiring personal injury or grievance, but liberalized in PIL.

**Traditional:** Only aggrieved person
**PIL:** Any person for public interest

---

## üìö **DOCTRINES & PRINCIPLES**

### 47. Doctrine of Eclipse

**Definition:**
> Doctrine of Eclipse means that pre-Constitution laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are not dead but eclipsed (overshadowed), and can become operative if Constitution is amended to remove the inconsistency.

**Bhikaji case** | Applies only to pre-1950 laws

---

### 48. Doctrine of Severability

**Definition:**
> Doctrine of Severability means that if part of a law is unconstitutional, only that part will be void and the remaining valid portion will survive, provided the invalid part is severable from the rest.

**Test:** Can parts be separated? Is remainder complete code?

---

### 49. Basic Structure Doctrine

**Definition:**
> Basic Structure Doctrine established in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) means that while Parliament can amend the Constitution, it cannot alter or destroy the basic features or essential framework of the Constitution.

**Basic features:** SURFS-JRP

---

### 50. Doctrine of Prospective Overruling

**Definition:**
> Doctrine of Prospective Overruling means court declares a law unconstitutional only for future, while past transactions under that law remain valid.

**Purpose:** Avoid chaos from retrospective invalidation

---

### 51. Doctrine of Harmonious Construction

**Definition:**
> Doctrine of Harmonious Construction is the principle of interpretation where apparently conflicting provisions are reconciled to give effect to both, avoiding inconsistency.

**Application:** Reading FRs with DPs harmoniously (Minerva Mills)

---

## üîç **JUDICIAL TERMS**

### 52. Judicial Review

**Definition:**
> Judicial Review is the power of courts to examine the constitutional validity of legislative enactments and executive actions, and to declare them void if they violate the Constitution.

**Source:** USA | **Articles:** 13, 32, 226

---

### 53. Judicial Activism

**Definition:**
> Judicial Activism is the proactive role of judiciary in interpreting law broadly, filling legislative gaps, and protecting rights, going beyond traditional adjudication to address social issues.

**Example:** PIL, Vishaka guidelines, environmental directions

---

### 54. Judicial Restraint

**Definition:**
> Judicial Restraint is the judicial philosophy of showing deference to legislative and executive branches, intervening only when clear constitutional violation, and not engaging in policy-making.

**Opposite of:** Judicial Activism

---

### 55. Mala Fide

**Definition:**
> Mala fide means "in bad faith" - action taken with improper motive, ulterior purpose or with knowledge of illegality.

**Effect:** Mala fide action violates Art. 14 (arbitrary)

**Example:** Arvind Dhande case (transfer of honest officer)

---

### 56. Bona Fide

**Definition:**
> Bona fide means "in good faith" - action taken honestly, without intent to deceive or defraud, with honest belief in its propriety.

**Presumption:** State action presumed bona fide unless proved otherwise

---

### 57. Natural Justice

**Definition:**
> Natural Justice refers to fundamental principles of fair procedure including right to be heard (audi alteram partem) and rule against bias (nemo judex in causa sua), which are integral to fair decision-making.

**Maneka Gandhi:** Natural justice is part of Art. 14

---

### 58. Laches

**Definition:**
> Laches means unreasonable delay in asserting or enforcing one's right, which may result in court refusing relief on ground that petitioner slept over his rights.

**Maxim:** Equity aids the vigilant, not the indolent

---

## üìä **CLASSIFICATION TERMS**

### 59. Intelligible Differentia

**Definition:**
> Intelligible Differentia means a real, substantial and reasonable basis of distinction between persons or things grouped in a class and those left out, which is not arbitrary, artificial or evasive.

**Part of:** I-R test under Art. 14

---

### 60. Rational Nexus

**Definition:**
> Rational Nexus means a reasonable and logical connection between the classification adopted and the object sought to be achieved by the law.

**Part of:** I-R test under Art. 14

---

### 61. Protective Discrimination

**Definition:**
> Protective discrimination, also called affirmative action or positive discrimination, means special provisions for socially and educationally backward classes to bring them to equal level with others.

**Constitutional basis:** Arts. 15(4), 15(5), 16(4)

---

### 62. Affirmative Action

**Definition:**
> Affirmative action means positive steps taken to improve the status of disadvantaged groups through preferential treatment in education, employment, etc.

**Same as:** Protective discrimination, positive discrimination

---

### 63. Horizontal Application

**Definition:**
> Horizontal application of Fundamental Rights means enforceability not only against State but also against private persons and institutions.

**Only for:** Articles 17, 23, 24

---

## üåü **RESERVATION TERMS**

### 64. Reservation

**Definition:**
> Reservation under Article 16(4) means setting aside of certain percentage of posts or seats in public services or educational institutions for backward classes who are not adequately represented.

**Nature:** Enabling provision (not mandatory)

---

### 65. Carry Forward

**Definition:**
> Carry forward rule means unfilled reserved posts in one year can be carried forward to next year and added to reserved quota for that year, subject to 50% ceiling in any single year.

**Cases:** Devadasan (invalid if exceeds 50%), A.B.S.K. Sangh (valid for SC/ST)

---

### 66. Adequate Representation

**Definition:**
> Adequate representation under Article 16(4) means representation in State services in reasonable proportion to the population of that backward class, and lack of adequate representation is pre-condition for reservation.

**Determination:** By State based on factual data

---

## üîê **LIFE & LIBERTY TERMS**

### 67. Life

**Definition:**
> "Life" under Article 21 means not mere animal existence but life with human dignity, including all those aspects which go to make life meaningful, complete and worth living.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha:** Life with dignity

---

### 68. Personal Liberty

**Definition:**
> Personal Liberty under Article 21 means freedom from physical restraint and also includes various rights and freedoms which make up personal liberty of man.

**Maneka Gandhi:** Expanded beyond physical restraint

---

### 69. Procedure Established by Law

**Definition:**
> Procedure established by law under Article 21 means procedure prescribed by a valid law made by Legislature. After Maneka Gandhi, this procedure must also be reasonable, fair and just.

**Not just:** Any procedure | **Must be:** Fair procedure

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è **WRIT-RELATED TERMS**

### 70. Locus Standi

**Definition:**
> Locus standi means the legal standing or right to bring an action before court, traditionally requiring personal interest or injury in the matter.

**PIL:** Liberalized to allow any person for public interest

---

### 71. Alternative Remedy

**Definition:**
> Alternative remedy means other legal remedy available to aggrieved person besides writ jurisdiction, such as appeal, revision, departmental proceedings, etc.

**Effect:** Court may refuse writ if adequate alternative remedy available (but not absolute bar)

---

### 72. Judicial/Quasi-Judicial Function

**Definition:**
> Judicial or quasi-judicial function means decision-making involving determination of rights and liabilities of parties based on evidence and legal principles, with requirement of natural justice.

**Distinguished from:** Administrative function

---

## üß† **EXAM STRATEGY FOR DEFINITIONS**

### Always Include in Introduction

**For any FR question:**
1. Define the right
2. State Article number
3. Mention beneficiary (all persons/citizens)
4. Give context (why important)

**Example:**
> "Article 14 guarantees equality before law and equal protection of laws to all persons. It is the cornerstone of Indian democracy and a basic feature of the Constitution as held in Kesavananda Bharati case."
