## Q.1) Explain the meaning of Citizenship. Discuss the various kinds of Citizenship provided under the Citizenship Act 1955.

### INTRODUCTION (2 marks)

Citizenship is the legal bond between an individual and a state, conferring rights and imposing duties. Under the Indian Constitution, Articles 5 to 11 define citizenship at commencement, while the **Citizenship Act, 1955**, enacted under Article 11, provides comprehensive provisions for acquisition and loss of citizenship.

India follows the principle of **single citizenship**‚Äîthere is only Indian citizenship with no separate state citizenship. This contrasts with federal countries like the USA which have dual citizenship (federal and state). Single citizenship promotes national unity and ensures uniform rights for all citizens throughout India.

### MEANING OF CITIZENSHIP (2 marks)

**Definition:** A citizen is a person who owes permanent allegiance to a state and is entitled to its protection and full civil and political rights.

**Key Features of Indian Citizenship:**
- **Single citizenship** only (Article 5-11)
- **No dual citizenship** permitted (Article 9)
- Parliament has exclusive power to legislate (Article 11)
- Uniform rights throughout India

### CITIZENSHIP ACT, 1955 - FIVE METHODS (10 marks)

The Act provides five ways of acquiring citizenship, remembered by mnemonic **BDRNI**:

#### 1. CITIZENSHIP BY BIRTH (Section 3) - 2 marks

**Principle:** Person born in India is citizen by birth.

**Provisions:**
- Born in India on/after Jan 26, 1950 = Citizen
- **Exceptions:** (a) Father has diplomatic immunity and not Indian citizen, OR (b) Father is enemy alien, birth during enemy occupation

**Amendment Impact:**
- Pre-1986: Jus soli (birthright) - anyone born in India was citizen
- Post-1986: At least one parent must be Indian citizen
- Prevents automatic citizenship to illegal immigrants' children

#### 2. CITIZENSHIP BY DESCENT (Section 4) - 2 marks

**Principle:** Person born outside India can be citizen if parent was Indian at time of birth.

**Provisions:**
- Pre-1992: Father must be Indian citizen
- Post-1992: Either parent can confer citizenship (gender-neutral)

**Proviso:** If parent was citizen by descent only, requires:
- Birth registration at Indian consulate, OR
- Parent in Government of India service

#### 3. CITIZENSHIP BY REGISTRATION (Section 5) - 2 marks

**Eligible Categories:**
- Persons of Indian origin resident in India (6 months)
- Persons of Indian origin resident outside undivided India
- Women married to Indian citizens
- Minor children of Indian citizens
- Commonwealth country citizens

**Overseas Citizenship (2003):**
- Sections 7A, 7B provide OCI
- For persons of Indian origin (except Pakistan, Bangladesh)
- Limited rights, can be cancelled (Section 7D)

#### 4. CITIZENSHIP BY NATURALISATION (Section 6) - 2 marks

**For:** Non-Commonwealth foreign nationals

**Conditions:**
- Not from country preventing Indian naturalization
- Renounce other citizenship
- Resident 12 months immediately preceding
- Resident 4 years out of preceding 7 years
- Good character
- Knowledge of Indian language
- Intent to reside/serve in India

#### 5. CITIZENSHIP BY INCORPORATION (Section 7) - 2 marks

**Principle:** When territory becomes part of India, Central Government notifies who becomes citizen based on connection with territory.

**Applied:** Goa, Pondicherry integration

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

The Citizenship Act provides a comprehensive framework supplementing constitutional provisions. The five methods (BDRNI) balance birth connection, heritage, voluntary adoption, merit-based acquisition, and territorial integration. Single citizenship promotes national unity, prevents discrimination based on state of residence, and strengthens federal structure while maintaining unity in diversity.

## Q.2) Explain the Meaning, object, purpose and scope of the preamble. Discuss whether preamble is part of the constitution or not.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

The Preamble to the Constitution embodies fundamental values, philosophy, and objectives of the constitutional framework. It serves as the "key to open the mind of the makers," reflecting aspirations of the people of India and providing interpretative guidance for constitutional provisions.

### TEXT (1 mark)

"WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA, having solemnly resolved to constitute India into a SOVEREIGN SOCIALIST SECULAR DEMOCRATIC REPUBLIC and to secure to all its citizens JUSTICE, LIBERTY, EQUALITY, FRATERNITY..."

### MEANING OF KEY WORDS (5 marks)

#### SOVEREIGN
India is free from external control, supreme within territory, not subordinate to any foreign power. Complete independence achieved.

#### SOCIALIST (Added 1976)
Democratic socialism‚Äîmixed economy, welfare state, reduce inequalities. Not communist socialism; private property allowed with social obligations.

#### SECULAR (Added 1976)
No state religion, equal respect to all religions, freedom of religion (Arts. 25-28). **S.R. Bommai v. UOI, AIR 1994:** Secularism is **basic feature**.

Indian secularism differs from Western (complete separation)‚Äîfollows principled distance with positive engagement for reform.

#### DEMOCRATIC
Government by the people, representative democracy, universal adult franchise (Art. 326), periodic elections, rule of law.

#### REPUBLIC
Elected head of state (President), not hereditary monarch. Fixed 5-year term. Contrast: UK has hereditary Queen.

### OBJECTIVES (3 marks)

#### JUSTICE - Social, Economic, Political
- **Social:** No discrimination, equal opportunity, special protection for weaker sections (Arts. 15(4), 16(4), 46)
- **Economic:** No exploitation, equal pay (Art. 39(d)), wealth distribution (Art. 39(b)), living wage (Art. 43)
- **Political:** Equal political rights, universal franchise, participation in governance

#### LIBERTY - Thought, Expression, Belief, Faith, Worship
Essential for human development and democracy. Guaranteed through Part III (Arts. 19, 21, 25-28).

#### EQUALITY - Status and Opportunity
- **Status:** No discrimination, abolition of untouchability (Art. 17), abolition of titles (Art. 18)
- **Opportunity:** Equal opportunity in employment (Art. 16), education, no discrimination (Arts. 15, 16)

#### FRATERNITY - Dignity, Unity & Integrity
Spirit of brotherhood. **Dignity of individual** (Arts. 21, 23, 24). **Unity and integrity** (added 1976 after territorial threats).

### IS PREAMBLE PART OF CONSTITUTION? (3 marks)

**Berubari Union Case, AIR 1960:**
- Held: Preamble **NOT part** of Constitution
- Reasoning: Not enacted under Art. 394, merely introductory

**Kesavananda Bharati v. Kerala, AIR 1973:**
- **Overruled Berubari**
- Held: Preamble **IS part** of Constitution
- Reasoning: Integral part, adopted together Nov 26, 1949

**Current Position:**
- Preamble is part but NOT enforceable
- Not source of substantive powers
- Serves as aid to interpretation

### SCOPE & PURPOSE (1 mark)

1. **Key to makers' mind** - Shows framers' intentions
2. **Aid to interpretation** - When Constitution ambiguous
3. **Embodies basic structure** - Ideals cannot be destroyed by amendment
4. **Can be amended** - 42nd Amendment added Socialist, Secular

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

The Preamble is the soul and spirit of the Constitution. As held in Kesavananda Bharati, it is integral to the Constitution. While not directly enforceable, it guides interpretation of constitutional provisions. The objectives‚ÄîJustice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity‚Äîare not mere declarations but foundational principles upon which the entire constitutional edifice stands, continuing to guide India's journey seven decades after adoption.

## Q.3) Explain the concept of Fundamental Rights. Elaborate its origin and historical development in India.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Fundamental Rights in Part III (Arts. 12-35) are basic human rights guaranteed by the Constitution, justiciable and enforceable through courts. Essential for individual development and human dignity. Dr. Ambedkar called Art. 32 (Constitutional Remedies) the "soul of the Constitution."

### CONCEPT (3 marks)

**Definition:** Basic rights essential for all-round development and dignity, entrenched in Constitution, enforceable through courts.

**Dual Aspect:**
- **Individual perspective:** Justiciable rights enforceable against government
- **State perspective:** Restrictions on government action (Centre, States, local governments)

**Entrenchment:** Rights cannot be taken away by ordinary law. Purpose: "Safeguard basic human rights from political controversy and place beyond reach of political parties."

**Nature:** Substantive and procedural; not absolute; subject to reasonable restrictions; can be suspended during emergency (except Arts. 20, 21); core rights (14, 19, 21) are basic structure.

### ORIGIN & DEVELOPMENT (10 marks)

#### A. PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATIONS (2 marks)

**John Locke (17th Century):**
Man born "with title to perfect freedom and uncontrolled enjoyment of rights of Law of Nature." Power "to preserve his property‚Äîlife, liberty, and estate."

**French Revolution (1789):**
Declaration: "Aim of political association is conservation of natural and inalienable rights of man."

**Core Concept:** Human rights protect individual from oppression and injustice.

#### B. INTERNATIONAL DEVELOPMENTS (2 marks)

**USA (1787):**
- First modern Constitution with justiciable rights
- Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments, 1791)
- Enforceable through courts, pioneered judicial review

**United Nations (1948):**
- Charter of Human Rights
- Universal Declaration: "Recognition of inherent dignity and equal inalienable rights is foundation of freedom, justice, peace"

**European Convention:** Regional protection instrument

**UK:** Traditionally no Bill of Rights (Rule of Law), Human Rights Act 1998 enacted

#### C. INDIA - PRE-INDEPENDENCE (2 marks)

**Problem:** British rule violated rights‚Äîarbitrary detention, speech restrictions, discriminatory laws, denial of political rights.

**Response:**
- 1895: Dadabhai Naoroji demanded self-government
- 1917: Annie Besant's Commonwealth of India Bill (included rights)
- 1928: **Nehru Report** proposed Fundamental Rights
- 1931: **Karachi Resolution** listed basic rights
- 1935: Government of India Act (NO Bill of Rights‚Äîinadequate)

#### D. CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY (1946-1950) (2 marks)

**Need for FRs in India:**
- **Fragmented society:** Religious, cultural, linguistic diversity‚Äîneed security
- **New democracy:** No democratic traditions‚Äîrisk of majority oppression
- **Historical experience:** Framers suffered colonial suppression

**Advisory Committee:** Chairman Sardar Patel. Examined various models, borrowed from USA.

#### E. POST-INDEPENDENCE EVOLUTION (2 marks)

**Phase 1 (1950):** Part III enacted (Arts. 12-35), seven categories

**Phase 2 (1951):** First Amendment‚ÄîArt. 15(4) for backward classes, Arts. 31A, 31B for land reforms

**Phase 3:** Judicial expansion
- Gopalan (1950): Narrow interpretation of Art. 21
- **Maneka Gandhi (1978):** Revolutionary expansion‚Äîdue process, Arts. 14,19,21 interconnected
- Implied rights: livelihood, privacy, environment, dignity

**Phase 4 (1978):** 44th Amendment deleted property as FR (Arts. 19(1)(f), 31)

**Phase 5 (2002):** 86th Amendment added Art. 21A (Right to Education)

**Phase 6 (1973):** **Kesavananda Bharati**‚ÄîFRs (especially 14, 19, 21) declared **basic structure**, cannot be destroyed by amendment

### CLASSIFICATION (1 mark)

Seven categories: Equality (14-18), Freedom (19-22), Against Exploitation (23-24), Religion (25-28), Cultural/Educational (29-30), Property (diluted), Constitutional Remedies (32-35)

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Fundamental Rights evolved from 17th century philosophy to concrete constitutional guarantees, influenced by international movements and national freedom struggle. From Gopalan's narrow view to Maneka's expansive interpretation, from excluding privacy to recognizing it (Puttaswamy), evolution continues. These rights remain bulwark of Indian democracy, protecting liberty while adapting to contemporary challenges‚Äîdynamic provisions responding to felt necessities of times.

## Q.4) Explain the Nature and Importance of Directive Principles. Write a detailed note on Directive Principles enumerated in constitution of India.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV, Arts. 36-51), borrowed from Irish Constitution, embody socio-economic objectives for independent India. Though non-justiciable per Article 37, they are "fundamental in governance" with State duty to apply in lawmaking.

### NATURE (3 marks)

**Article 37:** "Provisions not enforceable by court, but principles fundamental in governance, State's duty to apply."

**Characteristics:**

**1. Non-Justiciable:** Cannot be enforced in court, no legal remedy if not implemented. Why? Require financial resources, involve policy matters, need progressive realization, depend on economic capacity.

**2. Fundamental in Governance:** Despite non-justiciability, State has constitutional duty to apply.

**3. Positive Obligations:** Impose duty on State to **do** (unlike most FRs‚ÄîState not to do).

**4. Socio-Economic Rights:** Provide economic democracy complementing political democracy (FRs).

### IMPORTANCE (2 marks)

1. **Welfare State Foundation:** Roadmap for transformation
2. **Complement FRs:** FRs=political democracy; DPs=economic democracy; together=comprehensive
3. **Conscience of Constitution:** Reflect ideals and aspirations
4. **Aid Interpretation:** Courts read FRs with DPs (**Unnikrishnan:** education from Art. 21 + Art. 45)
5. **Political Accountability:** Citizens judge government performance
6. **Inspire Legislation:** Legal Services Act (39A), Maternity Act (42), Environment Act (48A), 73rd Amendment (40)

**Minerva Mills, 1980:** "Balance between Parts III and IV is basic structure."

### DIRECTIVE PRINCIPLES ENUMERATED (8 marks)

#### A. SOCIALISTIC PRINCIPLES (3 marks)

**Article 38:** Welfare state‚Äîjustice (social, economic, political), minimize inequalities

**Article 39 (Most Important):** Six clauses (LWWCH):
- (a) Adequate **livelihood** for men and women
- (b) Material resources subserve common good (**wealth**)
- (c) No concentration of **wealth**
- (d) Equal pay for equal work (**women**)
- (e) Workers' health/strength not abused (**children**)
- (f) **Children** opportunities in **healthy** manner

**Art. 31C:** Laws for clauses (b),(c) protected from Arts. 14, 19 challenge

**Article 39A (1976):** Free legal aid‚Äîpromote justice, equal opportunity. **Khatri v. Bihar:** State cannot plead inability. Implementation: Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987.

**Article 41:** Right to work, education, public assistance (unemployment, old age, sickness)

**Article 42:** Just/humane work conditions, maternity relief. Implementation: Maternity Benefit Act, 1961

**Article 43:** Living wage, decent standard, cottage industries. Implementation: Minimum Wages Act

**Article 43A (1976):** Workers' participation in management

**Article 43B (2011):** Promote co-operative societies

**Article 47:** Public health, nutrition, prohibit intoxicating drinks (except medicinal)

#### B. GANDHIAN PRINCIPLES (2 marks)

Based on Gandhi's philosophy:

**Article 40:** Village panchayats, self-government. **Implementation:** 73rd Amendment, 1992 (Panchayati Raj)

**Article 43:** Cottage industries (covered above)

**Article 46:** SC/ST welfare‚Äîeducational/economic interests, protect from exploitation. **Implementation:** Reservations, SC/ST Atrocities Act, 1989

**Article 48:** Agriculture/animal husbandry on scientific lines, **prohibit cow slaughter**

**Article 48A (1976):** Protect environment, forests, wildlife. Implementation: Environment Protection Act, 1986. **M.C. Mehta cases** relied on this.

#### C. LIBERAL-INTELLECTUAL PRINCIPLES (2 marks)

**Article 44:** **Uniform Civil Code** ‚≠ê Most controversial. **Status: NOT IMPLEMENTED.** Different personal laws continue. Debate: religious freedom vs gender justice. Goa exception.

**Article 45:** Originally education up to 14; **After 86th Amendment, 2002:** Early childhood care up to 6 years (education 6-14 now FR under Art. 21A)

**Article 49:** Protect monuments of national importance. Implementation: Ancient Monuments Act

**Article 50:** Separate judiciary from executive. Implementation: Largely achieved

**Article 51:** Promote international peace, respect international law, arbitration for disputes

### FRs-DPs RELATIONSHIP (1 mark)

**Evolution:**
- **Champakam (1951):** FRs prevail over DPs
- **Minerva Mills (1980):** FRs and DPs **complementary**, balance is **basic structure**, neither supreme

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Directive Principles envision welfare state ensuring socio-economic justice. Though non-justiciable, they guide policy and have been progressively implemented. Balance between FRs and DPs (Minerva Mills) is constitutional genius‚ÄîFRs ensure political democracy and liberty, DPs aim at economic democracy and welfare. Together they chart journey from negative state to welfare state, making DPs the conscience and hope of the Constitution.

## Q.5) Discuss the various freedoms under the Indian Constitution. Discuss relevant reasonable restriction on those freedoms.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Article 19 guarantees six fundamental freedoms to all **citizens** of India, essential for democracy and human development. Originally seven freedoms; property (19(1)(f)) deleted by 44th Amendment, 1978. These freedoms are not absolute but subject to reasonable restrictions for specified grounds.

### THE SIX FREEDOMS (6 marks - 1 mark each)

**Mnemonic: SAMMPR**

#### 1. Speech and Expression (19(1)(a))

**Most important freedom**

**Scope:** Freedom of press (implied‚Äî**Romesh Thappar, 1950**), right to know, commercial speech, broadcast, symbolic speech, right to silence.

**Restrictions (19(2) - 8 grounds):** Sovereignty, Security, Foreign relations, Public order, Decency, Morality, Contempt, Defamation, Incitement (Mnemonic: SS-FP-DC-DI)

**Shreya Singhal v. UOI, 2015:** Section 66A IT Act struck down‚Äîterms "offensive," "annoying" too vague, violated 19(1)(a).

#### 2. Assembly (19(1)(b))

Peaceable, unarmed assemblies‚Äîmeetings, demonstrations, processions.

**Restrictions (19(3) - 2 grounds):** Sovereignty, Public order

#### 3. Association (19(1)(c))

Form trade unions, political parties, societies, co-operatives.

**Restrictions (19(4) - 3 grounds):** Sovereignty, Public order, Morality

**Note:** Right to strike NOT fundamental right.

#### 4. Movement (19(1)(d))

Move freely throughout India, no internal passports.

**Restrictions (19(5) - 2 grounds):** General public, Protection of Scheduled Tribes

#### 5. Residence (19(1)(e))

Reside and settle anywhere in India. Permanent settlement (differs from temporary movement).

**Restrictions (19(5) - Same):** General public, Scheduled Tribes protection

#### 6. Profession (19(1)(g))

**Most litigated.** Includes profession, occupation, trade, business.

**Restrictions (19(6) - 3 grounds):** General public, Professional/technical qualifications, State monopoly/nationalization

**Mirzapur case, 2006:** Cow slaughter ban valid‚Äîreasonable restriction, Art. 48 (DP) supports.

### TEST FOR REASONABLENESS (2 marks)

**Two-Fold:**

**Procedural:** Competent authority, proper procedure, legislative competence

**Substantive:** Not arbitrary/excessive, proportionate, necessary, fair and just

**Burden:** On State to prove reasonableness

**Maneka Gandhi, 1978:** Arts. 14, 19, 21 interconnected. Restriction must satisfy Art. 14 (not arbitrary) and be reasonable, fair, just (Art. 21).

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Six freedoms are essential for democracy and individual development. Reasonable restrictions balance individual liberty with social welfare and national security. Constitution wisely recognizes absolute freedom impossible in organized society while ensuring restrictions don't become oppressive tools. Maneka Gandhi doctrine strengthened freedoms by requiring all restrictions be reasonable, fair, and just.

## Q.6) Discuss fundamental freedom of protection in Respect of Conviction for offences under the Indian Constitution.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Article 20 provides fundamental protections to persons in respect of conviction for offences. These safeguards ensure criminal law is not used as oppression tool and accused have basic protections. Article 20 available to **all persons** (citizens and non-citizens), reflects natural justice and fairness in criminal proceedings.

### THREE PROTECTIONS (7 marks)

#### 1. PROTECTION AGAINST EX-POST FACTO LAW (Art. 20(1)) - 2.5 marks

**Text:** "No person convicted except for violation of law in force at time of commission of act, nor subjected to penalty greater than might have been inflicted under law in force at commission time."

**Two Protections:**

**a) No Conviction Without Law:** Cannot be convicted except for law violation **in force** at act time. Retrospective criminal laws prohibited. Person must know act was offence.

**b) No Greater Penalty:** Penalty cannot exceed prescribed at offence time. If reduced later, benefit available. If increased, original lower penalty applies.

**Principle:** Nullum crimen sine lege (No crime without law)

**Rationale:** Fair warning, certainty in criminal law, protection against arbitrary prosecution

**Scope:** Only conviction/penalty; NOT procedural changes (can be retrospective unless affects rights under Art. 21)

#### 2. PROTECTION AGAINST DOUBLE JEOPARDY (Art. 20(2)) - 2.5 marks

**Text:** "No person prosecuted and punished for same offence more than once."

**Principle:** Nemo debet bis vexari (No one vexed twice for same matter)

**Requirements:**
1. **Prosecuted** by court/judicial tribunal
2. Prosecution resulted in **punishment** (conviction or acquittal)
3. Subsequent prosecution for **same offence**

**"Same Offence":** Facts substantially same, legal ingredients same

**Protected:** Against second prosecution after conviction/acquittal, against second punishment for same offence

**NOT Protected:** Administrative proceedings after trial, departmental proceedings, different offences from same facts, appeals/revisions

**Exception:** Higher court sets aside, orders retrial‚Äîcontinuation of first, not second prosecution

#### 3. PROTECTION AGAINST SELF-INCRIMINATION (Art. 20(3)) - 2 marks

**Text:** "No person accused of offence shall be compelled to be witness against himself."

**Principle:** Nemo tenetur seipsum accusare (No one bound to accuse himself)

**Scope:** Compulsion to give oral testimony, produce documents, give statements, provide evidence against oneself

**Requirements:** (1) Person **accused** of offence (2) **Compulsion** exists (3) To be witness **against himself**

**Protected:** Right to remain silent, not answer questions, not give statements

**NOT Protected (Can be compelled):** Physical examination, blood samples, fingerprints, specimen signatures, handwriting, voice samples‚Äînot "testimony"

**Application:** Protects during interrogation and trial, basis for lawyer right (Art. 22), confessions to police inadmissible (except S.27 Evidence Act)

### RELATIONSHIP (1 mark)

**Article 21:** Life/liberty includes fair criminal procedure

**Article 22:** Specific arrest safeguards‚Äîinformation, lawyer, production before magistrate

**Together:** Arts. 20, 21, 22 provide comprehensive criminal justice protection

**Maneka Gandhi:** All read together; procedure reasonable, fair, just

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Article 20's three protections ensure fairness in criminal proceedings. Against retrospective criminalization, double jeopardy, and self-incrimination‚Äîuniversally recognized natural justice principles. Prevent criminal law misuse for oppression, ensure State's prosecution power exercised fairly and justly. With Arts. 21 and 22, create constitutional shield protecting personal liberty and dignity in criminal justice system.

## Q.7) Write the critical view on relevancy of caste for reservation to the Backward Classes.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Caste-based reservations remain most debated constitutional issue. Art. 16(4) empowers State to reserve for "backward class" inadequately represented. Critical question: Should caste be criterion, or economic/other factors? Debate peaked in **Indra Sawhney v. UOI, 1993** (Mandal Commission case).

### CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK (1 mark)

**Art. 15(4):** Special provisions for "socially and educationally backward classes"
**Art. 16(4):** Reservation for "backward class" inadequately represented

**Judicial interpretation:** "Backward class" = socially AND educationally backward‚Äînot merely economic or solely caste.

### JUDICIAL EVOLUTION (3 marks)

**K.C. Vasanth Kumar, 1985:** Five judges, five opinions:
- Chandrachud: Economic backwardness
- Desai: Only economic
- Chinnappa Reddy: Poverty, **caste**, occupation, habitation
- Sen: Poverty predominant; caste for identification
- Venkataramiah: **Caste factor relevant**

**Result:** No unanimous view‚Äîdiscretion to government.

**Indra Sawhney, 1993 (Mandal):**

**Background:** Mandal Commission (1979) identified OBCs (52% population), recommended 27% reservation based substantially on caste.

**Court held:** **Caste ONE factor, NOT SOLE factor** to assess backwardness.

**Reasoning:** Caste may indicate social backwardness (historical discrimination), but insufficient alone. Must combine with educational/social indicators.

**Creamy Layer (Most Important):**
- Socially/economically advanced members within backward caste **must be excluded**
- Even if belong to backward caste
- Purpose: Benefit truly disadvantaged, not privileged

### ARGUMENTS FOR CASTE-BASED (2 marks)

1. **Historical:** Centuries of caste-based discrimination, lower castes denied opportunities
2. **Social Reality:** Caste still determines status, occupational segregation
3. **Identifiability:** Easily identifiable, administrative convenience
4. **Constitutional Recognition:** SC/ST identified by caste, Arts. 15(4), 16(4) recognize indirectly
5. **Substantive Equality:** Formal equality insufficient, affirmative action needed

### ARGUMENTS AGAINST SOLE RELIANCE (2 marks)

1. **Over-Inclusive:** Includes affluent backward caste members, benefits monopolized
2. **Under-Inclusive:** Excludes poor from forward castes, ignores economic backwardness
3. **Perpetuates Caste:** Reinforces caste identity, contradicts casteless society goal (Art. 16(2) prohibits caste discrimination, Art. 17 abolishes untouchability)
4. **Merit/Efficiency:** May compromise merit, affects administration efficiency
5. **Social Tension:** Creates divisions, forward-backward conflicts

### BALANCED APPROACH (1 mark)

**Optimal Position:**
1. **Caste as indicator, not sole:** Combine with economic status, education, occupation, social indicators
2. **Creamy layer mandatory:** Per Indra Sawhney, exclude advanced, periodic review
3. **Economic criteria:** 103rd Amendment, 2019: 10% for EWS beyond caste
4. **Sunset clause:** After 70+ years, reassess; graduation of beneficiaries
5. **Comprehensive policy:** Quality education, employment generation, skill development, address root causes

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Caste relevancy is nuanced. Cannot ignore given India's reality, but shouldn't be sole criterion. Indra Sawhney's creamy layer provides balance‚Äîrecognizing caste as indicator while ensuring benefits reach truly disadvantaged. EWS reservations show move toward economic criteria. Ultimate goal: society where reservations unnecessary due to achieved equality. Till then, balanced approach considering caste with socio-economic indicators and mandatory creamy layer exclusion is most reasonable constitutional path.

## Q.8) Discuss the Nature and Importance of Fundamental Duties enumerated in Indian Constitution.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Fundamental Duties in Article 51A added by **42nd Amendment, 1976** on Swaran Singh Committee recommendation. Originally 10 duties; **86th Amendment, 2002** added 11th. Moral and civic obligations of every citizen. Borrowed from erstwhile USSR Constitution, adapted to Indian conditions.

### BACKGROUND (1.5 marks)

**Why Added:**
1. **Rights without duties incomplete:** Constitution had elaborate rights but no corresponding duties
2. **Emergency period (1975-77):** Need for greater discipline, promote patriotism and unity
3. **National integration:** Strengthen unity bonds, build national character
4. **International trend:** Many Constitutions include duties, UNESCO recommendation

### NATURE (1.5 marks)

**1. Non-Justiciable:** Like DPs, cannot be enforced in court. No legal remedy for non-performance.

**2. Moral/Civic Obligations:** Ethical nature, appeal to conscience, educational value

**3. Not Absolute:** Cannot override FRs. **Bijoe Emmanuel v. Kerala, 1987:** Jehovah's Witness children refused to sing anthem (religious belief), stood respectfully. Expulsion quashed. **FDs cannot override FRs.**

**4. Citizens Only:** Apply to citizens, not all persons

**5. Indirect Enforcement:** Through legislation, judicial interpretation, education

### ELEVEN DUTIES (4 marks)

**Article 51A - Mnemonic: CHAIRS-VIP-ED**

**(a)** Abide by Constitution, respect ideals/institutions, National Flag and Anthem

**(b)** Cherish and follow noble ideals inspiring freedom struggle

**(c)** Uphold and protect sovereignty, unity and integrity of India

**(d)** Defend country, render national service when called

**(e)** Promote harmony and common brotherhood transcending diversities; renounce practices derogatory to women's dignity

**(f)** Value and preserve rich heritage of composite culture

**(g)** Protect and improve natural environment (forests, lakes, rivers, wildlife), compassion for living creatures

**(h)** Develop scientific temper, humanism, spirit of inquiry and reform

**(i)** Safeguard public property, abjure violence

**(j)** Strive towards excellence in all spheres so nation rises to higher levels

**(k)** **[Added 2002]** Parent/guardian provide education opportunities to child/ward age 6-14 (corresponds to Art. 21A)

### IMPORTANCE (1.5 marks)

1. **Complete Framework:** Rights + Duties = Balance
2. **Promote Patriotism:** Love for country, respect national symbols, integration
3. **Build Character:** Scientific temper, harmony, excellence
4. **Environmental Consciousness:** Duty to protect (Art. 51A(g)) supports Art. 48A (DP), Art. 21 (clean environment). **M.C. Mehta cases** relied on this.
5. **Social Reform:** Renounce practices derogatory to women, promote composite culture, combat superstition
6. **Aid Interpretation:** Courts consider while deciding. **AIIMS case:** 51A(j) (excellence) supports merit
7. **Legislative Basis:** Can inspire legislation‚ÄîPrevention of Insults to National Honour Act, 1971

### ENFORCEMENT (0.5 mark)

**Direct:** Not possible (non-justiciable)

**Indirect:** (1) Legislation by Parliament (2) Judicial interpretation as aid (3) Education‚Äîcurriculum, awareness

### CONCLUSION (0.5 mark)

Important addition completing rights-duties framework. Though non-justiciable and cannot override FRs, serve as reminders of citizens' responsibilities toward nation, society, fellow citizens. In democracy, rights empower, duties ensure contribution to nation-building. Balance between rights and duties essential for healthy democracy. Duties on national integration, women's dignity, environment, excellence remain relevant guides for responsible citizenship.

## Q.9) State and explain equality before law and equal protection of law with relevant case laws.

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Article 14: "State shall not deny to any person equality before law or equal protection of laws within territory of India."

Cornerstone of equality, declared **basic feature** (**Kesavananda, 1973**). Necessary corollary of Rule of Law. Embodies two concepts‚Äîequality before law (negative) and equal protection (positive)‚Äîensuring substantive equality.

### EQUALITY BEFORE LAW (3 marks)

**Concept:** Negative concept from British Rule of Law (Dicey).

**Meaning:** No special privilege, all equally subject to ordinary law, no one above law.

**Implications:** Same law for all, same courts, no privileged class.

**Exceptions (Reasonable):**
- President, Governors (Art. 361)‚Äîoffice protection
- Foreign diplomats‚Äîinternational law
- Judges‚Äîindependence

**Sri Srinivasa Theatre:** "Equality before law" dynamic‚Äîno privileged class (one facet); State obligation to bring equal society (another facet).

### EQUAL PROTECTION (3 marks)

**Concept:** Positive concept from American Constitution (XIV Amendment).

**Meaning:** NOT identical law for all or universal application. MEANS: Equality in equal circumstances. Like treated alike without discrimination.

**Reasonable Classification Permitted:**

**Art. 14 forbids CLASS LEGISLATION but permits REASONABLE CLASSIFICATION**

**Two-Fold Test (I-R):**

**Test 1‚ÄîIntelligible Differentia:** Real, substantial distinction; not arbitrary/artificial

**Test 2‚ÄîRational Nexus:** Differentia must have rational nexus with object sought

**Courts decide reasonableness.**

### CASE LAWS (3 marks)

**Bachan Singh, 1982:** "Rule of law excludes arbitrariness. Where arbitrariness/unreasonableness, denial of rule of law." **Art. 14 primarily guarantee against arbitrariness.**

**Valid Classifications:**

**Balsara, 1951:** Prohibition law differentiating Indians and short-stay foreigners‚Äîvalid (reasonable basis).

**Kishan Singh, 1955:** Rajasthan Act for Marwar region only‚Äîvalid. Art. 14 prohibits unequal treatment of **similarly situated**. Act bad only if similar conditions elsewhere (not proved). Not discriminatory merely because doesn't apply to whole State.

**Charanjit Lal, 1951:** Act for single company's takeover‚Äîvalid. Single entity can constitute class if reasonable basis.

**Invalid Classification:**

**Anwar Ali Sarkar:** Bengal law for special courts, Government directs which offences, procedure less advantageous to accused‚Äî**invalid**. No reasonable classification, arbitrary power.

**Arbitrariness:**

**E.P. Royappa, 1974:** Chief Secretary transferred allegedly arbitrarily. **"Arbitrary action necessarily involves negation of equality."** Expanded Art. 14 from classification to anti-arbitrariness. Abuse of power hit by Art. 14.

**Maneka Gandhi, 1978:** Arts. 14, 19, 21 **not mutually exclusive**, read together. **Principles of natural justice integral** to Art. 14. Order without hearing=arbitrary=violates Art. 14.

**Ramana Shetty, 1979:** Airport Authority awarded contract to unqualified person. Should have stuck to prescribed conditions. Discriminatory, didn't give equal chance. **"Plainly arbitrary without reason."**

**Administrative Discretion:**

**Air India v. Nergesh, 1981:** Retirement age 35 for air hostesses, MD could extend to 45 at discretion‚Äî**invalid**. Uncanalized, unguided discretion, no guidelines. Violates Art. 14.

### CONCLUSION (1 mark)

Art. 14's two concepts provide comprehensive protection against discrimination. Equality before law ensures none above law; equal protection ensures affirmative action for equals. Reasonable classification allows different treatment if intelligible differentia exists with rational nexus. Modern interpretation expanded Art. 14 to strike arbitrariness, making it powerful tool against executive excess. As observed, Art. 14 is truly "heart" of FRs chapter, protecting against discriminatory laws and arbitrary state action.

## Q.10(a) Protection against Ex-post Facto Law

### PROVISION (1 mark)

**Article 20(1):** "No person convicted of offence except for violation of law in force at time of commission of act, nor subjected to penalty greater than might have been inflicted under law in force at time of commission."

### MEANING (2 marks)

**Ex-post facto law:** Retrospective criminal law criminalizing act not offence when committed.

**Two Protections:**
1. **No conviction without law:** Cannot be convicted for act not offence when done. Law must be in force at commission time. Retrospective criminal legislation prohibited.
2. **No greater penalty:** Penalty cannot exceed what prescribed when offence committed. If reduced later, benefit available.

### PRINCIPLE (1 mark)

**Nullum crimen sine lege** (No crime without law)

**Rationale:** Fair warning to citizens, certainty in criminal law, protection against arbitrary state action, fundamental natural justice principle.

**Limitations:** Applies only to conviction/penalty for offences, NOT to procedural laws (changeable retrospectively), civil liabilities, tax laws, preventive detention.

### SIGNIFICANCE (1 mark)

Ensures rule of law in criminal jurisprudence. Prevents State from using criminal law arbitrarily. Guarantees no punishment for lawful act. Universally recognized principle of criminal justice, essential safeguard of personal liberty.

## Q.10(b) Doctrine of Eclipse with recent judicial pronouncement

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Doctrine of Eclipse applies to **pre-Constitution laws** inconsistent with Fundamental Rights. Under Art. 13(1), laws in force before Jan 26, 1950 inconsistent with FRs are void **to extent of inconsistency**.

### PRINCIPLE (2 marks)

**Doctrine:** Pre-Constitution law inconsistent with FR not **dead** but **eclipsed** (overshadowed). Remains dormant, can become operative if:
- Constitution amended to remove inconsistency, OR
- FR itself amended/deleted

**Rationale:** Pre-Constitution laws enacted without knowledge of FRs, shouldn't be completely struck down‚Äîremain dormant with revival possibility.

**Bhikaji v. M.P.:** Pre-Constitution law restricted trade, violated Art. 19(1)(g). Eclipsed when Constitution commenced. Not dead, only eclipsed. Inoperative against citizens but operative against non-citizens (19(1)(g) only for citizens). Can revive if 19(1)(g) amended.

### DISTINCTION (1 mark)

| Aspect | Pre-Constitution (13(1)) | Post-Constitution (13(2)) |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Status | Eclipsed (dormant) | Void ab initio (dead) |
| Revival | Can revive if FR amended | Cannot revive |
| Rationale | No FR knowledge | Made with FR knowledge |

### SIGNIFICANCE (1 mark)

Saves pre-existing laws from nullification, allows flexibility for future amendments, recognizes different treatment for before/after laws, practical constitutional transition approach. Still relevant when examining old laws. Occasionally invoked for pre-1950 law challenges (though most repealed/replaced).

## Q.10(c) Right to Free Legal Aid

### BASIS (1 mark)

**Article 39A (DP, added 1976):** "State ensure legal system promotes justice on equal opportunity basis, provide free legal aid ensuring opportunities for justice not denied due to economic/other disabilities."

**Article 21:** Fair procedure includes legal aid

**Implementation:** Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987

### JUDICIAL RECOGNITION (2 marks)

**Khatri v. Bihar (II):**
- Facts: Under-trials in Bihar not provided legal aid
- Held: Free legal aid constitutional obligation of State, flows from Art. 21
- State **cannot plead financial/administrative inability**
- Free legal service is fundamental right of poor accused
- "Procedure without legal services to poor accused cannot be reasonable, fair or just"

**Hussainara Khatoon:** Under-trials in Bihar jails for years. Right to speedy trial includes legal aid. State directed to provide.

**Sheela Barse:** Women/children in custody‚Äîlegal aid emphasized.

### SCOPE (1.5 marks)

**Entitled:** Poor/indigent accused, unable to afford lawyer, vulnerable sections (women, children, SC/ST)

**When:** During trial, investigation (custody), appeal

**Implementation:** Legal Services Authorities (national, state, district levels), Lok Adalats, panel of lawyers, free of cost

**Services:** Legal advice, document drafting, court representation, appeals

### CONCLUSION (0.5 mark)

Essential for access to justice and equality. Ensures poverty not barrier to justice. Not charity but constitutional right flowing from Arts. 21 and 39A. Legal Services Authorities Act institutionalized this, making justice promise reality for poor and disadvantaged.

## Q.10(d) Right to Privacy

### INTRODUCTION (1 mark)

Though not express in Constitution, Supreme Court recognized as **implicit part of right to life/personal liberty** under Art. 21. Landmark nine-judge bench in **Puttaswamy v. UOI, 2017** unanimously held privacy is fundamental right under Part III.

### CONSTITUTIONAL BASIS (1.5 marks)

**Article 21:** "No person deprived of life/personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."

**Interpretation:** "Life" means life with human dignity, includes all aspects making life meaningful. Privacy essential for dignity, therefore implied in Art. 21.

**Also protected under:** Art. 19(1)(a) (right not to speak), Art. 19(1)(d) (privacy of movement)

### SCOPE (1.5 marks)

**1. Physical Privacy:** Home sanctity, protection against unlawful search, bodily integrity

**2. Informational Privacy:** Control over personal information, data protection, right to be forgotten

**3. Decisional Privacy:** Personal choices‚Äîmarriage, family, procreation, sexual orientation, food, clothing

**4. Communicational Privacy:** Correspondence, telephone, electronic communications

### JUDICIAL RECOGNITION (1 mark)

**Puttaswamy, 2017 (Nine-judge bench):**
1. Privacy **intrinsic to life and liberty**
2. Fundamental right under Art. 21
3. Also protected under Art. 19
4. Not absolute‚Äîcan be restricted
5. Restrictions must pass: **Legality** (by law), **Legitimate aim** (need), **Proportionality** (least restrictive)

**Overruled:** M.P. Sharma, Kharak Singh (denied FR status)

**Impact:** Aadhaar validity examined, data protection laws required, surveillance regulation needed

**Earlier:** Gobind v. M.P. (recognized but not FR status); PUCL (telephone tapping regulated)

### CONCLUSION (0.5 mark)

Recognition in Puttaswamy is landmark in constitutional jurisprudence. In digital age with vulnerable personal data, critically important. Not absolute‚Äîbalance against national security, public order, crime prevention. Proportionality test ensures invasions justified, necessary, minimal. Empowers individuals to control information and make autonomous decisions, enhancing dignity and freedom.
