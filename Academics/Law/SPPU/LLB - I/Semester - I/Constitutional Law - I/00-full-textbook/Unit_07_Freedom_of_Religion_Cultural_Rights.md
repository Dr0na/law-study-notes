## ‚úùÔ∏è **SECTION 1: RIGHT TO FREEDOM OF RELIGION (ARTICLES 25-28)**

### üåü **Introduction**

**India is a secular state** - 42nd Amendment, 1976 added "Secular" to Preamble

**Secularism in Indian Context:**
- No state religion
- State treats all religions equally
- Equal respect to all religions
- Positive engagement (not strict separation)
- Principled distance approach

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 25: FREEDOM OF CONSCIENCE AND RELIGION**

### Text of Article 25(1)

> **"Subject to public order, morality and health and to the other provisions of this Part, all persons are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and the right freely to profess, practice and propagate religion."**

---

### üîë **Key Features**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Beneficiary** | **All persons** (not just citizens) |
| **Negative Aspect** | Freedom of conscience (inner belief) |
| **Positive Aspects** | Profess, practice, propagate |
| **Subject to** | Public order, morality, health |

---

### Three Components - "PPP"

#### 1. **Profess**
- Declare one's religious beliefs
- Outward expression of faith

#### 2. **Practice**
- Perform religious worship
- Observe religious rituals
- Follow religious practices

#### 3. **Propagate**
- Spread one's religion
- Right to convince others
- **Does NOT include** forced conversion

---

### üìñ **Freedom of Conscience**

**Meaning:**
- Inner freedom of mind
- Freedom to believe or not believe
- Absolute freedom (cannot be restricted)

**Distinguished from:**
- **Profess, practice, propagate** - can be restricted

---

### ‚ö†Ô∏è **Limitations on Article 25(1)**

#### 1. Public Order

**State can restrict** if practice disturbs public order

**Example:** Processions that block traffic

---

#### 2. Morality

**State can regulate** immoral religious practices

**Examples:**
- Sati (widow burning) - prohibited
- Human sacrifice - prohibited
- Devadasi system - prohibited

---

#### 3. Health

**State can restrict** practices harmful to health

**Example:** Compulsory vaccination

---

#### 4. Other Provisions of Part III

**Cannot violate** other Fundamental Rights

**Example:** Untouchability prohibited (Art. 17) even if claimed as religious practice

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Ratilal Panachand Gandhi v. State of Bombay, AIR 1954**

**Facts:** Bombay Prevention of Hindu Bigamous Marriages Act challenged

**Argument:** Polygamy is religious practice for some Hindus

**Held:**
- Act valid
- Polygamy **not essential religious practice**
- State can regulate in interest of reform

---

### Article 25(2) - Social Reform

**State empowered to make laws for:**

#### Article 25(2)(a)
> Regulating or restricting **economic, financial, political or secular** activity associated with religious practice

**Examples:**
- Regulating temple finances
- Managing religious endowments
- Controlling temple administration

---

#### Article 25(2)(b)
> Providing for **social welfare and reform** or throwing open Hindu religious institutions of public character to **all classes of Hindus**

**Explanation:** "Hindus" includes Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists

**Purpose:**
- Remove untouchability
- Allow all Hindus to enter temples
- Social reform of Hinduism

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar, AIR 1954**

**Issue:** What is "religion" under Constitution?

**Held:**

**Religion consists of:**
1. **Essential religious practices** - protected
2. **Non-essential practices** - can be regulated

**Test:** Whether practice is **essential and integral** part of religion?

---

### Essential Religious Practices Doctrine

**Courts will examine:**
- Is practice essential to religion?
- Is it integral to faith?
- Can religion exist without it?

**If NO:** Practice can be regulated/prohibited

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Sardar Syedna Taher Saifuddin v. State of Bombay, AIR 1962**

**Facts:** Dawoodi Bohra community - excommunication challenged

**Issue:** Is excommunication essential religious practice?

**Held:**
- Yes, essential to Bohra community
- Protected under Art. 26

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: M. Ismail Faruqui v. Union of India, AIR 1994**

**Facts:** Babri Masjid-Ram Janmabhoomi dispute

**Issue:** Is mosque essential for Islamic worship?

**Held:**
- Mosque **not essential** for namaz
- Muslims can offer namaz anywhere
- State acquisition of disputed land valid

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 26: FREEDOM TO MANAGE RELIGIOUS AFFAIRS**

### Text

> **"Subject to public order, morality and health, every religious denomination or any section thereof shall have the right:**
>
> **(a) to establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes;**
>
> **(b) to manage its own affairs in matters of religion;**
>
> **(c) to own and acquire movable and immovable property; and**
>
> **(d) to administer such property in accordance with law."**

---

### üîë **Key Features**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Beneficiary** | Religious denomination |
| **Subject to** | Public order, morality, health |
| **Four Rights** | EMOA (Establish, Manage, Own, Administer) |

---

### What is "Religious Denomination"?

**Requirements:**
1. Collection of individuals
2. Common system of beliefs/doctrines
3. Common organization
4. Designation by distinctive name

**Examples:**
- Dawoodi Bohras (Muslim sect)
- Lingayats (Hindu sect)
- Roman Catholics (Christian denomination)

---

### Four Rights - "EMOA"

#### (a) **Establish and Maintain Institutions**

**Can establish:**
- Temples, churches, mosques, gurudwaras
- Charitable institutions (schools, hospitals)
- Monasteries, ashrams

---

#### (b) **Manage Own Affairs**

**In matters of religion:**
- Appointment of priests
- Religious rituals and ceremonies
- Internal management
- Religious doctrines

**State cannot interfere** in these matters

---

#### (c) **Own and Acquire Property**

**Property rights:**
- Movable property
- Immovable property
- For religious purposes

---

#### (d) **Administer Property**

**In accordance with law:**
- State can regulate administration
- Prevent misuse of funds
- Ensure proper accounting
- But cannot interfere with religious practices

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Durgah Committee, Ajmer v. Syed Hussain Ali, AIR 1961**

**Facts:** Durgah Committee Act - State took over management of Ajmer Dargah

**Challenge:** Violates Art. 26

**Held:**
- Act valid
- Management of property is **secular** activity
- State can regulate under Art. 26(d)
- Only **religious practices** protected, not property administration

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Shirur Mutt Case, AIR 1954**

**Facts:** Madras Government took over administration of Hindu mutt

**Held:**
- State can regulate financial administration
- But cannot interfere with religious practices
- Balance between Art. 25 (individual) and Art. 26 (denomination)

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 27: FREEDOM FROM TAXATION FOR RELIGION**

### Text

> **"No person shall be compelled to pay any taxes, the proceeds of which are specifically appropriated in payment of expenses for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or religious denomination."**

---

### üîë **Principle**

**Protects:**
- Freedom from being forced to support a religion
- Through taxation

**Requirements for prohibition:**
1. Tax must be specifically appropriated
2. For promoting/maintaining particular religion

---

### What is Permitted

**General taxes** can be used for:
- Maintaining religious properties (as historical monuments)
- Regulating pilgrimages
- Managing religious endowments

**Why?** Not **"specifically appropriated"** for religion

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 28: FREEDOM FROM RELIGIOUS INSTRUCTION**

### Text of Article 28(1)

> **"No religious instruction shall be provided in any educational institution wholly maintained out of State funds."**

---

### Article 28(2)

> **"Nothing in clause (1) shall apply to an educational institution which is administered by the State but has been established under any endowment or trust which requires that religious instruction shall be imparted in such institution."**

---

### Article 28(3)

> **"No person attending any educational institution recognised by the State or receiving aid out of State funds shall be required to take part in any religious instruction that may be imparted in such institution or to attend any religious worship that may be conducted in such institution or in any premises attached thereto unless such person or, if such person is a minor, his guardian has given his consent thereto."**

---

### üîë **Three Categories of Institutions**

| Category | Religious Instruction? | Article |
|----------|----------------------|---------|
| **1. Wholly State-funded** | ‚ùå Prohibited | 28(1) |
| **2. State-administered, Endowment/Trust** | ‚úÖ Allowed | 28(2) |
| **3. State-recognized/aided** | ‚úÖ Allowed (with consent) | 28(3) |

---

### Rationale

**Article 28 ensures:**
- Secular character of State education
- No compulsion to receive religious instruction
- Parental choice in child's religious education

## ‚úùÔ∏è **SECULARISM IN INDIAN CONTEXT**

### üåç **Western vs Indian Secularism**

| Aspect | Western Secularism | Indian Secularism |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Concept** | Separation of Church and State | Principled distance |
| **State Role** | Passive | Active for reform |
| **Approach** | Negative (no interference) | Positive (equal respect) |
| **Religion & State** | Complete separation | State can regulate secular aspects |

---

### Positive Secularism

**India follows "Positive Secularism":**
- State can regulate religious practices
- For social reform
- To remove superstitions
- To promote social justice

**Examples:**
- Hindu Code Bills
- Entry of women to Sabarimala
- Abolition of Triple Talaq

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: S.R. Bommai v. Union of India, AIR 1994**

**Held:**
- **Secularism is basic feature** of Constitution
- Cannot be destroyed even by amendment
- State must maintain neutrality towards religions

## üéì **SECTION 2: CULTURAL AND EDUCATIONAL RIGHTS (ARTICLES 29-30)**

### üåü **Introduction**

**Purpose:**
- Protect interests of minorities
- Preserve cultural diversity
- Ensure educational rights

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 29: PROTECTION OF INTERESTS OF MINORITIES**

### Text of Article 29(1)

> **"Any section of the citizens residing in the territory of India or any part thereof having a distinct language, script or culture of its own shall have the right to conserve the same."**

---

### Article 29(2)

> **"No citizen shall be denied admission into any educational institution maintained by the State or receiving aid out of State funds on grounds only of religion, race, caste, language or any of them."**

---

### üîë **Two Protections**

#### Article 29(1): Right to Conserve

**Who:** Any section of citizens

**What:** Distinct language, script, culture

**Right:** To conserve

**Beneficiary:** Not just minorities, **any section**

---

#### Article 29(2): No Discrimination in Admission

**Where:** State or State-aided institutions

**Prohibited:** Discrimination on:
- Religion
- Race
- Caste
- Language

**Note:** "Only" is important - reasonable classification allowed

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: State of Bombay v. Bombay Education Society, AIR 1954**

**Issue:** Can State reserve seats for backward classes?

**Held:**
- Yes, Art. 29(2) prohibits discrimination on **"grounds only"**
- Reservation for backward classes valid
- Not discrimination if other factors also considered

---

## üìú **ARTICLE 30: RIGHT OF MINORITIES TO ESTABLISH INSTITUTIONS**

### Text of Article 30(1)

> **"All minorities, whether based on religion or language, shall have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice."**

---

### Article 30(1A)

> **"In making any law providing for the compulsory acquisition of any property of an educational institution established and administered by a minority, referred to in clause (1), the State shall ensure that the amount fixed by or determined under such law for the acquisition of such property is such as would not restrict or abrogate the right guaranteed under that clause."**

---

### Article 30(2)

> **"The State shall not, in granting aid to educational institutions, discriminate against any educational institution on the ground that it is under the management of a minority, whether based on religion or language."**

---

### üîë **Key Features**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Beneficiary** | Minorities (religious or linguistic) |
| **Right** | Establish and administer |
| **Type** | Educational institutions |
| **Protection** | From State discrimination |

---

### Who is "Minority"?

**Two types:**
1. **Religious minority**
2. **Linguistic minority**

**Determination:** At State level (not national level)

**Example:**
- Muslims are minority in most States
- But majority in J&K (earlier)
- Hindus minority in Punjab, Meghalaya, etc.

---

### Two Rights Under Art. 30(1)

#### 1. Right to Establish

**Meaning:**
- Set up educational institution
- Found new institution
- Without State permission (generally)

#### 2. Right to Administer

**Meaning:**
- Manage the institution
- Control day-to-day functioning
- Make rules for admission, fees, etc.
- Appoint staff

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Very Rev. Mother Provincial v. State of Madras, AIR 1970**

**Issue:** Can State regulate minority institutions?

**Held:**
- Yes, State can regulate
- But cannot destroy right to administer
- Regulations must not be excessive

---

### Extent of Right to Administer

**Minority institutions CAN:**
- Give preference to students of same community (subject to limits)
- Charge fees as decided
- Frame own rules
- Appoint teachers of choice

**Minority institutions CANNOT:**
- Charge exorbitant fees
- Deny quality education
- Violate minimum standards
- Discriminate unreasonably

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Landmark Case: T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka, AIR 2005**

**Most important case on minority rights**

**Issues:**
1. Who determines minority status?
2. Scope of right to administer?
3. Can State regulate admissions, fees?

**Held:**

#### On Minority Status:
- **Determined at State level**, not national
- Minority in that State protected

#### On Right to Administer:
- **Extensive autonomy** for minority institutions
- Right to admit students
- Right to set reasonable fees
- **But:** Must maintain excellence
- Cannot be exploited for profit

#### On State Regulation:
- State can ensure academic standards
- Can prescribe minimum qualifications for teachers
- Can regulate conditions of employment
- **Cannot** take over management
- **Cannot** destroy minority character

#### Private Unaided Institutions:
- Greater autonomy than aided institutions
- Capitation fee not allowed
- Must follow transparent admission process

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: St. Stephen's College v. University of Delhi, AIR 1992**

**Facts:** Christian minority college reserved 50% seats for Christians

**Held:**
- Reservation valid
- Minority institution can give **preference** to minority community students
- But admission must be based on merit within community

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Pa. Inamdar v. State of Maharashtra, AIR 2005**

**Issue:** Unaided minority professional colleges - State control over admissions?

**Held:**
- Unaided minority institutions have autonomy
- But must follow transparent procedure
- Cannot charge capitation fee
- Minority character doesn't give unfettered discretion

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Islamic Academy of Education v. State of Karnataka, AIR 2003**

**Facts:** State issued regulations for admission to professional colleges

**Challenge:** By minority institutions

**Held:**
- State can ensure fair procedure
- Centralized admission process permissible
- But minority institutions retain character

---

### Article 30(1A) - Acquisition Protection

**Added by:** 44th Amendment, 1978

**Provides:**
- If State acquires property of minority institution
- Must pay **adequate compensation**
- Must not abrogate right under Art. 30(1)

---

### Article 30(2) - No Discrimination in Aid

**State cannot:**
- Refuse aid because institution is minority-run
- Discriminate in granting aid

**State can:**
- Attach reasonable conditions for aid
- Ensure quality standards
