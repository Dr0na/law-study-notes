## ‚öñÔ∏è **ARTICLE 20: PROTECTION IN RESPECT OF CONVICTION FOR OFFENCES**

### Three Protections Under Article 20

```
Article 20
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ (1) Protection against Ex-post Facto Law
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ (2) Guarantee against Double Jeopardy
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ (3) Privilege against Self-Incrimination
```

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **PROTECTION AGAINST EX-POST FACTO LAW - Article 20(1)**

#### Text

> **"No person shall be convicted of any offence except for violation of a law in force at the time of the commission of the act, nor be subjected to a penalty greater than that which might have been inflicted under the law in force at the time of commission of the offence."**

#### Two Protections

| Protection | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| **(a)** | No conviction except for **violation of law in force** at time of act |
| **(b)** | No penalty **greater than** what law prescribed at time of offence |

#### Principle

> üîë **No retrospective criminal law can be enacted.**

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **GUARANTEE AGAINST DOUBLE JEOPARDY - Article 20(2)**

#### Text

> **"No person shall be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once."**

#### Requirements

For Art. 20(2) to apply:
1. ‚úÖ **Prosecution and punishment** for an offence
2. ‚úÖ **Second prosecution** for same offence

#### Principle

> üîë **"Nemo debet bis vexari"** - No one should be vexed twice for the same cause.

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **PRIVILEGE AGAINST SELF-INCRIMINATION - Article 20(3)**

#### Text

> **"No person accused of any offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself."**

#### Protection

**Against:**
- ‚ùå Compulsion to be witness
- ‚ùå Against oneself

**Includes:**
- ‚úÖ Oral testimony
- ‚úÖ Documents/materials producing evidence

#### Principle

> üîë **Right to remain silent.** No person can be forced to give evidence that may incriminate them.

## üåü **ARTICLE 21: PROTECTION OF RIGHT TO LIFE AND PERSONAL LIBERTY**

### Text of Article 21

> **"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."**

---

### üîë **KEY FEATURES**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Beneficiary** | **ALL persons** (citizens + non-citizens) |
| **Rights Protected** | Life + Personal Liberty |
| **Limitation** | Subject to "procedure established by law" |
| **Most Expansive FR** | Implies numerous other rights |

> ‚öñÔ∏è **"Articles 21 and 14 are the heart of the chapter on Fundamental Rights."**

---

## üìö **EVOLUTION OF INTERPRETATION**

### Stage 1: A.K. Gopalan Era (1950-1978)

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras, AIR 1950**

**Narrow Interpretation:**

| Aspect | Gopalan View |
|--------|--------------|
| **Personal Liberty** | Limited to freedom from physical restraint |
| **Scope** | Only punitive & preventive detention |
| **Relationship** | Arts. 19, 21, 22 mutually exclusive |
| **Due Process** | ‚ùå Rejected - word 'due' absent |
| **Natural Justice** | Not required in law |

#### Two Major Principles from Gopalan

**Principle 1:**
> Arts. 19, 21, 22 are **mutually exclusive and independent**. Art. 19 not applicable to law affecting personal liberty (Art. 21 applies).

**Principle 2:**
> Law affecting life/personal liberty cannot be unconstitutional merely for lack of natural justice/due procedure. Legislature free to lay down any procedure.

---

### Stage 2: Softening of Rigid View (1970-1978)

#### üèõÔ∏è **Bank Nationalization Case (1970)**

Beginning of new trend:
- Art. 19(1)(f) applied to law enacted under Art. 31(2)

#### üèõÔ∏è **Bennett Coleman Case**

- Law affecting freedom of speech assessable under Art. 19(2)
- Even if not enacted directly to control speech

#### üèõÔ∏è **P.L. Lakhanpal v. Union of India**

- Review of detention is **quasi-judicial function**
- Right of representation should be given
- Court can impose natural justice by interpretation

> üìå **Significant:** While lack of natural justice in law would not invalidate it, Court could still impose natural justice interpretatively.

---

### Stage 3: Maneka Gandhi Revolution (1978 onwards)

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India, AIR 1978** ‚≠ê

> **LANDMARK CASE - Practically overruled Gopalan**

**The New Approach:**

| Aspect | Maneka Gandhi View |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Personal Liberty** | Broad interpretation |
| **Relationship** | Arts. 14, 19, 21 **NOT mutually exclusive** |
| **Due Process** | **Introduced** - procedure must be reasonable, fair, just |
| **Review** | Law itself can be declared unconstitutional |
| **Natural Justice** | Integral part of Art. 21 |

---

#### üîë **KEY PRINCIPLES FROM MANEKA GANDHI**

##### Principle 1: Interconnection of Articles

> ‚öñÔ∏è **Arts. 14, 19 and 21 are NOT mutually exclusive.** 
> 
> Nexus established between these three Articles.

##### Principle 2: Higher Standard

> Law prescribing procedure for depriving personal liberty must meet requirements of **Art. 19**.

##### Principle 3: Dual Compliance

> Procedure established by law in Art. 21 must answer requirement of **Art. 14** as well.

##### Principle 4: Judicial Review

> Court may declare **law itself unconstitutional** by reading Art. 21 with Arts. 14 and 19.

##### Principle 5: Anti-Arbitrariness

> Art. 14 outlaws **unreasonableness and arbitrariness**.

#### Final Principle

> üí° **"The attempt of the court should be to expand the reach and ambit of fundamental rights rather than to attenuate their meaning."**

---

## üîç **DUE PROCESS OF LAW**

### U.S. Constitutional Concept

**V Amendment (USA):**
> "No person shall be deprived of life, liberty or property, **without due process of law**"

**Under 'due process':**
- Courts become arbiter of reasonableness
- Both **substantive** and **procedural** provisions examined

---

### Indian Position

#### Gopalan's Rejection of Due Process

**Reasons for rejection:**

| # | Reason |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Word **'due' absent** in Art. 21 - significant omission |
| 2 | **Drafting history** - 'due process' words deliberately dropped |
| 3 | **Complexity concerns** - importing doctrine would make things complicated |

---

#### Maneka Gandhi's Introduction of Due Process

> ‚úÖ **Post-Maneka: "Procedure" for Art. 21 must be reasonable, fair and just.**

**This is introduction of due process through back door.**

| Type | Included? |
|------|-----------|

| **Ordinances** | ‚úÖ Yes |

## ‚öñÔ∏è **"PROCEDURE" UNDER ARTICLE 21**

### Post-Maneka Standard

> **Procedure must be:**
> - ‚úÖ Reasonable
> - ‚úÖ Fair
> - ‚úÖ Just

**Example:** Procedure of imprisoning poor person for debt failure violative of Art. 21:
> "Unless there is proof of wilful failure to pay despite sufficient means."

## üéØ **ONUS OF PROOF**

### Distinction

| Articles | Burden of Proof |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Art. 14** | On challenger (initial presumption of validity) |
| **Arts. 19 & 21** | Once right shown infringed, **burden on State** to justify |

### For Articles 19 and 21

**Once petitioner shows right taken away:**

State must justify by showing:
- ‚úÖ Restriction is reasonable (Art. 19), **OR**
- ‚úÖ Procedure is just, fair, reasonable (Art. 21)

## ‚è±Ô∏è **SPEEDY TRIAL**

### Principle

> ‚úÖ There is a **right to speedy trial** under Art. 21.

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Abdul Rehman Antulay v. R.S. Naik**

**Held:**
- Right to speedy trial exists
- **But no time limit** within which trial must be completed
- Obligation to proceed with **reasonable promptitude**

#### Scope

> üìå **Right to speedy trial encompasses ALL stages:**
> - Investigation
> - Inquiry
> - Trial
> - Appeal
> - Revision
> - Re-trial

---

### Long Pre-Trial Confinement

**Problem:**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Grievous aspect of criminal justice administration
- Poor persons languish in prisons awaiting trial
- Cannot post bail
- Perpetrates great injustice

**Principle:**
> Imposing **unjust or harsh conditions** while granting bail is **violative of Art. 21**.

---

### TADA Cases

**Ordinarily:** Release of undertrials on bail **extremely restricted** in TADA cases.

**But SC ruled:**
> Even in TADA cases, where no prospect of trial concluding within reasonable time, release on bail may be necessary as embedded in right to speedy trial under Art. 21.

---

### Maximum Imprisonment Period

**SC Directive:**
> üî¥ **Release all undertrials** who have been in jail for periods **longer than maximum term of imprisonment** for the offence.

**Rationale:** Continued detention violates:
- Human dignity
- Fundamental Right under Art. 21

## üìã **RIGHT OF APPEAL**

## üéì **ARTICLE 21A: RIGHT TO EDUCATION**

### Background

**Initially:**
- ‚ùå NOT included as fundamental right
- Included as directive principle under **Art. 45**
- Required State to provide free and compulsory education (up to age 14) within 10 years of Constitution commencement
- Should have been achieved by **1960**

---

### Judicial Recognition Before Amendment

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Unnikrishnan**

**Held:**
> SC implied **right to education from Art. 21**.

**Complementarity of FRs and DPs:**
> ‚öñÔ∏è **"A child (citizen) has Fundamental Right to free education up to age of 14 years."**

**Discharged by State through:**
- Government schools, **OR**
- Private schools (aided and recognized)

---

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka**

**Issue:** Capitation fee for admission to educational institutions

**Held:**
> Reading Art. 21 with Directive Principles (Arts. 38, 39(a), 41, 45):
> 
> **Framers made it obligatory for State to provide education.**

> üî¥ **Charging capitation fee is wholly arbitrary** and violative of Art. 14.

---

### Constitutional Amendment

#### **Constitution (86th Amendment) Act, 2002**

**Introduced:** **Article 21A**

> **"The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine."**

---

### Fundamental Duty Added

#### Article 51A(k)

**Added by same amendment:**

> Parent/guardian to provide opportunities for education to child/ward between age of **six and fourteen years**.

**Ensures:** Compulsoriness of education.

---

**Introduced:** **Article 15(5)**

**Purpose:** Promote educational advancement of:

## üö® **ARTICLE 22: PROTECTION AGAINST ARREST AND DETENTION**

### Introduction

**Importance:**
> üéØ Protection from oppression and abuse by police/enforcement officers is **major interest in free society**.

**Impact of arrest:**
- Very traumatic
- Incalculable harm
- Loss of reputation

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Denying person of liberty is serious matter.**

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Joginder Kumar v. State of UP**

**Principle:**
> **No arrest merely because it is lawful** for police officer to do so.

**Distinction:**
- **Existence of power** to arrest is one thing
- **Justification** for its exercise is quite another

---

## üîê **FOUR SAFEGUARDS FOR ARRESTED PERSONS**

**Clauses (1) and (2) of Art. 22** ensure:

```
Four Safeguards under Art. 22(1) & 22(2)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 1. Information about grounds of arrest
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 2. Right to consult lawyer
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 3. Production before magistrate within 24 hours
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ 4. No detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate's authority
```

---

### üîë **Safeguard 1: Information About Grounds**

**Rule:**
> Detained person must be informed **as soon as may be** of grounds of arrest.

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Shobharam**

**Principle:**
> **"Personal liberty cannot be curtailed by arrest without informing him, as soon as possible, why he is arrested."**

**Requirements:**
- ‚úÖ Grounds must be **intelligible**
- ‚úÖ Enable preparation of defence
- ‚úÖ Enable moving court for bail/habeas corpus

---

### üîë **Safeguard 2: Right to Consult Lawyer**

**Art. 22(2):** Right to consult and be defended by legal practitioner of choice.

#### Rights from Art. 21 & 22(2)

**Arrested person has right:**
- ‚úÖ Upon request, to have someone informed
- ‚úÖ Consult **privately** with lawyer

**These rights inherent in Arts. 21 and 22(2).**

**Timing:** Right starts **from day of arrest**.

**Connection with Art. 20(3):**
> Right against self-incrimination best promoted by conceding right to consult legal practitioner.

---

### üîë **Safeguard 3: Production Before Magistrate**

**Rule:**
> Arrested person to be produced before **nearest magistrate** within **24 hours** of arrest (excluding journey time).

**Example:** 
- Station officer arrested person without warrant
- City magistrate came in executive capacity
- Person produced before him and remanded
- ‚úÖ **Art. 22(2) fulfilled**
- Arrest not made under magistrate's orders/supervision
- Immaterial whether magistrate sits in Court or not

---

### üîë **Safeguard 4: No Detention Beyond 24 Hours**

**Rule:**
> No detention beyond 24 hours without **magistrate's authority**.

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Gunpati v. Nafisul Hasan**

**Facts:**
- Person arrested in Bombay on warrant by UP Legislative Assembly Speaker
- Taken to Lucknow to answer breach of privilege
- Not produced before magistrate within 24 hours

**Held:**
- ‚ùå **Clear breach** of Art. 22(2)
- Petitioner **released**

---

## ‚ùå **NON-APPLICATION OF ART. 22(1) & 22(2)**

| Category | Reason |
|----------|--------|
| **Enemy Aliens** | National security |
| **Preventive Detention** | Special regime under Art. 22(3)-(7) |

**Delhi HC held:**
> ‚úÖ He had been **arrested and detained** within meaning of Art. 22.

## üö® **PREVENTIVE DETENTION**

### Definition

> **Preventive detention** = Detention **without trial and conviction** by court, merely on **suspicion** of executive authority.

---

### Distinction

| Conviction | Preventive Detention |
|------------|---------------------|
| Punished for **past act** | Detained to prevent **future act** |
| Offence proved **beyond reasonable doubt** | Based on **subjective satisfaction** |
| Purpose: **Punishment** | Purpose: **Prevention** |

> üìå **"In preventive detention, past act is merely material for inference about future probable conduct."**

---

## üìú **CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS FOR PREVENTIVE DETENTION**

### Parliamentary Power

#### Entry 9, List I (Exclusive Power)

**Parliament can enact** preventive detention law for:
- Defence
- Foreign affairs
- Security of India

---

#### Entry 3, List III (Concurrent Power)

**Parliament AND State Legislatures** can enact for:
- Security of a State
- Maintenance of public order
- Maintenance of supplies and services essential to community

## üîê **RIGHTS OF DETENU - ARTICLE 22(5)**

### Two Rights

```
Rights under Art. 22(5)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 1. Communication of grounds to detenu
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ 2. Right to make representation
```

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **If ANY right violated, detention order becomes bad.**

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **COMMUNICATION OF GROUNDS**

**Meaning:**
> Communication of **all basic facts, documents, materials** which went into subjective satisfaction of authority to detain.

**Purpose:**
> Place detenu in position to make **effective representation** against detention.

**Cannot do so unless:**
> Grounds communicated in **clear and unambiguous terms** with as much particulars as possible.

---

#### Language Issue

**Example:**
- Grounds served in **English**
- Detenu did not know English
- Asked for **Hindi** version
- Not supplied

**SC held:** ‚ùå Detention **quashed**.

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **CONSIDERATION OF REPRESENTATION**

**Art. 22(5) does not say:**
- To whom representation to be made
- What to be done with it

**SC interpretation:**
> To make Art. 22(5) meaningful, **government must consider representation BEFORE sending to Advisory Board**.

**This consideration independent** of reference to Advisory Board.

---

#### Example

**Facts:**
- Detention order by Home Minister
- Representation rejected by Secretary

**Held:**
- ‚ùå Secretary had **no jurisdiction**
- Further detention **invalid**

---

## üìã **ADVISORY BOARD - ARTICLE 22(4)**

### Safeguard

**Art. 22(4)(a):**
> Preventive detention for **over 3 months** possible only when Advisory Board holds there is **sufficient cause**.

**Board must report before expiry of 3 months.**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **If report not made within 3 months, detention becomes illegal.**

---

### Confirmation

**Government must:**
- ‚úÖ Peruse report of Advisory Board
- ‚úÖ **Apply mind** to material on record

---

### Parliament's Power - Art. 22(7)(a)

**Parliament may prescribe:**
> "Circumstances and classes of cases where person may be detained beyond 3 months **without Advisory Board**."

---

## ‚è∞ **MAXIMUM PERIOD OF DETENTION**

**Art. 22(4)(b):** Parliament may prescribe maximum period.

**Judicial interpretation:**
- Provision is **permissive**
- Does not oblige Parliament to prescribe maximum
- Not necessary to fix in terms of years/months/days
- Valid to fix in terms of **specific event**

**Example:** Until expiry of emergency under Art. 352.

## üìã **LAWS AUTHORISING PREVENTIVE DETENTION**

### Central Laws

#### Evolution

| Act | Period | Landmark Case |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| **Preventive Detention Act, 1950** | 1950-1969 | A.K. Gopalan |
| **MISA, 1971** | 1971-1977 | - |
| **NSA, 1980** | 1980-present | Current law |

> üìå Law has been **liberalised** over time with better safeguards.

---

#### (a) National Security Act (NSA), 1980

**Grounds for Detention:**

To prevent person from acting in manner prejudicial to:
- Defence of India
- Relations with foreign powers
- Security of India
- Security of a State
- Maintenance of public order
- Maintenance of supplies and services essential to community

---

#### (b) COFEPOSA Act, 1974

**Full Name:** Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974

**Purpose:**
- Prevent smuggling
- Prevent acts prejudicial to conserving/augmenting foreign exchange

---

#### (c) PBMSECA, 1980

**Full Name:** Prevention of Blackmarketing and Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act, 1980

**Purpose:**
- Prevent blackmarketing
- Prevent acts prejudicial to maintenance of supplies of essential commodities

---

  - (Bootleggers, Drug Offenders, Forest Offenders, Goondas, Immoral Traffic, Slum Grabbers)

- Maharashtra Prevention of Dangerous Activities Act, 1981

## ‚öñÔ∏è **JUDICIAL REVIEW OF PREVENTIVE DETENTION**

### Narrow Margin

Law designed to leave **broad discretion** with administrative authorities.

**Only narrow margin** for judicial review.

---

### Grounds for Judicial Intervention

| # | Ground |
|---|--------|
| 1 | **Mala fides** of detaining authority established |
| 2 | Order based on **non-existent ground** |
| 3 | **Art. 21** - cannot deprive except by procedure established by law |
| 4 | Administration must follow **scrupulously and strictly** procedural norms in Art. 22(4)-(7) |

## üìù **CONCLUDING REMARKS**

**Status:**

**Future:**
> No prospect of law being withdrawn in near future.

**Need:**
> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Rigours of preventive detention need to be softened** by providing more safeguards to detenu.
