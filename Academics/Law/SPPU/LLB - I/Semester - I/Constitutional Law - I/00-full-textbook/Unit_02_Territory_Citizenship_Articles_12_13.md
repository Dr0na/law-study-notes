## üáÆüá≥ **SECTION 1: UNION AND ITS TERRITORY (Articles 1-4)**

### Article 1: India, that is Bharat

**Article 1** declares:
> "India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States."

### Key Features

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Name** | India / Bharat |
| **Nature** | Union of States (not Federation) |
| **Territory** | Consists of States and Union Territories |
| **Significance** | Emphasizes indestructibility of the Union |

### Constitutional Significance

üîπ **"Union of States"** - Deliberate choice of words
- Not a "Federation" 
- States have no right to secede
- Union is permanent and indestructible

üîπ **Territory Comprises:**
1. Territories of the States
2. Union Territories
3. Such other territories as may be acquired

### Articles 2-4: Territory Modifications

| Article | Power |
|---------|-------|
| **Article 2** | Parliament may admit new States into the Union |
| **Article 3** | Parliament may form new States, alter boundaries, or change names |
| **Article 4** | Laws made under Arts. 2-3 are not amendments to the Constitution |

#### Important Note
> ‚ö†Ô∏è **No state consent required** for boundary alterations under Article 3 (unlike USA)

## üë• **SECTION 2: CITIZENSHIP OF INDIA**

### üåü **Importance of Citizenship**

Though India is a Federation, there is **only single citizenship**:
- ‚úÖ **Indian Citizenship** (one and only)
- ‚ùå **NO separate State citizenship**

> üìå **This contrasts with USA** which has dual citizenship (Federal + State)

---

## üìú **CONSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS (Articles 5-11)**

**Articles 5-11** define who are citizens at the **commencement of the Constitution** (January 26, 1950).

### Classification of Citizens

```
Citizens at Commencement
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Citizens by Domicile (Art. 5)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Citizens by Migration (Art. 6)
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Citizens by Registration (Art. 8)
```

---

### üè† **1. CITIZENS BY DOMICILE (Article 5)**

Under **Article 5**, every person with domicile in India on January 26, 1950, and fulfilling **ANY ONE** of the following is a citizen:

| Condition | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **(a)** | Born in India |
| **(b)** | Either parent was born in India |
| **(c)** | Ordinarily resident in India for **5+ years** immediately before Jan 26, 1950 |

> üìå **Note:** Conditions are **alternative, not cumulative**

#### Concept of Domicile

**'Domicile'** = **Permanent Home**

**Essential Elements:**
1. **Residence** in the country
2. **Intention** to make it permanent home

##### Key Principles

| Person | Domicile Rule |
|--------|---------------|
| **Minor** | Takes father's domicile |
| **Married Woman** | Takes husband's domicile |
| **Indian Citizens** | One citizenship only, no State citizenship |

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case Law: Central Bank of India v. Ram Narain**

> **Held:** Intention to reside forever in a country is an **essential element** for domicile.

---

### üö∂ **2. CITIZENS BY MIGRATION (Article 6)**

**Background:** Large-scale migration from Pakistan during Partition necessitated special provisions.

#### Eligibility Criteria

A person becomes an Indian citizen if:
- ‚úì He/either parent/any grandparent was born in **undivided India**, **AND**
- ‚úì Fulfills **EITHER** condition below:

| Timeline of Migration | Requirement |
|----------------------|-------------|
| **Before July 19, 1948** | Ordinarily resident since migration |
| **On/After July 19, 1948** | Registered as citizen + 6 months residence |

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important:** Migration must have occurred **before** Constitution commencement (Jan 26, 1950)

---

### ‚úçÔ∏è **3. CITIZENS BY REGISTRATION (Article 8)**

**Article 8** covers **Overseas Indians**.

#### Who Can Register?

Person who (or whose parent/grandparent) was:
- Born in **undivided India**, **BUT**
- Ordinarily residing in a country **outside India and Pakistan**

#### Procedure
‚úÖ Register with **diplomatic/consular representative** of India in country of residence

> üìå **Purpose:** To confer citizenship on **overseas Indians**

---

### ‚ùå **4. TERMINATION OF CITIZENSHIP (Article 7)**

**Article 7** provides for **loss of citizenship** by migration to Pakistan.

#### Rule

**Lost Indian Citizenship if:**
- Migrated to Pakistan **after March 1, 1947**
- Even if originally citizen under Art. 5 or Art. 6

#### Exception: Return Migrants

Can **regain citizenship** by:
1. Returning to India under **permit of resettlement/permanent return**
2. Registering in same manner as post-July 19, 1948 migrants

#### Key Points

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Overrides** | Articles 5 and 6 |
| **Period Covered** | March 1, 1947 to Jan 26, 1950 |
| **Post-1950 Migration** | Governed by Citizenship Act, 1955 |

##### ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important:** Woman born in India going to Pakistan after March 1, 1947 **loses citizenship** even if husband remains in India.

---

### üö´ **5. DUAL CITIZENSHIP (Article 9)**

**Article 9:** No person can be Indian citizen if he **voluntarily acquired foreign citizenship**.

#### Principle

> üî¥ **NO DUAL/PLURAL CITIZENSHIP** under Constitution

#### Scope

- Applies to those who acquired foreign citizenship **before** Constitution commencement
- Post-commencement cases governed by **Citizenship Act, 1955**

---

## üìã **THE CITIZENSHIP ACT, 1955**

### Constitutional Framework

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Art. 10** | Citizenship continues subject to Parliamentary law |
| **Art. 11** | Parliament empowered to legislate on citizenship matters |

### Five Ways of Acquiring Citizenship

```
Citizenship Acquisition
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 1. Birth (Section 3)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 2. Descent (Section 4)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 3. Registration (Section 5)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 4. Naturalisation (Section 6)
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ 5. Incorporation of Territory (Section 7)
```

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **CITIZENSHIP BY BIRTH (Section 3)**

#### Rule

Person born in India is citizen **EXCEPT:**

| Exception | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **(a)** | Father has **diplomatic immunity** + not Indian citizen |
| **(b)** | Father is **enemy alien** + birth during enemy occupation |

#### Amendment (1986/1992)

**Post-1986:** At least **one parent** must be Indian citizen at time of birth.

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **CITIZENSHIP BY DESCENT (Section 4)**

#### Rule

Person born **outside India** is citizen if:
- ‚úÖ **Father** (pre-1992) or **either parent** (post-1992) was Indian citizen at birth

#### Proviso

If parent citizen **by descent only**, requires:
- Birth registered at Indian consulate, **OR**
- Parent in Government of India service at time of birth

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **CITIZENSHIP BY REGISTRATION (Section 5)**

#### Eligible Categories

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **(a)** | Persons of Indian origin resident in India (6 months) |
| **(b)** | Persons of Indian origin resident outside undivided India |
| **(c)** | Women married to Indian citizens |
| **(d)** | Minor children of Indian citizens |
| **(e)** | Citizens of Commonwealth countries |

#### Overseas Citizenship (Sections 7A & 7B)

**Introduced:** 2003

**Features:**
- Available to persons of Indian origin
- **EXCEPT:** Pakistan and Bangladesh nationals
- Can be cancelled under Section 7D

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ **CITIZENSHIP BY NATURALISATION (Section 6)**

#### Eligibility

For **non-Commonwealth** foreign nationals.

#### Conditions

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 1 | Not from country preventing Indian naturalisation |
| 2 | Renounces other citizenship |
| 3 | Resident/in govt service for **12 months** immediately preceding |
| 4 | Resident/in govt service for **4 years** during preceding 7 years |
| 5 | Good character |
| 6 | Adequate knowledge of Indian language |
| 7 | Intends to reside/serve in India |

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ **CITIZENSHIP BY INCORPORATION (Section 7)**

When territory becomes part of India:
- Central Government **notifies** who shall become citizens
- Based on connection with that territory

---

## üîÑ **LOSS OF CITIZENSHIP**

### Three Ways to Lose Citizenship

```
Loss of Citizenship
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 1. Renunciation (Section 8)
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ 2. Cessation (Section 9)
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ 3. Deprivation (Section 10)
```

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ **RENUNCIATION (Section 8)**

#### Who Can Renounce?

- Indian citizen of **full age and capacity**
- Also citizen/national of another country

#### Procedure

Make **declaration** ‚Üí Get it **registered**

#### Effect on Children

- Minor children also **cease to be citizens**
- Child can **resume citizenship** within 1 year of attaining full age

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Withheld during war** in which India is engaged

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ **CESSATION (Section 9)**

#### Automatic Loss

Citizenship ceases upon **voluntarily acquiring** foreign citizenship by:
- Naturalization
- Registration
- Otherwise

> üî¥ **Not during war**

#### Important Principle

**Voluntary passport acquisition ‚â† Automatic loss**

**Central Government** must determine loss under **Section 9(2)**.

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case: S. Nalini Srikaran v. Union of India, AIR 2007**

**Facts:**
- Child of Sri Lankan parents (Rajiv Gandhi assassination convicts)
- Born in Indian prison
- Taken to Sri Lanka on Sri Lankan passport

**Held:**
- She is **Indian citizen**
- Does **NOT** cease to be citizen until Central Govt determines under S. 9(2)

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ **DEPRIVATION (Section 10)**

Central Government may deprive citizenship if:

| Ground | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **(a)** | Obtained by **fraud/false representation** |
| **(b)** | **Disloyal** to Indian Constitution |
| **(c)** | **Unlawfully traded with enemy** during war |
| **(d)** | **Sentenced** to 2+ years imprisonment within 5 years of registration |
| **(e)** | **Ordinarily resident abroad** for 7 years continuously |

#### Exception to Ground (e)

Does NOT apply if person is:
- Student abroad, **OR**
- In service of Indian government, **OR**
- In service of international organisation (India member), **OR**
- Registers annually at Indian consulate

#### Procedure

1. **Written notice** stating grounds
2. Person may request **Committee of Inquiry**
3. Committee consists of:
   - **Chairman:** Person with 10+ years judicial office
   - **Two members** appointed by Government
4. Government ordinarily guided by Committee report

> üìå Ground (e) does NOT require Committee inquiry

---

## üö´ **EXPULSION OF FOREIGNERS**

### Government Powers

> ‚öñÔ∏è **"The power to expel a foreigner is absolute and unlimited."** - Supreme Court

### Key Principles

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Unlimited Power** | Government can expel without assigning reasons |
| **No Right to Stay** | No foreigner can claim to stay as a matter of right |
| **Art. 21 Protection** | Foreigner CAN claim life & liberty protection |
| **Art. 19 NOT Available** | Right to reside (Art. 19(1)(d)) only for citizens |

## ‚öñÔ∏è **SECTION 3: GENERAL PRINCIPLES RELATING TO FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS**

### üåç **ORIGIN AND DEVELOPMENT**

#### Historical Background

**17th Century onwards:** Human rights theory emerged

> *"Man has certain essential, basic, natural and inalienable rights"*

---

### üìö **Philosophical Foundations**

#### John Locke

> *"Man is born with a title to perfect freedom and an uncontrolled enjoyment of all the rights and privileges of the Law of Nature"*

#### French Revolution (1789)

**Declaration:**
> *"The aim of all political association is the conservation of the natural and inalienable rights of man"*

#### Core Concept

> üéØ **Human rights = Protection from oppression and injustice**

---

### üîÄ **DUAL ASPECT OF FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS**

#### Aspect 1: Rights of People
- Confer **justiciable rights**
- Enforceable through **courts**
- Against the **government**

#### Aspect 2: Restrictions on Government
- Limitations on government action
- Apply to Centre, States, local governments
- Applies to **legislative and administrative** actions

#### Entrenchment

**Meaning:** Rights cannot be taken away by ordinary law.

**Purpose:** 
> *"To safeguard basic human rights from political controversy and place them beyond reach of political parties"*

---

### üåé **INTERNATIONAL DEVELOPMENTS**

#### United States of America (1787)

- **First modern Constitution** with justiciable human rights
- Bill of Rights enforceable through courts

#### United Nations

- **Charter of Human Rights**
- **Universal Declaration of Human Rights**

> *"Recognition of inherent dignity and equal inalienable rights is the foundation of freedom, justice and peace"*

#### European Convention on Human Rights

- Regional human rights instrument

#### United Kingdom

- **Traditionally:** No formal Bill of Rights
- **Rule of Law** prevails
- **1998:** Human Rights Act enacted
  - Gives effect to European Convention

#### Australia

- No comprehensive Bill of Rights
- Few guaranteed rights (e.g., freedom of religion)

#### Canada

- Initially few rights
- Later: Parliamentary law on basic rights
- Not binding on Parliament itself

**Historical Context:**

| # | Reason |
|---|--------|

## üìú **ARTICLE 12: DEFINITION OF 'STATE'**

### Text of Article 12

> **"State"** includes:
> - Government and Parliament of India
> - Government and Legislature of each State
> - All local authorities
> - Other authorities within territory of India or under control of Government of India

### Significance

**Article 12 defines "State" for Part III (Fundamental Rights).**

#### Components

```
"State" under Article 12
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Union Government
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Parliament
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ State Governments
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ State Legislatures
    ‚îú‚îÄ‚îÄ Local Authorities (Municipalities, Panchayats)
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ Other Authorities
```

---

### üè¢ **"Other Authorities"**

#### Judicial Interpretation

**Supreme Court has held:**

| Entity | State? | Reasoning |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **LIC** | ‚úÖ Yes | Statutory corporation, deep state control |
| **ONGC** | ‚úÖ Yes | Government company, public function |
| **Statutory Universities** | ‚úÖ Yes | Created by statute, state control |
| **Private Companies** | ‚ùå Generally No | Unless state-controlled |

#### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib, AIR 1981**

**Test for "Other Authority":**

An entity is "State" if:
1. Created by statute, **OR**
2. Deep and pervasive state control, **OR**
3. Performs governmental/public function, **OR**
4. Financial assistance from state (monopoly indicator)

> üìå **Holistic approach:** Consider totality of facts

## üìú **ARTICLE 13: LAWS INCONSISTENT WITH FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS**

### Text of Article 13

#### Article 13(1)

> All laws **in force** before Constitution commencement, inconsistent with Fundamental Rights, are **void** to extent of inconsistency.

#### Article 13(2)

> State shall not make any law which takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights; any law made in contravention is **void** to extent of contravention.

#### Article 13(3)

> **"Law"** includes:
> - Ordinance
> - Order
> - Bye-law
> - Rule
> - Regulation
> - Notification
> - Custom
> - Usage having force of law

---

### üîç **MEANING OF "LAW"**

#### Broad Definition

| Type | Included? |
|------|-----------|
| **Acts of Parliament/Legislature** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Ordinances** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Statutory Rules/Regulations** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Bye-laws** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Customs having force of law** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Personal Laws** | ‚úÖ Yes |
| **Executive/Administrative Orders** | ‚ö†Ô∏è If has force of law |

---

### ‚öñÔ∏è **JUDICIAL REVIEW UNDER ARTICLE 13**

#### Power of Courts

Courts can declare laws **void** if:
- Inconsistent with Fundamental Rights
- Violate any Part III provision

#### Effect

**Void ab initio** (from the beginning) to extent of inconsistency.

---

### üõ°Ô∏è **DOCTRINES UNDER ARTICLE 13**

#### 1. Doctrine of Eclipse

**Principle:**
- Pre-Constitution law inconsistent with FRs is **eclipsed** (not dead)
- Becomes **operative** if Constitution amended to remove inconsistency

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Bhikaji v. State of M.P.**

**Held:** Pre-Constitution law violating Art. 19(1)(g) was eclipsed, not dead.

---

#### 2. Doctrine of Severability

**Principle:**
- If part of law is unconstitutional, only that part is void
- **Remainder** survives if **severable**

**Test for Severability:**
1. Can invalid part be separated?
2. Does remainder form complete code?
3. Was remainder intended to operate independently?

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case: A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras, AIR 1950**

**Held:** Court can sever invalid portion if remainder can stand independently.

---

#### 3. Doctrine of Prospective Overruling

**Principle:**
- Court declares law unconstitutional for **future** only
- Past transactions under the law remain **valid**

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Golak Nath v. State of Punjab, AIR 1967**

**Held:** Court can limit effect of its decision to future cases only.

---

#### 4. Waiver of Fundamental Rights

**General Rule:** Fundamental Rights **cannot be waived**.

**Exception:** Some rights may be waived:
- Art. 32 right (to approach SC)
- Art. 226 right (to approach HC)

**Rights that CANNOT be waived:**
- Rights having **public element**
- Rights against State action

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Basheshar Nath v. CIT, AIR 1959**

**Held:** A person **cannot waive** Fundamental Rights by contract.

---

### ‚öñÔ∏è **CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENTS AND ARTICLE 13**

#### Key Question

**Is Constitution Amendment Act a "law" under Art. 13?**

#### Judicial Evolution

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case 1: Shankari Prasad v. Union of India, AIR 1951**

**Held:** Constitutional Amendment **NOT** "law" under Art. 13.
- Parliament can amend FRs

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case 2: Sajjan Singh v. State of Rajasthan, AIR 1965**

**Held:** Reaffirmed Shankari Prasad
- But suggested possibility of revisiting

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case 3: Golak Nath v. State of Punjab, AIR 1967**

**Held:** Constitutional Amendment **IS** "law" under Art. 13
- Parliament **CANNOT** amend FRs
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Overruled Shankari Prasad**

##### üèõÔ∏è **Case 4: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, AIR 1973** ‚≠ê

**Held:** Constitutional Amendment NOT "law" under Art. 13
- **BUT** Parliament cannot alter **Basic Structure**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Overruled Golak Nath**

---

### üèõÔ∏è **BASIC STRUCTURE DOCTRINE**

#### Origin

**Case:** Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, AIR 1973

#### Principle

Parliament can amend Constitution **EXCEPT:**
- Cannot alter **Basic Structure**
- Cannot destroy **essential features**

#### Basic Features (Illustrative)

| # | Feature |
|---|---------|
| 1 | Supremacy of Constitution |
| 2 | Republican & Democratic form of government |
| 3 | Secular character |
| 4 | Federal character |
| 5 | Separation of powers |
| 6 | Judicial review |
| 7 | **Fundamental Rights (especially Arts. 14, 19, 21)** |
| 8 | Rule of Law |
| 9 | Independence of Judiciary |
| 10 | Parliamentary system |

> ‚öñÔ∏è **Impact:** Even constitutional amendments can be struck down if they violate basic structure.
