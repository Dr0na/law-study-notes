## ‚öñÔ∏è **ARTICLE 32: RIGHT TO CONSTITUTIONAL REMEDIES**

### üåü **Introduction**

> üí¨ **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar:**
> *"Article 32 is the very soul of the Constitution and the very heart of it."*

**Why so important?**
- Gives teeth to Fundamental Rights
- Without remedy, rights are meaningless
- Ensures judicial protection

---

### üìú **Text of Article 32**

#### Article 32(1)

> **"The right to move the Supreme Court by appropriate proceedings for the enforcement of the rights conferred by this Part is guaranteed."**

---

#### Article 32(2)

> **"The Supreme Court shall have power to issue directions or orders or writs, including writs in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari, whichever may be appropriate, for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by this Part."**

---

### üîë **KEY FEATURES OF ARTICLE 32**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Nature** | Itself a Fundamental Right |
| **Guaranteed** | Constitutional guarantee to approach SC |
| **Scope** | Only for enforcement of Fundamental Rights |
| **Power** | SC can issue writs |
| **Jurisdiction** | Original jurisdiction of SC |
| **Cannot be suspended** | Except during Emergency (Art. 359) |

---

### Unique Nature

**Article 32 is itself a Fundamental Right**

**Significance:**
- Right to have rights enforced
- Remedy is as important as right itself
- Can be directly enforced

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Minerva Mills v. Union of India, AIR 1980**

**Issue:** Can Art. 32 be taken away by Constitutional Amendment?

**Held:**
- **No, Article 32 is basic feature** of Constitution
- Cannot be taken away even by amendment
- Judicial review is essential
- Right to approach SC for FRs is integral to Constitution

---

## 1Ô∏è‚É£ **HABEAS CORPUS** - "Produce the Body"

### Meaning

**Latin:** "You may have the body"

**Purpose:** Determine legality of detention

---

### When Issued

**To secure release of person:**
- Illegally detained
- Wrongfully imprisoned
- Unlawfully restrained

---

### Against Whom

**Can be issued against:**
- State authorities
- Public authorities
- **Private persons** (in certain cases)

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Rudul Shah v. State of Bihar, AIR 1983**

**Facts:**
- Rudul Shah acquitted by trial court
- Kept in jail for **14 years** after acquittal
- Due to negligence

**Held:**
- Released through habeas corpus
- **Awarded compensation** of Rs. 35,000
- State liable for illegal detention

**Significance:**
- First case of compensation for illegal detention
- Habeas corpus can be compensatory, not just preventive

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: A.D.M. Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla, AIR 1976**

**Facts:**
- During Emergency (1975-77)
- Art. 21 suspended under Art. 359
- Question: Can habeas corpus be filed?

**Held (Majority):**
- **No right to habeas corpus** during Emergency
- Art. 21 suspended, hence no remedy

**Dissent (Justice H.R. Khanna):**
- Famous dissent
- Right to life not dependent on Art. 21 alone
- Habeas corpus should be available

**Result:**
- 44th Amendment, 1978 amended Art. 359
- Now Art. 20 and 21 **cannot be suspended** even during Emergency

---

### Scope

**Most important writ for personal liberty**

**Cannot be issued:**
- Against contempt by court
- Against detention under valid order after inquiry

---

## 2Ô∏è‚É£ **MANDAMUS** - "We Command"

### Meaning

**Latin:** "We command"

**Purpose:** Command to perform public duty

---

### When Issued

**To compel:**
- Performance of public duty
- Which has been refused or neglected

---

### Against Whom

**Can be issued against:**
- Public officials
- Government
- Public authorities
- Tribunals
- **Cannot** be issued against private persons (generally)

---

### Requirements

For mandamus to issue:

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 1 | Legal right in petitioner |
| 2 | Legal duty on respondent |
| 3 | Duty must be public |
| 4 | Duty must be clear and enforceable |
| 5 | No alternative remedy (generally) |

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Rajasthan v. Union of India, AIR 1977**

**Facts:** Central Government not implementing award by tribunal

**Held:**
- **Mandamus issued**
- Government must implement award
- Public duty to comply

---

### When NOT Issued

**Mandamus not issued:**
- Against President or Governor (Art. 361)
- To enforce private contract
- When duty is discretionary (not mandatory)
- When alternative remedy available
- Against private persons

---

### Examples of Mandamus

- Compelling admission to college (if right exists)
- Directing promotion (if due)
- Restoration to service (if illegally removed)
- Payment of salary (if withheld)

---

## 3Ô∏è‚É£ **PROHIBITION** - "Forbid"

### Meaning

**Latin:** "To forbid"

**Purpose:** Prevent inferior court/tribunal from exceeding jurisdiction

---

### When Issued

**To prohibit:**
- Inferior court/tribunal
- From acting **beyond jurisdiction**
- **OR** acting **against natural justice**

---

### Nature

**Preventive in nature:**
- Stops proceedings **before** decision
- Different from Certiorari (which quashes after decision)

---

### Against Whom

**Can be issued against:**
- Lower courts
- Tribunals
- Quasi-judicial authorities

**Cannot** be issued against:
- Legislative bodies
- Private persons
- High Courts
- Supreme Court

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Kharak Singh v. State of UP, AIR 1963**

**Principle:** Prohibition can be issued when proceedings without jurisdiction.

---

### Scope

**Issued for:**
- Excess of jurisdiction
- Lack of jurisdiction
- Violation of natural justice

**Not issued for:**
- Mere errors within jurisdiction

---

## 4Ô∏è‚É£ **CERTIORARI** - "To be Certified"

### Meaning

**Latin:** "To be certified" or "To be informed"

**Purpose:** Quash order of inferior court/tribunal

---

### When Issued

**To quash:**
- Orders/decisions already made
- By inferior courts/tribunals
- **After** decision (unlike Prohibition which is before)

---

### Grounds

**Certiorari issued on:**

| Ground | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **1. Excess of jurisdiction** | Acted beyond powers |
| **2. Absence of jurisdiction** | Had no power to decide |
| **3. Violation of natural justice** | Fair hearing not given |
| **4. Error of law apparent on face** | Clear legal error |

---

### Against Whom

**Can be issued against:**
- Lower courts
- Tribunals
- Administrative authorities exercising judicial/quasi-judicial powers

**Cannot** be issued against:
- Private persons
- Administrative authorities exercising administrative powers
- Legislative bodies

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Province of Bombay v. Khushaldas S. Advani, AIR 1950**

**Held:**
- Certiorari lies against orders of tribunals
- When excess/lack of jurisdiction
- Or when principles of natural justice violated

---

### Certiorari vs Prohibition

| Aspect | Prohibition | Certiorari |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Stage** | Before decision | After decision |
| **Nature** | Preventive | Curative |
| **Effect** | Stops proceedings | Quashes order |

---

## 5Ô∏è‚É£ **QUO WARRANTO** - "By What Authority"

### Meaning

**Latin:** "By what authority" or "By what warrant"

**Purpose:** Question authority to hold public office

---

### When Issued

**To inquire:**
- By what authority person holds public office
- When office held illegally
- When person not qualified

---

### Scope

**Applicable only for:**
- **Public offices**
- Of **substantive** nature
- **Permanent** (not temporary)

**Not applicable for:**
- Private offices
- Ministerial offices
- Temporary appointments

---

### Against Whom

**Can be issued against:**
- Person holding public office without authority
- Person not qualified to hold office
- Person whose appointment is invalid

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: University of Mysore v. Govinda Rao, AIR 1965**

**Facts:** Vice-Chancellor's appointment challenged

**Held:**
- Quo Warranto can be issued
- To question authority to hold office of VC
- VC is public office

---

### Requirements

For Quo Warranto:

| # | Requirement |
|---|-------------|
| 1 | Office must be public |
| 2 | Office must be substantive (not just honorary) |
| 3 | Created by statute or Constitution |
| 4 | Holder must claim to act as holder |

---

### Examples

**Quo Warranto issued for:**
- Vice-Chancellor of University
- Member of Municipal Corporation
- Public Prosecutor

**Not issued for:**
- Company director (private)
- Club president (private)
- Minister (holds office at pleasure)

---

## üìä **WRITS COMPARISON TABLE**

| Writ | Purpose | Stage | Against Whom | Main Use |
|------|---------|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Habeas Corpus** | Release from detention | Any time | Public/Private | Personal liberty |
| **Mandamus** | Command to do duty | Any time | Public authorities | Enforce duty |
| **Prohibition** | Stop proceedings | Before decision | Lower courts | Prevent excess |
| **Certiorari** | Quash order | After decision | Lower courts | Correct error |
| **Quo Warranto** | Question authority | Any time | Office holder | Public office |

## ‚öñÔ∏è **ARTICLE 226: HIGH COURT POWERS**

### Text

> **"Notwithstanding anything in Article 32, every High Court shall have power, throughout the territories in relation to which it exercises jurisdiction, to issue to any person or authority, including in appropriate cases, any Government, within those territories directions, orders or writs, including writs in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari, or any of them, for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by Part III and for any other purpose."**

---

### üîë **Key Features**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Power** | Every High Court has power |
| **Scope** | FRs + any other purpose |
| **Territorial** | Within jurisdiction of HC |
| **Not a FR** | Power of HC, not right of citizen |
| **Cannot be suspended** | Even during Emergency |

---

## üìä **ARTICLE 32 vs ARTICLE 226**

### Detailed Comparison

| Aspect | Article 32 (SC) | Article 226 (HC) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Nature** | Fundamental Right itself | Discretionary power of HC |
| **Scope** | Only Fundamental Rights | FRs + any other legal right |
| **Territorial** | All India | Within HC jurisdiction |
| **Can be suspended** | Yes, during Emergency (Art. 359) | No, not suspended |
| **Can refuse** | Cannot refuse (if FR violated) | Can refuse (discretionary) |
| **Alternative remedy** | No bar | May refuse if alternative available |
| **Original jurisdiction** | Yes | Yes |
| **Appeals** | To same court (review) | To Supreme Court |

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras, AIR 1950**

**Principle:**
- Petitioner can approach **either** SC under Art. 32 **or** HC under Art. 226
- Choice of forum with petitioner
- **But** cannot approach both simultaneously

---

### When to Approach HC vs SC

**Approach HC when:**
- Convenience (geographically nearer)
- Local matter
- Fact investigation needed
- Legal right (not just FR) violated

**Approach SC when:**
- Matter of national importance
- FR violation
- Quick remedy needed
- Declaratory relief

## üèõÔ∏è **PUBLIC INTEREST LITIGATION (PIL)**

### üåü **Introduction**

**PIL** = Litigation for public interest

**Revolutionary concept** in Indian jurisprudence

---

### Evolution

**Traditional Rule:**
- **Locus standi** required
- Only aggrieved person can file
- Personal injury necessary

**PIL Liberalization:**
- **Any public-spirited person** can file
- On behalf of those who cannot approach court
- For enforcement of public duty

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Pioneer Case: S.P. Gupta v. Union of India, AIR 1982**

**"Judges Transfer Case"**

**Held:**
- Locus standi liberalized
- Any member of public can approach
- If public injury/public duty involved

**Justice Bhagwati:**
> *"It is necessary to democratize judicial process and make legal remedies available to poor and disadvantaged."*

---

### When PIL Can Be Filed

**Circumstances:**

| # | Situation |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | Violation of FR of poor/disadvantaged |
| 2 | Public duty not performed |
| 3 | Public injury |
| 4 | Victims unable to approach court |

**Examples:**
- Bonded labour
- Child labour
- Prison conditions
- Environmental pollution
- Corruption in public office

---

### Who Can File PIL

- Any public-spirited citizen
- Social action group
- NGO
- Advocate
- Even by **letter** to Supreme Court

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Landmark PIL Cases**

#### 1. Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India

**Subject:** Bonded labour in stone quarries

**Held:**
- Court took suo motu cognizance
- Directed release and rehabilitation
- Linked Arts. 21 and 23

---

#### 2. M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Multiple cases)

**Subjects:**
- **Oleum gas leak** (industrial safety)
- **Ganga pollution** (environmental protection)
- **Vehicular pollution** in Delhi (CNG)
- **Taj Mahal** protection

**Principle:** Absolute liability for hazardous industries

---

#### 3. Sheela Barse Cases

**Subjects:**
- Women in police lock-ups
- Children in jails
- Undertrial prisoners

**Result:** Guidelines issued for protection

---

#### 4. Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan

**Subject:** Sexual harassment at workplace

**Result:**
- Guidelines issued (Vishaka Guidelines)
- Until legislation enacted
- Judicial legislation

---

### Limitations of PIL

**PIL should NOT be used for:**
- Private interest disguised as public
- Publicity
- Political vendetta
- Settling personal scores

**Court will dismiss** if:
- Mala fide intent
- Not genuine public interest
- Busybody litigation

---

### üèõÔ∏è **Case: Ashok Kumar Pandey v. State of West Bengal, AIR 2004**

**Principle:**
- PIL is not for private gain
- Must be genuine public interest
- Court can impose costs for frivolous PIL

## üö® **FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS DURING EMERGENCY**

### Article 359

**During National Emergency (Art. 352):**

**President can suspend:**
- Right to move court for enforcement of FRs
- **Except** Arts. 20 and 21 (after 44th Amendment)

---

### Original Position

**Pre-44th Amendment:**
- All FRs including Art. 21 could be suspended
- No remedy during Emergency

---

### After 44th Amendment, 1978

**Articles 20 and 21 protected:**
- Cannot be suspended even during Emergency
- Right to life and personal liberty always available
- Habeas corpus always available

---

### Effect of Suspension

**When Art. 359 order issued:**
- Cannot approach court under Art. 32
- Cannot approach court under Art. 226
- FRs still exist but no remedy
- After Emergency, can approach for compensation

---

## üìù **IMPORTANT PRINCIPLES**

### 1. Alternative Remedy

**General Rule:**
- Writ not issued if alternative remedy available
- Should exhaust alternative remedies first

**Exceptions:**
- Alternative remedy inadequate
- Gross violation of FR
- Natural justice violated
- Matter of public importance

---

### 2. Delay and Laches

**Unreasonable delay:**
- Court may refuse writ
- Doctrine of laches
- Should approach court promptly

**But:** No limitation period prescribed

---

### 3. Balance of Convenience

**Court considers:**
- Public interest
- Private interest
- Irreparable injury
- Status quo

---

### 4. Clean Hands

**Petitioner must:**
- Come with clean hands
- Not suppress facts
- Act bona fide

**If suppression:** Writ dismissed
