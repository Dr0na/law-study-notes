## ðŸŒŸ **INTRODUCTION TO ARTICLE 19**

### Constitutional Position

**Article 19** is one of the most important provisions in the Constitution.

**Location:** Part III (Fundamental Rights)

**Beneficiary:** **Citizens only** (not available to non-citizens or legal persons like companies, except associations of citizens)

---

### Historical Background

**Originally (1950):** Article 19 contained **seven freedoms**

**Currently:** **Six freedoms** remain

**Deleted:** Article 19(1)(f) - Right to acquire, hold and dispose of property (deleted by 44th Amendment, 1978)

## 1ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(a): FREEDOM OF SPEECH AND EXPRESSION**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to freedom of speech and expression."**

---

### ðŸ”‘ **Importance**

> â­ **Most important freedom** in a democracy

**Reasons:**
- Essential for democratic functioning
- Foundation of all other liberties
- Enables self-governance
- Promotes truth and knowledge

---

### ðŸ“‹ **SCOPE OF FREEDOM OF SPEECH**

#### Positive Content (What It Includes)

| Right | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1. Freedom of Press** | Though not expressly mentioned, implied |
| **2. Right to Know/Information** | Implied in free speech |
| **3. Commercial Speech** | Advertising, business communications |
| **4. Right to Broadcast** | Radio, TV, internet |
| **5. Right to Silence** | Negative right not to speak |
| **6. Symbolic Speech** | Flags, armbands, peaceful protests |
| **7. Right to Criticize** | Government, public officials (within limits) |
| **8. Academic Freedom** | Teaching, research |

---

#### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras, AIR 1950**

**Facts:**
- Madras Government banned entry of English journal "Cross Road"
- Published by Romesh Thappar
- Alleged to contain communist views

**Held:**
- Ban struck down
- **Freedom of press** is essential part of freedom of speech
- Restrictions must be in interest of grounds mentioned in Art. 19(2)

**Significance:** First major case on freedom of speech after Constitution

---

#### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Bennett Coleman v. Union of India, AIR 1973**

**Facts:**
- Government imposed restrictions on newsprint import
- Limited pages newspapers could publish
- Indirect censorship through economic control

**Held:**
- Restrictions invalid
- Freedom of press includes freedom to circulate
- Cannot be restricted through economic strangulation

---

#### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Indian Express Newspapers v. Union of India, AIR 1986**

**Facts:**
- Import policy discriminated against certain newspapers
- Favored some, restricted others

**Held:**
- Policy arbitrary and discriminatory
- Violates Art. 19(1)(a) and Art. 14

---

### ðŸš« **What is NOT Protected**

| Not Protected | Reason |
|---------------|--------|
| **Obscenity** | Against decency and morality |
| **Defamation** | Harms reputation |
| **Contempt of Court** | Interferes with administration of justice |
| **Incitement to Violence** | Threatens public order |
| **Hate Speech** | Threatens communal harmony |
| **Sedition (debatable)** | Threatens security of State |

---

### ðŸ“± **DIGITAL ERA & FREEDOM OF SPEECH**

#### ðŸ›ï¸ **Landmark Case: Shreya Singhal v. Union of India, AIR 2015**

**Facts:**
- **Section 66A of IT Act, 2000** criminalized sending "offensive messages" through electronic communication
- Two girls arrested for Facebook post criticizing bandh after politician's death
- Vague terms: "grossly offensive," "annoying," "inconvenient"

**Challenges:**
1. Vague and over-broad
2. Chilling effect on free speech
3. No safeguards against misuse

**Held:**
- **Section 66A struck down** as unconstitutional
- Violates Art. 19(1)(a)
- Fails test of proportionality

**Reasons:**
- Terms too vague
- No clear standards
- Creates fear of arbitrary prosecution
- Reasonable restrictions must be narrowly tailored

**Significance:**
- Landmark judgment for digital rights
- Protected online free speech
- Recognized internet as important medium

---

### ðŸ“º **Right to Broadcast**

**Government monopoly on broadcasting challenged:**

**SC held:**
- Airwaves are public property
- Citizens have right to use for broadcasting
- Government monopoly not justified

---

### ðŸ“° **Prior Restraint**

**Prior Restraint** = Censorship before publication

**General Rule:** Prior restraint **NOT allowed**

**Exception:** 
- During emergency
- Extreme cases (e.g., war)
- Cinematograph Act allows pre-censorship of films

**Rationale:** 
- Free press essential for democracy
- Self-regulation preferred over censorship

## 2ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(b): RIGHT TO ASSEMBLE PEACEABLY AND WITHOUT ARMS**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to assemble peaceably and without arms."**

---

### ðŸ”‘ **Key Elements**

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **1. Peaceable** | No violence or disorder |
| **2. Without Arms** | No weapons |
| **3. Assembly** | Gathering of persons |

---

### Scope

**Includes:**
- âœ… Public meetings
- âœ… Demonstrations
- âœ… Processions
- âœ… Picketing (peaceful)
- âœ… Hartals/Bandhs (if peaceful)

**Does NOT include:**
- âŒ Violent mobs
- âŒ Armed gatherings
- âŒ Riots

---

### Reasonable Restrictions [Art. 19(3)]

**State can impose restrictions in interest of:**
1. Sovereignty and integrity of India
2. Public order

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Babulal Parate v. State of Maharashtra, AIR 1961**

**Facts:** Organizers of bandh prosecuted for inciting people to close shops

**Held:**
- Peaceful bandh is legitimate
- Part of right to protest
- But cannot force others to participate

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Ramlila Maidan Incident Case**

**Facts:** Police action to disperse sleeping protesters at Ramlila Maidan

**Held:**
- Right to protest is fundamental right
- State must balance law and order with fundamental rights
- Force should be proportionate

---

## 3ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(c): RIGHT TO FORM ASSOCIATIONS OR UNIONS**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to form associations or unions."**

---

### Scope

**Includes right to form:**
- Trade unions
- Political parties
- Companies
- Societies
- Co-operative societies (after 97th Amendment)

---

### Reasonable Restrictions [Art. 19(4)]

**Restrictions in interest of:**
1. Sovereignty and integrity of India
2. Public order
3. Morality

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: All India Bank Employees Association v. National Industrial Tribunal, AIR 1962**

**Facts:** Right to form association challenged by employer

**Held:**
- Right to form association is fundamental
- Includes right to effective association
- Right to collective bargaining implied

---

### Trade Unions

**Trade unions protected** under Art. 19(1)(c)

**But:** Right to strike **NOT a fundamental right**

**Rationale:**
- Strike is mode of action, not association itself
- Can be regulated by law

---

## 4ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(d): RIGHT TO MOVE FREELY THROUGHOUT INDIA**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to move freely throughout the territory of India."**

---

### Scope

- Right to move from one place to another
- Within territory of India
- Without restriction

---

### Reasonable Restrictions [Art. 19(5)]

**Restrictions in interest of:**
1. General public
2. Protection of scheduled tribes

**Examples:**
- Inner line permits in some NE states
- Protected areas
- Cantonment areas

---

## 5ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(e): RIGHT TO RESIDE AND SETTLE**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to reside and settle in any part of the territory of India."**

---

### Distinction from 19(1)(d)

| 19(1)(d) Movement | 19(1)(e) Residence |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Temporary movement | Permanent settlement |
| Travel | Live and establish home |

---

### Reasonable Restrictions [Art. 19(5)]

**Same as 19(1)(d):**
1. General public
2. Protection of scheduled tribes

---

### Special Provisions

**Article 370 (J&K)** - Now repealed
- Earlier restricted right of non-J&K citizens to settle

**Article 371D (Andhra Pradesh)**
- Domicile requirements for certain areas

## 6ï¸âƒ£ **ARTICLE 19(1)(g): RIGHT TO PRACTICE ANY PROFESSION, OCCUPATION, TRADE OR BUSINESS**

### Text

> **"All citizens shall have the right to practice any profession, or to carry on any occupation, trade or business."**

---

### ðŸ”‘ **Most Litigated Freedom**

**Why?**
- Affects economic rights
- Government regulation common
- Licensing, permits required

---

### Scope

**Four Aspects:**

| Aspect | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **1. Profession** | Requiring specialized training (doctor, lawyer) |
| **2. Occupation** | Any gainful work (not necessarily specialized) |
| **3. Trade** | Buying and selling |
| **4. Business** | Commercial activity for profit |

---

### What is Protected

- âœ… Right to choose profession
- âœ… Right to practice without unreasonable restrictions
- âœ… Right to carry on trade/business

---

### What is NOT Protected

- âŒ Right to carry on harmful/illegal business
- âŒ Absolute right (subject to regulations)
- âŒ Right to monopoly

---

### Reasonable Restrictions [Art. 19(6)]

**Restrictions in interest of:**
1. General public
2. Professional/technical qualifications
3. Nationalization

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Sodan Singh v. NWFP, AIR 1952**

**Principle:** 
> Right to carry on business **does NOT** include right to import goods.

**Rationale:** Import is not "trade or business" within India.

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: State of Gujarat v. Mirzapur Moti Kureshi Kassab Jamat, AIR 2006**

**Facts:** Gujarat banned slaughter of cows and progeny

**Challenge:** Violates Art. 19(1)(g) of butchers

**Held:**
- Ban valid
- Protection of cows is Directive Principle (Art. 48)
- Reasonable restriction in public interest

---

### Professional Qualifications

**State can prescribe:**
- Minimum qualifications
- Registration requirements
- Code of conduct
- Disciplinary proceedings

**Examples:**
- Bar Council for lawyers
- Medical Council for doctors
- ICAI for Chartered Accountants

---

### Nationalization

**State can nationalize** industries

**Examples:**
- Banks
- Insurance
- Coal mines

**Compensation:** Must be provided (Art. 31C)

### ðŸ”‘ **General Principles**

#### Two-Part Test

**For restriction to be valid:**

```
Reasonable Restriction Test

    â”‚   â”œâ”€â”€ Made by competent authority
    â”‚   â”œâ”€â”€ Proper procedure followed
    â”‚   â””â”€â”€ Within legislative competence
    â”‚

        â”œâ”€â”€ Proportionate to object
        â”œâ”€â”€ Necessary (not merely convenient)

```

---

#### Burden of Proof

**General Rule:**
- **State must prove** restriction is reasonable
- Burden on State to justify

**Why?**
- Fundamental Right presumed
- Restriction is exception
- Liberty should be protected

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Chintaman Rao v. State of MP, AIR 1951**

**Principle:**
> Restrictions must be **reasonable**, not merely in interest of specified grounds.

**Two requirements:**
1. Must be **in interest of** grounds mentioned
2. Must be **reasonable**

---

### Grounds for Restrictions

#### Article 19(2) - Speech and Expression

**Eight grounds:**

| # | Ground |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Sovereignty and integrity of India |
| 2 | Security of the State |
| 3 | Friendly relations with foreign States |
| 4 | Public order |
| 5 | Decency or morality |
| 6 | Contempt of court |
| 7 | Defamation |
| 8 | Incitement to an offence |

**Note:** Grounds added over time (originally only 4)

---

#### Article 19(3) - Assembly

**Two grounds:**
1. Sovereignty and integrity of India
2. Public order

---

#### Article 19(4) - Association

**Three grounds:**
1. Sovereignty and integrity of India
2. Public order
3. Morality

---

#### Article 19(5) - Movement & Residence

**Two grounds:**
1. General public
2. Protection of interests of any Scheduled Tribe

---

#### Article 19(6) - Profession

**Three grounds:**
1. General public
2. Prescribing professional or technical qualifications
3. State can carry on trade/business to exclusion of others

---

### ðŸŽ¯ **Doctrine of Proportionality**

**Restriction must be:**
- Not excessive
- Proportionate to aim
- Least restrictive alternative

**Example:** 
- To prevent traffic congestion, total ban on vehicles = disproportionate
- Time restrictions on vehicles = proportionate

## ðŸ“œ **RIGHT TO PROPERTY - HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVE**

### ðŸ”‘ **Evolution**

#### Original Position (1950-1978)

**Article 19(1)(f):**
> Right to acquire, hold and dispose of property

**Article 31:**
> No person deprived of property except by authority of law + compensation

---

#### Why Property was Important

- Zamindari abolition
- Land reforms
- Nationalization
- Conflict with socialist goals

---

### Constitutional Amendments on Property

| Amendment | Year | Change |
|-----------|------|--------|
| **1st** | 1951 | Added Arts. 31A, 31B (Ninth Schedule) |
| **4th** | 1955 | Amended Art. 31 |
| **17th** | 1964 | Expanded Art. 31B |
| **25th** | 1971 | Art. 31C added; "compensation" â†’ "amount" |
| **44th** | 1978 | **Deleted Arts. 19(1)(f) and 31** |

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Kameshwar Singh v. State of Bihar, AIR 1952**

**Facts:** Bihar Land Reforms Act challenged

**Issue:** Whether compensation adequate?

**Held:**
- Compensation must be **just equivalent** to value
- Not merely symbolic

**Result:** Struck down inadequate compensation provisions

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: State of West Bengal v. Bela Banerjee, AIR 1954**

**Held:**
- Right to property is fundamental right
- Adequate compensation must be paid
- Court can review adequacy

---

### Government's Response

**Problem:** Courts striking down land reforms for inadequate compensation

**Solution:** Constitutional amendments

#### Article 31A - Saving of Laws

**Protects from challenge:**
- Acquisition of estates
- Land reforms
- Tenancy laws

**Cannot be challenged** on ground of violating Arts. 14, 19, or 31

---

#### Article 31B - Ninth Schedule

**Laws in Ninth Schedule protected** from judicial review

**Originally:** 13 laws
**Currently:** 280+ laws

**Purpose:** Protect land reforms and nationalization

---

#### Article 31C - Primacy to DPs

**Laws for implementing Arts. 39(b) and (c)** protected

**Originally:** All Directive Principles (struck down)
**Currently:** Only 39(b) and (c)

**Cannot be challenged** on ground of violating Arts. 14 or 19

---

### ðŸ›ï¸ **Case: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, AIR 1973**

**Challenged:** All three special provisions (31A, 31B, 31C)

**Held:**
- Property amendments valid
- **BUT:** Cannot destroy **basic structure**
- **Judicial review** is basic structure
- Cannot be completely taken away

**Significance:** Laid down Basic Structure Doctrine

---

### 44th Amendment, 1978

**Drastic Change:**

**DELETED:**
- Article 19(1)(f) - Property as Fundamental Right
- Article 31 - Compulsory acquisition provisions

**ADDED:**
- **Article 300A** - Property as constitutional right

---

### Article 300A - Present Position

**Text:**
> **"No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law."**

---

### New Position - Property Rights

| Aspect | Pre-1978 | Post-1978 |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Status** | Fundamental Right | Constitutional Right |
| **Article** | 19(1)(f), 31 | 300A |
| **Enforceable** | Under Art. 32 | Not under Art. 32 |
| **Compensation** | Required | Not required by Constitution |
| **Procedure** | Reasonable required | Just "authority of law" |
| **Review** | Under Arts. 14, 19, 21, 31 | Only under Arts. 14, 21 |

---

### Implications

**Easier for Government:**
- Land acquisition simpler
- Compensation not constitutional requirement
- Less judicial review

**Weaker for Citizens:**
- Cannot directly approach SC under Art. 32
- No constitutional guarantee of compensation
- Reduced protection

---

### Current Legal Framework

**Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013**

**Provides:**
- Compensation formula
- Rehabilitation measures
- Consent requirements
- Social Impact Assessment

---

## ðŸ”— **RELATIONSHIP WITH ARTICLES 14 AND 21**

### Interconnection - Maneka Gandhi Case

**Before Maneka Gandhi (1978):**
- Arts. 14, 19, 21 were **mutually exclusive**
- Separate water-tight compartments

**After Maneka Gandhi:**
- **Interconnected**
- Must be read together
- Procedure under Art. 21 must satisfy Art. 19

---

### Implications

**Any restriction on Art. 19 freedoms must:**
1. Satisfy Art. 14 (not arbitrary)
2. Be reasonable, fair, just (Art. 21)
3. Be in interest of grounds specified
4. Pass proportionality test
