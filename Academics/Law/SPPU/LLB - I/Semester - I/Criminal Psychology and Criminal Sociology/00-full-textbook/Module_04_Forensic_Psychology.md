<a name="definition-meaning-scope"></a>
## üìñ **DEFINITION, MEANING AND SCOPE OF FORENSIC PSYCHOLOGY**

### **Etymology**

**"Forensic"** is derived from Latin **"forensis"** meaning:
- "Of the forum"
- Public
- Relating to courts and law

---

### **Definitions**

#### **Definition 1**

> **Forensic Psychology** is the application of psychological knowledge and methods to the criminal justice system.

#### **Definition 2**

> The interface between psychology and the legal system, involving psychological assessment, treatment, and research in legal contexts.

#### **Definition 3 (Comprehensive)**

> Forensic psychology is the professional practice by psychologists working within or in consultation with a legal system that encompasses both criminal and civil law.

---

### **Scope of Forensic Psychology**

Forensic psychology operates in various areas:

| Area | Application |
|------|------------|
| **Criminal Investigation** | Profiling, interrogation techniques |
| **Courts** | Expert testimony, competency evaluation |
| **Corrections** | Treatment programs, risk assessment |
| **Police** | Selection, training, stress management |
| **Victims** | Witness preparation, victim support |
| **Juvenile Justice** | Assessment, rehabilitation |
| **Civil Cases** | Child custody, personal injury |

---

## üìú **HISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF FORENSIC PSYCHOLOGY**

### **Development Abroad (Western Countries)**

#### **Early Pioneers (1900s-1920s)**

**Hugo M√ºnsterberg (1863-1916)**
- German-American psychologist
- **"Father of Forensic Psychology"**
- Book: **"On the Witness Stand"** (1908)
- Applied psychology to legal issues
- Studied eyewitness testimony
- Memory and perception in court

**Key Contributions:**
- Psychology of testimony
- Detection of deception
- Memory reliability
- Suggestibility of witnesses

---

#### **William Stern (1871-1938)**

- German psychologist
- Studied reliability of eyewitness testimony
- Developed reality testing methods
- Pioneered applied psychology in law

---

#### **Early 20th Century Developments**

**1910s-1920s:**
- Psychology begins entering courtrooms
- Expert psychological testimony
- Mental testing of offenders
- IQ testing in prisons

**1920s-1930s:**
- Juvenile delinquency research
- Personality testing of criminals
- Prison psychology programs

---

#### **Mid-20th Century Growth**

**1940s-1950s:**
- Post-WWII expansion
- Military psychology applied to law enforcement
- Development of risk assessment tools
- Police selection procedures

**1960s-1970s:**
- Criminal profiling developed (FBI)
- Behavioral Science Unit established (1972)
- Systematic research on criminal behavior
- Psychology in corrections expanded

**1980s-Present:**
- Professionalization of field
- Academic programs in forensic psychology
- Evidence-based practices
- Integration with neuroscience

---

### **Landmark Cases (Abroad)**

#### **Jenkins v. United States (1962)**

**Significance:**
- U.S. case establishing psychologists as expert witnesses
- Psychologists could testify on mental illness
- Equal standing with psychiatrists in court

---

#### **Tarasoff v. Regents of University of California (1976)**

**Significance:**
- "Duty to warn" established
- Psychologists must warn potential victims
- Confidentiality vs public safety

## üîç **ROLE OF FORENSIC PSYCHOLOGY IN INVESTIGATION OF CRIME**

### **1. Criminal Profiling**

**Application:**
- Analyze crime scene
- Predict offender characteristics
- Narrow suspect pool
- Prioritize leads

**Crimes Most Suitable:**
- Serial murders
- Serial rapes
- Arson
- Bombings

---

### **2. Investigative Interview Techniques**

#### **Cognitive Interview**

**Purpose:** Enhance witness memory recall

**Techniques:**
- Context reinstatement
- Report everything
- Recall from different perspectives
- Recall in different orders

**Benefits:**
- Increases accurate information
- Reduces false memories
- Better than standard interview

---

#### **Reid Technique (Interrogation)**

**Purpose:** Elicit confession from suspects

**Phases:**
1. **Direct confrontation**: Present evidence
2. **Theme development**: Offer moral justification
3. **Handling denials**: Interrupt denials
4. **Overcoming objections**: Address excuses
5. **Gaining attention**: Ensure suspect listens
6. **Passive mood**: Suspect shows guilt
7. **Alternative question**: Offer two choices
8. **Oral confession**: Get admission
9. **Written confession**: Document statement

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Criticism**: Can lead to false confessions, especially with vulnerable suspects.

---

### **3. Polygraph (Lie Detection)**

#### **What it Measures**

**Physiological responses:**
- Heart rate
- Blood pressure
- Respiration
- Galvanic skin response (sweating)

**Theory:** Lying causes stress ‚Üí Physiological changes

---

#### **Limitations**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Not Admissible** in Indian courts as primary evidence

**Problems:**
- Can be beaten by training/drugs
- False positives (innocent people nervous)
- False negatives (psychopaths show no stress)
- Measures anxiety, not lying

**Status in India:**
- Results not admissible as evidence
- Can be used as investigative tool
- Requires consent (Selvi v. State of Karnataka, 2010)

---

### **4. Psychological Autopsy**

**Purpose:** Determine manner of death (suicide vs homicide vs accident)

**Method:**
- Interview family, friends
- Review personal documents
- Medical records
- Reconstruct psychological state
- Determine intent

**Used When:**
- Cause of death unclear
- Insurance claims
- Suicide vs accident determination

---

### **5. Statement Validity Analysis**

**Purpose:** Assess credibility of witness statements

**Techniques:**

**a) Statement Validity Assessment (SVA)**
- Content-based analysis
- Quality of details
- Consistency

**b) Reality Monitoring**
- Distinguish real from fabricated memories
- Sensory details vs cognitive operations

---

### **6. Hypnosis**

**Use:** Help witnesses recall forgotten details

**Issues:**
- Can create false memories
- Highly suggestible
- Not reliable evidence
- Rarely used now

## ‚öñÔ∏è **APPLICATION OF PSYCHOLOGY IN COURT**

### **1. Expert Witness Testimony**

#### **Role of Forensic Psychologist**

**Areas of Testimony:**
- Mental state of accused
- Competency to stand trial
- Criminal responsibility (insanity defense)
- Risk assessment
- Mitigation at sentencing
- Child custody cases

---

#### **Qualifications Required**

**Education:**
- Advanced degree (M.Phil./Ph.D.) in Psychology
- Specialized training in forensic psychology

**Experience:**
- Clinical experience
- Forensic assessment experience
- Court experience

**Credentials:**
- Registration with Rehabilitation Council of India (RCI)
- Professional memberships

---

#### **Standard of Expert Testimony**

**Daubert Standard (US - Influential globally):**

Expert testimony must be:
1. **Testable**: Based on testable theory
2. **Peer-reviewed**: Published in journals
3. **Error rate known**: Reliability established
4. **Accepted**: By relevant scientific community

**Indian Approach:**
- More liberal admissibility
- Court has discretion
- Section 45, Indian Evidence Act, 1872

---

### **2. Competency to Stand Trial**

#### **Definition**

> Accused's present ability to understand proceedings and assist in defense.

#### **Criteria**

**Accused must be able to:**
1. Understand nature and purpose of proceedings
2. Understand potential consequences
3. Communicate with attorney
4. Assist in preparing defense
5. Make rational decisions

#### **Assessment Process**

**Methods:**
- Clinical interview
- Psychological testing
- Review of records
- Functional assessment

**Outcome:**
- **Competent**: Trial proceeds
- **Incompetent**: Treatment to restore competency
- **Never competent**: Alternative procedures

---

### **3. Criminal Responsibility (Insanity Defense)**

#### **Legal Standard in India**

**Section 84, Indian Penal Code:**

Requires proof that at time of crime:
1. Accused had unsoundness of mind
2. Could not know:
   - Nature of act, OR
   - Act was wrong or contrary to law

---

#### **Psychological Assessment**

**Forensic Psychologist Evaluates:**
- Mental status at time of offense (retrospective)
- Current mental status
- Diagnosis of mental disorder
- Impact on criminal responsibility
- Causal link between illness and crime

**Methods:**
- Clinical interview
- Psychological testing
- Review of records (medical, criminal)
- Collateral interviews
- Mental Status Examination (MSE)

### **5. Eyewitness Testimony**

#### **Psychological Factors Affecting Accuracy**

**Encoding (During Event):**
- **Stress and Arousal**
  - High stress impairs memory
  - Weapon focus effect
  - Tunnel vision

- **Duration of Exposure**
  - Brief exposure ‚Üí Poor memory
  - Longer exposure ‚Üí Better memory

- **Witness Characteristics**
  - Age (children, elderly less accurate)
  - Intelligence
  - Expectations and biases

---

**Storage (Retention Period):**
- **Time Delay**
  - Memory fades over time
  - Details lost, gist remains

- **Post-Event Information**
  - New information contaminates memory
  - Suggestions from others
  - Media coverage

---

**Retrieval (During Testimony):**
- **Leading Questions**
  - Can distort memory
  - Create false memories

- **Lineup Procedures**
  - Biased lineups
  - Sequential vs simultaneous
  - Double-blind administration

---

#### **Cross-Race Effect**

> People are better at recognizing faces of their own race than other races.

**Explanation:**
- Lack of experience with other-race faces
- Encode different features
- Higher error rate in cross-race identification

---

#### **False Memories**

**Elizabeth Loftus Research:**
- Memories can be implanted
- Leading questions create false memories
- Suggestive procedures dangerous
- "Lost in the mall" experiment

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Legal Implication**: Eyewitness confidence ‚â† Accuracy
