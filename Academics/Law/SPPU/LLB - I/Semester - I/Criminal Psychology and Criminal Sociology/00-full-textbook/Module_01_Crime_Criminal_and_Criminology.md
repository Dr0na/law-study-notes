## üîç **FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS**

### üìñ What is Crime?

#### **Definition**

**Crime** can be defined as:

> An act or omission that constitutes an offense that may be prosecuted by the state and is punishable by law.

---

#### **Essential Elements of Crime**

For an act to be considered a crime, the following elements must be present:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Actus Reus** | The guilty act - physical element of crime |
| **Mens Rea** | The guilty mind - mental element of crime |
| **Causation** | Connection between act and harm |
| **Harm** | Injury to person, property, or society |
| **Legality** | Act must be prohibited by law |
| **Punishment** | Prescribed penalty under law |

---

#### **Characteristics of Crime**

1. **Violation of Law**
   - Crime involves breach of legal rules
   - Must be defined by statutory or common law

2. **Harmful to Society**
   - Causes injury to social order
   - Threatens public peace and security

3. **Punishable by State**
   - State has power to prosecute and punish
   - Sanctions range from fines to imprisonment

4. **Intent or Negligence**
   - Usually requires mens rea (criminal intent)
   - Some crimes punishable even without intent (strict liability)

---

### üë§ Who is the Criminal?

#### **Definition**

A **criminal** is:

> A person who has committed a crime or has been legally convicted of a crime.

---

#### **Classification of Criminals**

##### **1. By Nature of Crime**

- **Violent Criminals**: Murder, assault, robbery
- **Property Criminals**: Theft, burglary, fraud
- **White-Collar Criminals**: Embezzlement, corruption
- **Cyber Criminals**: Hacking, identity theft
- **Organized Criminals**: Mafia, drug cartels

##### **2. By Criminal Career**

- **Occasional Criminals**: Commit crime under specific circumstances
- **Professional Criminals**: Make crime their livelihood
- **Habitual Criminals**: Repeat offenders with criminal lifestyle

##### **3. By Age**

- **Juvenile Delinquents**: Below 18 years
- **Adult Criminals**: Above 18 years
- **Senior Criminals**: Elderly offenders

---

#### **Characteristics of Criminals**

> üìå **Exam Tip**: Remember the psychological and sociological factors that distinguish criminals from law-abiding citizens.

**Psychological Characteristics:**
- Antisocial personality traits
- Low self-control
- Aggression and impulsivity
- Emotional instability
- Lack of empathy

**Sociological Characteristics:**
- Poor family background
- Low socioeconomic status
- Association with criminal groups
- Lack of education
- Substance abuse

## üìö **WHAT IS CRIMINOLOGY?**

### **Definition**

**Criminology** is defined as:

> The scientific study of crime, criminals, criminal behavior, and the criminal justice system.

---

### **Etymology**

The word **"Criminology"** is derived from:
- **Latin**: "Crimen" (crime)
- **Greek**: "Logos" (study)

Thus, criminology literally means "the study of crime."

---

### **Scope of Criminology**

Criminology encompasses the study of:

1. **Nature of Crime**
   - Definition and classification
   - Types and patterns of crime

2. **Causes of Crime**
   - Individual factors (biological, psychological)
   - Social factors (poverty, education, family)
   - Environmental factors

3. **Criminal Behavior**
   - Why people commit crimes
   - How criminal behavior develops

4. **Criminal Justice System**
   - Police
   - Courts
   - Corrections (prisons, probation, parole)

5. **Crime Prevention**
   - Strategies to reduce crime
   - Rehabilitation programs

6. **Victimology**
   - Study of victims of crime
   - Victim-offender relationship

---

### **Objectives of Criminology**

> ‚úÖ **Key Objectives**

1. **Understanding Crime**
   - Analyze nature and extent of crime
   - Study crime patterns and trends

2. **Identifying Causes**
   - Determine factors leading to crime
   - Study biological, psychological, and social causes

3. **Preventing Crime**
   - Develop crime prevention strategies
   - Implement effective policies

4. **Reforming Criminals**
   - Rehabilitation and reintegration
   - Reduce recidivism

5. **Protecting Society**
   - Ensure public safety
   - Maintain social order

---

### **Relationship with Other Sciences**

Criminology is an **interdisciplinary science** that draws from:

| Science | Contribution to Criminology |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Psychology** | Understanding criminal mind and behavior |
| **Sociology** | Social factors causing crime |
| **Psychiatry** | Mental illness and crime |
| **Law** | Legal framework and criminal justice |
| **Economics** | Poverty and crime relationship |
| **Biology** | Genetic and biological factors |
| **Anthropology** | Cultural influences on crime |

---

## üèõÔ∏è **SCHOOLS OF CRIMINOLOGY**

### **1. THE PRE-CLASSICAL SCHOOL**

#### **Time Period**
- Before 18th century

#### **Key Features**

1. **Supernatural Explanation**
   - Crime was seen as result of supernatural forces
   - Belief in demonic possession
   - Divine punishment for sins

2. **Trial Methods**
   - **Trial by Ordeal**: Test by fire, water
   - **Trial by Combat**: Physical fight to determine guilt
   - **Trial by Oath**: Swearing innocence

3. **Punishment**
   - Cruel and barbaric
   - Public executions
   - Torture
   - No proportion between crime and punishment

4. **No Rational Basis**
   - No scientific investigation
   - Guilt determined by supernatural tests
   - No concept of evidence or proof

---

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Common Mistake**: Don't confuse Pre-Classical with Classical School. Pre-Classical is pre-18th century with supernatural beliefs, while Classical is 18th century with rational thinking.

---

### **2. THE CLASSICAL SCHOOL**

#### **Time Period**
- Mid-18th century (1764 onwards)

#### **Founding Fathers**

**1. Cesare Beccaria (1738-1794)**
- Italian criminologist
- Author: "On Crimes and Punishments" (1764)

**2. Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)**
- English philosopher
- Developer of "Utilitarian Philosophy"

---

#### **Core Principles**

> üß† **Memory Hook - "FREE WILL"**
> - **F**ree will and rationality
> - **R**eformative punishment
> - **E**quality before law
> - **E**qual punishment for equal crime
> - **W**ritten laws
> - **I**ndividual responsibility
> - **L**egality principle
> - **L**ess severe punishments

---

##### **1. Free Will**

- Human beings have **free will** to choose between right and wrong
- Criminals **rationally decide** to commit crime
- Crime is result of **rational choice**

##### **2. Hedonistic Calculus**

- Humans seek **pleasure** and avoid **pain**
- Before committing crime, person weighs:
  - **Benefits** of crime (pleasure)
  - **Costs** of crime (punishment/pain)
- If benefits > costs ‚Üí Crime occurs

##### **3. Punishment as Deterrent**

**Purpose of Punishment:**
- To deter the individual from committing future crimes (**Specific Deterrence**)
- To deter others in society (**General Deterrence**)

**Principles of Punishment:**
- Should be **proportionate** to crime
- Should be **certain** (inevitable)
- Should be **swift** (quick)
- Should be **public** (to deter others)

##### **4. Legal Reforms Advocated**

**Beccaria's Reforms:**
- Abolition of **torture**
- Abolition of **capital punishment**
- Punishment should fit the **crime, not the criminal**
- **Certainty** of punishment more important than severity
- **Prevention** better than punishment

---

#### **Criticism of Classical School**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Limitations**

1. **Ignores Individual Differences**
   - Treats all criminals alike
   - No consideration of age, mental state, circumstances

2. **Assumes Perfect Rationality**
   - Not all criminals act rationally
   - Ignores passion, emotion, mental illness

3. **Ignores Social Factors**
   - No consideration of poverty, education, family
   - Environmental factors ignored

4. **Rigid Approach**
   - Same punishment for same crime
   - No flexibility for mitigating circumstances

5. **Unrealistic View of Free Will**
   - Many factors limit free will
   - Biological, psychological, social influences

---

### **3. NEO-CLASSICAL SCHOOL**

#### **Time Period**
- Late 18th to early 19th century

#### **Background**

Neo-Classical School emerged as a **reaction** to the rigidity of the Classical School.

---

#### **Key Modifications**

> üìå **Main Contribution**: Introduced **flexibility** and **individualization** in criminal justice.

##### **1. Recognition of Individual Circumstances**

**Modifications introduced:**
- Consideration of **mental state** of offender
- Recognition of **diminished responsibility**
- Importance of **age** (children treated differently)
- Consideration of **first-time offenders**

##### **2. Mitigation and Aggravation**

**Mitigating Circumstances:**
- Mental illness or insanity
- Infancy (young age)
- Provocation
- Duress or coercion

**Aggravating Circumstances:**
- Premeditation
- Cruelty
- Multiple victims
- Repeat offender

##### **3. Judicial Discretion**

- Judges given discretion in sentencing
- Punishment can be individualized
- Consider circumstances of each case

---

#### **Comparison: Classical vs Neo-Classical**

| Aspect | Classical School | Neo-Classical School |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Free Will** | Absolute free will | Modified free will |
| **Punishment** | Fixed for each crime | Flexible based on circumstances |
| **Mental State** | Ignored | Considered important |
| **Age** | Not considered | Children treated differently |
| **Judicial Role** | Mechanical application | Discretion in sentencing |
| **Focus** | Crime-centered | Criminal-centered (partial) |

---

> üß† **Memory Hook**: Neo-Classical = Classical + **Modifications** (age, insanity, circumstances)

---

## üî¨ **POSITIVIST APPROACH**

### **Introduction**

#### **Time Period**
- Late 19th century (1870s onwards)

#### **Background**

Positivist School emerged as **radical departure** from Classical School:
- Based on **scientific method**
- Emphasis on **empirical research**
- Study of the **criminal, not the crime**

---

### **Core Principles of Positivism**

> üìå **Fundamental Shift**: From "What is the crime?" to "Who is the criminal?"

**Key Beliefs:**

1. **Criminal behavior is determined**
   - Not result of free will
   - Caused by factors beyond individual control

2. **Scientific Study**
   - Use of empirical data
   - Observation and measurement
   - Case studies and experiments

3. **Multiple Causes**
   - Biological factors
   - Psychological factors
   - Social factors

4. **Criminals are Different**
   - Born criminals (biological)
   - Defective individuals
   - Can be identified by characteristics

5. **Focus on Criminal**
   - Study individual offender
   - Not just the criminal act

6. **Treatment, not Punishment**
   - Rehabilitation
   - Medical treatment
   - Social reform

---

### **Types of Positivism**

#### **1. Radical Positivism (Italian School)**

**Characteristics:**
- Extreme biological determinism
- Criminals are "born" not "made"
- Hereditary factors dominant
- Physical stigmata identify criminals

**Associated with:** Lombroso, Ferri, Garofalo

---

#### **2. Liberal Positivism (French School)**

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate approach
- Social factors more important than biological
- Environment shapes criminal behavior
- Crime can be prevented through social reform

**Associated with:** Gabriel Tarde, Lacassagne

---

## üë®‚Äçüî¨ **KEY THINKERS OF POSITIVIST SCHOOL**

### **1. CESARE LOMBROSO (1835-1909)**

#### **Title**
- "Father of Modern Criminology"
- "Father of Criminal Anthropology"

#### **Main Work**
- **"L'Uomo Delinquente"** (The Criminal Man, 1876)

---

#### **Theory: Born Criminal**

> üß† **Lombroso's Core Idea**: Criminals are **born, not made**.

**Main Arguments:**

1. **Atavism Theory**
   - Criminals are **evolutionary throwbacks**
   - Represent earlier stage of human evolution
   - Resemble primitive humans or apes

2. **Physical Stigmata**
   - Criminals can be identified by physical characteristics
   - These are **visible marks** of criminality

---

#### **Physical Characteristics of Born Criminals**

> üì¶ **Lombroso's Stigmata Checklist**

**Cranial Features:**
- Abnormal skull shape
- Low, sloping forehead
- Large jaw
- Prominent cheekbones
- Asymmetrical face

**Facial Features:**
- Small, deep-set eyes
- Large ears
- Twisted nose
- Fleshy lips
- Sparse beard

**Body Features:**
- Long arms (like apes)
- Excessive tattoos
- Abnormal teeth
- Extra fingers or toes

---

#### **Classification of Criminals (Lombroso)**

**1. Born Criminals (Atavistic)**
   - 40% of all criminals
   - Possess physical stigmata
   - Cannot be reformed

**2. Insane Criminals**
   - Mentally ill offenders
   - Need medical treatment

**3. Criminaloids**
   - Not born criminals
   - Weak nature
   - Commit crime under certain conditions

**4. Criminals of Passion**
   - Act under strong emotion
   - Otherwise normal people

---

#### **Criticism of Lombroso**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Major Flaws**

1. **Unscientific Methodology**
   - Small sample size
   - No control group
   - Biased selection of subjects

2. **Physical Stigmata Discredited**
   - Many criminals have normal appearance
   - Many non-criminals have these features

3. **Ignores Social Factors**
   - Poverty, education, family ignored
   - Over-emphasis on biology

4. **Deterministic**
   - No room for free will
   - Fatalistic approach

5. **Ethical Issues**
   - Stigmatization based on appearance
   - Justification for harsh treatment

---

### **2. ENRICO FERRI (1856-1929)**

#### **Title**
- "Father of Positive School"

#### **Main Work**
- **"Criminal Sociology"** (1884)

---

#### **Theory: Multiple Factor Approach**

Ferri believed crime is caused by interaction of **three types of factors**:

> üß† **Memory Hook - "PAS"**
> - **P**hysical/Anthropological factors
> - **A**nthropological factors (individual)
> - **S**ocial factors

---

#### **Ferri's Three Causes of Crime**

##### **1. Anthropological Factors (Individual)**

**Biological:**
- Age
- Sex
- Race
- Heredity
- Physical constitution

**Psychological:**
- Intelligence
- Temperament
- Mental state

---

##### **2. Physical Factors (Natural Environment)**

- Climate and weather
- Temperature
- Geographical location
- Seasons
- Agricultural conditions

**Example:** Crimes against person increase in summer; property crimes increase in winter.

---

##### **3. Social Factors (Social Environment)**

- Population density
- Urbanization
- Economic conditions
- Education level
- Religion
- Family structure
- Public opinion
- Political system

---

#### **Ferri's Classification of Criminals**

| Type | Description | Treatability |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **1. Born/Instinctive Criminals** | Biological tendency to crime | Difficult to reform |
| **2. Insane Criminals** | Mental illness causes crime | Need medical treatment |
| **3. Habitual Criminals** | Become criminal due to environment | Can be reformed |
| **4. Occasional Criminals** | Commit crime by chance | Easy to reform |
| **5. Passionate Criminals** | Act under passion/emotion | Usually don't repeat |

---

#### **Ferri's Law of Criminal Saturation**

> **Theory**: Every society has a certain level of crime determined by social conditions, like a solution can only dissolve a certain amount of salt.

**Implication:**
- Crime level depends on social, physical, and anthropological conditions
- To reduce crime, must change these conditions
- Punishment alone cannot eliminate crime

---

#### **Ferri's Penal Substitutes**

Instead of punishment, Ferri proposed **social measures** to prevent crime:

**Examples:**
- Free trade to reduce unemployment
- Cheap housing for poor
- Better street lighting
- Reform of marriage laws
- Better education system
- Regulation of alcohol
- Tax reforms

> üìå **Exam Important**: Ferri shifted focus from **punishment** to **prevention** through social reform.

---

### **3. RAFFAELE GAROFALO (1852-1934)**

#### **Title**
- Italian jurist and criminologist

#### **Main Work**
- **"Criminologia"** (1885)

---

#### **Theory: Natural Crime**

Garofalo introduced concept of **"Natural Crime"**:

> Crime that violates two basic **moral sentiments** present in all civilized societies:
> 1. **Probity** (respect for property rights)
> 2. **Pity** (compassion, respect for human life)

---

#### **Natural vs Legal Crime**

| Aspect | Natural Crime | Legal Crime |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| **Definition** | Violates universal moral sentiments | Violates specific laws of a country |
| **Universality** | Same across all societies | Varies by country and time |
| **Examples** | Murder, robbery | Tax evasion, traffic violations |
| **Moral Basis** | Always immoral | May not be immoral |

---

#### **Garofalo's Classification of Criminals**

**Based on deficiency in moral sentiments:**

##### **1. Murderers**
- **Deficiency**: Complete lack of pity
- **Characteristics**: Cruel, violent
- **Treatment**: Elimination (death penalty)

##### **2. Violent Criminals**
- **Deficiency**: Deficient in pity
- **Characteristics**: Aggressive but not murderers
- **Treatment**: Life imprisonment or transportation

##### **3. Criminals Deficient in Probity**
- **Deficiency**: Lack respect for property
- **Characteristics**: Thieves, fraudsters
- **Treatment**: Transportation, forced labor

##### **4. Lascivious Criminals**
- **Deficiency**: Deficient in moral sentiments
- **Characteristics**: Sex offenders
- **Treatment**: Varies based on severity

---

#### **Garofalo's Theory of Punishment**

**Principles:**

1. **Elimination**
   - Death penalty for murderers
   - Incurable criminals must be eliminated

2. **Partial Elimination**
   - Life imprisonment
   - Transportation to colonies
   - For dangerous but not murderous criminals

3. **Enforced Reparation**
   - Compensation to victim
   - For less serious crimes

4. **Deterrence**
   - Punishment to deter others

---

> üìå **Exam Note**: Garofalo was more **harsh** than other positivists. He advocated **elimination** of incurable criminals.

---

### **4. GABRIEL TARDE (1843-1904)**

#### **Title**
- French sociologist and criminologist
- Leading figure of **French School**
- Opposed biological positivism

---

#### **Main Work**
- **"Penal Philosophy"** (1890)
- **"The Laws of Imitation"** (1890)

---

#### **Theory: Laws of Imitation**

> **Core Idea**: Criminal behavior is **learned through imitation**.

Tarde argued that:
- Crime is **not hereditary**
- Crime is **not biological**
- Crime is **learned from others**
- Criminals **imitate** other criminals

---

#### **Three Laws of Imitation**

##### **Law 1: Law of Close Contact**

> "Imitation occurs between individuals who are in close physical and social contact."

**Explanation:**
- People imitate those **close** to them
- Family members, friends, neighbors
- Urban residents imitate urban patterns
- Rural residents imitate rural patterns

**Application to Crime:**
- Children imitate parents
- Juveniles imitate gang members
- Close association with criminals leads to crime

---

##### **Law 2: Law of Imitation of Superiors by Inferiors**

> "The inferior imitates the superior."

**Explanation:**
- Lower class imitates upper class
- Subordinates imitate leaders
- Young imitate old
- Less educated imitate educated

**Application to Crime:**
- Lower class may imitate crimes of upper class
- Fashion, lifestyle crimes
- White-collar crimes spread downward

---

##### **Law 3: Law of Insertion**

> "New patterns of behavior supersede old patterns."

**Explanation:**
- When two patterns conflict, one replaces the other
- Modern methods replace traditional methods
- Innovation in crime techniques

**Application to Crime:**
- New crime methods replace old
- Technology changes crime patterns
- Cyber crimes replace traditional crimes

---

#### **Modes of Imitation**

**1. Direct Imitation**
- Copying exact behavior
- Example: Copying crime technique seen in news

**2. Indirect Imitation**
- General influence
- Example: Glamorization of crime in movies

---

#### **Social Environment and Crime**

Tarde emphasized **social factors**:

1. **Economic Conditions**
   - Poverty breeds crime
   - Unemployment leads to property crimes

2. **Education**
   - Lack of education increases crime
   - But educated commit sophisticated crimes

3. **Urban vs Rural**
   - Cities have more crime
   - Anonymity in cities facilitates crime

4. **Media Influence**
   - Newspapers spread crime techniques
   - Publicity encourages imitation

---

#### **Tarde vs Lombroso**

| Aspect | Lombroso | Tarde |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Cause of Crime** | Biological heredity | Social imitation |
| **Criminal Type** | Born criminal | Made criminal |
| **Focus** | Individual biology | Social environment |
| **Solution** | Eliminate born criminals | Change social conditions |
| **Determinism** | Biological determinism | Social determinism |
| **Reform** | Difficult/impossible | Possible through education |

---

> üìå **Exam Important**: Tarde's **Imitation Theory** is a precursor to modern **Social Learning Theories** (like Sutherland's Differential Association Theory in Module 07).

**From Classical School:**
- Principle of legality (No crime without law)
- Proportionate punishment
- Due process of law
- Presumption of innocence

**From Neo-Classical School:**
- Judicial discretion
- Consideration of mental state
- Different treatment for juveniles

- Scientific investigation of crime
- Rehabilitation programs
- Probation and parole
- Study of criminal psychology
- Crime prevention through social reform

## ‚úÖ **SUMMARY & KEY TAKEAWAYS**

### **Core Concepts**

1. **Crime** = Act + Intent + Harm + Illegality + Punishment

2. **Criminology** = Scientific study of crime, criminals, and criminal justice

3. **Schools represent evolution** of thinking about crime:
   - Pre-Classical: **Supernatural**

   - Neo-Classical: **Modified rational + circumstances**
