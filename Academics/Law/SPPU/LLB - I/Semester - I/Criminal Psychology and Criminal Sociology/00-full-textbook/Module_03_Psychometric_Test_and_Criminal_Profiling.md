<a name="measurement"></a>
## üìè **MEASUREMENT OF CRIMINAL BEHAVIOUR**

### **Introduction**

Criminal behaviour is complex and multifaceted. To understand and predict it, psychologists use various **measurement tools** and **assessment techniques**.

---

### **Why Measure Criminal Behaviour?**

**Purposes:**

1. **Assessment**
   - Evaluate offender's mental state
   - Determine risk level
   - Guide sentencing decisions

2. **Prediction**
   - Predict likelihood of re-offending
   - Assess dangerousness
   - Parole decisions

3. **Treatment Planning**
   - Identify psychological needs
   - Design intervention programs
   - Monitor progress

4. **Research**
   - Study patterns of criminal behavior
   - Test theories
   - Develop prevention strategies

---

<a name="psychological-tests"></a>
## üß™ **PSYCHOLOGICAL TESTS TO MEASURE CRIMINAL BEHAVIOUR**

### **1. INTELLIGENCE TESTS**

#### **Purpose**
- Measure cognitive abilities
- IQ testing

#### **Common Tests**

**a) Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS)**
- Most widely used IQ test
- Measures verbal and performance IQ
- Age-appropriate versions

**b) Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scale**
- Classic IQ test
- Measures abstract thinking, problem-solving

---

#### **Relevance to Crime**

**Research Findings:**
- Lower IQ associated with certain crimes
- Poor judgment and decision-making
- Difficulty understanding consequences
- Easily influenced by others

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important**: Low IQ does NOT cause crime, but may be a risk factor when combined with other factors.

---

### **2. PERSONALITY TESTS**

#### **Purpose**
- Assess personality traits
- Identify antisocial tendencies

---

#### **a) Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI)**

**Features:**
- 567 true/false questions
- Most widely used personality test
- Measures multiple dimensions

**Clinical Scales Relevant to Crime:**

| Scale | Measures | Criminal Relevance |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| **Pd (Psychopathic Deviate)** | Antisocial tendencies | High in criminals |
| **Ma (Hypomania)** | Impulsivity, energy | Risk-taking behavior |
| **Sc (Schizophrenia)** | Thought disorders | Bizarre crimes |
| **Pa (Paranoia)** | Suspiciousness | Violent crimes |

---

#### **b) Eysenck Personality Questionnaire (EPQ)**

**Measures Three Dimensions:**

**1. Extraversion (E)**
- High E: Sociable, impulsive, sensation-seeking
- Associated with criminal behavior

**2. Neuroticism (N)**
- High N: Anxious, emotional, unstable
- Interaction with E increases crime

**3. Psychoticism (P)**
- High P: Aggressive, cold, antisocial
- Strong predictor of criminality

**Eysenck's Theory:**
- High E + High N + High P = Increased crime risk

---

#### **c) Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R)**

**Developer:** Robert Hare

**Purpose:**
- Specifically designed to assess **psychopathy**
- Gold standard for psychopathy assessment

**Features:**
- 20-item checklist
- Score: 0-40
- Score above 30 indicates psychopathy

**Factors Measured:**

**Factor 1: Interpersonal/Affective**
- Glibness, superficial charm
- Grandiose sense of self-worth
- Pathological lying
- Cunning/manipulative
- Lack of remorse or guilt
- Shallow affect
- Callous/lack of empathy
- Failure to accept responsibility

**Factor 2: Social Deviance**
- Need for stimulation
- Parasitic lifestyle
- Poor behavioral controls
- Early behavior problems
- Lack of realistic long-term goals
- Impulsivity
- Irresponsibility
- Juvenile delinquency
- Revocation of conditional release

---

### **3. PROJECTIVE TESTS**

#### **Purpose**
- Reveal unconscious thoughts and feelings
- Bypass conscious defenses

---

#### **a) Rorschach Inkblot Test**

**Method:**
- 10 inkblot cards shown to subject
- Subject describes what they see
- Responses analyzed for psychological themes

**Relevance:**
- Aggressive themes indicate violent tendencies
- Bizarre responses may indicate psychosis
- Quality of responses shows thought process

---

#### **b) Thematic Apperception Test (TAT)**

**Method:**
- Picture cards with ambiguous scenes
- Subject tells story about each picture
- Stories analyzed for themes

**Relevant Themes:**
- Aggression
- Authority conflict
- Interpersonal relationships
- Morality

---

### **4. NEUROPSYCHOLOGICAL TESTS**

#### **Purpose**
- Assess brain function
- Identify brain damage or dysfunction

---

#### **Tests Used**

**a) Halstead-Reitan Battery**
- Comprehensive neuropsychological assessment
- Detects brain damage

**b) Wisconsin Card Sorting Test**
- Measures executive function
- Problem-solving abilities

**c) Stroop Test**
- Measures attention and impulse control

---

#### **Relevance to Crime**

**Brain dysfunction areas linked to crime:**
- **Frontal lobe damage**: Impulsivity, poor judgment
- **Temporal lobe dysfunction**: Aggression, violence
- **Executive function deficits**: Planning, decision-making problems

---

### **5. BEHAVIORAL ASSESSMENT**

#### **Methods**

**a) Behavioral Observation**
- Direct observation of behavior
- In natural or controlled settings

**b) Behavioral Rating Scales**
- Parents, teachers, prison staff rate behavior
- Standardized checklists

**c) Functional Analysis**
- Identify triggers (antecedents)
- Understand consequences
- Design interventions

---

### **6. RISK ASSESSMENT INSTRUMENTS**

#### **Purpose**
- Predict likelihood of future criminal behavior
- Assess risk to public

---

#### **Common Instruments**

**a) Violence Risk Appraisal Guide (VRAG)**
- Predicts violent recidivism
- Uses 12 variables
- Statistical prediction

**b) Historical-Clinical-Risk Management (HCR-20)**
- Assesses violence risk
- Historical, Clinical, and Risk Management factors
- Widely used in forensic settings

**c) Static-99/Static-99R**
- Predicts sexual offense recidivism
- Uses static (unchangeable) factors
- Age, offense history, victim characteristics

---

## üìä **INTERPRETING TEST RESULTS**

### **Important Considerations**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Ethical Guidelines**

1. **No Single Test is Definitive**
   - Use multiple assessment methods
   - Combine test results with interviews, history

2. **Cultural Sensitivity**
   - Tests may have cultural bias
   - Norms vary across populations
   - Consider cultural context

3. **Professional Competence**
   - Only trained psychologists should administer
   - Proper interpretation requires expertise

4. **Confidentiality**
   - Test results are confidential
   - Use only for intended purpose

5. **Limitations**
   - Tests not 100% accurate
   - Cannot predict with certainty
   - Many false positives/negatives

<a name="criminal-profiling"></a>
## üïµÔ∏è **CRIMINAL PROFILING**

### **Definition**

**Criminal Profiling** (also called **offender profiling** or **psychological profiling**) is:

> The process of identifying the personality traits, behavioral tendencies, and demographic characteristics of an offender based on characteristics of the crime.

---

### **Historical Development**

#### **Early Cases**

**1888: Jack the Ripper**
- Dr. Thomas Bond created first profile
- Analyzed crime scenes
- Predicted offender characteristics

**1950s-60s: FBI Development**
- Behavioral Science Unit established
- Systematic approach developed
- Interview with convicted criminals

---

### **Types of Criminal Profiling**

#### **1. Crime Scene Analysis**

**Focus:** Physical evidence at scene

**What is Analyzed:**
- Victim selection
- Location
- Time of crime
- Weapon used
- Level of violence
- Staging or posing
- Signature behaviors

---

#### **2. Investigative Psychology**

**Developer:** David Canter (British psychologist)

**Approach:**
- Scientific and statistical
- Based on research data
- Less intuitive than FBI method

**Five Aspects Analyzed:**
1. Interpersonal coherence
2. Significance of time and place
3. Criminal characteristics
4. Criminal career
5. Forensic awareness

---

#### **3. Geographical Profiling**

**Focus:** Location patterns

**Analysis:**
- Where crimes occur
- Travel patterns
- Anchor points (home, work)
- Comfort zones

**Use:** Predict where offender lives or will strike next

---

### **Process of Criminal Profiling**

> üìå **Standard Steps**

#### **Step 1: Assimilate**
- Collect all information
- Crime scene photos
- Autopsy reports
- Witness statements
- Victimology

#### **Step 2: Classify**
- Organized vs Disorganized offender
- Crime type and motive
- Risk level assessment

#### **Step 3: Reconstruct**
- Reconstruct crime sequence
- Understand offender's behavior
- Identify signature vs modus operandi

#### **Step 4: Profile**
- Generate offender profile
- Demographic characteristics
- Psychological traits
- Behavioral patterns

#### **Step 5: Investigate**
- Use profile to narrow suspects
- Guide interview strategies
- Predict next moves

#### **Step 6: Apprehend**
- Aid in capture
- Interview strategies
- Elicit confession

---

### **FBI's Organized vs Disorganized Offenders**

| Characteristic | Organized Offender | Disorganized Offender |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Intelligence** | Above average | Below average |
| **Planning** | Planned crime | Spontaneous crime |
| **Crime Scene** | Controlled | Chaotic |
| **Victim** | Targeted, stranger | Victim of opportunity, known |
| **Weapon** | Brings weapon | Uses weapon at scene |
| **Restraints** | Uses restraints | Little/no restraints |
| **Body** | Moved or hidden | Left at scene |
| **Evidence** | Little physical evidence | Much physical evidence |
| **Social Skills** | Good social skills | Poor social skills |
| **Employment** | Employed | Unemployed |
| **Sexual Behavior** | Sexually competent | Sexually incompetent |
| **Living Situation** | Lives with partner | Lives alone |
| **Post-Offense** | Follows media coverage | May change behavior drastically |

<a name="factors-profiling"></a>
## üîç **FACTORS UNDERLYING CRIMINAL PROFILING**

### **1. Crime Scene Characteristics**

#### **Physical Evidence**
- Type of weapon
- Amount of force used
- Mutilation or torture
- Positioning of body
- Items taken or left

#### **Behavioral Evidence**
- Level of planning
- Control of victim
- Ritual or signature acts
- Attempt to stage or mislead

---

### **2. Victimology**

**Analysis of:**
- Age, gender, race
- Occupation and lifestyle
- Physical characteristics
- Personality traits
- Last known movements
- Relationship to offender

**Risk Categories:**

**High-Risk Victim:**
- Prostitute, hitchhiker
- High-crime area
- Risky lifestyle
- Easier for offender

**Low-Risk Victim:**
- Suburban resident
- Cautious lifestyle
- Difficult for offender
- Suggests offender skill/boldness

---

### **3. Modus Operandi (MO) vs Signature**

#### **Modus Operandi (MO)**

> The **method** used to commit the crime.

**Characteristics:**
- **Learned behavior**
- **Can change** and evolve
- **Practical purpose** (accomplish crime, avoid capture)

**Examples:**
- Breaking window to enter
- Wearing gloves
- Disabling alarm

---

#### **Signature**

> The **psychological** or **emotional** need of offender.

**Characteristics:**
- **Unnecessary for crime commission**
- **Does not change** (stable)
- **Unique to offender**
- **Psychological gratification**

**Examples:**
- Specific positioning of victim
- Ritualistic behaviors
- Taking souvenirs (trophies)
- Excessive violence beyond what's necessary

> üìå **Key Distinction**: MO is **functional**, Signature is **psychological**.

---

### **4. Offender Characteristics**

#### **Demographic Factors**

Based on statistical patterns:
- **Age**: Most violent crimes by 18-30 years
- **Gender**: 90% violent crimes by males
- **Race**: Varies by crime type and region
- **Socioeconomic status**: Property crimes linked to poverty

---

#### **Psychological Factors**

**Personality Traits:**
- Impulsivity
- Aggression
- Lack of empathy
- Sensation-seeking
- Narcissism

**Mental State:**
- Mental illness
- Substance abuse
- Personality disorders
- Cognitive deficits

---

#### **Behavioral Factors**

**Criminal History:**
- Prior arrests
- Type of previous crimes
- Escalation pattern
- Age of onset

**Social Factors:**
- Employment status
- Relationship status
- Family background
- Education level

---

### **5. Psychological Themes**

#### **Power/Control**
- Need to dominate victim
- Compensate for powerlessness
- Bondage, restraints
- **Example:** Serial rapists

#### **Anger/Rage**
- Displaced anger
- Excessive violence
- Overkill
- **Example:** Domestic homicides

#### **Sexual Gratification**
- Sexual component to violence
- Sadistic behaviors
- Sexual mutilation
- **Example:** Lust murders

#### **Financial Gain**
- Instrumental motivation
- Calculated, planned
- Minimal violence necessary
- **Example:** Armed robbery

---

## üìñ **TYPES OF CRIMINAL PROFILES**

### **1. Psychological Profile**

**Contains:**
- Personality characteristics
- Mental state
- Psychological needs
- Behavioral patterns

**Example Profile:**
```
Age: 25-35 years
Gender: Male
IQ: Average to above average
Personality: Narcissistic, lacks empathy
Mental state: Possible psychopathy
Likely employed in technical field
Lives alone or with parents
Socially awkward but can appear normal
```

---

### **2. Geographical Profile**

**Contains:**
- Probable residence location
- Activity space
- Travel patterns
- Comfort zone

**Theories:**

**Circle Theory (Canter):**
- Offender commits crimes in a circle
- Lives within the circle
- Two types:
  - **Marauders**: Operate from home base
  - **Commuters**: Travel to crime area

---

### **3. Typological Profile**

**Classifies offenders into types:**

**Example - Rapist Typology:**
- Power-reassurance rapist
- Power-assertive rapist
- Anger-retaliation rapist
- Sadistic rapist

Each type has distinct characteristics and behaviors.

<a name="merit-demerit"></a>
## ‚öñÔ∏è **MERITS AND DEMERITS OF CRIMINAL PROFILING**

### **‚úÖ MERITS (ADVANTAGES)**

#### **1. Narrows Suspect Pool**

**Benefit:**
- Reduces number of suspects
- Focuses investigation
- Saves time and resources

**Example:**
- If profile suggests 25-35 year old male
- Can eliminate women, children, elderly

---

#### **2. Guides Investigation**

**Helps:**
- Prioritize leads
- Determine interview strategies
- Predict next move
- Allocate resources efficiently

---

#### **3. Links Cases**

**Advantage:**
- Identify serial crimes
- Connect cases across jurisdictions
- Recognize patterns
- Share information between agencies

---

#### **4. Generates New Leads**

**By understanding offender:**
- Predict where offender might be
- Anticipate future crimes
- Understand motivation
- Identify potential witnesses

---

#### **5. Courtroom Use**

**Applications:**
- Expert testimony
- Explain offender behavior
- Sentencing recommendations
- Risk assessment

---

#### **6. Prevention**

**Benefits:**
- Identify potential targets
- Increase patrols in predicted areas
- Warn potential victims
- Prevent future crimes

---

#### **7. Interview Strategies**

**Helps:**
- Tailor interrogation approach
- Understand offender psychology
- Elicit confession
- Build rapport or confrontation strategy

---

### **‚ùå DEMERITS (LIMITATIONS)**

#### **1. Lack of Scientific Rigor**

**Issues:**
- Not always based on empirical research
- Relies on intuition and experience
- Difficult to validate scientifically
- No standardized methodology

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Criticism**: Profiling more "art" than "science"

---

#### **2. Inaccuracy**

**Problems:**
- Profiles can be vague or ambiguous
- May fit many people
- High rate of false positives
- Can be completely wrong

**Example:**
- Profile suggests loner
- Actual offender is social and married

---

#### **3. Confirmation Bias**

**Issue:**
- Police may focus only on suspects matching profile
- Ignore evidence that doesn't fit
- Real offender may be overlooked
- **Result:** Wrongful investigation focus

---

#### **4. Limited Applicability**

**Works Best For:**
- Serial crimes
- Psychopathic offenders
- Crimes with clear patterns

**Less Useful For:**
- One-time crimes
- Organized crime
- White-collar crime
- Crimes of passion

---

#### **5. Resource Intensive**

**Requirements:**
- Highly trained professionals
- Extensive crime scene data
- Time-consuming analysis
- Expensive process

**Reality:** Many agencies lack resources

---

#### **6. Ethical Concerns**

**Issues:**

**a) Stereotyping**
- May reinforce stereotypes
- Profiling based on race, class
- Discrimination concerns

**b) Privacy**
- Invasion of privacy
- Surveillance of potential suspects
- Civil liberties concerns

**c) Tunnel Vision**
- Police focus on profile
- Ignore other evidence
- May lead to false accusations

---

#### **7. Unrealistic Expectations**

**Media Effect:**
- TV shows create unrealistic image (CSI effect)
- Public expects quick results
- Overestimation of profiling accuracy
- Pressure on profilers

---

#### **8. Legal Admissibility**

**Issues:**
- Courts may not admit profile as evidence
- Lack of scientific validation
- Cannot be used to establish guilt
- Only as investigative tool

---

## üéì **CASE STUDIES**

### **Successful Profiling Cases**

#### **Case 1: The Mad Bomber (1940s-50s, New York)**

**Profile by Dr. James Brussel:**
- Middle-aged man
- Foreign-born, possibly Slavic
- Lives with mother or aunt
- Paranoid
- Would be wearing double-breasted suit

**Result:**
- Suspect (George Metesky) matched profile almost perfectly
- Wearing double-breasted suit when arrested
- Profile was remarkably accurate

---

#### **Case 2: The Railway Rapist (1980s, UK)**

**Profile by David Canter:**
- Lives in same area
- Has relationship but problems
- Semi-skilled/skilled worker
- Knowledge of railway system

**Result:**
- John Duffy arrested and convicted
- Profile was very accurate
- Established profiling credibility in UK

---

### **Unsuccessful/Problematic Cases**

#### **Case 3: The Beltway Snipers (2002, USA)**

**FBI Profile:**
- White male
- Loner
- Age 20s-30s
- Military background

**Actual Offenders:**
- Two African-American males
- Older man (41) and teenager (17)
- Close relationship (mentor-apprentice)
- One had military background

**Lesson:** Profile was partially wrong, but military prediction helped.

## ‚úÖ **SUMMARY & KEY TAKEAWAYS**

### **Core Concepts**

1. **Psychological tests** help measure criminal tendencies:
   - Intelligence tests (WAIS)
   - Personality tests (MMPI, PCL-R)
   - Projective tests (Rorschach, TAT)
   - Neuropsychological tests
   - Risk assessment instruments

2. **Criminal profiling** predicts offender characteristics from crime analysis:
   - Psychological profile
   - Geographical profile
   - Behavioral analysis

3. **Key distinction**: MO (functional) vs Signature (psychological)

4. **Organized vs Disorganized** offenders have distinct characteristics

5. **Merits**: Narrows suspects, guides investigation, links cases

6. **Demerits**: Lack of scientific rigor, inaccuracy, bias, limited applicability
