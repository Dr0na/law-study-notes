## üìñ **MEANING, PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF CRIMINAL PSYCHOLOGY**

### **Meaning of Criminal Psychology**

Criminal psychology is a **comparatively new science** which developed during the 19th century. In India, it is still developing.

---

#### **Definitions**

The term criminal psychology has been defined in various ways:

1. **The study of the mind and its working in relation to crime**

2. **The study of the mental processes, motivational patterns, and behaviour of criminals**

3. **The study of the wills, thoughts, intentions, and reactions of criminals**

---

### üéØ **Core Concept**

> Criminal psychology is a science which helps investigative institutions in fulfilling their mission more effectively by applying psychological knowledge to it.

---

### **Etymology**

The word **"psychology"** means the study of the soul:
- Derived from Greek **"psuchi"** (soul)
- Latin **"psyche"** (soul)
- **"Logos"** (rational course of study/science)

---

### **Definition by L.R.C. Haward**

According to criminal psychologist **L.R.C. Haward**:

> Criminal psychology is that branch of applied psychology which is concerned with the **collection, examination, and presentation of evidence for judicial purposes**.

Thus, criminal psychology is concerned with **investigative and court processes**.

---

## üéØ **PURPOSE OF CRIMINAL PSYCHOLOGY**

### **Primary Objectives**

Criminal psychology studies the **wills, thoughts, intentions, and reactions** of criminals and all that occurs during criminal behaviour.

**Main Purpose:**

1. **Finding out the cause** - Why someone committed a crime
2. **Understanding reactions** - Reactions after the crime
3. **Assisting investigations** - Help police and courts
4. **Predicting behaviour** - Assess risk of re-offending

---

### **Role in Legal System**

> üìå **Important**: Criminal psychologists are often called as **expert witnesses** in courts to help judges understand the mind of the criminal.

---

## üß† **PSYCHOLOGICAL VS PSYCHOANALYTICAL APPROACH**

### **Psychoanalytical Approach to Crime**

#### **Foundation**

Based on **psychoanalytic theory of Sigmund Freud**.

**Sigmund Freud:**
- Austrian neurologist
- Put light on crime from psychoanalytic view

---

#### **Core Argument**

> **Criminal behaviour originates primarily in the personalities of offenders** rather than in their biology or situation.

---

### **SIGMUND FREUD'S THEORY**

#### **Personality Definition**

**Personality** refers to reasonably stable patterns of behaviour, including:
- Thoughts
- Feelings
- Emotions

That distinguish people from one another and control a person's way of adapting to life experiences.

---

#### **Three-Point Structure of Human Personality**

> üß† **Memory Hook - "IES"**
> - **I**d - Primitive drives
> - **E**go - Reality principle
> - **S**uperego - Moral conscience

---

##### **1. The Id**

**Nature:** 
- Primitive part of personality
- Present at birth
- Unconscious

**Represents:** 
- Unconscious biological drives
- Food, water, air, sleep
- Sex drive
- Immediate gratification

**Principle:** **Pleasure Principle** - Seeks immediate satisfaction

---

##### **2. The Ego**

**Nature:**
- Develops after Id
- Conscious and unconscious
- Mediator

**Function:**
- Compensates for demands of Id
- Helps guide actions
- Remains within boundaries of social convention
- Considers what is practical and conventional

**Principle:** **Reality Principle** - Adapts Id's demands to reality

---

##### **3. The Superego**

**Nature:**
- Force of self-criticism and conscience
- Moral component of personality
- Develops through socialization

**Development:**
- Superego develops when individual internalizes moral standards
- Values of parents and community
- Social norms and ethics

**Function:**
- Controls Id's impulses
- Guides Ego toward moral behavior
- Creates guilt when violated

---

### **Psychoanalytic Explanation of Crime**

#### **Imbalance Theory**

Crime occurs due to **imbalance** between Id, Ego, and Superego:

**Scenario 1: Overdeveloped Id**
- Strong primitive urges
- Weak Ego cannot control
- Weak Superego provides no guilt
- **Result:** Impulsive, violent crimes

**Scenario 2: Underdeveloped Superego**
- No moral conscience
- No guilt feelings
- **Result:** Psychopathic behavior

**Scenario 3: Overdeveloped Superego**
- Excessive guilt
- Person commits crime to be punished
- **Result:** Crime as self-punishment

---

> **Freud's Summary**: The psychoanalytic model depicts the criminal offender as **an aggressive, frustrated person dominated by events that occurred early in childhood**.

---

### **Emotional Imbalance as Main Factor**

Sigmund Freud considers **emotional imbalance** as the main factor in causation of crime.

> **Mentally and emotionally defective people are more prone to crime than persons who are emotionally stable.**

---

- Feeling of being neglected
- Sense of being outcast
- Compensation for inadequacy
- Attempt to prove superiority

### **2. AUGUST AICHORN**

#### **Main Contribution**

Most important causes of crime according to Aichorn:

---

##### **1. Faulty Methods of Child Training**

> **Core Argument**: Parents are responsible for making the child criminal in **BOTH cases**:
> - When parents have **no time to give love** (Neglect), OR
> - When parents **give love too much** (Over-indulgence)

**Explanation:**

**Case A: Parental Neglect**
- Child feels unloved
- Develops antisocial tendencies
- Seeks attention through negative behavior
- Becomes criminal

**Case B: Over-Indulgence**
- Child becomes spoiled
- Cannot accept limits
- Expects everything immediately
- When denied, turns to crime

> üìå **Key Point**: BOTH extremes are harmful - Balance is essential.

---

##### **2. Criminal Parents Influence**

**Effect:**
- Child with criminal parents generally upset
- Learns criminal behavior from parents
- Gets involved in crime commission

**Solution:**
> A child should be given proper treatment to make their life happy and provide them pleasurable environment.

---

### **3. CARL JUNG**

#### **Background**
- Closest student of Sigmund Freud
- Developed **Analytical Psychology**

#### **Main Contribution**

**Collective Unconscious:**
- Shared memories of human race
- Inherited psychological patterns
- Archetypes common to all humans
- Can influence criminal behavior

<a name="behaviourist-approach"></a>
## üéØ **BEHAVIOURIST APPROACH TO CRIME**

### **Introduction**

Behavior System is a very important aspect in the study of crimes. The common element of **violation of law** is found in every crime.

> **Key Point**: No specific or definite reason can be regarded as the sole cause of crime, but crime spoils the whole peace and order of society.

---

### **Nature of Criminal Acts**

Criminal acts differ among themselves in the following aspects:

1. **Motives and characteristics** of offenders
2. **Characteristics of victims**
3. **Situation** in which crimes are committed
4. **Techniques** used for commission of crime
5. **Damages** resulting from crime
6. **Reaction** of victims and public
7. **Quantum of loss** suffered by victim

---

### **Behaviour System in Crime**

> **Core Principle**: All criminal activities depend upon the behaviour system in crime.

The behaviourist approach examines:
- How behaviour patterns facilitate crime
- How criminal behaviour is learned and reinforced
- How social and environmental factors shape criminal conduct
- How criminals coordinate and cooperate

---

## üìã **CHARACTERISTICS OF BEHAVIOURIST APPROACH**

### **1. Individual or Group Participation**

A crime may be committed by:
- **Individual offender**, OR
- **Group of individuals** (directly or indirectly participating)

**Examples of group crimes:**
- Professional theft
- Drug trafficking
- Fraudulent advertising
- Organized crime

---

### **2. Common Behaviour Pattern**

The behaviour in a behaviour system is **not unique** to any particular individual:

> It operates in the same way in a large number of persons - it is **common behaviour**.

---

### **3. Joint Participation**

**Common and joint participation** in crime commission is the essential characteristic of the behaviourist approach towards crime.

---

### **4. Mutual Cooperation Among Criminals**

**Criminals get mutual cooperation from each other because of:**
- **Sentimental coordination** between them
- **Common desire** for commission of crime
- Shared goals and values
- Group solidarity

---

### **Key Differences from Psychoanalytic Approach**

| Feature | Psychoanalytic | Behaviourist |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Focus** | Individual unconscious | Observable group behavior |
| **Method** | Case studies | Observation of patterns |
| **Cause** | Internal conflicts | External reinforcement |
| **Social Factor** | Secondary | Primary |
| **Learning** | Not emphasized | Central concept |

---

## üîë **DEFINITION OF CRIMINAL BEHAVIOUR**

### **Four Definitions (Andrews and Bonta)**

#### **1. Legal Definition**

> **Acts prohibited by law and punished by the state**

- Most widely used definition
- Clear and objective
- Based on penal codes

---

#### **2. Moral/Religious Definition**

> **Acts which are violation of moral or religious code and believed to be punishable by supreme authority (God)**

- Based on religious teachings
- Universal moral principles
- May not coincide with legal definition

---

#### **3. Social Definition**

> **Acts which violate norms of society or traditions and believed to be punishable by community**

- Based on social customs
- Cultural variations
- May be legal but socially unacceptable

---

#### **4. Psychological Definition**

> **Acts causing serious psychological stress or mental damage to victim**

- Focuses on impact on victim
- May include acts not legally defined as crimes
- Emotional and mental harm

---

### **Comprehensive Definition**

From the above definitions, **criminal behaviour** can be defined as:

> **Any kind of anti-social behaviour which is punishable by law or norms stated by community.**

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Note**: It is very difficult to define criminal behaviour comprehensively because different disciplines have different perspectives.

## üß† **MENTAL ILLNESS AND CRIME**

### **Relationship Between Mental Illness and Crime**

Not all mentally ill persons are criminals, and not all criminals are mentally ill. However, certain mental illnesses are associated with increased risk of criminal behavior.

---

### **Types of Mental Illness Associated with Crime**

#### **1. Psychosis**

**Definition:** Severe mental disorder with loss of contact with reality

**Types:**
- **Schizophrenia**: Hallucinations, delusions
- **Paranoia**: Delusions of persecution
- **Manic Depression**: Extreme mood swings

**Criminal Behavior:**
- Violent crimes during psychotic episodes
- Crimes based on delusions
- Lack awareness of wrongdoing

---

#### **2. Neurosis**

**Definition:** Less severe mental disorder with anxiety

**Types:**
- Anxiety disorders
- Phobias
- Obsessive-compulsive disorder

**Criminal Behavior:**
- Compulsive stealing (kleptomania)
- Compulsive fire-setting (pyromania)
- Usually minor crimes

---

#### **3. Personality Disorders**

**Definition:** Persistent patterns of behavior deviating from norms

**Types:**

**a) Antisocial Personality Disorder (Psychopathy/Sociopathy)**
- No conscience or empathy
- Manipulative
- Impulsive
- **Most criminal type**

**b) Narcissistic Personality Disorder**
- Excessive self-importance
- Lack of empathy
- Exploitation of others

**c) Borderline Personality Disorder**
- Unstable emotions
- Impulsive behavior
- Self-harm

---

#### **4. Mental Retardation**

**Characteristics:**
- Low intelligence (IQ below 70)
- Poor judgment
- Easily influenced
- May not understand consequences

**Criminal Behavior:**
- May be used by others to commit crime
- Lack understanding of right/wrong
- Need special treatment

---

### **Legal Provisions for Mentally Ill Offenders**

#### **Insanity Defense**

**Indian Penal Code, Section 84:**

> "Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of unsoundness of mind, is incapable of knowing the nature of the act, or that he is doing what is either wrong or contrary to law."

**M'Naghten Rules (Common Law):**

To establish insanity defense, must prove:
1. Person suffered from mental disease
2. Did not know nature and quality of act, OR
3. Did not know act was wrong

---

### **Treatment vs Punishment**

> üìå **Modern Approach**: Mentally ill criminals should receive **treatment** rather than **punishment**.

**Facilities:**
- Psychiatric hospitals
- Special secure units
- Forensic mental health services
- Community treatment programs

## ‚ö° **HUMAN AGGRESSION AND VIOLENCE TO CRIME**

### **Definition of Aggression**

**Aggression** is:

> Behavior intended to harm another person who is motivated to avoid such treatment.

---

### **Types of Aggression**

#### **1. Hostile/Reactive Aggression**

**Characteristics:**
- Impulsive
- Emotional response
- Triggered by anger or frustration
- Goal: To harm the victim
- Example: Assault during fight

---

#### **2. Instrumental/Proactive Aggression**

**Characteristics:**
- Planned and deliberate
- Means to achieve goal
- Unemotional
- Goal: To obtain something
- Example: Armed robbery for money

---

### **Theories of Aggression**

#### **1. Instinct Theory (Freud)**

**Argument:**
- Aggression is **innate instinct**
- Death instinct ("Thanatos") drives aggression
- Must be expressed - if not outward, then inward (self-harm)

---

#### **2. Frustration-Aggression Hypothesis (Dollard)**

**Core Principle:**

> "Frustration always leads to aggression, and aggression is always caused by frustration."

**Explanation:**
- When goal-directed behavior is blocked ‚Üí Frustration
- Frustration ‚Üí Aggressive behavior
- Aggression releases frustration

**Application to Crime:**
- Economic deprivation ‚Üí Frustration ‚Üí Crime
- Social rejection ‚Üí Frustration ‚Üí Violence
- Blocked ambitions ‚Üí Frustration ‚Üí Aggression

---

#### **3. Social Learning Theory (Bandura)**

**Core Principle:**

> Aggression is **learned** through:
> - **Observation** (modeling)
> - **Reinforcement** (rewards/punishments)

**Bobo Doll Experiment:**
- Children who observed adults hitting doll
- Later imitated aggressive behavior
- Proved aggression is learned

**Application to Crime:**
- Children learn violence from parents
- Media violence teaches aggression
- Peer influence in gang violence

---

### **Biological Factors in Aggression**

#### **1. Neurological Factors**

**Brain Structures:**
- **Amygdala**: Emotional responses, fear, aggression
- **Prefrontal Cortex**: Impulse control, decision-making
- **Hypothalamus**: Regulates aggressive responses

**Damage to these areas** ‚Üí Increased aggression

---

#### **2. Neurotransmitters**

**Serotonin:**
- Low levels ‚Üí Increased aggression
- Regulates mood and impulse control

**Dopamine:**
- Involved in reward and motivation
- Abnormal levels ‚Üí Risk-taking behavior

**Testosterone:**
- Higher levels associated with aggression
- Males more aggressive than females

---

#### **3. Genetic Factors**

**Research Shows:**
- Genetic component to aggression
- Twin studies show heritability
- But genes not destiny - environment matters

---

### **Environmental Factors in Aggression**

#### **1. Family Environment**

- Child abuse ‚Üí Adult aggression
- Witnessing domestic violence
- Harsh parenting
- Lack of supervision

#### **2. Peer Influence**

- Gang membership
- Peer pressure
- Delinquent friends

#### **3. Media Violence**

- Violent TV, movies, video games
- Desensitization to violence
- Learning aggressive scripts

#### **4. Alcohol and Drugs**

- Reduce inhibitions
- Impair judgment
- Increase aggression

---

### **Types of Violent Crime**

#### **1. Homicide**

**Definition:** Unlawful killing of human being

**Types:**
- **Murder**: With premeditation
- **Manslaughter**: Without premeditation
- **Culpable homicide**: Indian equivalent

**Psychological Factors:**
- Anger, rage
- Mental illness
- Alcohol/drugs
- Psychopathy

---

#### **2. Assault and Battery**

**Definition:** Unlawful physical attack

**Psychological Factors:**
- Frustration-aggression
- Low self-control
- Learned violence
- Substance abuse

---

#### **3. Rape and Sexual Violence**

**Psychological Factors:**
- Power and control
- Sexual deviance
- Psychopathy
- Childhood abuse

---

#### **4. Robbery**

**Definition:** Taking property by force or threat

**Characteristics:**
- Instrumental aggression
- Combines property crime with violence
- Often involves weapons

---

#### **5. Domestic Violence**

**Psychological Factors:**
- Power imbalance
- Learned behavior
- Substance abuse
- Stress and frustration

---

### **Preventing Violence and Aggression**

#### **Individual Level**
