## üìö TOPIC 1: CRIME - MEANING & ELEMENTS

### What is Crime?

**Crime** = An act/omission punishable by law

**Important Definitions:**
- **Blackstone:** Act committed/omitted in violation of public law
- **Salmond:** Act deemed harmful to society even if victim is individual
- **Kenny:** Harmful conduct that sovereign desires to prevent

---

### Essential Elements: **ACTUS REUS + MENS REA**

#### Maxim: "Actus non facit reum, nisi mens sit rea"
*An act does not make a person guilty unless the mind is also guilty*

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ ACTUS REUS (Guilty Act)

**Components:**
- Voluntary act
- Omission to act (when duty exists)
- State of affairs

**Causation Tests:**
- **Causa causans:** Immediate operating cause ‚úÖ
- **Causa sine qua non:** Necessary cause but not sufficient ‚ùå

üìå **Cases:**
- **R v. Hilton:** Intervening act breaks chain of causation
- **R v. Martin:** Victim's voluntary act breaks causation
- **R v. Swindall:** Contributory negligence NO defense in crime

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ MENS REA (Guilty Mind)

**Hierarchy (Highest to Lowest):**

```
1. INTENTION
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Foresees + Wills = Highest culpability
   
2. KNOWLEDGE
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Aware but may not desire
   
3. RECKLESSNESS
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Foresees but takes unjustifiable risk
   
4. NEGLIGENCE
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Fails to foresee (breach of duty)
```

üß† **Memory:** "I-K-R-N"

üìå **Case:** *Basdev v. State of Pepsu* - Knowledge is awareness; intention is willingness

---

### MOTIVE vs. MENS REA

| **Motive**          | **Mens Rea**          |
|---------------------|-----------------------|
| WHY act done        | Mental state          |
| Not essential       | Essential             |
| May be good/bad     | Always guilty state   |
| Relevant for punishment | Relevant for liability |

**Example:** Stealing to feed poor = Good motive but guilty mens rea

## üìö TOPIC 2: STAGES OF CRIME

```

    ‚ùå          ‚ùå (usually)     ‚úÖ          ‚úÖ
```

### Analysis

1. **Intention** - Not punishable (*Cogitationis poenam nemo patitur*)
2. **Preparation** - Generally not punishable (exceptions: dacoity, war)

|----------------------|-----------------------|
| Arranging means      | Using means           |
| Remote from crime    | Proximate to crime    |
| Equivocal act        | Unequivocal act       |
| Example: Buying gun  | Example: Pulling trigger |

## üìö TOPIC 1: MISTAKE OF FACT & LAW

### Maxim: "Ignorantia facti excusat, ignorantia juris non excusat"

### Sections 76 & 79 IPC (14 & 17 BNS)

**Section 76:** Bound by law
**Section 79:** Justified by law

**Requirements:**
1. ‚úÖ Mistake of FACT (not law)
2. ‚úÖ In GOOD FAITH
3. ‚úÖ With DUE CARE

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **R v. Tolson (1889)**
- Woman remarried believing 1st husband dead (7 years)
- **Held:** NOT GUILTY - Reasonable mistake of fact
- **Principle:** Reasonable mistake in good faith is defense

üìå **R v. Prince (1875)**
- Took girl under 16 believing she was 18+
- **Held:** GUILTY - Act unlawful even if facts as believed
- **Principle:** No defense if act itself wrong

üìå **State of West Bengal v. Shew Mangal Singh**
- Police fired on mob under Commissioner's order
- **Held:** Protected under Section 76 if order lawful and acted in good faith

---

### Mistake of Law

**General Rule:** NO DEFENSE

**Limited Exceptions:**
1. Law not published
2. Relied on statute later held unconstitutional
3. Relied on judicial decision later overruled
4. Relied on official interpretation

üìå **R v. Bailey:** Ignorance of law no excuse (even if couldn't know)

---

### üìä Quick Comparison

| **Mistake of Fact**  | **Mistake of Law**    |
|----------------------|-----------------------|
| ‚úÖ Valid defense     | ‚ùå NO defense         |
| Example: Age, consent| Example: Not knowing theft is crime |
| Negatives mens rea   | Everyone presumed to know law |

---

## üìö TOPIC 2: ACCIDENT (SECTION 80 IPC / 18 BNS)

### Essential Ingredients (ALL MUST BE PRESENT)

```
1. ‚úÖ Act done by ACCIDENT/MISFORTUNE
2. ‚úÖ WITHOUT criminal intention/knowledge
3. ‚úÖ While doing LAWFUL act
4. ‚úÖ By LAWFUL means
5. ‚úÖ In LAWFUL manner
6. ‚úÖ With PROPER CARE & CAUTION
```

### "Proper Care" = Reasonable care (not utmost care)

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Tunda v. Rex**
- Death during wrestling match (both friends, consented)
- **Held:** NOT GUILTY - Accidental, no foul play, implied consent

üìå **Jageshwar v. Emperor**
- Beating X; blow struck X's wife's baby on her shoulder
- **Held:** GUILTY - Beating is unlawful act; Section 80 doesn't apply

üìå **Bhupendra Singh v. State of Gujarat (SC)**
- Constable fired without identifying target
- **Held:** GUILTY - Lack of proper care and caution

üß† **Key Point:** All 6 ingredients must be satisfied. If even one missing ‚Üí No protection under Section 80

---

## üìö TOPIC 3: NECESSITY (SECTION 81 IPC / BNS)

### Maxim: "Quod necessitas non habet legem"
*Necessity knows no law*

### Requirements

```
1. ‚úÖ Act in GOOD FAITH
2. ‚úÖ To prevent/avoid harm to person/property
3. ‚úÖ WITHOUT criminal intention
4. ‚úÖ Choosing LESSER evil to avoid GREATER evil
```

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **R v. Dudley and Stephens (1884) - Leading Case**
- Shipwreck; 3 men killed cabin boy to survive
- **Held:** GUILTY of murder
- **Principle:** Necessity NO defense for taking human life
- **Rationale:** Who decides whose life more valuable? Slippery slope

üìå **United States v. Holmes**
- Lifeboat overloaded; threw 16 passengers overboard
- **Held:** GUILTY of manslaughter (reduced from murder)
- **Court:** Should have drawn lots; can't selectively sacrifice

üìå **Gopal Naidu v. Emperor**
- Police restrained drunk man with revolver (non-cognizable offense)
- **Held:** Justified under Section 81 - Immediate danger

---

### Section 80 vs. 81

| **Section 80 (Accident)**    | **Section 81 (Necessity)**   |
|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| No knowledge of harm         | Knowledge harm likely        |
| Harm not foreseen            | Harm known but unavoidable   |
| Example: Brake failure       | Example: Amputation to save life |

## üìö TOPIC 4: INFANCY (SECTIONS 82-83 IPC / BNS)

### Principle: "Doli Incapax"

```
AGE 0-7 YEARS (Section 82)
‚îú‚îÄ> ABSOLUTELY NO liability
‚îú‚îÄ> Presumption CONCLUSIVE
‚îî‚îÄ> Cannot be rebutted

AGE 7-12 YEARS (Section 83)
‚îú‚îÄ> REBUTTABLE presumption
‚îú‚îÄ> Prosecution must prove maturity
‚îî‚îÄ> If maturity proved ‚Üí LIABLE

AGE 12+ YEARS
‚îú‚îÄ> FULL liability
‚îî‚îÄ> (But Juvenile Justice Act may apply)
```

### Test for Maturity (7-12 years)

**Prosecution must prove child:**
1. Understood NATURE of act
2. Understood CONSEQUENCES  
3. Knew act was WRONG/contrary to law

**How to determine:**
- Nature of offense
- Manner of commission
- Conduct before/after
- Child's demeanor in court

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Ulla Mahapatra v. The King**
- Boy 11 years; picked knife, threatened, cut person
- **Held:** GUILTY - Sufficient maturity (did what he planned)

üìå **Walters v. Lunt**
- 7-year-old stole tricycle
- Parents charged with receiving stolen property
- **Held:** Parents acquitted (child below 7 can't steal ‚Üí not stolen property)

## üìö TOPIC 5: INSANITY (SECTION 84 IPC / BNS)

### M'Naghten's Rule (1843)

**Section 84:** Person of unsound mind, incapable of:
1. Knowing NATURE of act, OR
2. Knowing act is WRONG or contrary to law

---

### Legal vs. Medical Insanity

```
MEDICAL INSANITY
‚îî‚îÄ> Wide concept (all mental disorders)

LEGAL INSANITY (Section 84)
‚îî‚îÄ> Narrow concept
    ‚îú‚îÄ> Only when cannot know nature of act
    ‚îî‚îÄ> OR cannot know it's wrong/illegal
```

üß† **Key Point:** Not all medical insanity = legal insanity

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ Person UNSOUND MIND at time of act
2. ‚úÖ By reason of unsoundness, INCAPABLE of:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> Knowing NATURE of act, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Knowing it's WRONG/contrary to law
3. ‚úÖ CAUSATION: Unsoundness CAUSED incapacity
```

### Two-Limb Test

**LIMB 1:** Cannot know NATURE of act (complete insanity)
**LIMB 2:** Cannot know act is WRONG (partial insanity covered)

---

### When Must Unsoundness Exist?

```
BEFORE ‚Üí Irrelevant (unless continuing)
DURING ‚Üí ‚úÖ CRUCIAL
AFTER ‚Üí Irrelevant (but may be evidence)
```

### Burden of Proof

**On Accused:** Preponderance of probabilities (not beyond reasonable doubt)

### Evidence

- Conduct before, during, after act
- Medical history
- Expert testimony
- Lack of motive

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Hari Singh Gond v. State of M.P. (SC)**
- **Principle:** Section 84 = legal test, not medical

üìå **Rattan Lal v. State of M.P. (SC)**
- **Principle:** Crucial time = when crime committed; determined by conduct precedent, attendant, subsequent

üìå **Kamala Bhuniya v. West Bengal**
- Wife killed husband; no motive, strange behavior
- **Held:** Entitled to Section 84 benefit

---

### NOT Covered Under Section 84

‚ùå Irresistible impulse
‚ùå Partial delusion (unless meets test)
‚ùå Psychopathic personality
‚ùå Diminished responsibility

## üìö TOPIC 6: INTOXICATION (SECTIONS 85-86 IPC / BNS)

### Two Types

```
SECTION 85: INVOLUNTARY INTOXICATION
‚îú‚îÄ> Administered WITHOUT knowledge
‚îú‚îÄ> OR AGAINST will
‚îî‚îÄ> ‚úÖ COMPLETE DEFENSE (like Section 84)

SECTION 86: VOLUNTARY INTOXICATION
‚îú‚îÄ> Knowingly consumed
‚îú‚îÄ> NO defense for basic intent crimes
‚îî‚îÄ> LIMITED defense for specific intent crimes
```

---

### Section 85: Requirements

```
1. ‚úÖ Intoxicated
2. ‚úÖ Incapable of knowing:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> Nature of act, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> That it's wrong/contrary to law
3. ‚úÖ Substance administered:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> WITHOUT knowledge, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> AGAINST will
```

---

### Section 86: Voluntary Intoxication

**Effect:**
- Presumed to have same knowledge as if sober
- Exception: If intoxication was involuntary

**Limited Defense For:**
- Specific intent crimes (murder, theft, cheating)
- May negative specific intent
- Courts reluctant to accept

**NO Defense For:**
- Basic intent crimes (rape, assault, hurt)

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Bablu v. State of Rajasthan (SC)**
- Evidence of drunkenness must prove accused INCAPABLE of forming intent

üìå **Basdev v. State of Pepsu**
- Killed wife under influence of Bhang
- **Held:** Voluntary intoxication not defense

üìå **DPP v. Beard (English - Persuasive)**
- Three rules on intoxication:
  1. Long-term drinking causing insanity ‚Üí insanity defense
  2. Drunkenness preventing specific intent ‚Üí defense
  3. Drunkenness NO excuse for basic intent crimes

---

### üìä Section 85 vs. 86

| **Section 85**          | **Section 86**          |
|-------------------------|-------------------------|
| Involuntary             | Voluntary               |
| Complete defense        | Limited/No defense      |
| All offenses            | Only specific intent    |
| Like insanity defense   | Presumed sober          |

## üìö TOPIC 1: RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENSE (SECTIONS 96-106 IPC / 34-44 BNS)

### Fundamental Principle

**Section 96:** Nothing is offense done in exercise of right of private defense

**Philosophy:**
- Natural right of self-preservation
- When no time to seek state help
- Preventive, not punitive

---

### Two Types

```
1. PRIVATE DEFENSE OF BODY (Sections 100-101)
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Against assault, rape, kidnapping, etc.

2. PRIVATE DEFENSE OF PROPERTY (Sections 102-105)
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Against theft, robbery, mischief, trespass
```

---

### When Right Arises & Ends

**Commencement:**
- When reasonable apprehension of danger
- Need not wait for actual assault

**Continuation:**
- So long as danger continues

**Termination:**
- When danger ceases
- When attacker escapes/disabled
- Cannot pursue fleeing attacker

üß† **Key:** Right is PREVENTIVE, not PUNITIVE

---

### When Can DEATH Be Caused?

#### For BODY (Section 100)

üß† **Memory:** "Death-GH-Rape-Lust-Kidnap-Acid"

```
1. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Reasonable apprehension of DEATH
2. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Reasonable apprehension of GRIEVOUS HURT
3. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Intent to RAPE
4. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Intent to gratify UNNATURAL LUST
5. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Intent to KIDNAP/ABDUCT
6. ‚ö∞Ô∏è Assault ‚Üí Intent to WRONGFULLY CONFINE (if can't seek help)
7. ‚ö∞Ô∏è ACID attack ‚Üí Causing/likely to cause GH
```

#### For PROPERTY (Section 103)

üß† **Memory:** "RoBN-FIRE-TMT"

```
1. ‚ö∞Ô∏è ROBBERY
2. ‚ö∞Ô∏è HOUSE-BREAKING by NIGHT
3. ‚ö∞Ô∏è MISCHIEF by FIRE (to dwelling/building)
4. ‚ö∞Ô∏è THEFT/MISCHIEF/TRESPASS causing apprehension of death/GH
```

---

### Restrictions (Section 99)

```
1. ‚ùå NO right if time to seek public authority
2. ‚ùå NO right against public servant in good faith
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Unless causes apprehension of death/GH
3. ‚ùå NO right against innocent person
4. ‚ùå MUST NOT inflict MORE harm than necessary
5. ‚ùå MUST NOT be vindictive/retributive
```

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Vidhya Singh v. State of M.P. (SC)**
- **Principle:** Right available only while necessity exists; not retributive

üìå **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab (SC)**
- **Principle:** No right against public servants acting in good faith

üìå **Munshi Ram v. Delhi Administration**
- **Principle:** Force must be proportionate to injury apprehended

---

### üìä Analysis Framework

```
STEP 1: Did right exist?
        ‚îî‚îÄ> Offense/apprehension of offense?

STEP 2: Against whom?
        ‚îî‚îÄ> Cannot be innocent/public servant in good faith

STEP 3: When?
        ‚îî‚îÄ> Danger must be continuing

STEP 4: Was force proportionate?
        ‚îî‚îÄ> Not excessive

STEP 5: Did it continue after danger ceased?
        ‚îî‚îÄ> Cannot be retributive
```

## üìö TOPIC 2: ABETMENT (SECTIONS 107-116 IPC / 45-60 BNS)

### Definition (Section 107)

**Three Ways to Abet:**

```
1. INSTIGATION
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Actively suggesting, inciting, encouraging

2. CONSPIRACY
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Agreement between two or more persons

3. INTENTIONAL AID
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Helping by act or illegal omission
```

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ Abettor must INTEND to abet
2. ‚úÖ Abettor must do one of three acts:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> Instigate, OR
   ‚îú‚îÄ> Conspire, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Intentionally aid
3. ‚úÖ Connection between abetment and offense
```

---

### Types of Abetment

#### 1. Abetment by Instigation

**Instigation** = Active suggestion, support, stimulation

**Methods:**
- Direct incitement
- Goading
- Urging
- Creating atmosphere of encouragement

**Not Instigation:**
- Mere advice
- Casual remark
- Reporting offense to authority

---

#### 2. Abetment by Conspiracy

**Requirements:**
- Two or more persons
- Agreement to do illegal act or legal act by illegal means
- Overt act not necessary for abetment liability

---

#### 3. Abetment by Intentional Aid

**Requirements:**
- Aid must be INTENTIONAL
- Aid must be by ACT or ILLEGAL OMISSION
- Knowledge that act being aided is offense

**Examples:**
- Keeping watch during burglary
- Providing weapons
- Driving getaway vehicle

---

### Abetment of Abetment (Section 111)

**Allowed:** A abets B to abet C to commit offense
- A can be punished as if he abetted C directly

---

### Abettor Present vs. Absent (Sections 109, 113, 114, 115, 116)

```
ABETTOR PRESENT AT SCENE
‚îî‚îÄ> Section 109: Same punishment as principal

ABETTOR ABSENT
‚îî‚îÄ> Section 113: Same punishment as principal

DIFFERENT OFFENSE COMMITTED (Section 111)
‚îú‚îÄ> If consequence of abetment
‚îî‚îÄ> Abettor liable for that offense too

ABETMENT OF OFFENSE PUNISHABLE WITH DEATH/LIFE
‚îî‚îÄ> If offense not committed but act done
    ‚îî‚îÄ> Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine
```

---

### Important Principles

**Impossibility No Defense:**
- A abets B to kill C
- C already dead
- A still liable for abetment

**Abettor Liability Even If Principal Not Identified:**
- Can punish abettor even if principal not caught

**Abettor May Be Punished Though Principal Not Punished:**
- If principal has defense (insanity, infancy)
- Abettor still liable

---

### üìä Quick Reference

| **Type**        | **Definition**                | **Example**                  |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Instigation     | Active incitement             | "Go kill him!"               |
| Conspiracy      | Agreement to do illegal act   | "Let's rob the bank"         |
| Intentional Aid | Help by act/omission          | Providing weapon             |

## üìö TOPIC 3: CONSPIRACY (SECTION 120A-B IPC / 61 BNS)

### Definition (Section 120A)

**Criminal Conspiracy:**
- Agreement between two or more persons
- To do illegal act OR
- Legal act by illegal means

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ Agreement between TWO or more persons
2. ‚úÖ To do:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> ILLEGAL act, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> LEGAL act by ILLEGAL means
3. ‚úÖ Meeting of minds
```

---

### Key Principles

**Agreement is Essence:**
- Overt act NOT necessary
- Conspiracy complete when agreement made
- Even if nothing further done

**Wheel Conspiracy:**
- One person (hub) conspires with many (spokes)
- Each spoke need not know others

**Chain Conspiracy:**
- A conspires with B, B with C, C with D
- All liable even if don't know all others

---

### Punishment (Section 120B)

```
CONSPIRACY TO COMMIT SERIOUS OFFENSE
‚îú‚îÄ> Punishable with death/imprisonment 2+ years/life
‚îî‚îÄ> Punishment: Same as abetment of that offense

CONSPIRACY TO COMMIT MINOR OFFENSE
‚îú‚îÄ> Imprisonment 6 months or less or fine only
‚îî‚îÄ> Punishment: Imprisonment 6 months + fine

CONSPIRACY TO COMMIT FALSE INFO/OFFENSE
‚îî‚îÄ> As specified in section
```

---

### Conspiracy vs. Abetment

| **Conspiracy**               | **Abetment**                 |
|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Agreement is offense itself  | Offense must be committed    |
| Two or more persons essential| One person can abet          |
| Overt act not needed         | Act towards offense needed   |
| Continuing offense           | Complete when act done       |

## üìö TOPIC 4: ATTEMPT (SECTION 511 IPC / 62 BNS)

### Definition

**Attempt** = Direct movement towards commission after preparation

**Section 511:** Attempt to commit offense punishable with imprisonment for life/imprisonment

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ Intention to commit offense
2. ‚úÖ Offense punishable with imprisonment for life/imprisonment
3. ‚úÖ Act done towards commission
4. ‚úÖ Act falls SHORT of completion
5. ‚úÖ No express provision for punishment of attempt
```

---

### Preparation vs. Attempt

| **Preparation**         | **Attempt**              |
|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| Arranging means         | Using means              |
| Remote from crime       | Proximate to crime       |
| Equivocal act           | Unequivocal act          |
| Generally not punishable| Punishable (Section 511) |
| Example: Buying poison  | Example: Administering poison |

---

### Tests to Distinguish

**1. Proximity Test**
- How close to actual commission?
- Immediate step = attempt

**2. Equivocality Test**
- Does act unequivocally show criminal intent?
- If yes = attempt

**3. Substantial Step Test**
- Has accused taken substantial step towards commission?

---

### Impossibility

**Legal vs. Factual Impossibility:**

```
FACTUAL IMPOSSIBILITY: NOT A DEFENSE
‚îú‚îÄ> A tries to steal from empty pocket
‚îî‚îÄ> A still liable for attempt

LEGAL IMPOSSIBILITY: MAY BE DEFENSE
‚îú‚îÄ> Act attempted is not actually criminal
‚îî‚îÄ> No liability
```

---

### Punishment

**Section 511:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 1/2 of longest term provided for offense
- OR Fine provided for offense
- OR Both

**Example:**
- Murder punishable with death/life ‚Üí Attempt to murder: Life or less

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Abhayanand Mishra v. State of Bihar (SC)**
- Applied for exam with forged certificates
- **Held:** GUILTY of attempt to cheat
- **Principle:** Preparation complete when application ready; dispatch = attempt

üìå **Illustration (a) to Section 511**
- A makes attempt to steal jewels by breaking box
- Finds box empty
- **Held:** GUILTY - Did act towards commission

üìå **Illustration (b) to Section 511**
- A attempts to pick Z's pocket by thrusting hand
- Z's pocket empty
- **Held:** GUILTY - Factual impossibility no defense

---

### When is Attempt Complete?

```
OFFENSE ATTEMPTED BUT NOT COMPLETED
‚îî‚îÄ> ‚úÖ Liable under Section 511

OFFENSE ATTEMPTED AND COMPLETED
‚îî‚îÄ> ‚ùå Not Section 511; liable for main offense

OFFENSE ATTEMPTED, DIFFERENT OFFENSE COMMITTED
‚îî‚îÄ> Liable for offense actually committed
```

## üìö TOPIC 1: CULPABLE HOMICIDE (SECTION 299 IPC / 100 BNS)

### Definition

**Culpable Homicide:** Causing death:
1. With intention of causing death, OR
2. With intention of causing such bodily injury as is likely to cause death, OR
3. With knowledge that likely to cause death

---

### Three Clauses Explained

```
CLAUSE 1: Intention to cause DEATH
‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A shoots B in heart intending to kill

CLAUSE 2: Intention to cause BODILY INJURY likely to cause death
‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A stabs B in vital organ knowing it may cause death

CLAUSE 3: Knowledge that act likely to cause DEATH
‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A pushes B from height knowing fall likely fatal
```

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ ACT or OMISSION by accused
2. ‚úÖ Act CAUSED DEATH
3. ‚úÖ Causation between act and death
4. ‚úÖ Intention or knowledge as specified
```

---

## üìö TOPIC 2: MURDER (SECTION 300 IPC / 101 BNS)

### Relationship

**All murders are culpable homicide, but all culpable homicides are not murder**

Murder is aggravated form of culpable homicide.

---

### When is Culpable Homicide MURDER?

**Four Situations:**

```
1. Intention to cause DEATH
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A shoots B in head to kill

2. Intention to cause such bodily injury as offender knows is LIKELY TO CAUSE DEATH
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A knows B has weak heart; frightens him severely

3. Intention to cause bodily injury SUFFICIENT in ordinary course of nature to cause death
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A stabs B in heart (such injury ordinarily causes death)

4. Knowledge that act so IMMINENTLY DANGEROUS that in all probability will cause death
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Example: A fires into crowd knowing likely to kill someone
```

---

### üìä Culpable Homicide vs. Murder

**Key Difference:** Degree of intention/knowledge

| **Culpable Homicide**            | **Murder**                      |
|----------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Intention to cause injury likely to cause death | Intention to cause injury sufficient to cause death |
| Knowledge act likely to cause death | Knowledge act in all probability will cause death |
| PROBABILITY | CERTAINTY/HIGH PROBABILITY |
| Lesser degree | Greater degree |

üß† **Remember:** Difference is only in DEGREE

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab (SC)**
- **Four-step test:**
  1. Was there intention to cause bodily injury?
  2. What bodily injury was intended?
  3. Was injury sufficient in ordinary course to cause death?
  4. Was injury intended of the type specified?

üìå **Kesar Singh v. State of Haryana (SC)**
- **Principle:** Distinction between intention and knowledge is difference of degrees

üìå **Rampal Singh v. State of U.P. (SC)**
- **Principle:** Mental attitude distinguishes CH from murder

---

### Illustrations

**Example 1:**
A strikes B once on head with stick ‚Üí B dies
- **Analysis:** Single blow, not on vital spot ‚Üí Likely CH not murder

**Example 2:**
A stabs B multiple times in chest with knife ‚Üí B dies
- **Analysis:** Multiple stabs in vital organ ‚Üí Murder

**Example 3:**
A pushes B in sudden fight ‚Üí B hits head on wall, dies
- **Analysis:** Single push, no intention of severe injury ‚Üí CH not murder

---

## üìö TOPIC 3: EXCEPTIONS TO MURDER (SECTION 300 IPC)

### Five Exceptions (Murder ‚Üí Culpable Homicide)

```
EXCEPTION 1: GRAVE & SUDDEN PROVOCATION
‚îî‚îÄ> Loss of self-control due to sudden provocation

EXCEPTION 2: EXCEEDING PRIVATE DEFENSE in good faith
‚îî‚îÄ> Went beyond right in genuine mistake

EXCEPTION 3: EXCEEDING POWER OF PUBLIC SERVANT
‚îî‚îÄ> Official exceeded authority in good faith

EXCEPTION 4: SUDDEN FIGHT
‚îî‚îÄ> In heat of passion, without premeditation

EXCEPTION 5: CONSENT OF PERSON KILLED
‚îî‚îÄ> Person 18+ consented to risk of death
```

üß† **Memory:** "P-P-P-F-C" (Provocation-Private defense-Public servant-Fight-Consent)

---

### Exception 1: Grave & Sudden Provocation

**Requirements:**
1. ‚úÖ Provocation must be GRAVE
2. ‚úÖ Provocation must be SUDDEN
3. ‚úÖ Loss of self-control
4. ‚úÖ Act done before cooling time
5. ‚úÖ Provocation not self-invited

**Not Provocation:**
- Lawful exercise of right
- Legal action
- Self-invited provocation

üìå **Cases:**
- **K.M. Nanavati v. State of Maharashtra:** Words alone not enough; time lapse negates suddenness
- **Mancini v. DPP:** Reasonable man test

---

### Exception 2: Exceeding Private Defense

**Requirements:**
1. ‚úÖ Exercising right of private defense
2. ‚úÖ In GOOD FAITH
3. ‚úÖ Exceeded right without intending more harm than necessary

---

### Exception 4: Sudden Fight

**Requirements:**
1. ‚úÖ Fight must be SUDDEN
2. ‚úÖ In heat of PASSION
3. ‚úÖ Without PREMEDITATION
4. ‚úÖ Without undue advantage
5. ‚úÖ Without cruel/unusual manner

**Difference from Provocation:**
- Fight = mutual combat
- Provocation = one-sided

---

### Exception 5: Consent

**Requirements:**
1. ‚úÖ Person 18 years or above
2. ‚úÖ Voluntarily consented
3. ‚úÖ To death or risk of death

**Not Applicable:**
- Person below 18
- Consent obtained by fraud/undue influence
- Consent for suicide (abetment of suicide still punishable)

---

## üìö TOPIC 4: DEATH BY NEGLIGENCE (SECTION 304A IPC / 106 BNS)

### Definition

**Section 304A:** Causing death by rash or negligent act not amounting to culpable homicide

---

### Essential Ingredients

```
1. ‚úÖ Death of person
2. ‚úÖ Caused by RASH or NEGLIGENT act
3. ‚úÖ Act NOT amounting to culpable homicide
   ‚îî‚îÄ> No intention to cause death
   ‚îî‚îÄ> No knowledge death would result
```

---

### Rash Act vs. Negligent Act

```
RASHNESS
‚îú‚îÄ> Doing something with consciousness of risk
‚îú‚îÄ> Hoping evil won't happen
‚îú‚îÄ> Example: Driving at 100 km/h in city
‚îî‚îÄ> HIGHER degree

NEGLIGENCE
‚îú‚îÄ> Failing to exercise reasonable care
‚îú‚îÄ> No foresight of consequences
‚îú‚îÄ> Example: Not checking brakes before driving
‚îî‚îÄ> LOWER degree
```

---

### Criminal Negligence

**Not mere carelessness, but:**
- Gross negligence
- Reckless disregard for life
- Culpable neglect
- Beyond civil negligence

---

### Leading Cases

üìå **Shivder Singh v. State**
- Passenger on footboard; driver negotiated sharp turn without slowing
- **Held:** GUILTY under 304A - Rash and negligent

üìå **Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (SC) - Medical Negligence**

**Guidelines for Doctors:**
1. Simple lack of care/error of judgment not negligence
2. Practice acceptable to medical profession = no negligence
3. Equipment not available at time = no negligence
4. Standard = ordinary competent person, not highest expertise
5. Criminal negligence must be gross/very high degree
6. Private complaint needs prima facie evidence from another doctor
7. Doctor not to be arrested routinely

---

### Contributory Negligence

**NO defense in criminal law**
- Victim's negligence doesn't excuse accused
- Even if victim partly contributed to death

üìå **R v. Swindall:** Victim drunk/negligent ‚Üí Still guilty

---

## üìö TOPIC 5: HURT & GRIEVOUS HURT (SECTIONS 319-320 IPC / 114-125 BNS)

### Hurt (Section 319)

**Definition:** Causing bodily pain, disease, or infirmity

**Essential Ingredients:**
1. ‚úÖ Bodily pain, disease, or infirmity caused
2. ‚úÖ Caused voluntarily

**Examples:**
- Punching in face
- Pulling hair
- Pricking with needle
- Causing hysteria/terror

üß† **Key:** Physical pain (not mental pain); duration immaterial

---

### Grievous Hurt (Section 320)

**Eight Types:**

```
1. EMASCULATION
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Depriving masculine vigor

2. PERMANENT PRIVATION of sight (either eye)

3. PERMANENT PRIVATION of hearing (either ear)

4. PRIVATION of any member or joint

5. DESTRUCTION/IMPAIRING powers of member/joint

6. PERMANENT DISFIGURATION of head/face

7. FRACTURE/DISLOCATION of bone/tooth

8. Any hurt causing:
   ‚îú‚îÄ> Endangers life, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Severe bodily pain 20+ days, OR
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Unable to follow ordinary pursuits 20+ days
```

üß† **Memory:** "E-S-H-M-D-D-F-20"

---

### Hurt vs. Grievous Hurt

| **Hurt**              | **Grievous Hurt**      |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| Simple bodily pain    | Specified 8 types      |
| Temporary             | Permanent (mostly)     |
| Example: Bruise       | Example: Fracture      |
| Section 323 (1 year)  | Section 325 (7 years)  |

---

### Punishment

**Voluntarily Causing Hurt (323):** Imprisonment 1 year or fine ‚Çπ1000 or both

**Voluntarily Causing Grievous Hurt (325):** Imprisonment 7 years + fine

**Aggravated Forms:** Higher punishment if:
- On provocation
- By dangerous weapons
- By poison/corrosive substance
- On public servant
