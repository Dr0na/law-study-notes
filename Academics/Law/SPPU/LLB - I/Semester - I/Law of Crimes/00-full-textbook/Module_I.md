## üìö CRIME AND ITS MEANING

### üîç What is Crime?

**Crime** may be defined as an act or omission which the society has thought fit to punish or otherwise deal with under its laws for the time being in force.

### Scholarly Definitions

**üìå Blackstone:**
> "Crime is an act committed or omitted in violation of public law forbidding or commanding it."

**üìå Glanville Williams:**
> "A crime is a legal wrong that can be followed by criminal proceedings which may result in punishment."

**üìå Professor Kenny:**
> "Crime is a harmful human conduct that the sovereign desires to prevent."

**üìå Salmond:**
> "An act deemed by law to be harmful to society in general even though its immediate victim is an individual."

**üìå John Austin:**
> "A wrong which is pursued by the sovereign or his subordinates is a crime."

> üí° **Key Point:** Crime differs from civil wrongs (torts) as it is an offense against the state and society, not just against an individual.

## ‚öñÔ∏è ESSENTIAL ELEMENTS OF CRIME

### The Cardinal Principle

**Latin Maxim:** 
```
"ACTUS NON FACIT REUM, NISI MENS SIT REA"
```

**Translation:** *An act does not make a person guilty of a crime unless the mind is also guilty.*

### Two Essential Elements

```
CRIME = ACTUS REUS + MENS REA
```

1. **Actus Reus** - The Guilty Act (Physical Element)
2. **Mens Rea** - The Guilty Mind (Mental Element)

> üß† **Mnemonic: "ARM"**
> - **A**ctus **R**eus + **M**ens Rea = Crime

### Analysis of Causation

#### Types of Causation

1. **Direct Causation**
   - A directly causes B's death by stabbing

2. **Indirect Causation**
   - A poisons B's drink; C serves it; B dies

3. **Intervening Act**
   - A injures B; doctor's negligence causes death

4. **Victim's Own Conduct**
   - A injures B; B refuses treatment; B dies

5. **Contributory Negligence**
   - Both A and B's actions lead to result

### Legal Principles

**Causa Causans vs. Causa Sine Qua Non**

| **Causa Causans**               | **Causa Sine Qua Non**           |
|---------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Immediate/operating cause       | Necessary/inevitable cause       |
| This is what matters in law     | Not sufficient for liability     |
| Example: Direct stab causing death | Example: Birth (without birth, no crime possible) |

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Legal Rule:** For criminal liability, the act must be the **causa causans** (proximate cause)

---

### Leading Cases on Actus Reus

#### üìå R v. Hilton

**Facts:** Accused left steam engine unattended; unauthorized person started it causing death

**Held:** Death was consequence of unauthorized person's act, not accused

**Principle:** Intervening act breaks chain of causation

---

#### üìå R v. Martin

**Facts:** Accused gave gin to 4-year-old; child snatched glass and drank, died

**Held:** Acquitted - death due to child's own act

**Principle:** Victim's voluntary act breaks causation

---

#### üìå R v. Swindall and Osborne

**Facts:** Accused driving dangerously; killed drunk pedestrian

**Held:** Guilty - victim's contributory negligence is no defense in criminal law

**Principle:** Contributory negligence not a defense in crime

## 2Ô∏è‚É£ MENS REA (The Guilty Mind)

### Definition

**Mens Rea** means the evil intent or guilty state of mind - the psychological element of crime.

### Tests for Mens Rea

1. Was the act voluntary? (Section 39 IPC - "Voluntarily")
2. Did the accused foresee the consequences?

### 1. Intention

**Definition:** Fixed purpose to reach a desired objective; foresees AND wills the consequence

**Example:** A chops off B's head ‚Üí Clearly intends death

**Characteristics:**
- Highest degree of mens rea
- Both foreseeability AND willingness required
- Direct purpose to achieve result

---

### 2. Knowledge

**Definition:** Awareness of consequences; knows outcome likely but may not desire it

**Example:** Doctor uses infected syringe knowingly on patient

**Distinction from Intention:**
- Knowledge: Aware but may not desire
- Intention: Aware AND desires

**üìå Basdev v. State of Pepsu (AIR 1956 SC)**
> "Knowledge is awareness of consequences. The demarcating line between knowledge and intention is thin but perceivable - they connote different things."

---

### 3. Recklessness

**Definition:** Foresees possible/probable consequences but knowingly takes unjustifiable risk

**Example:** Driving at 120 km/h in crowded area

**Characteristics:**
- Person foresees risk
- Hopes harm won't happen
- Takes risk anyway
- Doesn't desire harmful outcome

---

### 4. Negligence

**Definition:** Failure to exercise reasonable care; no foresight of consequences

**Example:** Careless driver hits pedestrian without seeing

**Characteristics:**
- Lowest degree of mens rea
- No foreseeability
- Breach of duty of care
- Essentially a tort law principle incorporated in criminal law

> üí° **Key Point:** Under IPC, negligence specifically incorporated for death by negligence (Section 304A)

## üé≠ MOTIVE vs. MENS REA vs. INTENTION

### Definitions

```

‚îÇ   MOTIVE vs. INTENTION vs. MENS REA      ‚îÇ
‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îò

MOTIVE
‚îú‚îÄ> WHY the act was done
‚îú‚îÄ> The emotion/desire behind act
‚îú‚îÄ> Example: Jealousy, revenge, greed
‚îî‚îÄ> NOT an essential element of crime

INTENTION
‚îú‚îÄ> WHAT the accused wanted to achieve
‚îú‚îÄ> The aim or object of the act
‚îú‚îÄ> Example: To kill, to steal
‚îî‚îÄ> Essential element of many crimes

MENS REA
‚îú‚îÄ> The GUILTY STATE OF MIND
‚îú‚îÄ> Includes intention, knowledge, recklessness
‚îî‚îÄ> Essential for criminal liability
```

### Example Illustration

**Scenario:** A steals food to feed poor people

- **Motive:** Good (helping the poor)
- **Intention:** To commit theft (bad)
- **Mens Rea:** Dishonest (wrongful gain)
- **Result:** Guilty of theft despite good motive

### Legal Relevance of Motive

üìå **Motive is NOT an ingredient of crime but may be relevant to:**
- Determine guilt where evidence is doubtful
- Assess severity of punishment
- Understand the nature of the offense

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Remember:** Good motive ‚â† No crime

> üß† **Mnemonic: "I-PAC"**
> - **I**ntention (Not punishable)
> - **P**reparation (Not punishable)
> - **A**ttempt (Punishable)
> - **C**ompletion (Fully punishable)

---

### Stage 1: Intention

**Latin Maxim:** *"Cogitationis poenam nemo patitur"*
(No one is punished for mere thoughts)

**Why Not Punishable:**
- Difficult to prove
- No harm yet caused
- Right to freedom of thought

---

### Stage 2: Preparation

**Definition:** Arranging necessary measures for commission of crime

**Why Generally Not Punishable:**
- Difficult to distinguish from lawful acts
- Prosecution fails to prove specific crime intended
- Person hasn't committed to crime yet

**Exceptions (Preparation IS Punishable):**
1. Preparation for dacoity
2. Preparation for waging war against state
3. Certain other serious offenses

**Example:**
- Buying knife for cooking: Lawful
- Buying knife + following victim + noting routine: Preparation for murder

---

### Stage 3: Attempt

**Definition:** Direct movement towards commission after preparation

**Essential Elements:**
1. Guilty intention to commit offense
2. Some act done towards commission
3. Act falls short of completed offense

**Punishable under:** Section 511 IPC / Section 62 BNS

---

### Stage 4: Accomplishment/Completion

**Definition:** Successful commission of intended crime

**Legal Effect:**
- Full punishment applies
- All elements of offense satisfied
- Actus reus + Mens rea both present

---

### üìä Distinguishing Preparation from Attempt

| **Aspect**          | **Preparation**                    | **Attempt**                           |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Nature**          | Arranging means                       | Using means                            |
| **Proximity**       | Remote from crime                     | Proximate to crime                     |
| **Example**         | Buying gun                            | Pulling trigger                        |
| **Punishability**   | Generally not punishable              | Punishable under Section 511           |
| **Test**            | Equivocal act (ambiguous)             | Unequivocal act (clearly criminal)     |

## üë• PARTIES TO CRIME

### Classical Classification (Common Law)

```
1. PRINCIPALS IN THE FIRST DEGREE
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Actually committed the crime
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Present at crime scene
   
2. PRINCIPALS IN THE SECOND DEGREE  
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Present at crime scene
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Assisted in commission
   
3. ACCESSORIES BEFORE THE FACT
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Not present at crime scene
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Assisted in preparation
   
4. ACCESSORIES AFTER THE FACT
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Helped offender escape after crime
   ‚îî‚îÄ> Not present during commission
```

---

### Modern Classification (IPC/BNS)

**1. Principal Offender**
- Person who commits the actual offense
- Fully liable for the crime

**2. Abettor**
- Person who instigates, conspires, or aids
- Liable under abetment provisions (Sections 107-116 IPC / 45-60 BNS)
- Three ways: Instigation, Conspiracy, Aid

**3. Accomplice**
- Person who knowingly aids in commission
- May turn witness for prosecution
- Accomplice evidence requires corroboration

> üß† **Remember:** All accomplices abet, but not all abettors are accomplices

## üèõÔ∏è HISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF CRIMINAL LAW IN INDIA

### Pre-Colonial Era

**Ancient Period (Vedic Era)**
- Criminal law based on Dharmashastra
- Sources: Manusmriti, Arthashastra
- King was supreme authority
- Punishment: Retributive and deterrent

**Medieval Period (Mughal Era)**
- Islamic criminal law (Sharia)
- Qazi courts
- Hodood offenses (fixed punishments)
- Diyat (blood money) system

---

### Colonial Era

**1773 - Regulating Act**
- Supreme Court established in Calcutta
- English criminal law for Europeans
- Indians governed by personal laws

**1860 - Indian Penal Code**
- Drafted by Lord Macaulay's Commission (1834-1837)
- Based on Code Napoleon
- Came into force: January 1, 1862
- One of the best criminal codes worldwide

**Features of IPC 1860:**
- Comprehensive and exhaustive
- Simple language
- Secular in nature
- Based on English Common Law principles

---

### Post-Independence Era

**1950 onwards**
- Constitution of India came into force
- Fundamental Rights added protections
- Numerous amendments to IPC
- Special laws enacted (POCSO, SC/ST Act, etc.)

---

### üÜï 2023 - Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)

**Why New Law?**
- Remove colonial legacy
- Use Indian terminology
- Update for modern crimes
- Simplify language
- Include technology-related offenses

**Key Changes:**
- IPC 1860 ‚Üí BNS 2023
- Sections renumbered
- Some sections added, modified, deleted
- Greater focus on women's safety
- Enhanced punishments for certain crimes
- Recognition of new age crimes

## üìñ DEFINITIONS (SECTION 2 BNS)

### Important Definitions

#### 1. Person
Includes:
- Natural persons (human beings)
- Legal persons (companies, associations)
- For certain offenses: idols, deities

#### 2. Public Servant
Wide definition including:
- Government employees
- Judges, magistrates
- Police officers
- Military personnel
- Panchayat members
- Bank employees (for certain purposes)

#### 3. Voluntarily (Section 39 IPC)
A person causes an effect "voluntarily" when:
- He intends to cause it, OR
- He knows or has reason to believe he is likely to cause it

#### 4. Dishonestly (Section 24 IPC)
Intention of:
- Wrongful gain to oneself or another, OR
- Wrongful loss to another

#### 5. Fraudulently
Doing something with intent to defraud

#### 6. Good Faith
An act done with:
- Due care and attention
- Without corrupt motive
- Belief in legality of act

#### 7. Injury
Any harm whatever illegally caused to:
- Body, mind, reputation, or property

#### 8. Wrongful Gain/Wrongful Loss
- **Wrongful Gain:** Gain by unlawful means of property to which person not legally entitled
- **Wrongful Loss:** Loss by unlawful means of property to which person losing is legally entitled

| Good Faith      | With due care, no corrupt motive         | Police arrests on reasonable belief|
| Reason to Believe | Based on reasonable grounds            | Knowing stolen goods are stolen    |

## üéØ PRINCIPLES OF CRIMINAL LIABILITY

### Fundamental Principles

#### 1. No Crime Without Law (Nullum Crimen Sine Lege)
- Act not a crime unless declared so by law
- Ex post facto laws generally not allowed

#### 2. No Punishment Without Law (Nulla Poena Sine Lege)
- No punishment except for violation of law
- Punishment must be prescribed by law

#### 3. Presumption of Innocence
- Every person presumed innocent until proven guilty
- Burden of proof on prosecution

#### 4. Proof Beyond Reasonable Doubt
- Guilt must be proved beyond reasonable doubt
- Not merely preponderance of probabilities (civil standard)

#### 5. Benefit of Doubt to Accused
- If two views possible, one favorable to accused must be accepted

#### 6. Fair Trial
- Right to be heard
- Right to defend
- Right to legal representation

---

### Strict Liability Offenses

**Exception to Mens Rea Requirement**

Some offenses don't require mens rea:
- Public welfare offenses
- Regulatory offenses
- Environmental violations
- Food adulteration
- Traffic violations

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Note:** BNS primarily deals with offenses requiring mens rea. Strict liability offenses usually in special laws.

> ‚úÖ **KEY TAKEAWAYS**
>
> 1. **Crime = Actus Reus + Mens Rea** (except strict liability)

> 8. **Fundamental principles:** Presumption of innocence, burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt

## üß† MNEMONICS FOR MODULE I

**Elements of Crime: "ARM"**
- **A** - Actus Reus (Guilty Act)
- **R** - Reus (Guilty)
- **M** - Mens Rea (Guilty Mind)

- **I** - Intention (Not punishable)
- **P** - Preparation (Not punishable)
- **A** - Attempt (Punishable)
- **C** - Commission (Fully punishable)

- **I** - Intention (Highest)
- **K** - Knowledge
- **R** - Recklessness
- **N** - Negligence (Lowest)
