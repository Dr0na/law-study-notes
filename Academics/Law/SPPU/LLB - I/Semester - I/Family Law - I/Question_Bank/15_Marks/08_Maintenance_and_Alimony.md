#### **INTRODUCTION**

Maintenance and alimony are essential financial support mechanisms ensuring that economically vulnerable spouses (typically wives) are not left destitute during or after matrimonial breakdown. The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provides two types of maintenance: Section 24 (maintenance pendente lite during proceedings) and Section 25 (permanent alimony after decree). Additionally, Section 125 of Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 provides a parallel criminal remedy for maintenance. These provisions recognize economic vulnerabilities, prevent destitution, and ensure access to justice and post-divorce financial security.

---

### **PART I: MAINTENANCE PENDENTE LITE [Section 24]**

#### **A. STATUTORY PROVISION**

**Section 24 Text:**
"Where in any proceeding under this Act it appears to the court that either the wife or the husband, as the case may be, has no independent income sufficient for her or his support and the necessary expenses of the proceeding, it may, on the application of the wife or the husband, order the respondent to pay to the petitioner the expenses of the proceeding, and monthly during the proceeding such sum as, having regard to the petitioner's own income and the income of the respondent, it may seem to the court to be reasonable.

**Provided** that the application... shall, as far as possible, be disposed of within sixty days from the date of service of notice..."

---

#### **B. CONCEPT AND PURPOSE**

**Meaning:**
"Pendente lite" (Latin) = "During litigation/pending suit"

**Purpose:**
1. Enable financially weaker spouse to contest/defend proceedings
2. Ensure access to justice irrespective of economic status
3. Prevent economic hardship during litigation
4. Level playing field between economically unequal parties
5. Cover litigation expenses (court fees, lawyer fees)
6. Provide monthly sustenance during proceedings

---

#### **C. WHO CAN CLAIM**

**Gender Neutral:**
Either **wife** OR **husband** (whoever lacks sufficient income)

**Practical Reality:**
Predominantly claimed by wives due to economic dependence

**Conditions:**
- Proceeding under HMA pending
- Applicant has no sufficient independent income for:
  - Support/maintenance, AND
  - Litigation expenses
- Respondent has means to pay

---

#### **D. WHAT CAN BE CLAIMED**

**Two Components:**

**1. Expenses of Proceedings:**
- Court fees
- Advocate's fees
- Other litigation costs

**2. Monthly Sum:**
- Regular monthly maintenance
- For livelihood
- Throughout pendency

---

#### **E. FACTORS CONSIDERED**

**Mnemonic: "RICO"**

1. **Respondent's** income and property
2. **Income** and property of petitioner
3. **Conduct** of parties
4. **Other** circumstances (needs, obligations, children)

---

#### **F. QUANTUM, DURATION, AND TIME**

**Quantum:**
- Court's discretion
- Guideline: **25-33% of respondent's net income**
- Not rigid rule; depends on facts

**Duration:**
- From date of order
- Until final decree
- Or death/remarriage of recipient

**Time for Disposal:**
Application disposed within **60 days** from service of notice (as far as possible).

**Case Law:**
**Chaturbhuj v. Sita Bai (AIR 1970 SC 259):**
Section 24 is remedial provision; should be liberally construed to enable weaker party.

---

### **PART II: PERMANENT ALIMONY [Section 25]**

#### **A. STATUTORY PROVISION**

**Section 25(1) Key Points:**
Court may order respondent to pay applicant:
- Gross sum (lump sum), OR
- Monthly/periodical sum
- For term not exceeding life of applicant
- Having regard to: Respondent's income/property, applicant's income/property, conduct, other circumstances
- May be secured by charge on immovable property

---

#### **B. CONCEPT**

**Definition:**
Financial support granted at time of or after decree in matrimonial proceedings.

**"Permanent":**
Misnomer - not truly permanent as can be modified/terminated.

**When Available:**
At time of or after decree of:
- Divorce
- Nullity of marriage
- Judicial separation
- Restitution of conjugal rights

---

#### **C. FORMS OF ALIMONY**

**Three Forms: "LMS"**

**1. Lump Sum:**
- Single one-time payment
- Final settlement
- Immediate payment or installments

**2. Monthly/Periodical:**
- Regular payments
- Ongoing obligation
- Can be monthly, quarterly, etc.

**3. Secured Payment:**
- Payment secured by charge on immovable property
- Ensures compliance
- Protection against default

**Court's Choice:**
Court decides form based on:
- Parties' preference
- Financial situation
- Practicality

---

#### **D. FACTORS [Section 25(1)]**

**Same RICO Factors:**

**1. Respondent's Income and Property:**
- Salary, business income, investments
- Movable and immovable assets
- Earning capacity and future prospects

**2. Applicant's Income and Property:**
- Own income sources and assets
- Earning capacity
- Employment status and qualifications

**3. Conduct of Parties:**
- Matrimonial conduct
- Fault in breakdown
- Treatment during marriage

**Note:** Even "guilty" party may get maintenance based on need.

**4. Other Circumstances:**
- Duration of marriage
- Age of parties
- Standard of living during marriage
- Health and medical needs
- Children (custody, needs)
- Contributions to marriage (homemaking, career sacrifice)
- Educational qualifications and skills
- Future prospects and retirement

---

#### **E. QUANTUM**

**No Fixed Formula:**
Court exercises discretion.

**General Practice:**
Approximately **33% of respondent's net income** (guideline).

**Case Laws:**

**Jasbir Kaur Sehgal v. District Judge (AIR 1997 SC 1796):**
- No fixed formula exists
- Depends on facts and circumstances
- Should be fair and reasonable

**Shamima Farooqui v. Shahid Khan (2015) 5 SCC 705:**
- Maintenance should enable dignified living
- Not penury level, not luxurious
- Reasonable standard considering all circumstances

**Rajnesh v. Neha (2021) 2 SCC 324:**
- Detailed guidelines on alimony determination
- Comprehensive factors enumerated

---

#### **F. MODIFICATION [Section 25(2)]**

**Change in Circumstances:**
Court can **vary, modify, or rescind** on material change.

**Examples:**
- Increase/decrease in income
- Recipient obtains employment
- Health deterioration
- Change in living expenses

**Who Can Apply:** Either party

---

#### **G. TERMINATION [Section 25(3)]**

**Three Grounds: "RAU"**

**1. Remarriage:**
If recipient remarries, alimony generally terminated.

**2. Adultery/Unchastity:**
- Wife's unchastity (sexual relations outside marriage)
- **Exception [Proviso]:** If wife was:
  - Living separately from husband
  - Under circumstances beyond control
  - Without fault on her part

**3. Husband's Adultery:**
If husband (receiving alimony - rare) has sexual relations outside wedlock.

---

### **PART III: SECTION 125 CrPC**

#### **A. NATURE AND SCOPE**

**Nature:**
- **Criminal remedy**
- Under Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973
- Independent of HMA proceedings

**Who Can Claim:**
1. Wife (including divorced wife)
2. Children (legitimate/illegitimate minor; disabled major)
3. Parents (unable to maintain)

**Focus:** Wife's claim

---

#### **B. CONDITIONS FOR WIFE'S CLAIM**

**Four Conditions:**

1. **Legally Wedded Wife:**
   - Valid marriage exists OR was dissolved
   - Hindu, Muslim, Christian, etc. (applies to all religions)

2. **Unable to Maintain Herself:**
   - No sufficient means
   - No independent adequate income

3. **Husband Has Sufficient Means:**
   - Husband has income/capacity to pay
   - Can meet maintenance obligation

4. **Refuses/Neglects to Maintain:**
   - Husband refusing to provide maintenance
   - Or neglecting to do so

---

#### **C. BARS TO WIFE'S CLAIM**

**Two Bars:**

**1. Living in Adultery:**
- Wife living in adulterous relationship
- Unless condoned by husband

**2. Refuses to Live Without Sufficient Reason:**
- Wife refuses to live with husband
- Without valid justification

**Sufficient Reasons Include:**
- Cruelty by husband
- Husband has another wife
- Ill-treatment or abuse
- Any valid matrimonial ground

---

#### **D. QUANTUM**

**Old Position:**
Maximum ‚Çπ500 per month (highly inadequate).

**After 2001 Amendment:**
**No upper limit**. Magistrate can award any reasonable amount based on:
- Husband's income
- Wife's needs
- Standard of living
- Circumstances of case

**Significance:**
Removal of limit made Section 125 more effective and relevant.

---

#### **E. ADVANTAGES OF SECTION 125**

**1. Speed:**
- Magistrate Court (not District Court)
- Summary procedure
- Disposal in months (not years)

**2. No Divorce Required:**
- Can claim during subsistence of marriage
- No need for divorce proceedings
- Immediate remedy

**3. Accessibility:**
- Simple procedure
- Lower court fees
- More accessible to poor/rural women
- Less intimidating forum

**4. Criminal Enforcement:**
- **Arrest possible** for non-compliance
- Criminal contempt proceedings
- Stronger enforcement mechanism
- Recovery as fine possible

**5. No Upper Limit:**
- Since 2001, unlimited quantum possible
- Based on actual needs and capacity

---

#### **F. DIVORCED WIFE [Section 125(4)]**

**Provision:**
Divorced wife can claim if:
- Has not remarried
- Has not received adequate provision from ex-husband at time of divorce

**Time:** Within reasonable time

**Case Law:**
**Shah Bano Begum v. Mohd. Ahmed Khan (AIR 1985 SC 945):**
- Landmark case (though Muslim law)
- Established divorced wife entitled to Section 125 maintenance
- If not adequately provided for

### **PART IV: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS**

#### **DETAILED COMPARISON TABLE:**

| **Aspect** | **Sec 24 HMA** | **Sec 25 HMA** | **Sec 125 CrPC** |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|------------------|
| **Type** | Interim | Permanent | Summary criminal |
| **When** | During proceedings | After decree | Anytime |
| **Court** | District Court | District Court | Magistrate Court |
| **Procedure** | Civil | Civil | Criminal |
| **Who Can** | Either spouse | Either spouse | Wife, children, parents |
| **Divorce Required** | Proceeding pending | Yes (or other decree) | No |
| **Speed** | 60-day target | Slow | Fast (summary) |
| **Quantum** | 25-33% (guideline) | 33% (guideline) | No limit (since 2001) |
| **Covers** | Litigation + Monthly | Alimony only | Basic maintenance |
| **Duration** | Till final decree | Life of applicant (max) | Till change |
| **Enforcement** | Civil execution | Civil execution | Criminal (arrest) |
| **Modification** | Based on circumstances | Yes [25(2)] | Yes |
| **Termination** | On final decree | Remarriage [25(3)] | Remarriage/circumstances |

---

### **INTERACTION BETWEEN PROVISIONS**

**Can Both Be Claimed?**
Yes, but:
- Double maintenance not awarded
- Section 125 amount may be adjusted against HMA maintenance
- Strategic use common

**Strategy:**
Lawyers often advise:
1. File Section 125 immediately (fast interim relief)
2. File HMA divorce petition
3. Section 125 provides support during lengthy HMA proceedings
4. Section 24 maintenance in HMA proceedings
5. After divorce, Section 25 permanent alimony

**Magistrate's Discretion:**
If HMA proceeding pending, Magistrate may:
- Stay Section 125 proceedings, OR
- Dispose Section 125 but adjust against HMA amount

---

### **JUDICIAL PRINCIPLES**

**Maintenance Philosophy:**

**1. Prevent Destitution:**
Not charity but legal right ensuring basic sustenance.

**2. Recognize Contributions:**
Acknowledge homemaking, child-rearing, career sacrifice.

**3. Ensure Dignity:**
Maintain reasonable standard of living.

**4. Not Punishment:**
Alimony not to punish payer; balance required.

**5. Changed Circumstances:**
Flexible - can modify based on changes.

---

#### **CONCLUSION**

Maintenance provisions under Hindu Marriage Act (Sections 24 and 25) and Section 125 CrPC provide comprehensive financial protection framework for economically vulnerable spouses. Section 24 ensures interim support enabling litigation access; Section 25 provides post-decree financial security; and Section 125 offers speedy criminal remedy without requiring divorce proceedings.

The three provisions complement each other: Section 24 for interim relief during HMA proceedings, Section 25 for permanent post-decree alimony, and Section 125 for quick relief without HMA proceedings. The RICO factors ensure court considers all relevant circumstances in determining fair quantum.

Despite being gender-neutral provisions, they predominantly protect wives, reflecting continuing socio-economic reality of women's economic dependence. The removal of upper limit in Section 125 (2001 Amendment) and judicial emphasis on dignified living standards (Shamima Farooqui) demonstrate evolution toward more realistic and adequate maintenance levels.

Strategic use of all three provisions, with Section 125 providing immediate relief while HMA proceedings continue, offers effective protection mechanism. As economic roles evolve and more women become financially independent, maintenance law continues to adapt, though the core principle remains: preventing destitution and ensuring financial security during and after matrimonial breakdown.
