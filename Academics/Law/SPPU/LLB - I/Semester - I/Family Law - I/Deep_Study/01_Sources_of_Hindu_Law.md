# üìò SOURCES OF HINDU LAW (Deep Study)

## üîç Introduction

Hindu Law is one of the oldest and most complex legal systems in the world. Understanding its sources is fundamental to comprehending how Hindu Law has evolved and continues to govern millions of Indians today.

---

## üìö CLASSIFICATION OF SOURCES

Hindu Law sources are traditionally classified into two categories:

### 1. **Ancient (Traditional) Sources**
### 2. **Modern Sources**

---

## üèõÔ∏è ANCIENT (TRADITIONAL) SOURCES

Ancient sources form the foundation of Hindu Law and are derived from religious and philosophical texts.

### A. SHRUTI (‡§∂‡•ç‡§∞‡•Å‡§§‡§ø) - "That which is heard"

**Definition:**
Shruti literally means "that which is heard" and refers to the most sacred and authoritative texts in Hindu tradition.

**Components:**
- **Four Vedas:**
  1. Rig Veda
  2. Yajur Veda
  3. Sama Veda
  4. Atharva Veda

**Legal Significance:**
- Considered divine revelation
- Highest authority in Hindu Law
- Contains principles of dharma (righteousness)
- Limited direct legal content but establishes foundational principles
- Not directly applied in modern courts but influences interpretation

**Illustration:**
The concept of *Rita* (cosmic order) from the Vedas forms the basis of dharma and legal obligations in Hindu society.

---

### B. SMRITI (‡§∏‡•ç‡§Æ‡•É‡§§‡§ø) - "That which is remembered"

**Definition:**
Smriti means "that which is remembered" and represents human interpretation and codification of Vedic principles.

**Major Smritis:**
1. **Manu Smriti (Manusmriti)** - Most authoritative
2. **Yajnavalkya Smriti**
3. **Narada Smriti**
4. **Parasara Smriti**
5. **Vishnu Smriti**
6. **Gautama Smriti**
7. **Apastamba Smriti**
8. **Vasistha Smriti**

**Legal Content:**
- Detailed rules on marriage, inheritance, adoption
- Criminal and civil procedures
- Rights and duties of individuals
- Social and religious obligations

**Authority:**
- Secondary to Shruti
- Widely used in practice
- Extensively commented upon by jurists

**Illustration:**
Manu Smriti lays down eight forms of marriage (Ashtavidha Vivaha), which influenced Hindu Marriage Act, 1955.

**Master Mnemonic Sentence (Primary)**
"My Young Nephew Plays Very Good Ancient Violin"

---

### C. DIGESTS AND COMMENTARIES (Nibandhas)

**Definition:**
Digests and commentaries are scholarly works that interpret and systematize the Smritis.

**Purpose:**
- Resolve conflicts between different Smritis
- Adapt ancient texts to contemporary conditions
- Provide authoritative interpretations

**Major Works:**

#### 1. **Mitakshara** (11th Century)
- **Author:** Vijnaneshwara
- **Commentary on:** Yajnavalkya Smriti
- **Significance:** Basis of Mitakshara School of Hindu Law
- **Geographical Application:** Most of India except Bengal and Assam

#### 2. **Dayabhaga** (12th Century)
- **Author:** Jimutavahana
- **Subject:** Primarily inheritance
- **Significance:** Basis of Dayabhaga School of Hindu Law
- **Geographical Application:** Bengal and Assam

#### 3. **Vyavahara Mayukha**
- **Author:** Nilakantha
- **Application:** Maharashtra and Gujarat

#### 4. **Vivada Ratnakara**
- **Author:** Chandeswara
- **Application:** Mithila region

**Legal Impact:**
These commentaries created different schools of Hindu Law, leading to variations in legal principles across India.

---

### D. CUSTOM (ACHARA)

**Definition:**
Custom refers to practices and usages that have been continuously and uniformly observed by a particular community or locality.

**Legal Recognition:**
Custom is a vital source of Hindu Law, often superseding written texts in certain matters.

**Types of Custom:**

#### 1. **Local Custom**
- Confined to a particular locality
- Example: Customs in Punjab, Kerala, or Maharashtra

#### 2. **Family Custom**
- Peculiar to a particular family
- Example: Special rules of inheritance in royal families

#### 3. **Class or Caste Custom**
- Applicable to a particular caste or community
- Example: Marriage customs among certain trading communities

**Essential Conditions for Valid Custom:**
1. **Antiquity:** Must be ancient and immemorial
2. **Certainty:** Must be clear and definite
3. **Reasonableness:** Must not be opposed to public policy
4. **Continuity:** Must be continuously observed
5. **Uniformity:** Must be uniformly followed
6. **Recognition:** Must be recognized by community

**Statutory Recognition:**
Section 3(a) of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 recognizes custom and usage as applicable law.

**Case Law:**
**Collector of Madura v. Moottoo Ramalinga** (1868) 12 MIA 397
- Privy Council held that custom must be proved as a fact
- Burden of proof on party claiming custom

**Illustration:**
In certain communities of South India, cross-cousin marriages are valid by custom, though generally not preferred under classical Hindu Law.

---

## üè¢ MODERN SOURCES

Modern sources are those that have developed during and after British rule in India.

### A. JUDICIAL DECISIONS (PRECEDENTS)

**Definition:**
Judgments and interpretations by courts, especially higher courts, form binding precedents.

**Hierarchy:**
1. **Supreme Court of India** - Binding on all courts
2. **High Courts** - Binding on subordinate courts within jurisdiction
3. **Subordinate Courts** - Persuasive value only

**Significance:**
- Filled gaps in traditional sources
- Adapted Hindu Law to modern conditions
- Resolved conflicts between different schools

**Landmark Cases:**

#### 1. **Gowli Buddanna v. Commissioner of Income Tax** (1966)
- Established that judicial decisions are important source of Hindu Law

#### 2. **Mulla's Principles of Hindu Law** (Judicial Recognition)
- Courts frequently rely on this authoritative text

**Doctrine of Precedent (Stare Decisis):**
Under Article 141 of Constitution of India, Supreme Court decisions are binding on all courts in India.

---

### B. LEGISLATION (STATUTES)

**Definition:**
Acts of Parliament and State Legislatures that codify, modify, or abolish Hindu Law principles.

**Major Enactments:**

#### 1. **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955**
- Codifies law relating to Hindu marriages
- Defines conditions, void and voidable marriages
- Provides for matrimonial remedies

#### 2. **Hindu Succession Act, 1956**
- Governs intestate succession among Hindus
- Amended in 2005 to give equal rights to daughters

#### 3. **Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956**
- Deals with guardianship of minors

#### 4. **Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956**
- Codifies law on adoption and maintenance

#### 5. **Hindu Women's Rights to Property Act, 1937**
- First major legislation giving property rights to Hindu women

#### 6. **Hindu Marriage Disabilities Removal Act, 1946**
- Removed certain marriage disabilities

**Supremacy of Legislation:**
Statutes override all other sources of Hindu Law. Where legislation exists, it prevails over traditional sources and customs (unless custom is specifically saved).

**Constitutional Provisions:**
- Article 44: Uniform Civil Code (Directive Principle)
- Article 372: Continuation of existing laws

---

### C. EQUITY, JUSTICE, AND GOOD CONSCIENCE

**Definition:**
Where no specific rule exists in traditional sources, statutes, or precedents, courts apply principles of equity, justice, and good conscience.

**Origin:**
British courts introduced this concept to fill gaps in Hindu Law.

**Application:**
- Used as last resort
- Must not conflict with established principles
- Often draws from natural justice and English law principles

**Statutory Recognition:**
Various statutes authorize courts to apply principles of justice, equity, and good conscience.

**Case Law:**
**Bai Vajia v. Thakorbhai Chelabhai** (1953)
- Court applied principles of justice and equity in property dispute

---

## üìä HIERARCHY OF SOURCES

In case of conflict, the following hierarchy applies:

1. **Legislation (Statutes)** - Highest authority
2. **Judicial Precedents** - Binding interpretations
3. **Custom** - If proved and valid
4. **Digests and Commentaries** - Authoritative interpretations
5. **Smritis** - Traditional texts
6. **Shruti** - Foundational principles
7. **Equity, Justice, and Good Conscience** - Last resort

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è APPLICABILITY OF SOURCES

**Section 3 of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955** provides that:
- Hindu Law consists of rules of Shruti, Smriti, Digests, and Commentaries
- Custom and usage form part of Hindu Law
- Rule of interpretation in case of conflict

**Modern Position:**
Where legislation exists, it prevails. In absence of statute:
- Judicial decisions guide
- Custom, if proved, applies
- Traditional texts provide interpretative guidance

---

## üéì KEY CONCEPTS TO REMEMBER

### 1. **Dharma**
The foundational principle underlying all sources - moral, religious, and legal duty.

### 2. **Smriti vs Shruti**
- Shruti = Divine revelation (Vedas)
- Smriti = Human memory/interpretation

### 3. **Schools Created by Commentaries**
Mitakshara and Dayabhaga schools arose from different interpretations of Smritis.

### 4. **Codification Movement**
Post-independence India codified major areas of Hindu Law through legislation.

### 5. **Living Nature**
Hindu Law continues to evolve through judicial interpretation and legislative amendments.

## üìã IMPORTANT CASE LAWS

### 1. **Collector of Madura v. Moottoo Ramalinga** (1868) 12 MIA 397
**Principle:** Custom must be proved as a fact; burden on party claiming custom.

### 2. **Gowli Buddanna v. Commissioner of Income Tax** AIR 1966 SC 1788
**Principle:** Judicial decisions are important source of Hindu Law.

### 3. **Bai Vajia v. Thakorbhai Chelabhai** AIR 1953 SC 247
**Principle:** Courts can apply principles of equity and justice.

### 4. **Balakrishna Udayar v. Vasudeva Aiyar** (1917)
**Principle:** When Smriti conflicts with Shruti, Shruti prevails.

### 5. **Commissioner of Wealth Tax v. R. Sridharan** (1976)
**Principle:** Statutory law supersedes personal law.
