# üìò HINDU MARRIAGE ACT, 1955 (Deep Study)

## üîç Introduction

The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (Act No. 25 of 1955) is a landmark legislation that codified and reformed Hindu marriage law in India. It came into force on **18th May 1955** and applies to Hindus throughout India.

---

## üéØ HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

### Pre-1955 Position:
- Hindu marriage governed by uncodified personal law
- Based on ancient texts (Smritis, Dharmashastras)
- Regional variations due to different schools
- No uniform law on marriage, divorce, or matrimonial reliefs
- Women had limited rights
- Divorce not permitted in classical Hindu Law

### Need for Reform:
1. Social reform movements demanding equality
2. Constitutional mandate (Articles 14, 15, 44)
3. Removing gender discrimination
4. Providing matrimonial remedies including divorce
5. Uniform applicability across India
6. Removing caste and other barriers

### Legislative Process:
- Hindu Code Committee (B.N. Rau Committee) - 1941
- Draft Hindu Code Bill introduced but opposition
- Split into four Acts:
  1. **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955**
  2. Hindu Succession Act, 1956
  3. Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956
  4. Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956

---

## üìú STRUCTURE OF THE ACT

The Act contains **30 Sections** divided into **6 Chapters**:

### Chapter I: Preliminary (Sections 1-3)
- Title, extent, commencement
- Application of Act
- Definitions

### Chapter II: Hindu Marriages (Sections 5-16)
- Conditions for Hindu marriage
- Registration of marriages
- Ceremonies and customs
- Void and voidable marriages

### Chapter III: Matrimonial Reliefs (Sections 9-13)
- Restitution of conjugal rights
- Judicial separation
- Divorce
- Nullity of marriage

### Chapter IV: Jurisdiction and Procedure (Sections 14-21A)
- Court jurisdiction
- Procedural provisions
- Appeals

### Chapter V: Miscellaneous (Sections 22-29)
- Maintenance pendente lite
- Permanent alimony
- Custody of children
- Enforcement provisions

### Chapter VI: Special Provisions (Section 29A-30)
- Savings and repeals

## üë• APPLICABILITY (SECTION 2)

### **Section 2: Application of Act**

The Act applies to:

#### A. **Persons Who Are Hindus:**
1. **By Religion:**
   - Any person who is Hindu by religion in any of its forms or developments
   - Includes Virashaiva, Lingayat, followers of Brahmo, Prarthana, or Arya Samaj

2. **By Birth:**
   - Any person who is Buddhist, Jain, or Sikh by religion
   - Any child (legitimate or illegitimate) of Hindu parents
   - Any child (legitimate or illegitimate) where one parent is Hindu and child is raised as Hindu

3. **By Conversion:**
   - Any person converted or reconverted to Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, or Sikhism

#### B. **Territorial Application:**
- Extends to whole of India **except** State of Jammu & Kashmir
- Applies to Hindus domiciled in India
- Applies even if marriage solemnized outside India

#### C. **Persons Specifically Included:**
- Buddhists
- Jains  
- Sikhs
- Virashaivas
- Lingayats
- Brahmo Samajists
- Prarthana Samajists
- Arya Samajists

#### D. **Persons NOT Covered:**
- Muslims (governed by Muslim Personal Law)
- Christians (governed by Christian Marriage laws)
- Parsis (governed by Parsi Marriage & Divorce Act)
- Jews (governed by general law)
- Inter-religious marriages (governed by Special Marriage Act, 1954)

**Note:** If parties are Hindu and Buddhist, Hindu Marriage Act applies. If Hindu and Muslim, Special Marriage Act applies.

## üìö KEY DEFINITIONS (SECTION 3)

### Section 3: Definitions

#### 1. **"Custom" and "Usage"**
[Section 3(a)]
- Custom or usage governing parties or their families
- Having continuous and uniform observance
- Legally enforceable
- If Act makes special provision for custom, that applies

#### 2. **"District Court"**
[Section 3(b)]
- District Court having jurisdiction
- Court of District Judge
- Includes Additional and Assistant District Judge

#### 3. **"Full Blood" and "Half Blood"**
[Section 3(c), (d)]
- **Full blood:** Related through same parents
- **Half blood:** Related through one common parent
- **Uterine blood:** Related through common mother
- **Consanguinity:** Related through common father

**Illustration:**
A and B are children of same father and mother = Full blood relations
A and C share same father, different mothers = Half blood (consanguine)
A and D share same mother, different fathers = Uterine half blood

#### 4. **"Prohibited Relationship"**
[Section 3(g)]
Relationship by blood or adoption specified in **Schedule I** (explained in detail in Conditions of Marriage topic)

#### 5. **"Sapinda Relationship"**
[Section 3(f)]
- Two persons are sapindas of each other if:
  - One is lineal ascendant of other within **5 degrees** (through mother)
  - One is lineal ascendant of other within **3 degrees** (through father)
- Counting: First generation excluded, count upward

**Illustration:**
```
Father's side: Self ‚Üí Father (1) ‚Üí Grandfather (2) ‚Üí Great-grandfather (3) [3 degrees]
Mother's side: Self ‚Üí Mother (1) ‚Üí Maternal Grandfather (2) ‚Üí Great Maternal GF (3) ‚Üí GGGreat MGF (4) ‚Üí 5th degree
```

#### 6. **"Uterine Blood"**
[Section 3(e)]
Related through common mother only.

## üéØ OBJECTIVES OF THE ACT

### 1. **Codification**
- Consolidate and codify scattered Hindu marriage laws
- Bring uniformity across India

### 2. **Reform**
- Reform outdated practices
- Remove social evils (child marriage, polygamy)
- Provide equal rights to women

### 3. **Matrimonial Remedies**
- Introduce divorce in Hindu Law
- Provide judicial separation
- Restitution of conjugal rights
- Nullity of marriage

### 4. **Women's Rights**
- Maintenance rights
- Property rights
- Custody of children
- Equal grounds for divorce (amended 1976)

### 5. **Registration**
- Provide for registration of marriages
- Though not mandatory initially, registration provisions exist

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è SALIENT FEATURES

### 1. **Monogamy Made Mandatory**
- Second marriage during subsistence of first marriage is **void**
- Both husband and wife bound by monogamy
- Violation also a criminal offense (IPC Section 494)

### 2. **Minimum Age Requirement**
- Bridegroom: Completed 21 years
- Bride: Completed 18 years
- **Note:** Recently amended to 21 years for bride (check latest amendment status)

### 3. **Mental Capacity**
- Both parties must be capable of giving valid consent
- Must be of sound mind

### 4. **Sapinda and Prohibited Degrees**
- Marriages within sapinda relationship prohibited (unless custom permits)
- Marriages within prohibited relationship void (unless custom permits)

### 5. **Divorce Provisions**
- Introduced divorce in Hindu Law (revolutionary change)
- Various grounds for divorce provided
- Mutual consent divorce also provided (added 1976)

### 6. **Registration of Marriages**
- State governments can make rules for registration
- Registration not mandatory but strongly encouraged
- Serves as proof of marriage

### 7. **Maintenance and Alimony**
- Provisions for maintenance during proceedings (pendente lite)
- Permanent alimony and maintenance
- Based on needs and capacity

### 8. **Custody of Children**
- Court can make orders regarding custody
- Welfare of child is paramount consideration

### 9. **Void and Voidable Marriages**
- Clear distinction between void and voidable marriages
- Specific grounds enumerated

### 10. **Special Provisions for Customary Marriages**
- Recognizes customary forms of marriage
- Custom given due weightage

---

## üîÑ MAJOR AMENDMENTS

### 1. **Hindu Marriage (Amendment) Act, 1964**
- Grounds for divorce liberalized
- Certain additional grounds added

### 2. **Hindu Marriage (Amendment) Act, 1976**
- **Major reform**
- Mutual consent divorce introduced [Section 13B]
- Grounds for divorce made equal for husband and wife
- Irretrievable breakdown concept recognized
- Separation ground reduced from 3 years to 2 years (some grounds)

### 3. **Marriage Laws (Amendment) Act, 2001**
- Amendments to procedure
- Separation period in some grounds altered

### 4. **Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006**
- Impacts minimum age provisions
- Child marriages voidable at option of minor

## üìñ IMPORTANT CONCEPTS

### 1. **Nature of Hindu Marriage**

**Traditional View:**
- **Sacrament (Sanskar)**
- Indissoluble bond
- Religious and spiritual union
- No concept of divorce

**Modern View (Post-1955):**
- Retains sacramental character
- Also has contractual elements (consent required)
- Dissoluble through divorce
- Legal rights and obligations

**Judicial View:**
Courts have held Hindu marriage is essentially a sacrament but has acquired contractual elements due to legislation.

---

### 2. **Valid Hindu Marriage Requirements**

A Hindu marriage is valid if:
1. **Conditions of Section 5 satisfied** (essential conditions)
2. **Ceremonies performed** as per Section 7 (customary rites)
3. **Not void** under Section 11
4. **Not voidable** under Section 12 (unless decree of nullity obtained)

---

### 3. **Registration of Hindu Marriage**

**Section 8: Registration of Hindu Marriages**

**Provisions:**
- State Government may make rules for registration
- Prescribe manner and procedure
- Registration not compulsory (but desirable)

**Benefits of Registration:**
- Serves as conclusive proof of marriage
- Helpful in claiming rights (passport, insurance, succession)
- Prevents denial of marriage
- Useful for social security schemes

**Current Position:**
Many states have made registration mandatory through rules, though Section 8 itself doesn't mandate it.

**Procedure:**
- Application to Marriage Registrar
- Submission of prescribed documents
- Issuance of marriage certificate

---

### 4. **Presumption of Marriage**

**Section 16: Legitimacy of Children**

If certain conditions satisfied, marriage is presumed valid for purpose of legitimacy of children even if marriage is void/voidable.

**Courts Apply Presumption:**
When parties lived as husband and wife for substantial period, courts presume valid marriage in absence of evidence to contrary.

## ‚öñÔ∏è LANDMARK CASE LAWS

### 1. **Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum** AIR 1985 SC 945
Though about Muslim law, led to debates about uniform civil code and affected Hindu law discourse.

### 2. **Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India** (1995) 3 SCC 635
**Principle:** Hindu husband converting to Islam to marry again is fraud on law; first marriage remains valid.

### 3. **Lily Thomas v. Union of India** (2000) 6 SCC 224
**Principle:** Conversion to another religion solely to remarry is illegal; first marriage remains subsisting.

### 4. **Satya v. Teja Singh** AIR 1975 SC 105
**Principle:** Section 5 conditions are mandatory; marriage violating them is void.

### 5. **Yamunabai Anantrao Adhav v. Anantrao Shivram Adhav** AIR 1988 SC 644
**Principle:** Irretrievable breakdown of marriage is ground for divorce under cruelty.

### 6. **Naveen Kohli v. Neelu Kohli** (2006) 4 SCC 558
**Principle:** Court can grant divorce on ground of irretrievable breakdown even if not specifically pleaded.

### 7. **Ameer Khan v. Ameena Begum** AIR 1952 Mad 12 (Pre-Act case)
Historical importance in understanding pre-1955 position.

## üìä COMPARISON: Pre-1955 vs Post-1955

| **Aspect** | **Before 1955** | **After 1955** |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Nature** | Purely sacramental | Sacramental + some contractual elements |
| **Divorce** | Not permitted | Permitted on specified grounds |
| **Polygamy** | Permitted (in practice) | Prohibited (monogamy mandatory) |
| **Age** | Child marriages common | Minimum age prescribed |
| **Women's Rights** | Limited | Enhanced rights (maintenance, divorce) |
| **Uniformity** | Different schools, customs | Uniform code for all Hindus |
| **Registration** | No provision | Provision for registration |
| **Voidable Marriages** | Concept unclear | Clear definition and grounds |
