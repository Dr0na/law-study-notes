# üìò SCHOOLS OF HINDU LAW (Deep Study)

## üîç Introduction

Schools of Hindu Law represent different systems of interpretation and application of Hindu legal principles. These schools emerged from scholarly commentaries on ancient texts and created distinct regional variations in Hindu Law that persist even today.

---

## üéØ ORIGIN OF SCHOOLS

**Historical Background:**
- Ancient Smritis often contained conflicting or ambiguous provisions
- Different scholars wrote commentaries interpreting these texts
- Regional variations in customs and practices influenced interpretations
- Over time, distinct schools emerged with loyal followings in specific regions

**Two Major Schools:**
1. **Mitakshara School**
2. **Dayabhaga School**

---

## üìö I. MITAKSHARA SCHOOL

### A. BASIC INFORMATION

**Name Origin:**
- "Mitakshara" literally means "measured in words" or "brief commentary"

**Author:**
- **Vijnaneshwara** (11th Century CE)
- Court scholar of Chalukya King Vikramaditya VI of Kalyan

**Text Basis:**
- Commentary on **Yajnavalkya Smriti**
- Systematically explained and interpreted Yajnavalkya's verses

**Geographical Application:**
- Covers majority of India
- **Excludes:** Bengal and Assam (where Dayabhaga applies)

**Sub-Schools:**
The Mitakshara School has **FIVE** major sub-schools operating in different regions.

---

### B. SUB-SCHOOLS OF MITAKSHARA (5 Sub-Schools)

#### 1. **BENARAS SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:**
- Uttar Pradesh
- Parts of North India

**Basis:**
- Mitakshara text itself
- Followed Vijnaneshwara's commentary most closely

**Special Features:**
- Orthodox and conservative approach
- Strict interpretation of ancient texts
- Daughters excluded from coparcenary property (before 2005 Amendment)

---

#### 2. **MITHILA SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:**
- Bihar
- Northern parts of Jharkhand

**Principal Text:**
- **Vivada Ratnakara** by Chandeswara
- **Vivada Chintamani** by Vachaspati Mishra

**Special Features:**
- Gave more liberal interpretation
- Recognized certain rights of women
- Emphasis on custom and local usage

---

#### 3. **BOMBAY (MAHARASHTRA) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:**
- Maharashtra
- Gujarat
- Parts of Madhya Pradesh

**Principal Text:**
- **Vyavahara Mayukha** by Nilakantha

**Special Features:**
- More progressive approach
- Recognized daughter's rights in certain cases
- Balanced traditional texts with practical considerations

**Important:**
Mumbai (Bombay) High Court follows this sub-school's principles.

---

#### 4. **DRAVIDA (MADRAS) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:**
- Tamil Nadu
- Parts of Andhra Pradesh
- Kerala

**Principal Text:**
- **Smriti Chandrika** by Devanna Bhatta

**Special Features:**
- Most liberal of Mitakshara sub-schools
- Recognized widow's rights extensively
- Acknowledged married daughter's maintenance rights
- Permitted cross-cousin marriages by custom

**Distinctive Rule:**
A widow could inherit property absolutely in certain circumstances (more liberal than other sub-schools).

---

#### 5. **PUNJAB (CUSTOMARY LAW) SUB-SCHOOL**

**Region:**
- Punjab
- Haryana
- Himachal Pradesh
- Delhi

**Special Features:**
- Heavily influenced by local customs
- **Punjab Custom (Riwaj-i-Am)** given significant weightage
- Agricultural land governed by customary law
- Concept of "ancestral land" more restricted

**Important Statutes:**
- Punjab Laws Act, 1872 (now repealed)
- Punjab Customary Law recognized sons only as heirs to agricultural land

**Note:**
After Hindu Succession Act, 1956, and 2005 Amendment, statutory law now prevails, but customary influences remain in non-statutory matters.

---

### C. KEY PRINCIPLES OF MITAKSHARA SCHOOL

#### 1. **COPARCENARY CONCEPT**

**Definition:**
Coparcenary is a narrower body than joint family. It consists of male lineal descendants within **four degrees** from common ancestor.

**Who are Coparceners:**
- Under classical Mitakshara: **Only males**
  - Son
  - Grandson
  - Great-grandson
  - Great-great-grandson

**Important Change:**
Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 made **daughters coparceners** equal to sons.

**Illustration:**
A (Propositus) ‚Üí B (Son) ‚Üí C (Grandson) ‚Üí D (Great-grandson) ‚Üí E (Great-great-grandson)

A, B, C, D, E form coparcenary. E's son cannot be coparcener with A (exceeds 4 degrees).

---

#### 2. **BIRTH RIGHT**

**Principle:**
Under Mitakshara, a son acquires an **interest by birth** in ancestral (coparcenary) property.

**Significance:**
- No act of father required to confer right
- Son becomes coparcener immediately upon birth
- Father cannot alienate coparcenary property without son's consent (except for legal necessity or benefit of estate)

**Case Law:**
**Suraj Narain v. Iqbal Narain** AIR 1953 All 221
- Court held son's birthright in coparcenary property cannot be defeated by father's alienation without legal necessity

---

#### 3. **SURVIVORSHIP (DOCTRINE OF SURVIVAL)**

**Principle:**
On death of a coparcener, his share does not pass by succession but by **survivorship** to surviving coparceners.

**Rule:**
Interest of deceased coparcener expands and merges with shares of surviving coparceners.

**Exception:**
If coparcener leaves behind:
- Widow
- Mother
- Daughter
- Daughter's son

These relatives inherit (not by survivorship but as Class I heirs under Hindu Succession Act, 1956).

**Illustration:**
A and his three sons B, C, D are coparceners. D dies without leaving Class I heirs. D's 1/4 share merges with shares of A, B, and C by survivorship.

---

#### 4. **JOINT FAMILY PROPERTY**

**Types of Property:**

##### a) **Ancestral/Coparcenary Property**
- Property inherited from father, grandfather, or great-grandfather
- All coparceners have birthright
- Cannot be alienated without consent (except legal necessity)

##### b) **Self-Acquired/Separate Property**
- Property acquired by individual's own efforts
- Property received through will, gift, or partition
- Full ownership rights; can alienate freely

**Illustration:**
A inherits property from his grandfather. This is ancestral property for A's sons. But if A purchases land from his salary, it is A's self-acquired property.

---

#### 5. **PARTITION**

**Definition:**
Partition is severance of joint status and division of joint family property.

**Types:**
- **Partial Partition:** Some coparceners separate
- **Total Partition:** Complete disruption of joint family

**Effect:**
- Coparcenary status ends
- Property divided per capita (by headcount)
- Each coparcener gets definite share

**Rights:**
Any coparcener can demand partition as matter of right.

---

### D. INHERITANCE RULES (MITAKSHARA)

**Order of Succession:**

1. **First Preference:** Surviving coparceners (by survivorship)
2. **Second Preference:** Heirs under Hindu Succession Act, 1956
   - Class I Heirs
   - Class II Heirs
   - Agnates
   - Cognates

**Special Rules:**
- Preference to male heirs historically
- 2005 Amendment gave daughters equal rights

---

## üìö II. DAYABHAGA SCHOOL

### A. BASIC INFORMATION

**Name Origin:**
- "Dayabhaga" means "Division of Inheritance"

**Author:**
- **Jimutavahana** (12th Century CE)
- Scholar from Bengal region

**Text Basis:**
- Original treatise (not a commentary)
- Focused primarily on inheritance law

**Geographical Application:**
- **Bengal** (West Bengal)
- **Assam**
- Parts of Odisha

**Fundamental Difference:**
Dayabhaga rejects Mitakshara's doctrine of birthright and survivorship.

---

### B. KEY PRINCIPLES OF DAYABHAGA SCHOOL

#### 1. **NO BIRTH RIGHT**

**Principle:**
Sons do **NOT** acquire any interest in father's property during father's lifetime merely by birth.

**Significance:**
- Father has absolute ownership of property
- Father can alienate property freely without son's consent
- Sons inherit only after father's death

**Contrast with Mitakshara:**
- **Mitakshara:** Son has birthright
- **Dayabhaga:** Son has only right to inherit (spes successionis)

**Illustration:**
Under Dayabhaga, if father sells all his property during lifetime, sons have no legal remedy as they had no present interest.

---

#### 2. **SUCCESSION, NOT SURVIVORSHIP**

**Principle:**
Property passes by **succession** (inheritance) upon death, not by survivorship.

**Significance:**
- Deceased's share distributed among his heirs
- Follows rules of succession
- Surviving coparceners don't automatically take deceased's share

**Contrast with Mitakshara:**
- **Mitakshara:** Share passes by survivorship to coparceners
- **Dayabhaga:** Share passes by succession to heirs

**Illustration:**
A, B (son), C (son) form joint family. B dies leaving widow W and daughter D. Under Dayabhaga, B's share passes to W and D by succession, not to A and C.

---

#### 3. **SAPINDA RELATIONSHIP**

**Definition:**
Sapinda means "connected by particles of the same body."

**Scope:**
Relationship through offering of *pinda* (rice balls) in funeral ceremonies.

**Extent:**
- Through father: Up to **7 degrees**
- Through mother: Up to **5 degrees**

**Significance:**
Sapinda relationship determines:
- Who can inherit
- Marriage prohibitions

**Illustration:**
A's son's son's son (great-grandson) is sapinda to A within 7 degrees.

---

#### 4. **ORDER OF SUCCESSION (DAYABHAGA)**

Dayabhaga has its own distinctive order of succession:

##### **First Preference:** Sapindas (Blood Relations)
- Son, grandson, great-grandson
- Daughter, daughter's son
- Widow, mother

##### **Second Preference:** Sakulyas (Cognates)
- Relations beyond sapinda degree but within 14 generations

##### **Third Preference:** Spiritual Teacher (Guru)

##### **Fourth Preference:** Disciple (Shishya)

##### **Fifth Preference:** King (State)

**Important:**
After Hindu Succession Act, 1956, this order is largely superseded by statutory order (Class I, Class II, Agnates, Cognates).

---

#### 5. **PROPERTY DIVISION**

**Rule:**
Division is **per capita** (by head count) among heirs.

**No Doctrine of Representation:**
Classical Dayabhaga didn't recognize representation (though modern law does).

**Father's Authority:**
- Father has complete control over property during lifetime
- Can gift, sell, or bequeath
- Sons have no say

---

### C. WIDOW'S POSITION (DAYABHAGA)

**Better Position than Mitakshara:**
- Widow inherits as heir
- Gets share equal to son's share
- Has limited ownership (not absolute in classical law)

**Restrictions (Historically):**
- Could not alienate property without necessity
- Property reverted to husband's heirs after her death

**Modern Law:**
Hindu Succession Act, 1956 gives widow absolute rights as Class I heir.

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: MITAKSHARA vs DAYABHAGA

| **Aspect** | **Mitakshara School** | **Dayabhaga School** |
|------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Geographical Area** | Most of India (except Bengal & Assam) | Bengal, Assam |
| **Author/Text** | Vijnaneshwara's commentary on Yajnavalkya Smriti | Jimutavahana's original treatise |
| **Birth Right** | Son has birthright in ancestral property | No birthright; right to inherit only |
| **Father's Power** | Limited; cannot alienate coparcenary property | Absolute; full control over property |
| **Coparcenary** | Recognized; 4-degree rule | Not recognized in same manner |
| **Succession** | By survivorship (among coparceners) | By inheritance (succession) |
| **Partition** | Coparcener can demand as right | Different rules apply |
| **Widow's Rights** | Limited (historically) | Better position; equal to son |
| **Sub-schools** | Five sub-schools | No sub-schools |
| **Degree of Relations** | Not specifically defined | Sapinda (7/5 degrees) |

## üìä CURRENT LEGAL POSITION

### Impact of Codification:

#### 1. **Hindu Succession Act, 1956**
- Unified succession rules across India
- Both schools now follow same statutory rules for succession
- Distinction remains relevant only for:
  - Coparcenary property matters
  - Joint family property issues
  - Partition rights

#### 2. **Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005**
- Made daughters coparceners in Mitakshara coparcenary
- Reduced practical differences between schools
- Gave equal rights to daughters

#### 3. **Surviving Distinctions**
Despite codification, schools still differ in:
- Coparcenary concepts
- Joint family property interpretation
- Partition procedures
- Certain customary practices

---

## ‚öñÔ∏è LANDMARK CASE LAWS

### 1. **Commissioner of Wealth Tax v. Chander Sen** AIR 1986 SC 1753
**Principle:** Explained difference between Mitakshara and Dayabhaga coparcenary systems.

### 2. **Gurupad Khandappa Magdum v. Hirabai Khandappa Magdum** AIR 1978 SC 1239
**Principle:** Father has limited power to alienate coparcenary property under Mitakshara law.

### 3. **Mayne's Hindu Law & Usage** (Judicial Recognition)
Courts frequently refer to this authoritative text for understanding school distinctions.

### 4. **Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma** (2020) 9 SCC 1
**Principle:** Daughter's coparcenary rights under 2005 Amendment apply retrospectively; clarified Mitakshara coparcenary post-amendment.

### 5. **Uttam v. Saubhag Singh** AIR 2016 SC 1169
**Principle:** Coparcenary property and birthright principles explained in context of partition.

## üéì KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

### 1. **Geographic Division**
- Mitakshara = Most of India
- Dayabhaga = Bengal + Assam

### 2. **Fundamental Difference**
Birthright (Mitakshara) vs No Birthright (Dayabhaga)

### 3. **Five Mitakshara Sub-Schools**
Benaras, Mithila, Bombay, Dravida, Punjab

### 4. **Survivorship vs Succession**
Core conceptual difference

### 5. **Modern Convergence**
Statutory law has reduced practical differences, but schools remain relevant for coparcenary and joint family matters.
