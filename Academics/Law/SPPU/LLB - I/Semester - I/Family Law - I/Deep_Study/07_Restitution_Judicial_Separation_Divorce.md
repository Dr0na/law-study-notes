# üìò RESTITUTION, JUDICIAL SEPARATION & DIVORCE (Deep Study)

## üîç Introduction

Sections 9, 10, and 13 of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provide three progressive matrimonial reliefs: Restitution of Conjugal Rights (least drastic), Judicial Separation (intermediate), and Divorce (most drastic). These remedies represent a spectrum from reconciliation to complete dissolution.

---

## üìö PART I: RESTITUTION OF CONJUGAL RIGHTS [Section 9]

### **SECTION 9: Restitution of Conjugal Rights**

**Complete Text:**
"When either the husband or the wife has, without reasonable excuse, withdrawn from the society of the other, the aggrieved party may apply, by petition to the district court, for restitution of conjugal rights and the court, on being satisfied of the truth of the statements made in such petition and that there is no legal ground why the application should not be granted, may decree restitution of conjugal rights accordingly."

---

### **A. CONCEPT AND NATURE**

**Definition:**
Restitution of conjugal rights is a remedy by which one spouse can compel the other to resume cohabitation and consortium.

**Conjugal Rights Include:**
- Right to cohabitation
- Right to marital relations
- Right to mutual comfort
- Right to assistance and support
- Right to consortium (companionship)

**Objective:**
- Promote reconciliation
- Preserve marriage
- Enforce matrimonial duty
- Prevent breakdown

**Nature:**
- Equitable relief
- Discretionary remedy
- Aimed at reconciliation, not punishment

---

### **B. ESSENTIAL CONDITIONS**

For decree of restitution, following must be established:

#### **1. Withdrawal from Society**
- One spouse has withdrawn from society of other
- "Society" includes:
  - Physical cohabitation
  - Companionship
  - Marital relations
  - Mutual comfort and assistance

**Forms of Withdrawal:**
- Leaving matrimonial home
- Refusing to cohabit
- Denying marital relations
- Mental/emotional withdrawal

#### **2. Without Reasonable Excuse**
Withdrawal must be without justifiable reason.

**Reasonable Excuses (Defenses):**
- Cruelty by petitioner
- Adultery by petitioner
- Desertion by petitioner
- Ill-treatment or abuse
- Lack of maintenance
- Willful neglect
- Harassment for dowry
- Any matrimonial offense by petitioner

**Burden of Proof:**
Respondent must prove reasonable excuse.

#### **3. No Legal Ground to Refuse**
Court must be satisfied no legal ground exists to deny decree.

**Legal Grounds to Refuse:**
- Petitioner guilty of matrimonial offense
- Petitioner's conduct justifies withdrawal
- Decree would cause injustice
- Undue hardship to respondent

---

### **C. PROCEDURE**

**1. Petition:**
- File petition in District Court (Section 19 jurisdiction)
- State facts of withdrawal
- Seek restitution

**2. Service:**
- Notice to respondent
- Respondent files reply

**3. Evidence:**
- Parties lead evidence
- Examination/cross-examination

**4. Decree:**
- If satisfied, court passes decree
- Orders resumption of cohabitation

---

### **D. EFFECT OF DECREE**

#### **Immediate Effect:**
- Decree orders resumption of cohabitation
- Not executable by force (cannot physically compel)
- Moral and legal pressure to comply

#### **If Decree Not Complied:**
If restitution decree not obeyed for **ONE YEAR** from date of decree:
- Becomes ground for **divorce** [Section 13(1A)(ii)]
- Becomes ground for **judicial separation** [Section 10]

**Section 13(1A)(ii):**
"there has been no restitution of conjugal rights as between the parties to the marriage for a period of one year or upwards after the passing of a decree for restitution of conjugal rights in a proceeding to which they were parties"

---

### **E. CONSTITUTIONAL VALIDITY**

Section 9 has been challenged as violating fundamental rights:

#### **Challenge Grounds:**
- Violates **Article 21** (Right to Privacy)
- Violates **Article 14** (Equality)
- Infringes on personal liberty and dignity
- Amounts to "marital rape"

#### **Andhra Pradesh High Court:**
**T. Sareetha v. T. Venkata Subbaiah** AIR 1983 AP 356
- Struck down Section 9 as unconstitutional
- Violates privacy, dignity, freedom under Article 21
- Cannot compel one to live with another

#### **Delhi High Court:**
**Harvinder Kaur v. Harmander Singh** AIR 1984 Del 66
- Upheld Section 9 as constitutional
- Promotes reconciliation
- Doesn't violate fundamental rights
- Decree not executable by force

#### **Supreme Court:**
**Saroj Rani v. Sudarshan Kumar** AIR 1984 SC 1562
- **Upheld constitutional validity** of Section 9
- Doesn't violate Articles 14, 21
- Purpose is reconciliation, not forced cohabitation
- Reasonable restriction in interest of marriage institution
- Decree not executed by physical force

**Current Position:**
Section 9 is **valid** and continues to operate.

---

### **F. IMPORTANT CASE LAWS**

#### **1. Sushila Bai v. Prem Narayan** AIR 1968 MP 07
**Held:**
- Withdrawal must be against will of other spouse
- If both agree to live separately, no restitution

#### **2. Bipinchandra v. Prabhavati** AIR 1957 SC 176
**Held:**
- Refusal to perform marital obligations without justification amounts to withdrawal

#### **3. Moonshee Buzloor Ruheem v. Shumsoonissa Begum** (1867) 11 MIA 551
**Held:**
- Withdrawal means abandonment of cohabitation
- Without reasonable cause

---

### **G. MODERN RELEVANCE**

**Criticism:**
- Rarely leads to actual reconciliation
- Often used as stepping stone to divorce
- May cause further bitterness
- Questioned on human rights grounds

**Practical Use:**
- Parties often don't comply with decree
- After 1 year non-compliance, divorce sought
- Effectively prolongs inevitable divorce

---

## üìö PART II: JUDICIAL SEPARATION [Section 10]

### **SECTION 10: Judicial Separation**

**Complete Text:**
"(1) Either party to a marriage, whether solemnized before or after the commencement of this Act, may present a petition praying for a decree for judicial separation on any of the grounds specified in sub-section (1) of section 13, and in the case of a wife also on any of the grounds specified in sub-section (2) thereof, as grounds on which a petition for divorce might have been presented.

(2) Where a decree for judicial separation has been passed, it shall no longer be obligatory for the petitioner to cohabit with the respondent."

---

### **A. CONCEPT AND NATURE**

**Definition:**
Judicial separation is a decree by court suspending marital obligations without dissolving marriage.

**Nature:**
- **Marriage continues** to exist legally
- Parties **live separately**
- No duty to cohabit
- **Cannot remarry**
- Less drastic than divorce

**Objective:**
- Provide breathing space
- Allow cooling period
- Test whether parties wish to reconcile
- Alternative to divorce for those with religious/moral objections

---

### **B. GROUNDS**

Grounds for judicial separation are **same as grounds for divorce**.

**From Section 13(1):**
1. Adultery
2. Cruelty
3. Desertion (2 years)
4. Conversion to another religion
5. Insanity
6. Leprosy (virulent and incurable)
7. Venereal disease (communicable)
8. Renunciation of world
9. Presumption of death (7 years)

**Additional for Wife [Section 13(2)]:**
1. Husband's bigamy (pre-Act marriage)
2. Husband guilty of rape, sodomy, bestiality
3. Maintenance decree not complied
4. Marriage before 15, repudiated before 18

**See detailed explanation of each ground in Divorce section below.**

---

### **C. EFFECT OF DECREE**

#### **Immediate Effects:**
1. **No obligation to cohabit** [Section 10(2)]
2. Parties can **live separately**
3. **Marriage continues** to exist
4. **Cannot remarry**

#### **Property Rights:**
- Property rights may be affected
- Wife may lose right to husband's property upon his death (depends on circumstances)

#### **Maintenance:**
- Wife entitled to maintenance during judicial separation
- Court can order alimony

#### **Resumption of Cohabitation:**
If parties **resume cohabitation**, judicial separation terminates.

#### **Conversion to Divorce:**
After **1 year** of judicial separation, either party can apply for divorce on this ground [Section 13(1A)(i)].

---

### **D. DIFFERENCE: JUDICIAL SEPARATION vs DIVORCE**

| **Aspect** | **Judicial Separation** | **Divorce** |
|------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Marriage Bond** | Continues | Dissolved |
| **Cohabitation** | No obligation | No relation |
| **Remarriage** | Not allowed | Allowed |
| **Reversible** | Yes (by resuming cohabitation) | No (irreversible) |
| **Grounds** | Same as divorce | Section 13 |
| **Effect** | Suspension of obligations | Termination of marriage |
| **Status** | Still married | Single/divorced |

---

### **E. PRACTICAL USE**

**Rarely Sought:**
Judicial separation is rarely sought in practice because:
- Doesn't allow remarriage
- Doesn't provide finality
- Parties prefer direct divorce

**When Preferred:**
- Religious or moral objections to divorce
- Uncertainty about reconciliation
- Trial separation
- Strategic reasons (cooling period before divorce)

---

### **SECTION 13: Divorce**

**Complete Text:**
"(1) Any marriage solemnized, whether before or after the commencement of this Act, may, on a petition presented by either the husband or the wife, be dissolved by a decree of divorce on the ground that the other party‚Äî

[Grounds (i) to (ix) listed below]

(1A) Either party to a marriage, whether solemnized before or after the commencement of this Act, may also present a petition for the dissolution of the marriage by a decree of divorce on the ground‚Äî

[Additional grounds under 1A]

(2) A wife may also present a petition for the dissolution of her marriage by a decree of divorce on the ground‚Äî

[Special grounds for wife]"

---

### **A. CONCEPT AND NATURE**

**Definition:**
Divorce is complete dissolution of marriage bond by decree of competent court.

**Nature:**
- **Marriage dissolved completely**
- Parties free to **remarry**
- All marital obligations cease
- **Irreversible** (once final)

**Revolutionary Change:**
- Classical Hindu Law did not permit divorce
- Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 introduced divorce (revolutionary reform)
- Reformed in 1976 to liberalize grounds and introduce mutual consent

---

### **B. TYPES OF DIVORCE**

### **I. CONTESTED DIVORCE [Section 13(1) & 13(2)]**
Based on matrimonial offense by one party.

### **II. MUTUAL CONSENT DIVORCE [Section 13B]**
Both parties jointly seek divorce by mutual agreement.

---

## üî¥ CONTESTED DIVORCE GROUNDS

### **GROUNDS UNDER SECTION 13(1) - Available to Both Spouses**

---

### **Ground 1: ADULTERY [Section 13(1)(i)]**

**Text:**
"has, after the solemnization of the marriage, had voluntary sexual intercourse with any person other than his or her spouse"

**Definition:**
Voluntary sexual intercourse by married person with someone other than spouse.

**Essential Elements:**
1. **Sexual intercourse** (actual intercourse necessary)
2. **Voluntary** (not by force/rape)
3. **After marriage**
4. **With person other than spouse**

**Proof:**
- Direct evidence rarely available
- Can be proved by circumstantial evidence
- Doctrine of preponderance of probabilities
- Strong evidence of opportunity and inclination

**Single Act Sufficient:**
Even **one act** of adultery sufficient for divorce.

**Illustrations:**

**Illustration 1:**
Wife discovers husband having sexual relations with another woman. Sufficient ground for divorce.

**Illustration 2:**
Husband finds wife and another man in compromising circumstances in bedroom late at night. Adultery can be inferred.

**Case Laws:**

**Smt. Jorden Diengdeh v. S.S. Chopra** AIR 1985 SC 935
- Adultery can be inferred from circumstances
- Direct proof not always necessary
- Opportunity + Inclination = Inference

**Swati Verma v. Rajan Verma** (2004) 1 SCC 123
- Condonation bars relief if parties resumed cohabitation after knowledge

---

### **Ground 2: CRUELTY [Section 13(1)(ia)]**

**Text:**
"has treated the petitioner with cruelty"

**Types of Cruelty:**

#### **A. Physical Cruelty**
- Physical violence
- Assault and battery
- Causing bodily harm
- Threatening violence

#### **B. Mental Cruelty**
- Conduct causing mental pain and suffering
- Reasonable apprehension of harm
- Making life miserable

**Mental Cruelty Includes:**

**Samar Ghosh v. Jaya Ghosh** (2007) 4 SCC 511 gave **illustrative list** (not exhaustive):

1. Repeated rude and discourteous behavior
2. Constant demands for money/property
3. Persistent false accusations
4. Denial of sexual relations
5. Persistent malicious behavior
6. Unfounded criticism
7. Public humiliation
8. Desertion without cause
9. Persistent denial of access to children
10. Unreasonable jealousy
11. Making false allegations of adultery/cruelty
12. Habitual drunkenness/drug addiction
13. Unwillingness to have children
14. Persistent nagging
15. Refusal to cohabit
16. (Many others - conduct making life unbearable)

**Test for Cruelty:**
Would conduct cause reasonable apprehension of harm (physical/mental) to petitioner?

**Subjective and Objective:**
- Subjective: Effect on particular spouse
- Objective: Would reasonable person consider it cruel?

**Illustrations:**

**Illustration 1:**
Husband regularly beats wife, abuses her. Clear **physical cruelty**.

**Illustration 2:**
Wife constantly nags husband, makes false allegations, refuses to cook or care. **Mental cruelty**.

**Illustration 3:**
Husband makes unreasonable demands for dowry, harasses wife. **Cruelty** (also covered under Dowry Prohibition Act).

**Important Case Laws:**

**Dastane v. Dastane** AIR 1975 SC 1534
- Cruelty is conduct causing reasonable apprehension of harm
- Not limited to physical violence

**Samar Ghosh v. Jaya Ghosh** (2007) 4 SCC 511
- Comprehensive definition of mental cruelty
- Illustrative list of acts constituting cruelty

**Naveen Kohli v. Neelu Kohli** (2006) 4 SCC 558
- Irretrievable breakdown can amount to cruelty
- Forcing spouse to continue dead marriage is cruelty

**V. Bhagat v. D. Bhagat** AIR 1994 SC 710
- Cruelty is conduct of such character as to cause danger to life/limb or health
- Or reasonable apprehension thereof

---

### **Ground 3: DESERTION [Section 13(1)(ib)]**

**Text:**
"has deserted the petitioner for a continuous period of not less than two years immediately preceding the presentation of the petition"

**Definition:**
Permanent abandonment of one spouse by other without reasonable cause and without consent.

**Essential Elements:**

1. **Factum of Separation**
   - Actual living apart
   - Physical separation

2. **Animus Deserendi** (Intention to Desert)
   - Intention to permanently end marital relationship
   - Intention to abandon

3. **Without Consent**
   - Against will of deserted spouse
   - If mutual separation, not desertion

4. **Without Reasonable Cause**
   - No justification for leaving
   - If valid reason exists, not desertion

5. **Continuous Period of 2 Years**
   - Immediately preceding petition
   - No substantial resumption

**Types:**

#### **A. Actual/Physical Desertion**
Leaving matrimonial home and living separately.

#### **B. Constructive Desertion**
- Not leaving physically
- But withdrawing from marital obligations
- Making life unbearable forcing other to leave

**Illustrations:**

**Illustration 1:**
Husband leaves wife and goes to another city. No communication for 3 years. **Desertion** established (exceeds 2 years).

**Illustration 2:**
Wife leaves due to husband's cruelty. Not desertion by wife (reasonable cause exists).

**Illustration 3:**
Both spouses mutually agree to live separately. Not desertion (consent exists).

**Case Laws:**

**Moonshee Buzloor Ruheem v. Shumsoonissa Begum** (1867) 11 MIA 551
- Desertion includes withdrawal from society of other spouse
- Without reasonable cause
- Without consent

**Lachman Utamchand Kirpalani v. Meena** AIR 1964 SC 40
- Animus deserendi essential
- Mere living apart insufficient

**Bipin Chandra v. Prabhavati** AIR 1957 SC 176
- Desertion is total repudiation of marital obligations
- Withdrawal from consortium

---

### **Ground 4: CONVERSION [Section 13(1)(ii)]**

**Text:**
"has ceased to be a Hindu by conversion to another religion"

**Explanation:**
If one spouse converts from Hinduism to another religion, other spouse can seek divorce.

**Rationale:**
- Personal laws differ across religions
- Hindu Marriage Act applies only to Hindus
- Conversion affects marital obligations

**Important:**
Conversion **solely to remarry** is fraud on law (Sarla Mudgal case).

**Illustration:**
Hindu husband converts to Islam. Wife can seek divorce under this ground.

**Case Law:**

**Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India** (1995) 3 SCC 635
- Conversion merely to evade bigamy law is fraud
- First marriage under Hindu law continues

---

### **Ground 5: INSANITY [Section 13(1)(iii)]**

**Text:**
"has been incurably of unsound mind, or has been suffering continuously or intermittently from mental disorder of such a kind and to such an extent that the petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with the respondent"

**Explanation:**
Incurable insanity or mental disorder making cohabitation unreasonable.

**Requirements:**
1. **Incurable unsound mind**, OR
2. **Mental disorder** (continuous/intermittent) making living together unreasonable

**Test:**
Whether petitioner can reasonably be expected to live with respondent given mental condition.

**Not Ground:**
Temporary mental illness (if curable) not sufficient.

**Proof:**
Medical evidence essential.

---

#### **Explanation (a): Severe Mental Disorder - 2 Years**

**Text of Explanation (a):**
"In this clause, the expression 'mental disorder' means mental illness, arrested or incomplete development of mind, psychopathic disorder or any other disorder or disability of mind and includes schizophrenia."

**Explanation (b):**
"The expression 'psychopathic disorder' means a persistent disorder or disability of mind (whether or not including subnormality of intelligence) which results in abnormally aggressive or seriously irresponsible conduct on the part of the other party, and whether or not it requires or is susceptible to medical treatment."

**Case Law:**

**Kailash Wati v. Ayodhya Parkash** AIR 1971 P&H 281
- Burden to prove insanity on petitioner
- Expert medical evidence required

---

### **Ground 6: LEPROSY [Section 13(1)(iv)]**

**Text:**
"has been suffering from a virulent and incurable form of leprosy"

**Requirements:**
1. Leprosy must be **virulent** (severe, infectious)
2. Must be **incurable**

**Modern Relevance:**
With medical advances, leprosy now largely curable. This ground rarely invoked.

---

### **Ground 7: VENEREAL DISEASE [Section 13(1)(v)]**

**Text:**
"has been suffering from venereal disease in a communicable form"

**Requirements:**
1. Suffering from **venereal disease** (STD)
2. In **communicable form** (transmissible)

**Examples:**
- Syphilis
- Gonorrhea
- HIV/AIDS (debated - some courts accept)

**Rationale:**
Risk of transmission to petitioner.

**Modern Relevance:**
Many venereal diseases now treatable. Ground may be difficult to establish if curable.

---

### **Ground 8: RENUNCIATION OF WORLD [Section 13(1)(vi)]**

**Text:**
"has renounced the world by entering any religious order"

**Explanation:**
Spouse has become *sanyasi* (renounced worldly life) and entered religious order.

**Requirements:**
- Formal renunciation
- Entry into religious order (monk, nun, sanyasi)
- Complete abandonment of marital life

**Rare Ground:**
Seldom invoked in modern times.

**Illustration:**
Husband becomes monk in Buddhist monastery, takes vows of celibacy, renounces world. Wife can seek divorce.

---

### **Ground 9: PRESUMPTION OF DEATH [Section 13(1)(vii)]**

**Text:**
"has not been heard of as being alive for a period of seven years or more by those persons who would naturally have heard of it, had that party been alive"

**Requirements:**
1. Spouse **not heard of** for **7 years or more**
2. By persons who would naturally have heard (family, friends)
3. Presumption spouse is dead

**Effect:**
Not declaration of death, but ground for divorce.

**After Divorce:**
If "dead" spouse reappears, divorce decree already passed is valid. Marriage dissolved.

**Illustration:**
Husband goes missing in 2010. No trace for 8 years. Wife can seek divorce in 2018 on presumption of death.

---

### **Ground 10: NO RESUMPTION AFTER JUDICIAL SEPARATION [Section 13(1A)(i)]**

**Text:**
"there has been no resumption of cohabitation as between the parties to the marriage for a period of one year or more after the passing of a decree for judicial separation in a proceeding to which they were parties"

**Requirements:**
1. Decree of **judicial separation** obtained
2. **No resumption of cohabitation** for **1 year or more** after decree
3. Either party can then seek divorce

---

### **Ground 11: NO RESTITUTION AFTER DECREE [Section 13(1A)(ii)]**

**Text:**
"there has been no restitution of conjugal rights as between the parties to the marriage for a period of one year or more after the passing of a decree for restitution of conjugal rights in a proceeding to which they were parties"

**Requirements:**
1. Decree of **restitution of conjugal rights** obtained
2. Decree **not complied with** for **1 year or more**
3. Either party can then seek divorce

### **Ground 12: HUSBAND'S BIGAMY (Pre-Act) [Section 13(2)(i)]**

**Text:**
"in the case of any marriage solemnized before the commencement of this Act, that the husband had married again before such commencement or that any other wife of the husband married before such commencement was alive at the time of the solemnization of the marriage of the petitioner"

**Explanation:**
If husband had another wife living at time of petitioner's marriage (before 1955), wife can seek divorce.

**Applies to:** Pre-1955 marriages only.

---

### **Ground 13: HUSBAND GUILTY OF SERIOUS OFFENSES [Section 13(2)(ii)]**

**Text:**
"that the husband has, since the solemnization of the marriage, been guilty of rape, sodomy or bestiality"

**Explanation:**
Husband guilty of:
- **Rape** (of any person)
- **Sodomy** (unnatural sexual offenses)
- **Bestiality** (sexual intercourse with animal)

**Rationale:**
Serious moral turpitude justifying divorce for wife.

---

### **Ground 14: NON-COMPLIANCE WITH MAINTENANCE DECREE [Section 13(2)(iii)]**

**Text:**
"that a decree or order for maintenance under section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, or under the corresponding section 488 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, has been passed against the husband and he has failed to comply with such decree or order for a period of one year or more"

**Requirements:**
1. Maintenance decree/order under Section 125 CrPC
2. Husband **failed to comply** for **1 year or more**

---

### **Ground 15: REPUDIATION OF CHILD MARRIAGE [Section 13(2)(iv)]**

**Text:**
"that her marriage (whether consummated or not) was solemnized before she attained the age of fifteen years and she has repudiated the marriage after attaining that age but before attaining the age of eighteen years"

**Requirements:**
1. Marriage solemnized **before age 15**
2. Wife **repudiates** marriage
3. Repudiation **after 15** but **before 18**

**Note:** Age limit amended - now minimum age is 18 for bride. This ground has limited application.

## üíö MUTUAL CONSENT DIVORCE [Section 13B]

### **SECTION 13B: Divorce by Mutual Consent**

**Added by 1976 Amendment - Revolutionary Provision**

**Complete Text:**
"(1) Subject to the provisions of this Act a petition for dissolution of marriage by a decree of divorce may be presented to the district court by both the parties to a marriage together, whether such marriage was solemnized before or after the commencement of the Marriage Laws (Amendment) Act, 1976 (68 of 1976), on the ground that they have been living separately for a period of one year or more, that they have not been able to live together and that they have mutually agreed that the marriage should be dissolved.

(2) On the motion of both the parties made not earlier than six months after the date of the presentation of the petition referred to in sub-section (1) and not later than eighteen months after the said date, if the petition is not withdrawn in the meantime, the court shall, on being satisfied, after hearing the parties and after making such inquiry as it thinks fit, that a marriage has been solemnized and that the averments in the petition are true, pass a decree of divorce declaring the marriage to be dissolved with effect from the date of the decree."

---

### **A. REQUIREMENTS**

#### **1. Joint Petition**
Both parties must **jointly petition** together.

#### **2. Living Separately**
Parties must have been **living separately** for period of **1 year or more** immediately preceding petition.

**"Living Separately":**
- Need not be physically in different locations
- Mental separation sufficient
- No matrimonial relationship

#### **3. Unable to Live Together**
Parties have not been able to live together (marriage has broken down).

#### **4. Mutual Agreement**
Parties have **mutually agreed** that marriage should be dissolved.

**Consent Must Be:**
- Free
- Voluntary
- Not obtained by force/fraud/coercion

---

### **B. TWO-STAGE PROCESS**

#### **FIRST MOTION:**
- File joint petition
- State that lived separately for 1 year+
- State mutual agreement to divorce
- Court records petition

#### **COOLING PERIOD:**
- **Minimum 6 months** from first motion
- **Maximum 18 months** from first motion
- Purpose: Ensure parties are certain
- Either party can withdraw during this period

#### **SECOND MOTION:**
- After 6 months (but before 18 months), parties file second motion
- Confirm they still want divorce
- If petition not withdrawn, court passes decree

---

### **C. NO REQUIREMENT TO PROVE GROUNDS**

**Vishnu Dutt Sharma v. Manju Sharma** AIR 2009 SC 489:
- No need to prove any matrimonial offense
- Mutual consent itself sufficient
- Court should not deny decree if parties agree
- Purpose is to ease divorce for consenting parties

---

### **D. WITHDRAWAL**

Either party can **withdraw consent** any time before **second motion**.

If withdrawn:
- Petition dismissed
- No divorce granted
- Marriage continues

**Case Law:**

**Sureshta Devi v. Om Prakash** AIR 1992 SC 1904
- Right to withdraw exists until second motion
- After second motion and decree, no withdrawal

---

### **E. EFFECT OF DECREE**

Once decree passed:
- Marriage **dissolved**
- Parties free to **remarry**
- Irreversible (except by appeal)

---

### **F. ADVANTAGES**

1. **No fault-finding** required
2. **Dignified exit** from marriage
3. **Faster** than contested divorce
4. **Less acrimonious**
5. **Parties retain control**
6. **Privacy maintained**

---

### **G. IMPORTANT CASE LAWS**

**Vishnu Dutt Sharma v. Manju Sharma** AIR 2009 SC 489
- Mutual consent divorce doesn't require proof of traditional grounds
- Court should grant if parties agree

**Sureshta Devi v. Om Prakash** AIR 1992 SC 1904
- Withdrawal possible before second motion

**Hitesh Bhatnagar v. Deepa Bhatnagar** (2011) 12 SCC 542
- Court cannot refuse divorce if Section 13B conditions satisfied

## üéì KEY POINTS TO REMEMBER

### **Restitution:**
- Compels resumption of cohabitation
- If not complied for 1 year ‚Üí divorce ground
- Constitutionally valid

### **Judicial Separation:**
- Marriage continues, no cohabitation
- Same grounds as divorce
- After 1 year ‚Üí divorce ground
- Rarely used

### **Divorce:**
- Complete dissolution
- **9 common grounds** + **2 additional (1A)** + **4 for wife only** = **15 grounds total**
- **Mutual consent** most popular (Section 13B)
- 1976 Amendment liberalized divorce

### **Irretrievable Breakdown:**
- Not explicit ground
- Judicially recognized under cruelty and Article 142

### **Cruelty Most Common:**
- Physical + mental
- Subjective + objective test
- Includes wide range of conduct
