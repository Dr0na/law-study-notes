# Divorce under Hindu Law

## Introduction

In divorce the relationship of husband and wife **ceases to exist**. After divorce the original marital status of the parties cannot be restored. Once the decree of divorce is passed, parties to such marriage become entitled to remarry after the lapse of a statutory period. Divorce put an end to conjugal duties of the parties.

---

## Old Hindu Law

Divorce was **unknown to the laws of the Dharmashastra** as marriage was regarded as an indissoluble union of the husband and wife. But on the other hand we find some texts in Narada and Parashar codes of law, which permit divorce to wife in certain conditions of life.

### According to Narada

There are **5 conditions** in which a woman could renounce her husband and choose another:

1. Husband lost and unheard of for a period of **7 years**
2. Husband is **dead**
3. Husband **renounced the world**
4. Husband became **impotent**
5. Husband is **ex-communicated**

### According to Kautilya's Arthashastra

Dissolution of marriage is permitted in the following cases:

1. Husband's misconduct with the wife
2. Husband's prolonged absence
3. Husband's banishment
4. Husband becoming dangerous to the wife's life
5. Husband ex-communicated
6. Husband having become impotent

---

## Modern Law - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955

The provisions for divorce in the existing **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955** have radical change in the legal concept of Hindu marriage. The significant situation arising since the Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976 by the creation of identical grounds of divorce and judicial separation.

## Grounds of Divorce under Section 13(1)

### 1. Adultery

- A **single act of voluntary sexual intercourse** with any person other than his or her spouse has been made a ground for divorce
- The term **"living in adultery"** has been replaced by expression **"voluntary sexual intercourse"** by the Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976
- The burden of proving adultery is on the party who alleges it

#### Circumstantial Evidence of Adultery

In absence of direct evidence of adultery, it can be proved by circumstantial evidence as:

- Man and woman stayed in one house together for a long time
- Man/woman had refused to go back to matrimonial home
- Man and woman not coming in witness box to deny charge of adultery
- No reasonable answer to prove why they are living together

> **ðŸ“Œ Veera Reddi v. Kistamma (1969 Mad. 235)**
>
> The wife brought to an end her every relation with the husband on 17/11/1957 and started living with her parents. After 17/11/1957 there was no meeting between them. On 23/12/1958 after 402 days of separation between them wife delivered a child. The Madras High Court held the wife is guilty of adultery.

> **ðŸ“Œ P v. P and R (A.I.R. 1982 Bom. 498)**
>
> It is difficult to produce evidence to prove adultery. In such a situation court may rely on the circumstantial evidence. The circumstantial evidence will consist not only of the two offending parties being found together in unusual circumstances or being together alone in a scheduled place for a long period of time but also will consist of the social conditions of the parties.

> **ðŸ“Œ Vimlakshi v. Smt. Sarojini (AIR 1991 Karn. 128)**
>
> Where the husband had witnessed the adultery but filed the petition for divorce on that ground 8 years later. The court held that divorce cannot be granted because lapse of 8 years can consider as condonation.

### 2. Cruelty

- The word **"Cruelty"** has not been defined in the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955
- It is course of conduct of one which is adversely affecting the other
- Earlier cruelty had to be such which would affect the physical or mental health of other spouse

#### Different Kinds of Cruelty

1. **Actual or threatened physical injury** - It includes danger to life, limb or health (bodily or mental)
2. **Verbal abuse or insults** - It includes things which bring shame and mental agony to other spouse
3. **Excessive sexual intercourse**
4. **Refusal of intercourse**
5. **Neglect by spouse** in the discharge of his/her duties

> **ðŸ“Œ Rajendra Singh v. Taramati (AIR 1980 Del. 213)**
>
> The husband was seriously injured in an accident and was admitted in a hospital. He remained in the hospital for treatment for about 8 months. But the wife did not care to visit him regularly despite the information of his condition. The court held that wife is guilty of cruelty.

6. **Knowingly communicating the venereal disease**
7. **Drunkenness and use of drugs** - If spouse is violently behaving after drinking then it is injurious to health of other spouse and amounts to cruelty
8. **Refusal to speak** may cause worry and anxiety to other spouse
9. **Forcing association/intercourse** with a stranger or improper person
10. **False charge of immorality** against the wife including false complaints of theft, writing letters to superiors containing baseless allegations etc.
11. **Ill-treatment of children** in presence of the spouse
12. **Denies wife the fair opportunity** of having a child or wife is refusing husband's desire for having children
13. **Tearing of Mangalsutra**, beating a child mercilessly, switching on the light at night and sitting by the side of spouse also amounts to cruelty

> **ðŸ“Œ Suman Kapur v. Sudhir Kapur (AIR 2009 SC 589)**
>
> In this case the court observed that, mens rea is not necessary element in cruelty. So, divorce cannot be denied to party on the ground that there was no wilful ill treatment.

> **ðŸ“Œ Ashoka Kumar v. Vijayalakshmi (AIR 1992 Del. 1082)**
>
> In this case wife alleged that her husband sprinkled kerosene oil on her and accordingly attempted to burn her. But it was false allegation. The court held that such false allegations amount to cruelty.

#### Condonation

It means **forgiveness of the matrimonial offence** and the restoration of the offending spouse to the same position as he or she occupied before the offence was committed.

**Condonation = Forgiveness + Restoration**

### 3. Desertion

- Desertion is the act of forsaking or abandoning or the act of quitting with an intention not to return
- Desertion for a continuous period of **not less than 2 years** is now good ground for divorce

#### Essential Conditions for Desertion

1. **Separation** - The spouse must have parted or terminated joint living. It also includes separation without desertion and desertion without separation
2. **Intention** - The deserting spouse must have the intention to desert the other spouse
3. **Consent** - The deserted spouse must not have agreed or consented to the separation
4. **Without cause** - The desertion must have been without reasonable cause

> **ðŸ“Œ Smt. Santosh Devi v. Prem Chand (AIR 2007 Raj. 121)**
>
> In this case husband was pressurised by wife to live separate from his parents. And then she left her matrimonial home and went to her parental home. The court held that the wife is guilty of desertion.

#### Constructive Desertion

If one spouse by words and conduct compels the other to quit the matrimonial home the former will be guilty of desertion. Though the later has physically separated from the other and left the matrimonial home. Constructive desertion in the expression used to show that the spouse who forces the other to leave him or her is guilty of desertion and not the other party.

> **ðŸ“Œ Smt. Sushma v. Niranjan Singh (AIR 1983 Del. 449)**
>
> The wife had gone to her parental home with the consent of her husband and later the husband imputed against her that she had illicit relations with her sister's husband. Under the conditions of such imputation, the wife showed no willingness to return to her husband nor the husband took any interest to keep her with him. The court held the husband guilty of constructive desertion.

### 4. Conversion

- If spouse ceases to be a Hindu by change of religion, the petition for divorce can be granted on this ground
- For Hindu, conversion to another religion means **non-Hindu religion** such as Christianity, Mohammedanism
- Conversion to **Buddhist, Jain or Sikh is not conversion** to another religion because the Act deems Buddhist, Jains and Sikhs to be Hindus

> **ðŸ“Œ Chandra Shekhar v. Kuldan Velu (1963 SC 185)**
>
> Merely ceasing to be a Hindu would not amount to change of religion. It is only when he/she embraces other religion after renunciation of Hinduism that decree of divorce is obtainable on that ground.

---

### 5. Unsoundness of Mind

- An **incurable unsoundness of mind** in either party to the marriage constitutes a ground for divorce
- One of the essential conditions of a valid marriage under section 5(2) is that parties to marriage must not suffer from unsoundness of mind at the time of marriage. Otherwise such marriage can be declared as void

#### Requirements for Valid Marriage

It is necessary for valid marriage that at the time of marriage neither party be:

- Incapable of giving a valid consent
- Though capable of giving a valid consent, has been suffering from mental disorder of such a kind or to such an extent as to be unfit for marriage and the procreation of children
- Has been subject to recurrent attacks of insanity

- Unsoundness of mind must be **incurable or continuous or intermittent** and it is not possible for petitioner to live reasonably with such person
- **Mental disorder** means mental illness, arrested or incomplete development of mind, psychopathic disorder or any other disorder or disability of mind and includes schizophrenia

> **ðŸ“Œ Smt. Alka v. Abhinesh (AIR 1991 MP 205)**
>
> It was found that the wife was so cold and frigid and nervous on first night of marriage as not to be able to co-operate in sexual act. She was found unable to handle domestic appliances. She failed to explain the conduct of urinating in presence of all family members. It was held by the court that in such circumstances wife must be held to be suffering from schizophrenia, husband is entitled for nullity of marriage.

---

### 6. Leprosy (Omitted)

- **Omitted by Personal Laws (Amendment) Act, 2019**
- Where either spouse is a victim of serious leprosy in its incurable and virulent form, a decree of divorce will be passed in favour of petitioner
- Virulent means that the disease is considered to be extremely poisonous

> **ðŸ“Œ Swarajya Laxmi v. Dr. G. G. Padma Rao (AIR 1974 SC 165)**
>
> In this case court held that if either spouse is suffering from virulent and incurable form of leprosy, then the other spouse is entitled to a decree of divorce.

- No person can be forced to undergo a medical examination but if he/she refuses to do so, it raises a presumption against that person

---

### 7. Venereal Disease

- Where either spouse has been suffering from **venereal disease in a communicable form**, a decree of divorce will be granted in favour of other spouse
- The venereal diseases are only such diseases which are communicated by sexual intercourse
- No person can be forced to undergo a medical examination but if he/she refuses to do so, then the court may draw inference against that person

---

### 8. Renunciation of the World

- A person who wants to renounce the world by becoming a Sanyasi, can be held to have entered that order only if he has performed the necessary rites and ceremonies prescribed by the Shastras
- Only if a person wearing coloured clothes or shaving head cannot make him Sanyasi
- The renunciation implies a religious order which operates as a **civil death**
- If a person does not follow such religious ceremonies to become Sanyasi, then he will be held guilty of desertion or neglect

---

### 9. Presumptive Death

- If either spouse has not been heard of as being alive for a period of **7 years or more** then it can be presumed by law that such person is dead
- Such presumption can be drawn because if he/she were alive, then they would probably have communicated with some of his friends and relatives
- However, if the respondent returns after passing of the decree, the marital relationship cannot be restored

---

### 10. Non-Resumption of Cohabitation After the Decree of Judicial Separation

- After the passing of the decree for judicial separation if there has been **no resumption of cohabitation** between the parties for a period of **1 year or upwards** then it can be ground for divorce

> **ðŸ“Œ Bimla Devi v. Singh Raj (1976 RLR 480)**
>
> The court held that the question is not that who is at the fault? But the question is, have the parties been able to come together after the decree was passed, if they have not been able to come together, then they can seek divorce.

---

### 11. Failure to Comply with the Decree of Restitution of Conjugal Rights

- Either party to marriage would be entitled to a decree of divorce also when a decree for restitution of conjugal rights has been passed and it has **not been complied with within 1 year** of passing of such decree

> **ðŸ“Œ A.V. Janardhana Rao v. Aruna Kumari (AIR 2000 AP 127)**
>
> After passing of a decree for restitution of conjugal rights, both parties were not willing to reach to a compromise for leading marital life jointly. There was non-cohabitation for a period of 1 year. Husband filed a suit for divorce on such ground and succeeded in getting decree for divorce.

## Additional Grounds of Divorce in Favour of Wife - Section 13(2)

This section provides certain grounds of divorce available **only to wife** such as:

### 1. Husband's Bigamy - Clause (i)

- If husband perform second marriage without dissolution of his first marriage neither by way of divorce nor by way of death of first wife, then in that case husband is guilty of bigamy
- Either of the wives can move to the court for divorce subject to only one condition that the other wife was alive at the time of presentation of suit

> **ðŸ“Œ Smt. Nirmu v. Nikkar (AIR 1968 Del. 260)**
>
> In this case it was held that even if the first wife has given the consent for second marriage of her husband, still her right to file suit for divorce cannot be affected by such consent.

---

### 2. Rape, Sodomy or Bestiality - Clause (ii)

- After the solemnisation of marriage if husband is guilty of **rape, sodomy or bestiality** then wife can seek divorce on such ground
- If such offence is committed before marriage then that cannot be termed as ground for divorce
- **Rape** is defined under s.375 of IPC which means a forcible seizure and ravishment of a woman without her consent
- **Sodomy and bestiality** are provided under s.377 of IPC as unnatural offences

---

### 3. Non-Cohabitation After Passing of Maintenance Order

- This clause has been added by Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976
- Maintenance is awarded as per s.18 of Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act, 1956 or as per s.125 of Cr.P.C. to the wife
- After passing of decree of maintenance there is **no cohabitation** between parties for a period of **1 year or upwards**
- Then wife can file suit for divorce for abovementioned ground

---

### 4. Repudiation of Marriage

- This clause has been added by Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976
- A wife whose marriage was performed when she was below the age of **15 years** she can repudiate such marriage after attaining the age of 15 years but **not after the age of 18 years**

## Divorce by Mutual Consent - Section 13-B

- This provision is added by Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976
- This clause has **retrospective effect**

### Necessary Ingredients

1. Petition for divorce has to be presented to the district court by **both the parties jointly** along with affidavits
2. The parties should have been **living separately for a period of 1 year or more**
3. The parties **could not adjust with each other**
4. There should be **mutual request/consent** by both the parties is required
5. After the petition is presented, the parties have to wait for a minimum period of **6 months**, after which they have to move the court
6. If parties do not move to the court after 6 months and before 18 months then petition would lapse

> **ðŸ“Œ Anil Kumar Jain v. Maya Jain (AIR 2010 SC 229)**
>
> In this case it was held that appellate court is not bound to follow such statutory period. Because Article 142 of the Indian constitution provides that Supreme Court can pass orders to do complete justice. Hence, it is not bound by time limit.

> **ðŸ“Œ Sunvesha Devi v. Om Prakash (AIR 1992 SC 1904)**
>
> The court held that the party to petition at any time before passing of decree can withdraw its consent. After such withdrawal the court has no jurisdiction to try that matter.

> **ðŸ“Œ Yamanaji H. Jadhav v. Nirmala (AIR 2002 SC 971)**
>
> It was held that customary divorce on the ground of mutual consent is recognised only when law permits for it. Customary laws always form an exception to the general rule of law.

## Irretrievable Breakdown of Marriage - Section 13-C

- The Law commission of India recommended the insertion of a new Section 13-C in the Hindu Marriage Act, under which a petition for dissolution of marriage can be filed on the ground that marriage, has **broken down irretrievably**
- The Law commission of India in its **71st report, 1978** pointed out:

> "The theoretical basis and necessity for introducing irretrievable breakdown as a ground of divorce is that the marriage has all the external appearance of marriage, but none of the reality. The marriage is merely a 'shell out of which the substance is gone.' In such circumstances, there is hardly any utility in maintaining the marriage, when the emotional and other bonds, which are of essence of marriage, have disappeared."

> **ðŸ“Œ Chandra Kala Trivedi v. Dr. S.P. Trivedi ((1993) 4 SCC 232)**
>
> The court observed that, "If a marriage was dead and there was no chance of its being retrieved it was better to bring it to an end. In this case the marriage is dead both emotionally and practically. Continuance of marital alliance for name-sake is prolonging the agony and afflictions. Yet the marriage being dead, the continuance of it would be of no use."

## Petition for Divorce - Section 14

- After solemnisation of marriage the petition for divorce **cannot be filed till the expiration of period of 1 year**
- Before Marriage laws (Amendment) Act, 1976 such period was 3 years, but now it is 1 year
- In case of **'Exceptional hardship'** or **'Exceptional depravity'** petition for divorce can be file before expiration of 1 year
- **'Exceptional hardship'** includes uncommonness, extra-ordinary situations, life like complete hell, intolerable and commission of heinous moral offence etc.

### 'Exceptional Hardship' Explained

> **ðŸ“Œ Bowmen v. Bowmen ((1949) 2 All ER 127)**
>
> This is English case which provides general principles for better understanding of 'Exceptional hardship' as:
>
> - Adultery + Cruelty + Desertion
> - Adultery + Child by Adultery
> - Adultery with wife's sister or with servant
> - Cruelty + Aggravating circumstances like drunkenness & neglect etc.

## Remarriage - Section 15

Divorced person may marry again, if following conditions are satisfied:

1. When the marriage has been dissolved and there is **no right of appeal** against the decree of court
2. If there is a right of appeal but the **time has expired** without filing an appeal
3. An appeal has been filed but has been **dismissed**

> **ðŸ“Œ Kadimbini v. Roshan Lal (AIR 1990 MP 150)**
>
> The court has declared that the remarriage during the pendency of appeal against the decree of divorce would be invalid.

But then also some people have provided certain grounds for dissolution of marriage among them some are found nowadays also. In modern era the concept of divorce is well known to the society. Hindu marriage Act provides various grounds for divorce. After divorce the marital status of the parties come to an end.
