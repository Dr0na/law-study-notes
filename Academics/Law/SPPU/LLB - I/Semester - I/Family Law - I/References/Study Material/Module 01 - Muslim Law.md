## ‚öñÔ∏è Development and Nature of Muslim Law

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Understanding the historical development helps you explain how Muslim law evolved in India and why certain customs were abrogated. Questions often ask about the Shariat Act, 1937 and its impact.

> "To those not born and bred within the pale of Islam, the study of Muslim Law is usually attended with great difficulties."  
> ‚Äî **SYED AMEER ALI**

---

### üìú Development of Mohammedan Law in India

#### Historical Timeline

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| **Pre-British Era** | Mughal Emperors (Hanafis) administered Hanafi law throughout India |
| **British Rule** | Applied Mohammedan law as personal law to Muslims according to their school |
| **Modern Era** | Shariat Act, 1937 made Muslim personal law uniform across India |

#### Key Changes During British Rule

**Abolished:**
- ‚úÖ Banning of slavery
- ‚úÖ Forfeiture of rights on apostasy

**Altered:**
- ‚úÖ Customary laws modified to restore original Islamic law
- ‚úÖ Wakf Act, 1913 enacted

#### Current Status

**Uniform Application:**
- ‚úÖ Marriage, Divorce, Dower, Legitimacy
- ‚úÖ Guardianship, Wakf, Wills, Gift, Inheritance

> üß† **Remember:** Muslim Law in India = Shariat + English Common Law + Equity Principles

---

### üìã Application of Muslim Law

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions frequently test who Muslim law applies to and in which matters. Always distinguish between personal law matters and territorial laws.

#### Key Characteristics

- **Personal Law** (not territorial)
- Applies only to **Muslims** (by birth or conversion)
- Does NOT apply to all persons in a state/country

---

#### üë§ Who is a Muslim?

##### Definition

A person who **professes Islam** by acknowledging:
- There is no God but God (one God)
- Mohammed is His prophet

**Declaration:** "La ilahi ill lil lah Mohammad ur Rasul Allah"

##### Essential Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Profession of Faith** | Belief in unity of God and mission of Muhammad as prophet |
| **Sufficient Condition** | Profession alone is enough (no particular rites/ceremonies required) |
| **Status** | Born Muslim remains Muslim until unequivocal renunciation |

##### Important Points

- ‚úÖ Mere adoption of Hindu forms of worship ‚â† renunciation
- ‚úÖ Illegitimate son of Hindu by Muslim woman, raised as Hindu = may be regarded as Hindu

> üß† **Memory Aid:** Muslim = Profession of faith (Shahada) = "One God, One Prophet"

---

#### üë• To Whom Muslim Law Applies

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Muslim by Birth** | Born to Muslim parents |
| **Muslim by Conversion** | Converted to Islam from any other religion |

---

#### üó∫Ô∏è Scope of Application in India

##### Matters Where Muslim Law Applies

| Matter | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Inheritance** | Distribution of property after death |
| **Succession** | Rules of succession |
| **Wills** | Testamentary dispositions |
| **Gifts** | Hiba and other gifts |
| **Wakfs** | Religious and charitable endowments |
| **Marriage** | Nikah and its validity |
| **Dower** | Mahr and dower rights |
| **Divorce** | Talak, Khula, Mubarat, etc. |
| **Paternity** | Legitimacy and parentage |
| **Guardianship** | Custody and guardianship of minors |

##### Matters Where General Laws Apply

- ‚öñÔ∏è Law of Torts
- ‚öñÔ∏è Indian Penal Code
- ‚öñÔ∏è Indian Contract Act
- ‚öñÔ∏è Other territorial laws

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always mention that Muslim law applies only to personal law matters, not to criminal or commercial matters.

---

#### ‚öñÔ∏è Application by Courts

Courts apply Muslim law rules in three scenarios:

##### 1. Expressly Directed Rules

- Rules **expressly directed** to be applied to Muslims
- **Exception:** If abrogated by Indian legislature

**Example:**
- Rule excluding converts from inheritance ‚Üí **Abolished** by Freedom of Religion Act, 1850

##### 2. Rules Applied by Equity

- Rules **not expressly directed** but **not excluded** by legislature
- Applied as matter of **equity, justice, and good conscience**

##### 3. Rules Not Applied

- Rules **expressly abolished** by legislature
- Rules **excluded by implication**

**Examples of Non-Application:**

| Rule | Reason |
|------|--------|
| Pre-emption rules | Special Acts in UP and Punjab |
| Criminal law | Indian Penal Code applies |
| Evidence law | Indian Evidence Act applies |

> üß† **Remember:** Courts apply Muslim law only when not excluded by legislation.

---

#### üìç Special Provision: Bombay Regulation of 1827

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** This is a special provision for Maharashtra and Gujarat. Questions may ask about the hierarchy of laws in mofussil areas.

**Applicable to:** Mofussil areas of erstwhile Bombay State (now Maharashtra and Gujarat)

**Hierarchy of Laws (Section 26, Regulation IV of 1827):**

1. **Acts of Parliament and Regulations** applicable to the case
2. **Usage of the country** where suit arose
3. **Law of the defendant**
4. **Justice, equity and good conscience** (if none of above)

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Remember the order - Parliament ‚Üí Usage ‚Üí Defendant's Law ‚Üí Equity

### üìú The Shariat Application Act, 1937

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** This is one of the most important Acts. Questions frequently test Section 2 (mandatory application) and Section 3 (optional declaration). Know the matters covered and excluded.

#### Definition of Shariat

**Word Origin:** Derived from "Sharia"

**Meaning:** "Islamic Canonical law based on:
- Teaching of the Quran
- Traditions of the Prophet (Hadith and Sunna)
- Prescribing both religious and secular duties
- Sometimes retributive penalties for law breaking"

#### Object of the Act

**Preamble:** To make provision for application of Muslim personal law "Shariat" to Muslims in India

**Date of Enforcement:** 7th October 1937

---

#### Section 2: Mandatory Application

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important:** This section is **coercive** (mandatory), not optional.

##### Matters Covered (Notwithstanding any custom/usage to the contrary)

| Matter | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Intestate Succession** | When person dies without will |
| **Special Property of Females** | Personal property inherited/obtained under contract, gift, or personal law |
| **Marriage** | Nikah and validity |
| **Dissolution of Marriage** | Talak, Zihar, Lian, Khula, Mubarat |
| **Maintenance** | Nafaqa and maintenance rights |
| **Dower** | Mahr and dower |
| **Guardianship** | Custody and guardianship |
| **Gift** | Hiba and other gifts |
| **Trust and Trust Properties** | Wakfs (except charities and religious endowments) |

##### Matters Excluded

- ‚ùå **Agricultural Land** (customary law may still apply)
- ‚ùå **Pre-emption** (old law untouched)
- ‚ùå **Charitable and Religious Institutions** (excluded from scope)

##### Key Points

- ‚úÖ **Abrogates custom** in favor of Shariat
- ‚úÖ Applies to **all cases where parties are Muslims**
- ‚úÖ Seeks to **revive** Ila and Zihar as modes of divorce

---

##### Important Case Laws

| Case | Principle |
|------|-----------|
| **Noor Jahan v. E Tiscenko**<br>AIR 1941 Cal 582 | Shariat Act applies only if **both parties are Muslims**. If only one party is Muslim, Act does not apply. |
| **Ashraf Ali v. Mohammed Ali**<br>AIR 1947 Bom 122 | Testamentary trusts and testamentary wakfs are covered by **Section 2**, not Section 3. |

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always check if both parties are Muslims before applying Shariat Act.

---

#### Section 3: Optional Declaration

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important:** This section is **persuasive** (optional), unlike Section 2 which is coercive.

##### Who Can Make Declaration

A person who satisfies:
- ‚úÖ He is a **Muslim**
- ‚úÖ He is **competent to contract** (Section 11, Indian Contract Act, 1872)
- ‚úÖ He is a **resident of India**

##### Effect of Declaration

After declaration filed before prescribed authority:
- ‚úÖ Provisions of Section 2 apply to declarant
- ‚úÖ Applies to **all minor children** and their **descendants**
- ‚úÖ **Additional matters** covered:
  - Adoption
  - Wills
  - Legacies

##### Key Differences: Section 2 vs Section 3

| Aspect | Section 2 | Section 3 |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Nature** | Coercive (mandatory) | Persuasive (optional) |
| **Purpose** | Abrogate customs | Provide option to abandon customs |
| **Effect on Customs** | Invalidates customs | Does not invalidate, only provides option |
| **Appeal** | Not applicable | Available if authority refuses |

> üß† **Memory Aid:** Section 2 = Must apply, Section 3 = May apply (if declared)

---

#### Section 4 and Section 6

| Section | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Section 4** | Empowers state governments to make rules to carry out the Act |
| **Section 6** | Repeals certain Acts to the extent they permit inconsistent customs |

### üîÑ Effect of Apostacy (Conversion) from Islam

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions often test the effect of conversion on marriage and succession. Remember: husband's conversion dissolves marriage, but wife's conversion has different rules.

---

#### üìä Effect on Succession

##### Key Principle

**Freedom of Religion Act, 1850:**
- ‚úÖ Convert from Islam **does NOT lose** right of inheritance
- ‚ùå Hindu **cannot succeed** to estate of Muslim

##### Example Scenario

**Facts:**
- Hindu with Hindu wife and children
- Converts to Islam
- Marries Muslim wife
- Dies

**Result:**
- ‚úÖ Property passes to **Muslim wife**
- ‚ùå **NOT** to Hindu wife and children

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always mention Freedom of Religion Act, 1850 when discussing apostasy and succession.

---

#### üíî Effect on Marriage

##### Husband's Conversion

| Situation | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Muslim husband renounces Islam** | Marriage dissolved **ipso facto** (automatically) |

##### Wife's Conversion

| Situation | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| **Muslim wife (born Muslim) converts to Christianity** | Marriage **NOT dissolved** by mere conversion |
| **Wife converted to Islam from other faith, then re-embraces former faith** | Marriage **dissolved** |

> üß† **Memory Aid:** Husband converts = marriage ends. Wife converts = depends on whether she was born Muslim or converted.

---

### üîÑ Effect of Conversion to Islam

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions test whether conversion allows polygamy, changes succession rules, etc. Always check if conversion is bonafide or colorable.

#### General Principle

On conversion to Islam:
- ‚úÖ Convert **renounces** former religion and personal law
- ‚úÖ **Substitutes** Muslim religion and personal law
- ‚úÖ Applies to **all converts**, regardless of previous religion

---

#### Effects on Different Matters

| Matter | Effect of Conversion |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Marriage** | Indian Christian can contract **second marriage** with Muslim woman while Christian marriage subsists |
| **Divorce** | Muslim married Christian woman in Christian form. Wife converts to Islam. Husband divorces by Talak = **Valid** |
| **Succession** | Property of convert devolves according to **Muslim law** (not Indian Succession Act) |
| **Property** | Hindu convert's property devolves according to **Muslim law** |

---

#### ‚ö†Ô∏è Important Condition

**Conversion must be BONAFIDE:**
- ‚úÖ Genuine conversion
- ‚ùå **NOT** colorable (sole purpose of evading personal law)

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always check if conversion is bonafide. Colorable conversions are invalid.

## üèõÔ∏è Schools of Muslim Law

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions frequently test differences between Sunni and Shia schools, and among sub-schools. Know which school applies in India and presumptions.

### Historical Background

#### Origin of Division

**Event:** Prophet Muhammad died in 632 A.D. without male issue

**Dispute:** Succession of Imamate (spiritual and temporal headship)

| Group | Belief |
|------|--------|
| **Sunnis** | Principle of **election** in choosing Imam |
| **Shias** | Prophet had **appointed Ali** as successor |

**Nature of Division:**
- Initially **political and dynastic**
- Doctrinal and legal differences grew over time

---

#### Fundamental Differences

| Aspect | Sunnis | Shias |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Basis** | Entirety of traditions | Only traditions from Ali or his descendants |
| **Authority** | Decisions of successive Imams and jurists | Reject decisions of jurists |
| **Traditions** | All traditions accepted | Only traditions from those who saw Prophet |

> üß† **Memory Aid:** Sunnis = Election + All traditions, Shias = Appointment + Selective traditions

---

### ‚ò™Ô∏è Sunni Schools

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Hanafi is most important in India. Questions test which school applies when not specified. Know key differences, especially Maliki's shorter waiting period for divorce.

#### Overview

Sunni School has **four sub-schools:**

| School | Founder | Period (A.H.) |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Hanafi** | Abu Hanafee | 80-150 |
| **Maliki** | Malik-bin-Anas | 95-175 |
| **Shaffie** | Muhammad bin Idris Shaffie | 150-204 |
| **Hanbali** | Ahmad bin Hanbal | 164-241 |

---

#### üìñ Hanafi School

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Most Important in India**

##### Key Facts

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Original Name** | Koofa School (named after city of Koofa, Iraq) |
| **Founder** | Abu Hanafee |
| **Basis** | Customs and decisions of Muslim community |
| **Method** | Codified prevalent precedents |
| **Authoritative Book** | **Hedaya** (by Ali bin Abu Baker al Marghinani, created over 13 years) |
| **Coverage** | All aspects except inheritance law |

##### Historical Significance

- Lord Warren Hastings tried to translate Hedaya to English
- Appointed Muslim scholars for translation

##### Geographic Spread

**Countries with Hanafi majority:**
- ‚úÖ India
- ‚úÖ Pakistan
- ‚úÖ Syria
- ‚úÖ Turkey

##### Application in India

- ‚úÖ **Majority of Muslims** in India are Hanafi
- ‚úÖ Courts decide Sunni Muslim cases as per **Hanafi School** unless specified otherwise

> üìå **Exam Tip:** When question mentions "Sunni Muslim" without specifying school, assume Hanafi.

---

#### üìñ Maliki School

##### Key Facts

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Founder** | Malik-bin-Anas (Mufti of Madeena) |
| **Period** | During Khaleefa period (when Imam Abu Haneefa flourished) |
| **Traditions** | Discovered 8000, compiled 2000 |
| **Followers in India** | None (but laws used in legislation) |

##### Important Contribution

**Dissolution of Muslim Marriage Act, 1939:**
- ‚úÖ Adopted some Maliki provisions
- ‚úÖ Gives **more rights to women**

##### Key Difference: Waiting Period for Divorce

| School | Waiting Period (if husband missing) |
|--------|-------------------------------------|
| **Hanafi** | 7 years |
| **Maliki** | **2 years** (more favorable to women) |

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Remember Maliki's shorter waiting period - it's often tested.

---

#### üìñ Shaffie School

##### Key Facts

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Founder** | Muhammad bin Idris Shaffie (767-820 AD) |
| **Background** | Student of Imam Malik of Madeena, then worked with disciples of Imam Abu Haneefa |
| **Achievement** | Concluded ideas of Hanafi and Maliki schools |
| **Reputation** | Considered one of greatest jurists of Islam |
| **Contribution** | Created classical theory of Shaffie Islamic Jurisprudence |

##### Key Principles

- ‚úÖ **Ijma'a** considered important source
- ‚úÖ Provides validity to customs of Islam
- ‚úÖ **Main contribution:** Quiyas (Analogy)

##### Geographic Spread

- Egypt
- Southern Arabia
- South East Asia
- Indonesia
- Malaysia

---

#### üìñ Hanbali School

##### Key Facts

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Founder** | Ahmad bin Hanbal |
| **Recognition** | Imam Shafie declared him as best jurist after himself |
| **Geographic Spread** | Syria, Palestine, Saudi Arabia |

---

#### Presumption for Sunni Schools

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important Legal Presumption**

**Rule:** Unless contrary is shown, parties are presumed to be **Sunnis of Hanafi School**

**Case Law:**
- **Baftun v. Bilaiti Khanun** (1903 30 Cal. 683)

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always mention this presumption when school is not specified.

### ‚öñÔ∏è Applicability of Law of Different Schools

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions test which school's law applies when parties belong to different schools. Know the rules clearly.

#### Rules of Application

| Situation | Law Applicable |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Parties of same school** | Law of that school |
| **Parties of different schools** | **Law of defendant** |
| **Change of school** | Law changes with change of school |
| **Succession** | Law of school possessed **at time of death** |

---

#### Conclusion

> üß† **Philosophy of Schools**

Muslim law is governed by:
- ‚úÖ Teachings of Quran
- ‚úÖ Teachings of Prophet Muhammad

**When Quran is silent:**
- Different schools follow their own interpretations
- No school is "better" than others
- All paths lead to same destination

**Analogy:** Different paths leading to same destination

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always mention that schools differ only on points where Quran is silent, and all are equally valid.

## üìö Sources of Muslim Law

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions frequently test the hierarchy and nature of sources. Know which sources are primary, secondary, and modern. Understand Ijma'a and Quiyas in detail.

### Overview

Muslim Law has **seven sources:**

1. **Quran** (Primary)
2. **Sunna** (Primary)
3. **Ijma'a** (Secondary)
4. **Quiyas** (Secondary)
5. **Judicial Decisions** (Modern)
6. **Legislation** (Modern)
7. **Justice, Equity and Good Conscience** (Modern)

---

### üìñ Quran

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Primary Source - Most Authoritative**

#### Definition

**According to Muslim belief:**
- Sacred book revealed from **Allah to Prophet Muhammad**
- Period: **610 to 632 A.D.** (23 years)

---

#### Structure

| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Chapters** | 114 chapters (called **suras** in Arabic) |
| **Verses** | 6,236 verses (called **ayahs** in Arabic) |
| **Periods** | Two periods of revelation |

---

#### Periods of Revelation

| Period | Character | Content |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Meccan Period** | Theological character | Majority of suras with theological focus |
| **Medinan Period** | Legal, political, social | Predominantly legal, political, and social ayahs |

---

#### Legal Rulings in Quran

**Total Legal Verses:** 200 verses

**Classification:**

| Category | Number of Verses |
|----------|------------------|
| **Family and Inheritance Law** | 70 |
| **Obligations and Contracts** | 70 |
| **Criminal Law** | 30 |
| **Procedure** | 20 |

> üß† **Memory Aid:** 200 legal verses = 70+70+30+20

---

### üìñ Sunna (Traditions of the Prophet)

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Primary Source - Second Most Authoritative**

#### Definition

**Word Meaning:** "Path"

**Legal Meaning:**
- Practice and precedents of the Prophet
- Proper practical application of basic principles of Islam embodied in Quran

---

#### Relationship with Quran

- ‚úÖ **Quran and Sunna** are basic sources of Muslim law
- ‚úÖ Sunna explains and implements Quranic principles

---

#### Hadith

**Term:** Hadith means "occurring, taking place"

**Meaning:** "Report" of Prophet Muhammad's Sunna

**Transmission:**
- Handed over to Muslim community through **rightful disciples** of Prophet
- Memorization and transmission in literary form = **Hadith**

---

#### First Collection

**Book:** **Mu-atha of Imam Malik** (died AD 754)

**Details:**
- ‚úÖ Most authentic book of Hadiths
- ‚úÖ Collected about **10,000 traditions**
- ‚úÖ Omitted **8,000**
- ‚úÖ Final form contains about **2,000 traditions**

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Remember Imam Malik's Mu-atha as first and most authentic collection.

---

### ü§ù Ijma'a

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Ijma'a is frequently tested. Know its definition, authority, and that it cannot be repealed once established.

#### Definition

**Word Meaning:** "Consensus"

**Legal Meaning:**
- Unchallenged agreement of **all Jurists of Islam** of a particular period
- On a question of law

---

#### Authority

**Basis:**
1. **Hadith:** "My people will never agree on what is wrong"
2. **Quran:** "To be followed is the path of the men of faith"

**According to Imam Shafie:**
- Legal knowledge derived from Ijma'a
- Jurists took recourse to Ijma'a principle

---

#### First Instance

**Election of first Khaleefa Abu Bakr** = First instance of Ijma'a

---

#### Key Characteristics

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Nature** | Unchallenged agreement of all jurists |
| **Repeal** | **Cannot be repealed** once established |
| **Overruling** | **No question of overruling** Ijma'a |
| **Status** | Unchallenged source of law (through schools of law) |

> üß† **Memory Aid:** Ijma'a = Consensus = Cannot be changed = Permanent

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Always mention that Ijma'a cannot be repealed once established.

### üîç Quiyas

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Questions test whether Quiyas creates new law or applies existing law. Know it's a principle of interpretation, not a source.

#### Definition

**Arabic Word:** Quiyas means "Analogy"

**Legal Meaning:**
- Analogical deduction
- Argument from **known to unknown**
- Reasoning by analogy

---

#### Nature

| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Purpose** | Does NOT create new law |
| **Function** | Applies old established principles to new circumstances |
| **Character** | More a **principle of interpretation** than a source of law |

---

#### Other Principles of Interpretation

- **Ijithihad** (Personal reasoning)
- **Isthihsan** (Juristic preference)
- **Ikhthilaf** (Disagreement)

> üìå **Exam Tip:** Remember Quiyas does NOT create law, only applies existing principles.

### ‚öñÔ∏è Justice, Equity and Good Conscience

> üìå **Why this matters in exam:** Know when this principle applies and the important case law.
