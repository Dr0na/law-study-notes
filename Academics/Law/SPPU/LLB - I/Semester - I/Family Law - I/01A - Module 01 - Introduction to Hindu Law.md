### 1. Nature and Application of Hindu Law

**ðŸ“Œ Definition**
- Hindu Law = **Mixture of codified + uncodified law**
- Not passed by legislature (traditional view)
- Supposed to be of **divine origin** (Vedas)
- Branch of **Dharma** (duties and rules)

**ðŸ“¦ Codified Law (4 Main Acts)**
1. Hindu Marriage Act, 1955
2. Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956
3. Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act, 1956
4. Hindu Succession Act, 1956

**âš–ï¸ Legal Principle**
- Codified law **overrides** all prior law (custom or otherwise)
- Unless **express saving** provided

**ðŸ‘¥ Application (Section 2, HMA 1955)**

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hindu by Religion** | Hindus, Jains, Sikhs, Buddhists (including converts/reconverts) |
| **Hindu by Birth** | Child of Hindu parents OR one Hindu parent + brought up as Hindu |
| **Not Muslim/Christian/Parsi/Jew** | Presumed Hindu unless proved otherwise |

**ðŸš« Exception (Section 2(2))**
- Scheduled Tribes **excluded** unless Central Government directs otherwise

**ðŸ“œ Key Cases**
- **Peerumal v Poonuswami (1971)**: Conversion requires community acceptance
- **Mohandas vs. Dewaswan Board (1975)**: Mere declaration + actions sufficient

**ðŸ“œ Sruti**
- **Meaning**: "What is heard"
- **Contents**: 4 Vedas (Rig, Yajur, Sam, Atharva) + Brahmanas
- **Authority**: Ultimate source (divine revelation)

**ðŸ“ Smriti**
- **Meaning**: "That which was remembered"
- **Notable Writers**: Manu, Yajnavalkya, Narada, etc.
- **Three Categories**:
  - Achar (morality)
  - Vyavahar (procedural rules)
  - Prayaschit (penal provisions)

**Case Law:**
- **Shyam Sunder Prasad Singh v. State of Bihar (1980)**: Custom/usage can override Smriti when there is divergence

**ðŸ“š Digests and Commentaries**
- **Mitakshara**: Commentary on Yajnavalka smriti (Vijnaneshwar)
- **Dayabhag**: Digest by Jimutvahan
- **Result**: Formation of schools of law

**âš–ï¸ Custom**
- **Kinds**: Local, Class/Caste, Family, Business/Trade
- **Essentials (ACRON)**:
  - **A**ncient
  - **C**ertain
  - **R**easonable
  - **O**ngoing (continuous)
  - **N**ot against public policy/law

**ðŸ“œ Proof of Custom**
- Burden on person alleging
- Multiple instances required (one instance insufficient)
- Repeated judicial recognition = no further proof needed

**Case Laws:**
- **Prakash vs Parmeshwari**: One instance does not prove a custom
- **Ujagar vs Jeo**: Repeated judicial recognition - no further proof needed

**ðŸ›ï¸ Modern Sources**

1. **Justice, Equity and Good Conscience**
   - Applied when no rule available
   - **Case**: Kenchava V. Girimalappa (1924) - murderer disqualified from inheritance

2. **Precedent/Judicial Decisions**
   - Hierarchy: Supreme Court â†’ High Court
   - Binding force on lower courts

3. **Legislation**
   - Acts of Parliament
   - Codified law takes precedence

### 3. Schools of Hindu Law

**ðŸ“˜ Mitakshara School**
- **Author**: Vijnaneshwar (11th century)
- **Area**: All parts of India **except Bengal**
- **Type**: Orthodox school
- **Sub-schools**:
  1. Banaras School (Northern India)
  2. Mithila School (Bihar)
  3. Dravida/Madras School (Southern India)
  4. Maharashtra/Bombay School (Western India)

**ðŸ“— Dayabhaga School**
- **Author**: Jimutavahana (12th century)
- **Area**: Mainly **Bengal**
- **Type**: Reformist school
- **Sub-schools**: None
- **Note**: On matters where Dayabhaga silent, Mitakshara prevails

**ðŸ”„ Effect of Migration**
- **Presumption**: Family carries personal law from original state
- **Rebuttable**: Can be rebutted by showing adoption of new state's law
