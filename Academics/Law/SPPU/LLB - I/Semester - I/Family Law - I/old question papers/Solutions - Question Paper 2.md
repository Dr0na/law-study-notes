- Hindus by religion
- Hindus by birth
- Presumption for others
- Exception for Scheduled Tribes
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**

A Hindu is defined under **Section 2** of the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. The term includes persons who are Hindus by religion, Hindus by birth, or are presumed to be Hindus.

---

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**

- **Section 2(1)**: Definition of Hindu
- **Section 2(2)**: Exception for Scheduled Tribes

---

**Section 2(1): Three Categories of Hindus**

**1. Hindus by Religion (Section 2(1)(a))**
- Hindus, Jains, Sikhs, Buddhists
- Includes converts and reconverts to Hinduism
- **Case**: Peerumal v Poonuswami (1971) - conversion requires community acceptance

**2. Hindus by Birth (Section 2(1)(b))**
- Child of Hindu parents (both Hindu), OR
- Child of one Hindu parent + brought up as Hindu
- Brought up = follows Hindu customs, practices, way of life

**3. Presumption (Section 2(1)(c))**
- Not Muslim, Christian, Parsi, or Jew
- **Presumed Hindu** unless proved otherwise
- Burden of proof on person alleging non-Hindu status

**Section 2(2): Exception**
- Scheduled Tribes **excluded** unless Central Government directs otherwise
- Customary law applies to Scheduled Tribes

---

**Case Laws:**

- **Peerumal v Poonuswami (1971)**: Conversion to Hinduism requires community acceptance
- **Mohandas vs. Dewaswan Board (1975)**: Mere declaration + actions sufficient for conversion

---

**Conclusion:**

The definition of Hindu is broad and inclusive, covering not just Hindus by religion but also Jains, Sikhs, Buddhists, and persons presumed to be Hindus. The Act ensures that Hindu law applies to a wide range of persons while providing exception for Scheduled Tribes. The definition balances traditional concepts with modern legal requirements.

### Q1. (b) Is Hindu Law 'lex loci'?‚Äî Explain. (4 marks)

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Meaning of lex loci
- Hindu Law as personal law
- Not territorial law
- Application based on personal status
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**

**Lex Loci**: Law of the place/territory - applies to all persons within a territory regardless of personal status.

**Personal Law**: Applies based on person's religion, community, or personal status, not territory.

---

**Relevant Provisions:**

- Hindu Law is **personal law**, not lex loci
- Applies to Hindus wherever they are
- Not based on territory

---

Hindu Law is **NOT lex loci**. It is **personal law**.

**Reasons:**

1. **Applies Based on Personal Status**
   - Applies to Hindus by religion or birth
   - Not based on territory or place
   - Hindu in India = Hindu in foreign country (for personal matters)

2. **Not Territorial**
   - Does not apply to all persons in India
   - Only applies to Hindus
   - Non-Hindus in India not governed by Hindu Law

3. **Personal Matters**
   - Marriage, Divorce, Succession, Adoption
   - Governed by personal law
   - Not by law of territory

4. **Exception**
   - Scheduled Tribes excluded (customary law applies)
   - Shows it's personal, not territorial

**Comparison:**
- **Lex Loci**: Indian Penal Code (applies to all in India)
- **Personal Law**: Hindu Law (applies only to Hindus)

---

**Conclusion:**

Hindu Law is personal law, not lex loci. It applies based on personal status (being Hindu) rather than territory. A Hindu is governed by Hindu Law wherever they are, while a non-Hindu in India is not governed by Hindu Law. This distinguishes it from territorial laws that apply to all persons within a territory.

- Ongoing (continuous)

---

**Definition/Meaning:**

**Valid Custom**: Custom that has obtained force of law and is recognized by courts. Must fulfill five essential requisites.

---

**Relevant Provisions:**

- Custom must be proved if not judicially recognized
- Must fulfill essential requisites
- Burden of proof on person alleging

---

**Five Essential Requisites (ACRON):**

**1. Ancient (A)**
- Must be in existence from time immemorial
- **Time immemorial**: Beyond memory of man
- Generally: Must exist for long period (decades/centuries)
- Not required to prove exact origin
- Must be established usage

**2. Certain (C)**
- Must be definite and certain
- Not vague or ambiguous
- Clear and specific
- Can be precisely stated
- Must be capable of being proved

**3. Reasonable (R)**
- Must be reasonable
- Not arbitrary or capricious
- Must serve some purpose
- Should not be oppressive
- Courts can examine reasonableness

**4. Ongoing/Continuous (O)**
- Must be continuously observed
- Not abandoned or discontinued
- Must be in practice
- Recent origin = not valid custom
- Must be current usage

**5. Not Against Public Policy/Law (N)**
- Must not be against public policy
- Must not violate any law
- Must not be immoral
- Must not be opposed to justice
- Custom against law = void

**Proof Requirements:**
- Burden on person alleging custom
- Multiple instances required (one instance insufficient)
- Repeated judicial recognition = no further proof needed
- Must be proved by evidence

---

**Case Laws:**

- **Prakash vs Parmeshwari**: One instance does not prove a custom. Multiple instances required
- **Ujagar vs Jeo**: Repeated judicial recognition - no further proof needed once judicially recognized

---

**Conclusion:**

All five requisites (ACRON) must be fulfilled for a custom to be valid. The requisites ensure that only genuine, established customs are recognized. The requirement of proof and judicial recognition maintains legal certainty while allowing flexibility for local practices. The framework effectively balances tradition with legal requirements.

**1. Local Custom**

- Applies to **particular family**

- Applies to **particular trade or business**

- Scope: Profession-based

- Family custom > Class custom > Local custom
- More specific custom overrides general custom

**Conclusion:**

Customs are classified into four kinds based on their scope: local, class/caste, family, and business/trade. Each kind has different application and binds different groups. Family custom is most specific and overrides others. This classification helps determine which custom applies in specific situations.

### Q4. (b) What is the difference between void and voidable marriages under Hindu Law? (8 marks)

- Key differences

- Legal consequences
- Comparison table
- Case laws

---

**Definition/Meaning:**

**Void Marriage (Section 11)**: Marriage that is null and void from beginning. No marriage exists.

**Voidable Marriage (Section 12)**: Marriage that is valid until avoided by aggrieved party. Remains valid if not avoided.

---

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**

- **Section 11**: Void marriages
- **Section 12**: Voidable marriages
- **Section 16**: Legitimacy of children

---

**Key Distinctions:**

| Aspect | Void Marriage (Section 11) | Voidable Marriage (Section 12) |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Nature** | No marriage from beginning | Valid until avoided |
| **Who can avoid** | Either party (or declaratory suit) | Only parties to marriage |
| **Effect if not avoided** | Always void | Remains valid |
| **After death** | Can be challenged | Cannot be challenged |
| **Legal consequences** | None | All consequences flow until avoided |
| **Retrospective effect** | Always void | Void from date of marriage if avoided |
| **Children** | Legitimate (Section 16) | Legitimate if begotten/conceived before decree |
| **Grounds** | Section 5(i), (iv), (v) | Section 12(1)(a), (b), (c), (d) |

**Grounds of Void Marriage (Section 11):**
1. Bigamy (Section 5(i))
2. Prohibited relationship (Section 5(iv))
3. Sapinda relationship (Section 5(v))

**Grounds of Voidable Marriage (Section 12):**
1. Impotency (Section 12(1)(a))
2. Mental capacity (Section 12(1)(b))
3. Consent by force/fraud (Section 12(1)(c)) - within 1 year
4. Pre-marriage pregnancy (Section 12(1)(d)) - within 1 year, no intercourse

**Legal Consequences:**
- **Void**: No rights, no obligations, no need for decree
- **Voidable**: All rights flow until avoided, needs court decree

---

**Case Laws:**

- **G.S. Raj v. Bandaru Pavani (2009)**: Bigamy makes marriage void
- Cases on voidable marriages: Impotency, mental capacity, consent issues

---

**Conclusion:**

The key distinction is that void marriages are null from inception and can be challenged by anyone, while voidable marriages are valid until avoided by the aggrieved party. This affects rights, obligations, and legitimacy of children. The Act provides clear framework distinguishing between them, ensuring appropriate legal consequences.

### Q5. (a) What are the grounds for 'Divorce' under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955? (10 marks)

**Answer:**

**Synopsis:**
- Section 13(1): Nine grounds (both parties)
- Section 13(1-A): Two breakdown grounds
- Section 13(2): Four grounds (wife only)
- Section 13-B: Mutual consent
- Detailed explanation of each ground
- Case laws
- Conclusion

---

**Definition/Meaning:**

**Divorce**: Dissolution of marriage by decree of court. Available on specified grounds under Hindu Marriage Act, 1955.

---

**Relevant Provisions of the Act:**

- **Section 13(1)**: Grounds available to both husband and wife
- **Section 13(1-A)**: Breakdown theory grounds
- **Section 13(2)**: Grounds available only to wife
- **Section 13-B**: Divorce by mutual consent

---

**Section 13(1): Nine Grounds Available to Both Husband and Wife**

1. **Adultery (Section 13(1)(i))**
   - Voluntary sexual intercourse with person other than spouse
   - **Case**: Joseph Shine v. UOI (2018) - adultery not offence under IPC, but still ground for divorce

2. **Cruelty (Section 13(1)(ia))**
   - Treated with cruelty after marriage
   - Can be physical or mental
   - Courts interpret liberally

3. **Desertion (Section 13(1)(ib))**
   - Deserted for continuous period of not less than 2 years
   - Must be immediately preceding presentation of petition

4. **Conversion (Section 13(1)(ii))**
   - Ceased to be Hindu by conversion to another religion

5. **Mental Disorder (Section 13(1)(iii))**
   - Incurably of unsound mind OR
   - Mental disorder of such kind/extent that petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with respondent
   - Includes: Schizophrenia, psychopathic disorder

6. **Leprosy (Section 13(1)(iv))**
   - Virulent and incurable form

7. **Venereal Disease (Section 13(1)(v))**
   - In communicable form

8. **Renunciation (Section 13(1)(vi))**
   - Renounced the world by entering religious order

9. **Not Heard Of (Section 13(1)(vii))**
   - Not been heard of as being alive for 7 years or more

**Section 13(1-A): Breakdown Theory Grounds**

1. **No Resumption After Judicial Separation (Section 13(1-A)(i))**
   - No resumption of cohabitation for 1 year or more
   - After decree of judicial separation

2. **No Restitution After Decree (Section 13(1-A)(ii))**
   - No restitution of conjugal rights for 1 year or more
   - After decree of restitution

**Section 13(2): Grounds Available Only to Wife**

1. **Bigamy (Section 13(2)(i))**
   - Husband has married again
   - Previous wife living

2. **Rape, Sodomy, Bestiality (Section 13(2)(ii))**
   - Husband guilty of rape, sodomy, or bestiality

3. **Decree or Order for Maintenance (Section 13(2)(iii))**
   - Decree or order for maintenance in favor of wife
   - Living separately
   - No cohabitation for 1 year or more

4. **Repudiation of Marriage (Section 13(2)(iv))**
   - Marriage solemnized before 15 years
   - Repudiated before 18 years
   - Not consummated

---

**Case Laws:**

- **Joseph Shine v. Union of India (2018)**: Adultery not offence under IPC, but still ground for divorce
- Cases on cruelty, desertion, mental disorder

---

**Conclusion:**

The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provides comprehensive grounds for divorce, balancing fault theory (Section 13(1) and 13(2)) with breakdown theory (Section 13(1-A)). The grounds ensure that parties have adequate remedies when marriage breaks down irretrievably, while maintaining the sanctity of marriage institution. The Act provides additional protection to wives through Section 13(2).

**Definition/Meaning:**

**Testamentary Succession**: Succession through **will** made by person before death. Person can dispose of property by will.

---

**Relevant Provisions:**

- **Section 30, Hindu Succession Act, 1956**: Testamentary succession
- **Indian Succession Act, 1925**: Governs wills

---

**Section 30: Testamentary Disposition**

- Hindu can dispose of property by **will**
- Freedom to bequeath property
- Can exclude legal heirs
- Subject to legal requirements

**Requirements for Valid Will:**
- Person of sound mind
- Not minor
- Free consent
- Proper execution
- Witnesses (as required)

**Legal Effect:**
- Will takes precedence over intestate succession
- Property disposed as per will
- Legal heirs get only if not disposed by will

**Limitations:**
- Cannot dispose of coparcenary property (before 2005 amendment)
- Must comply with legal requirements
- Subject to legal heirs' rights (in some cases)

---

**Conclusion:**

Testamentary succession allows Hindu to dispose of property by will, taking precedence over intestate succession. It provides freedom while ensuring compliance with legal requirements. The provision balances individual freedom with protection of legal heirs.
