### Q1. What is valid adoption. Discuss its essential and effects. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
Adoption is the process by which a person takes someone into custody and all legal rights of a legitimate biological child are transferred. The relationship is severed from biological parents, and the child becomes the lawful child of adoptive parents. The Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 codifies adoption law for Hindus.

**Definition:**
- Legal affiliation of a child
- Relationship severed from biological parents
- Child becomes lawful child of adoptive parents
- All rights similar to biological child

**Objects:**
- **Religious**: Offers an heir to fulfill funeral obligations
- **Secular**: To secure an heir and perpetuate the adopter's name

---

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Section 6: Four Essential Requisites (All Must Be Present):**

1. **Person adopting has capacity + right**
   - **Section 7: Male Hindu**
     - Sound mind
     - Not a minor
     - **Wife's consent** required (unless exceptions)
     - Exceptions: Wife renounced world, ceased to be Hindu, declared unsound
   - **Section 8: Female Hindu**
     - Sound mind
     - Not a minor
     - Marital status: Not married/divorced/widowed/husband absent

2. **Person giving has capacity**
   - **Section 9: Priority**
     - **Father** (if alive, exclusive right with mother's consent)
     - **Mother** (if father dead/renounced/ceased to be Hindu/declared unsound)
     - **Guardian** (if both parents absent, needs court permission)

3. **Person adopted is capable**
   - **Section 10: Four Conditions**
     - âœ… Must be a Hindu
     - âœ… Not already adopted
     - âœ… Not married (unless custom/usage permits)
     - âœ… Not completed 15 years (unless custom/usage permits)

4. **Compliance with other conditions**
   - **Section 11: Six Conditions**

**Section 11: Other Conditions:**

1. **No Existing Son/Daughter**
   - Adoptive father or mother must not have:
     - Hindu son, OR son's son, OR son's son's son (for son adoption)
     - Hindu daughter, OR son's daughter (for daughter adoption)
   - Living at the time of adoption
   - Whether by legitimate blood relationship or by adoption

2. **Age Difference**
   - If male adopting female: Adoptive father must be **at least 21 years older**
   - If female adopting male: Adoptive mother must be **at least 21 years older**

3. **Simultaneous Adoption Prohibited**
   - Same child may **not** be adopted simultaneously by two or more persons

4. **Actual Giving and Taking**
   - Child must be **actually given and taken** in adoption
   - By parents or guardian concerned, OR under their authority
   - **Intent**: To transfer child from family of birth to family of adoption
   - **Proviso**: Performance of datta homan is **not essential** to validity of adoption

---

**Section 12: Effects of Adoption**

**General Rule:**
- Adopted child shall be **deemed to be the child** of his/her adoptive father or mother
- **For all purposes**
- **With effect from the date of adoption**
- **From such date**:
  - All ties in family of birth â†’ **severed**
  - Ties in adoptive family â†’ **created**

**Three Provisos (Exceptions):**

1. **Proviso (a): Marriage Restrictions**
   - Child **cannot marry** any person whom he/she could **not have married** if continued in family of birth
   - Prohibited relationships from birth family continue to apply

2. **Proviso (b): Property Vested Before Adoption**
   - Any property which **vested in adopted child before adoption**:
     - **Continues to vest** in such person
     - Subject to obligations (if any) attaching to ownership
     - Including obligation to **maintain relatives** in family of birth

3. **Proviso (c): No Divesting of Estate**
   - Adopted child **shall not divest** any person of any estate which **vested in him/her before adoption**

**Other Effects:**

- **Section 13**: Adoption does not deprive adoptive parents of power to dispose of property
- **Section 15**: Valid adoption **cannot be cancelled** - it is irrevocable and permanent
- **Section 16**: Registered documents create presumption of valid adoption

---

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 provides a comprehensive framework ensuring that adoptions are valid, legal, and in the best interests of the child. All conditions must be strictly complied with, and once valid, adoption cannot be cancelled. The effects are far-reaching, severing ties with birth family while creating new ties with adoptive family, subject to certain exceptions for marriage restrictions and property rights.

### Q2. "A Hindu Marriage is a sacrament but a Muslim Marriage is essentially contract." Comment on this statement highlighting the essential of both marriages. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
The statement reflects the traditional understanding of marriage in Hindu and Muslim law. While Hindu marriage has been viewed as a sacrament, Muslim marriage is essentially a civil contract. However, modern legal developments show that Hindu marriage also has contractual elements, and both systems have evolved.

---

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**PART I: HINDU MARRIAGE - SACRAMENT AND CONTRACT**

**Hindu Marriage as Sacrament:**
- Last of **10 sacraments (Samskara)** in Hindu religion
- Performed to purify body from inherited taint
- **Religious necessity** - not just social contract
- **Indissoluble union** - "union of flesh with flesh and bone with bone"
- Wife = **Ardhangini** (half body of husband)
- Shares fruits of all acts (good or bad) - concept of spiritual unity
- **Case**: Purshottamadas v. Purshottamadas - recognized sacramental nature

**Hindu Marriage as Contract:**
- **Kanyadan** fulfills requirements of gift (as per Manu)
- Concept continues even after HMA 1955
- Parties enter into marriage by **consent**
- Can be **dissolved by mutual consent** (Section 13-B)
- Breakdown theory recognizes contractual nature
- **Case**: Bhagwati Saran Singh v. Parmeshwari - recognized both aspects

**âœ… Legal Position**: **BOTH sacrament AND contract**

**Essentials of Valid Hindu Marriage (Section 5, HMA 1955):**

1. **Both parties must be Hindus** (Section 5)
2. **Bigamy (Monogamy)** - Section 5(i): Neither party has spouse living
3. **Mental capacity** - Section 5(ii): Not incapable of valid consent
4. **Age** - Section 5(iii): Male 21, Female 18
5. **Prohibited relationship** - Section 5(iv): Not within prohibited degrees
6. **Sapinda relationship** - Section 5(v): Not sapindas (3 gen maternal, 5 gen paternal)
7. **Ceremonies** - Section 7: Customary rites (Saptapadi essential)
8. **Registration** - Section 8: Optional but recommended

**Additional Features:**
- **Void marriages** (Section 11): Bigamy, Prohibited, Sapinda
- **Voidable marriages** (Section 12): Impotency, Mental, Consent, Pregnancy

---

**PART II: MUSLIM MARRIAGE - ESSENTIALLY CONTRACT**

**Muslim Marriage as Contract:**
- **Not a sacrament** (unlike Hindu marriage)
- **Civil contract** between man and woman
- **Ibadat** (devotional act) - but still contractual
- **Sunnat** (practice of Prophet)
- **Wajib** (obligatory) for physically fit Muslims
- Can be **dissolved** by talaq (husband) or through court (wife)
- **Polygamy allowed** (up to 4 wives) - contractual right

**Essentials of Valid Muslim Marriage (Nikah):**

1. **Proposal and Acceptance (Ijab and Qubul)**
   - Both at **one meeting** (essential)
   - Two male OR one male + two female witnesses
   - Witnesses must be sane, adult Muslims
   - Usual form: "I have married myself to you" / "I have consented"

2. **Capacity to Contract**
   - Sound mind + Puberty
   - **Puberty = Majority** (for marriage matters)
   - Indian Majority Act, 1875: Does NOT apply to marriage, dower, divorce
   - Hanafi: Presumed at 15 years
   - **Case**: Shafin Jahan v. Asokan K.M. (2018) - 16-year-old Muslim girl's marriage held valid

3. **Guardianship (Jabar)**
   - **Sunni Order**: Father â†’ Paternal Grandfather â†’ Brother â†’ Mother â†’ Others
   - **Shia**: Only Father + Paternal Grandfather
   - **Option of Puberty**: Minor can ratify/repudiate on attaining puberty

4. **Absence of Impediments**
   - **Absolute (Batil - Void)**: Polyandry, Consanguinity, Affinity, Fosterage
   - **Relative (Fasid - Invalid)**: 5th wife, No witnesses, Religion, Conjunction, Iddat

5. **Formalities**
   - No prescribed ceremonies
   - Customary practices (not essential): Nikah by Kazi, Exchange of gifts, Prayers
   - Registration NOT compulsory

**Key Differences from Hindu Marriage:**
- No mandatory ceremonies (unlike Saptapadi in Hindu law)
- Polygamy allowed (Hindu law prohibits)
- Easier dissolution (talaq vs court decree)
- Dower (mahr) is essential consideration

---

**Comparison Table:**

| Aspect | Hindu Marriage | Muslim Marriage |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| **Nature** | Sacrament + Contract | Civil Contract + Ibadat |
| **Ceremonies** | Mandatory (Saptapadi) | Not essential |
| **Registration** | Optional (recommended) | Not compulsory |
| **Polygamy** | Prohibited | Allowed (4 wives) |
| **Divorce** | By court decree | Talaq (husband's right) |
| **Witnesses** | Not required | Required (2 male or 1 male + 2 female) |
| **Dissolution** | Mutual consent or fault grounds | Talaq, Khula, Mubarat, Court decree |

---

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The statement is largely accurate but requires qualification. Hindu marriage is indeed sacramental in nature, but modern law recognizes contractual elements, especially in dissolution. Muslim marriage is essentially a contract, though it has religious significance as Ibadat. Both systems have evolved, with Hindu law incorporating contractual elements and Muslim law maintaining its contractual foundation while recognizing religious aspects. The essentials reflect these differences - Hindu law emphasizes ceremonies and sacramental nature, while Muslim law emphasizes contractual elements and formalities.

### Q3. What are the essentials of valid marriage under Christian Law? Discuss the grounds of divorce under Christian Law. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
The Indian Christian Marriage Act, 1872 governs marriages among Indian Christians. The Indian Divorce Act, 1869 provides for dissolution of Christian marriages. Both Acts work together to regulate Christian matrimonial law in India.

---

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**PART I: ESSENTIALS OF VALID MARRIAGE (Section 60, Indian Christian Marriage Act, 1872)**

| Condition | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Parties** | Indian Christians |
| **Age** | Male: 21, Female: 18 |
| **Bigamy** | Neither party has spouse living |
| **Mental Capacity** | Sound mind |
| **Prohibited Relationship** | Not within prohibited degrees |
| **Solemnization** | Licensed person + 2 credible witnesses |

**Procedural Requirements:**

1. **Section 5: Notice of Intended Marriage**
   - Parties give notice to Marriage Officer
   - At least one party must have resided in district for **30 days**

2. **Section 6: Marriage Notice Book and Publication**
   - Marriage Officer maintains notice book
   - Affixes copy to conspicuous place
   - Transmits to other district if party not permanently residing

3. **Section 7: Objection to Marriage**
   - Any person can object
   - Within **30 days** from date of publication
   - Grounds: Would contravene Section 4 conditions

4. **Section 8: Procedure on Receipt of Objection**
   - Marriage Officer makes inquiry (max 30 days)
   - Cannot solemnize until inquiry completed
   - Appeal to District Court (30 days)

5. **Section 11: Declaration by Parties and Witnesses**
   - Parties + **3 witnesses** sign declaration
   - In presence of Marriage Officer
   - Form in Third Schedule

6. **Section 12: Place and Form of Solemnization**
   - Office of Marriage Officer OR other place
   - Each party says: "I (A) take thee (B), to be my lawful wife (or husband)"

7. **Section 13: Certificate of Marriage**
   - Entered in Marriage Certificate Book
   - **Conclusive evidence** of marriage

8. **Section 14: New Notice When Marriage Not Solemnized Within Three Months**
   - Notice lapses if not solemnized within 3 months
   - New notice required

**Registration of Existing Marriages (Section 15):**
- Conditions: Ceremony performed + parties living together, No bigamy, Sound mind, Both parties completed **21 years** (not 18 for female), Not within prohibited degrees, Residence 30 days

---

**PART II: GROUNDS OF DIVORCE (Indian Divorce Act, 1869)**

**Section 10: Grounds Available to Both Husband and Wife:**

1. **Adultery**
   - Voluntary sexual intercourse with person other than spouse
   - After solemnization of marriage

2. **Desertion**
   - Deserted for continuous period of not less than 2 years
   - Immediately preceding presentation of petition

3. **Conversion**
   - Ceased to be Christian by conversion to another religion

4. **Insanity**
   - Incurably of unsound mind OR
   - Mental disorder of such kind/extent that petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with respondent
   - For continuous period of not less than 2 years immediately preceding presentation of petition

5. **Leprosy**
   - Virulent and incurable form
   - For period of not less than 1 year immediately preceding presentation of petition

6. **Venereal Disease**
   - In communicable form
   - For period of not less than 1 year immediately preceding presentation of petition

7. **Not Heard Of**
   - Not been heard of as being alive for 7 years or more

8. **Renunciation**
   - Renounced the world by entering religious order

9. **Cruelty**
   - Treated with cruelty after solemnization of marriage

**Section 10(1): Grounds Available Only to Wife:**

1. **Bigamy**
   - Husband has married again
   - Previous wife or husband living

2. **Rape, Sodomy, Bestiality**
   - Husband guilty of rape, sodomy, or bestiality
   - After solemnization of marriage

3. **Adultery + Cruelty or Desertion**
   - Husband guilty of adultery
   - Combined with cruelty or desertion

4. **Adultery + Bigamy**
   - Husband guilty of adultery
   - Combined with bigamy

**Section 10A: Divorce by Mutual Consent**
- Both parties consent to dissolution
- Living separately for 1 year or more
- Unable to live together
- Two-stage process: First motion (6 months), Second motion (18 months)

**Section 10B: Irretrievable Breakdown**
- Living separately for 1 year or more
- Both parties consent to dissolution
- Court satisfied that marriage has broken down irretrievably

---

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Indian Christian Marriage Act, 1872 provides comprehensive essentials for valid marriage, emphasizing procedural requirements and safeguards. The Indian Divorce Act, 1869 provides multiple grounds for divorce, with some grounds available to both parties and some exclusively to wives. The Act has been amended to include mutual consent and irretrievable breakdown, reflecting modern developments in matrimonial law.

### Q5. Discuss in detail law relating to 'Dower' under Muslim Law. (10 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (1 mark)**
Dower (Mahr) is a sum of money or other property which the wife is entitled to receive from the husband in consideration of marriage. It is an essential feature of Muslim marriage and represents the husband's obligation to the wife.

---

**Main Body (8 marks)**

**Definition and Nature:**
- **Mahr** or **Dower** = Consideration for marriage
- **Not a price** for wife, but **obligation** of husband
- **Essential** feature of Muslim marriage
- Can be **specified** (at time of marriage) or **unspecified** (customary)

**Types of Dower:**

1. **Specified Dower (Mahr-i-Musamma)**
   - Amount fixed at time of marriage
   - Can be fixed by:
     - Parties themselves
     - Guardians (for minor)
     - Arbitrators (if dispute)
   - Can be **prompt** (payable immediately) or **deferred** (payable on demand or on dissolution)

2. **Unspecified Dower (Mahr-i-Misl)**
   - Amount not fixed at time of marriage
   - Determined by:
     - Dower of female relatives (sisters, paternal aunts, maternal aunts)
     - Social status of wife
     - Age, beauty, education, family background
     - Custom and usage

**Amount of Dower:**
- **No maximum limit** - can be any amount
- **Minimum**: No fixed minimum, but should be reasonable
- **Case**: Hamid Ali v. Imtiazan (1932) - excessive dower can be reduced by court

**When Dower Becomes Payable:**

1. **Prompt Dower (Mahr-i-Muajjal)**
   - Payable **immediately** on demand
   - Wife can **refuse cohabitation** until prompt dower is paid
   - **Exception**: If marriage consummated, wife cannot refuse cohabitation

2. **Deferred Dower (Mahr-i-Muwajjal)**
   - Payable on:
     - **Dissolution of marriage** (divorce or death)
     - **Demand by wife** (if specified)
     - **Fixed time** (if specified)
   - Wife **cannot refuse cohabitation** for deferred dower

**Rights of Wife:**

1. **Right to Retain Possession**
   - Wife can **retain husband's property** until dower is paid
   - Known as **right of retention** (Haq-i-Mahr)
   - Can retain even after husband's death
   - **Priority**: Over other creditors (except government dues)

2. **Right to Sue**
   - Can sue for recovery of dower
   - **Limitation**: 3 years from date of demand or dissolution
   - Can claim from husband's estate after his death

3. **Right to Refuse Cohabitation**
   - Only for **prompt dower** (before consummation)
   - **Cannot refuse** after consummation
   - **Cannot refuse** for deferred dower

**Remission of Dower:**
- Wife can **remit** (waive) dower
- Must be **voluntary** and **with free consent**
- **Case**: Anis Begum v. Muhammad Istafa (1933) - remission must be voluntary

**Dower on Dissolution:**

1. **On Divorce**
   - **Prompt dower**: Payable immediately
   - **Deferred dower**: Payable on divorce
   - **Half dower**: If divorce before consummation (unless specified otherwise)

2. **On Death**
   - **Full dower** payable from husband's estate
   - Wife has **priority** over other creditors
   - Can claim from property in her possession

**Important Cases:**
- **Hamid Ali v. Imtiazan (1932)**: Excessive dower can be reduced
- **Anis Begum v. Muhammad Istafa (1933)**: Remission must be voluntary
- **Abdul Kadir v. Salima (1886)**: Wife can refuse cohabitation for prompt dower

---

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
Dower is a fundamental right of Muslim wife, representing husband's obligation and wife's security. It can be specified or unspecified, prompt or deferred, and gives wife important rights including retention of property and right to refuse cohabitation (in certain circumstances). The law ensures that wife is not left destitute and has financial security.

### Q6. When Hindu wife is entitled to separate residence and maintenance under the Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act, 1956? (10 marks)

The Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 provides comprehensive provisions for maintenance of wife. Section 18 deals with maintenance of wife, including circumstances when wife can live separately without forfeiting her right to maintenance.

---

**Main Body (8 marks)**

**Section 18(1): General Right**
- Hindu wife entitled to be maintained by husband **during her lifetime**
- Applies to marriages before or after commencement of Act
- **Presumption**: Wife entitled to live with husband and be maintained

**Section 18(2): Wife Can Live Separately WITHOUT Forfeiting Maintenance**

Wife can live separately and still claim maintenance if:

1. **Desertion (Section 18(2)(a))**
   - Husband has **abandoned** her without reasonable cause
   - Without her consent
   - **Case**: Bipinchandra v. Prabhavati (1957) - desertion must be without reasonable cause

2. **Cruelty (Section 18(2)(b))**
   - Husband has treated her with **cruelty**
   - Treatment causing reasonable apprehension of harm
   - Can be physical or mental cruelty
   - **Case**: Gurbachan Kaur v. Swaran Singh (1990) - mental cruelty included

3. **Virulent Leprosy (Section 18(2)(c))**
   - Husband suffering from **virulent form of leprosy**
   - Wife can live separately for health reasons

4. **Another Wife Living (Section 18(2)(d))**
   - Husband has **other wife living**
   - Wife can choose to live separately
   - Still entitled to maintenance

5. **Concubine (Section 18(2)(e))**
   - Husband **keeps concubine** in same house
   - OR **habitually resides** with concubine
   - Wife can live separately

6. **Conversion (Section 18(2)(f))**
   - Husband has **ceased to be Hindu** by conversion
   - Wife can live separately

7. **Other Cause (Section 18(2)(g))**
   - Any other cause justifying separate residence
   - **Examples**: Husband's immoral conduct, ill-treatment, etc.

**Section 18(3): Wife LOSES Right to Maintenance**

Wife **loses** right to maintenance if:

1. **Unchaste**
   - Wife is **unchaste**
   - Commits adultery
   - **Case**: Kamla Devi v. Bachulal Gupta (1957) - unchastity forfeits maintenance

2. **Ceases to be Hindu**
   - Wife **ceases to be Hindu** by conversion
   - Converts to another religion

**Amount of Maintenance (Section 23):**

Factors considered:
- Position and status of parties
- Reasonable wants of claimant
- Justification for separate living
- Claimant's property and income
- Number of persons entitled to maintenance

**Enforcement:**
- Wife can file suit for maintenance
- Court can order monthly or periodical payment
- Can be secured by charge on husband's property

**Important Cases:**
- **Bipinchandra v. Prabhavati (1957)**: Desertion must be without reasonable cause
- **Gurbachan Kaur v. Swaran Singh (1990)**: Mental cruelty included
- **Kamla Devi v. Bachulal Gupta (1957)**: Unchastity forfeits maintenance

---

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
The Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 provides comprehensive protection to Hindu wives, allowing them to live separately in various circumstances while still claiming maintenance. The Act balances the rights of wife with the obligations of husband, ensuring that wife is not left destitute when living separately is justified.

### Q7. Explain the concept of void, voidable and valid marriage under Parsi Act. (10 marks)

### Q9. Discuss nature and forms of marriage under ancient Hindu law. (10 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (1 mark)**
Ancient Hindu law recognized various forms of marriage, classified into approved and unapproved forms. These forms were based on different methods of acquiring a wife and reflected the social and religious values of ancient Hindu society.

---

**Main Body (8 marks)**

**NATURE OF MARRIAGE IN ANCIENT HINDU LAW:**

**Sacramental Nature:**
- Marriage was a **sacrament (Samskara)**
- Last of 10 sacraments
- **Indissoluble** union
- "Union of flesh with flesh and bone with bone"
- Wife = **Ardhangini** (half body of husband)
- Spiritual and religious significance
- Performed to purify body from inherited taint

**Religious Obligations:**
- Husband and wife share fruits of all acts (good or bad)
- Wife participates in religious ceremonies
- Marriage essential for fulfillment of religious duties
- Sons needed for performing funeral rites

**EIGHT FORMS OF MARRIAGE:**

**APPROVED FORMS (4):**

1. **Brahma**
   - **Method**: Father gives daughter to learned man
   - **No consideration** paid
   - **Status**: Most approved form
   - **Characteristics**: Gift of daughter, No dowry, Religious ceremony

2. **Daiva**
   - **Method**: Father gives daughter to priest as fee for sacrifice
   - **Consideration**: Service to gods
   - **Status**: Approved but lower than Brahma
   - **Characteristics**: Given to priest, Religious service

3. **Arsha**
   - **Method**: Father gives daughter receiving pair of cattle
   - **Consideration**: Symbolic (cattle)
   - **Status**: Approved
   - **Characteristics**: Nominal consideration, Not sale

4. **Prajapatya**
   - **Method**: Father gives daughter with instruction to perform duties together
   - **No consideration**
   - **Status**: Approved
   - **Characteristics**: Instruction to both, Joint duties

**UNAPPROVED FORMS (4):**

5. **Asura**
   - **Method**: Bridegroom gives money to bride and her relatives
   - **Consideration**: Money/wealth
   - **Status**: Unapproved (sale of daughter)
   - **Characteristics**: Purchase, Commercial transaction

6. **Gandharva**
   - **Method**: Mutual consent and love
   - **No consideration**
   - **Status**: Unapproved (no parental consent)
   - **Characteristics**: Love marriage, Mutual consent, No ceremony

7. **Rakshasha**
   - **Method**: Forcible abduction of maiden
   - **No consideration**
   - **Status**: Unapproved (force)
   - **Characteristics**: Force, Abduction, Against will

8. **Paishacha**
   - **Method**: Taking maiden when she is asleep, intoxicated, or mentally unsound
   - **No consideration**
   - **Status**: Most unapproved (worst form)
   - **Characteristics**: Taking advantage, No consent, Immoral

**Classification Basis:**
- **Approved**: Based on gift, religious service, or instruction
- **Unapproved**: Based on purchase, mutual consent without parents, force, or taking advantage

**Legal Status:**
- All forms recognized as valid marriage
- Children legitimate in all forms
- But social and religious approval varied
- **Modern Law**: HMA 1955 does NOT recognize these forms
- Only **Saptapadi** (7 steps) recognized as essential ceremony

**Evolution:**
- Ancient forms reflected social conditions
- Brahma form most preferred
- Asura form (dowry) became common but disapproved
- Modern law codified and simplified

---

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
Ancient Hindu law recognized eight forms of marriage, classified into approved and unapproved based on method of acquisition and consideration. While all forms were legally valid, approved forms (Brahma, Daiva, Arsha, Prajapatya) were socially and religiously preferred. Modern Hindu law (HMA 1955) has replaced these forms with codified provisions, but the sacramental nature of marriage continues to be recognized.
