### Q1. Explain the essentials of a valid Hindu marriage under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 codifies the law relating to Hindu marriages. Section 5 lays down the essential conditions that must be fulfilled for a valid marriage. Any marriage solemnized in contravention of these conditions may be void or voidable.

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Section 5 - Five Essential Conditions:**

1. **Both parties must be Hindus (Section 5)**
   - Act applies to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, Sikhs
   - Section 2 defines "Hindu" broadly
   - Exception: Scheduled Tribes (Section 2(2))

2. **Bigamy (Monogamy) - Section 5(i)**
   - Neither party has spouse living
   - Establishes rule of monogamy
   - Contravention = VOID (Section 11)
   - Punishment: Section 494-495 IPC

3. **Mental Capacity - Section 5(ii)**
   - Not incapable of valid consent (unsoundness)
   - Not suffering from mental disorder (unfit for marriage/procreation)
   - Not subject to recurrent attacks of insanity/epilepsy
   - Contravention = VOIDABLE (Section 12(1)(b))

4. **Age - Section 5(iii)**
   - Male: 21 years, Female: 18 years
   - Child Marriage = Voidable (PCMA 2006)
   - Case: Arunkumar v. Inspector General (2019) - trans woman included

5. **Prohibited Relationship - Section 5(iv)**
   - Not within degrees of prohibited relationship
   - Exception: Custom permits
   - Contravention = VOID (Section 11)

6. **Sapinda Relationship - Section 5(v)**
   - Not sapindas of each other
   - Limits: 3 generations (maternal), 5 generations (paternal)
   - Exception: Custom permits
   - Contravention = VOID (Section 11)

**Additional Requirements:**

7. **Ceremonies - Section 7**
   - Customary rites and ceremonies of either party
   - Saptapadi: Marriage complete when 7th step taken
   - Case: Sitabai v. Vithabai (1958) - presumption of valid ceremonies

8. **Registration - Section 8**
   - Optional but recommended
   - Case: Seema v. Ashwani Kumar (2006) - SC directed compulsory registration

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provides a comprehensive framework for valid marriages. All conditions must be strictly complied with. Violation of certain conditions makes marriage void, while others make it voidable. The Act balances traditional Hindu concepts with modern legal requirements.

### Q2. Distinguish between void and voidable marriages under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. (10 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (1 mark)**
The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 recognizes two types of invalid marriages - void marriages (Section 11) and voidable marriages (Section 12). Understanding the distinction is crucial for determining legal consequences.

**Main Body (8 marks)**

| Aspect | Void Marriage (Section 11) | Voidable Marriage (Section 12) |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Nature** | No marriage from beginning | Valid until avoided |
| **Who can avoid** | Either party (or declaratory suit) | Only parties to marriage |
| **Effect if not avoided** | Always void | Remains valid |
| **After death** | Can be challenged | Cannot be challenged |
| **Legal consequences** | None | All consequences flow until avoided |
| **Retrospective effect** | Always void | Void from date of marriage if avoided |
| **Children** | Legitimate (Section 16) | Legitimate if begotten/conceived before decree |
| **Grounds** | Section 5(i), (iv), (v) | Section 12(1)(a), (b), (c), (d) |

**Grounds of Void Marriage:**
1. Bigamy (Section 5(i))
2. Prohibited relationship (Section 5(iv))
3. Sapinda relationship (Section 5(v))

**Grounds of Voidable Marriage:**
1. Impotency (Section 12(1)(a))
2. Mental capacity (Section 12(1)(b))
3. Consent by force/fraud (Section 12(1)(c)) - within 1 year
4. Pre-marriage pregnancy (Section 12(1)(d)) - within 1 year, no intercourse

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
The key distinction is that void marriages are null from inception, while voidable marriages are valid until avoided by the aggrieved party. This affects rights, obligations, and legitimacy of children.

### Q3. Explain the grounds of divorce available to both husband and wife under the Hindu Marriage Act, 1955. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
The Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 provides for dissolution of marriage by decree of divorce. Section 13(1) enumerates nine grounds available to both husband and wife, based on fault theory. Section 13(1-A) provides two breakdown theory grounds.

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Section 13(1) - Nine Fault Grounds:**

1. **Adultery (Section 13(1)(i))**
   - Voluntary sexual intercourse with person other than spouse
   - Case: Joseph Shine v. UOI (2018) - adultery not offence under IPC, but still ground for divorce

2. **Cruelty (Section 13(1)(ia))**
   - Treated with cruelty after marriage
   - Can be physical or mental
   - Courts interpret liberally

3. **Desertion (Section 13(1)(ib))**
   - Deserted for continuous period of not less than 2 years
   - Must be immediately preceding presentation of petition

4. **Conversion (Section 13(1)(ii))**
   - Ceased to be Hindu by conversion to another religion

5. **Mental Disorder (Section 13(1)(iii))**
   - Incurably of unsound mind OR
   - Mental disorder of such kind/extent that petitioner cannot reasonably be expected to live with respondent
   - Includes: Schizophrenia, psychopathic disorder

6. **Leprosy (Section 13(1)(iv))**
   - Virulent and incurable form

7. **Venereal Disease (Section 13(1)(v))**
   - In communicable form

8. **Renunciation (Section 13(1)(vi))**
   - Renounced the world by entering religious order

9. **Not Heard Of (Section 13(1)(vii))**
   - Not been heard of as being alive for 7 years or more

**Section 13(1-A) - Breakdown Grounds:**

1. **No Resumption After Judicial Separation (Section 13(1-A)(i))**
   - No resumption of cohabitation for 1 year or more
   - After decree of judicial separation

2. **No Restitution After Decree (Section 13(1-A)(ii))**
   - No restitution of conjugal rights for 1 year or more
   - After decree of restitution

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Act provides comprehensive grounds for divorce, balancing fault theory with breakdown theory. The grounds ensure that parties have adequate remedies when marriage breaks down irretrievably, while maintaining the sanctity of marriage institution.

### Q4. Explain the concept of maintenance under Hindu law. Who are the persons entitled to maintenance? (10 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (1 mark)**
Maintenance is the right to claim subsistence and support from a person who has legal obligation. Hindu law provides comprehensive maintenance provisions under HMA 1955 and HAMA 1956.

**Main Body (8 marks)**

**Definition (Section 3(b), HAMA):**
- Food, Clothing, Residence, Education, Medical attendance
- For unmarried daughter: Marriage expenses

**Persons Entitled Under HMA 1955:**

1. **Section 24: Interim Maintenance**
   - Either wife or husband
   - During proceedings
   - Ceases when proceedings end

2. **Section 25: Permanent Maintenance**
   - Either wife or husband
   - At decree OR subsequent
   - Can be modified on change of circumstances

**Persons Entitled Under HAMA 1956:**

1. **Section 18: Wife**
   - During lifetime
   - Can live separately without forfeiting: Desertion, Cruelty, Leprosy, Another wife, etc.
   - Loses right: Unchaste, Ceases to be Hindu

2. **Section 19: Widowed Daughter-in-Law**
   - From father-in-law
   - If unable to maintain from other sources
   - Ceases on remarriage

3. **Section 20: Children and Parents**
   - Children: Till minority (unmarried daughter beyond)
   - Parents: Aged or infirm

4. **Section 21: Dependents**
   - Father, Mother, Widow, Minor sons, Unmarried daughters, Widowed daughters, Son's widows, Illegitimate children

**Section 23: Factors for Amount:**
- Position and status, Reasonable wants, Property, Conduct, Number of persons

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
Hindu law provides comprehensive maintenance rights ensuring that dependents are not left destitute. The provisions balance the rights of claimants with the capacity of persons liable to pay.

### Q5. What is adoption? Explain the essentials of a valid adoption under the Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
Adoption is the process by which a person takes someone into custody and all legal rights of a legitimate biological child are transferred. The Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 codifies adoption law for Hindus.

**Definition:**
- Legal affiliation of a child
- Relationship severed from biological parents
- Child becomes lawful child of adoptive parents
- All rights similar to biological child

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Section 6: Four Requisites (All Must Be Present):**

1. **Person adopting has capacity + right**
   - Section 7: Male Hindu - Sound mind, Not minor, Wife's consent (unless exceptions)
   - Section 8: Female Hindu - Sound mind, Not minor, Marital status (not married/divorced/widowed/husband absent)

2. **Person giving has capacity**
   - Section 9: Priority - Father ‚Üí Mother ‚Üí Guardian
   - Father: Exclusive right (with mother's consent)
   - Guardian: Needs court permission

3. **Person adopted is capable**
   - Section 10: Must be Hindu, Not already adopted, Not married (unless custom), Not completed 15 years (unless custom)

4. **Compliance with other conditions**
   - Section 11: Six conditions

**Section 11: Other Conditions:**

1. **No existing son/daughter** (by blood or adoption)
2. **Age difference**: 21 years (male adopting female, female adopting male)
3. **Not simultaneously by two persons**
4. **Actual giving and taking** - Essential requirement
5. **Datta Homan**: Not essential (proviso)

**Section 12: Effect of Adoption:**
- Deemed child for all purposes
- Ties severed from birth family
- **Provisos**: Marriage restrictions, Property vested, No divesting

**Section 15: Valid Adoption Not to Be Cancelled:**
- Irrevocable and permanent

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Act provides a comprehensive framework ensuring that adoptions are valid, legal, and in the best interests of the child. All conditions must be strictly complied with, and once valid, adoption cannot be cancelled.

### Q6. "Hindu marriage is both a sacrament and a contract." Discuss. (10 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (1 mark)**
The nature of Hindu marriage has been a subject of debate. While traditionally it was considered a sacrament, modern law recognizes it as having contractual elements too.

**Main Body (8 marks)**

**Hindu Marriage as Sacrament:**
- Last of 10 sacraments (Samskara)
- Performed to purify body from inherited taint
- Religious necessity
- Indissoluble union - "union of flesh with flesh and bone with bone"
- Wife = Ardhangini (half body of husband)
- Shares fruits of all acts (good or bad)

**Hindu Marriage as Contract:**
- Kanyadan fulfills requirements of gift (as per Manu)
- Concept continues even after HMA 1955
- Parties enter into marriage by consent
- Can be dissolved by mutual consent (Section 13-B)
- Breakdown theory recognizes contractual nature

**Legal Position:**
- **BOTH sacrament AND contract**
- Cases: Purshottamadas v. Purshottamadas, Bhagwati Saran Singh v. Parmeshwari

**Modern Developments:**
- HMA 1955 recognizes both aspects
- Divorce provisions show contractual element
- Religious ceremonies show sacramental nature

**Conclusion (1 mark)**
Hindu marriage uniquely combines sacramental and contractual elements. While it retains its religious sanctity, modern law recognizes the contractual aspects, especially in dissolution.

### Q7. Explain the grounds for divorce available to a Muslim wife under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939. (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
The Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939 was enacted to provide Muslim women with grounds for divorce. Prior to this Act, Muslim women had limited rights to seek divorce. Section 2 enumerates 8 grounds available to Muslim wives.

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Section 2 - 8 Grounds:**

1. **Whereabouts Unknown (Section 2(i))**
   - Whereabouts of husband not known
   - For **4 years**

2. **Neglect of Maintenance (Section 2(ii))**
   - Husband has neglected or failed to provide maintenance
   - For **2 years**

3. **Imprisonment (Section 2(iii))**
   - Husband sentenced to imprisonment
   - For **7 years or upwards**

4. **Failure to Perform Marital Obligations (Section 2(iv))**
   - Husband failed to perform marital obligations
   - Without reasonable cause
   - For **3 years**

5. **Impotency (Section 2(v))**
   - Husband was impotent at time of marriage
   - Continues to be so

6. **Insanity/Leprosy/Venereal Disease (Section 2(vi))**
   - Insanity for **2 years**
   - Leprosy
   - Virulent venereal disease

7. **Option of Puberty (Section 2(vii))**
   - Given in marriage by father or other guardian
   - Before attaining **15 years**
   - Repudiated before attaining **18 years**
   - Marriage has **not been consummated**

8. **Cruelty (Section 2(viii))**
   - Husband treats her with cruelty
   - **Includes**:
     - Habitually assaults or makes life miserable
     - Associates with women of ill-repute
     - Attempts to force immoral life
     - Disposes of property or prevents exercising legal rights
     - Obstructs in observance of religious profession
     - If more than one wife, does not treat equitably

**Historical Background:**
- Bill introduced: 17th April 1936 (Qazi Mohammad Ahmad Kazmi)
- Act became law: 17th March 1939

**Significance:**
- Gave Muslim women statutory rights to seek divorce
- Adopted some Maliki provisions (more favorable to women)
- Example: Maliki's shorter waiting period (2 years vs 7 years)

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
The Act was a significant step in empowering Muslim women. It provided them with comprehensive grounds to seek divorce when marriage becomes intolerable, balancing traditional Muslim law with modern needs of justice and equity.

### Q8. Explain the concept of guardianship under Hindu law. What is the paramount consideration in custody disputes? (15 marks)

**MODEL ANSWER:**

**Introduction (2 marks)**
Guardianship is the legal relationship where a person (guardian) has the care of person or property or both of a minor. The Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956 governs guardianship of Hindu minors.

**Main Body (10 marks)**

**Definition of Guardian (Section 4):**
- Person having the care of:
  - Person of a minor, OR
  - Property of a minor, OR
  - Both person and property

**Types of Guardians:**

1. **Natural Guardians (Section 6)**
   - **Boy/Unmarried Girl**: Father (then mother)
   - **Illegitimate**: Mother (then father)
   - **Minor Wife**: Husband
   - **Case**: Gita Hariharan v. RBI (1999) - "after" = "in absence of"

2. **Testamentary Guardians (Section 9)**
   - Appointed by will
   - Acceptance necessary
   - Ceases: Girl's marriage (cannot revive)

3. **Guardians Appointed by Court**
   - Under Guardians and Wards Act, 1890
   - Known as certificated guardian
   - Powers controlled by court

4. **De Facto Guardian (Section 11)**
   - **Prohibited** from disposing/dealing with property
   - No legal authority

**Paramount Consideration: Welfare of Child (Section 13)**

**Section 13** provides:
- In giving child in custody, OR
- While appointing guardian for him/her
- **Welfare of child** should be given **supreme importance**

**If court finds**:
- Giving child under someone's custody will be **detrimental to child**
- Court will **refrain from doing so**
- **No person** shall be appointed as guardian to minor

**Underlying Principle**:
- **Welfare of child** is underlying principle in:
  - Cases of custody
  - Cases of guardianship

**Section 26: Hindu Marriage Act, 1955**
- Court may give **interim orders** in cases related to:
  - **Custody** of minor children
  - **Education** of minor children
  - Other matters
- **With their consonance** (consent/consideration)

**Conclusion (3 marks)**
Guardianship under Hindu law is comprehensive, with welfare of child as the paramount consideration. This ensures that the best interests of the child are always protected, overriding even natural guardianship rights when necessary.

### Q9. Write short notes on:

**a) Muta Marriage**

**Answer:**
- Temporary marriage with fixed duration
- **Recognition**: Only **Ithna Ashari (Shia)** school
- **Requirements**: Period + Dower must be specified
- **Legal Incidents**:
  - ‚ùå No mutual inheritance
  - ‚úÖ Children legitimate
  - ‚úÖ Dower: Half (not consummated), Full (consummated)
  - ‚ùå No maintenance under personal law
  - ‚úÖ Dissolved by expiry of term
- **Status**: Almost obsolete in India

---

**b) Iddat**

**Answer:**
- Period of chastity after dissolution of marriage
- **Purpose**: Certainty of paternity, Avoid confusion, Restraint
- **Duration**:
  - Death: 4 months 10 days (or until delivery if pregnant)
  - Divorce: 3 menstrual periods (or 3 lunar months if no menstruation)
- **Women's Rights During Iddat**:
  - ‚úÖ Lodging in husband's house
  - ‚úÖ Maintenance during iddat of divorce (NOT death)

---

**c) Restitution of Conjugal Rights**

**Answer:**
- **Section 9, HMA 1955**
- **Requirements**: Valid marriage, Withdrawal from society, Without reasonable excuse
- **Legal Effect**: If cohabitation not resumed within 1 year ‚Üí becomes ground for divorce (Section 13(1-A)(ii))
- **Constitutional Validity**: Upheld in Saroj Rani v. Sudershan Kumar (1984 SC)
- **Execution**: Can attach property; Wife as petitioner can order periodical payments

---

**d) Judicial Separation**

**Answer:**
- **Section 10, HMA 1955**
- **Grounds**: Same as grounds for divorce
- **Legal Effect**:
  1. No obligation to cohabit
  2. Cannot contract another marriage
  3. If not resumed within 1 year ‚Üí ground for divorce (Section 13(1-A)(i))
  4. Succession rights continue
- **Can be rescinded** if parties reconcile

---

**e) Option of Puberty**

**Answer:**
- Minor can ratify/repudiate marriage on attaining puberty
- **When by Father/Paternal Grandfather**: Valid and binding (cannot repudiate unless manifest disadvantage)
- **When by Other Guardian**: Minor has option to repudiate (no reason required)
- **Dissolution of Muslim Marriage Act, 1939 Section 2(vii)**: Wife entitled to dissolution if married before 15, repudiated before 18, not consummated

---

**f) Sapinda Relationship**

**Answer:**
- **Section 3(f), HMA 1955**
- **Limits**: 3 generations (maternal), 5 generations (paternal)
- **Prohibition**: Parties cannot be sapindas (Section 5(v))
- **Exception**: Custom permits
- **Theories**: Oblation theory, Particles of same body theory
- **Effect**: Contravention = VOID (Section 11)

---

**g) Custom**

**Answer:**
- **Kinds**: Local, Class/Caste, Family, Business/Trade
- **Essentials (ACRON)**:
  - Ancient, Certain, Reasonable, Ongoing, Not against public policy/law
- **Proof**: Burden on person alleging; Multiple instances required
- **Case**: Ujagar vs Jeo - Repeated judicial recognition = no further proof needed
- **Can override** texts (Smriti) when there is divergence

---

**h) Ijma'a**

**Answer:**
- Consensus of all jurists on a question of law
- **Authority**: "My people will never agree on what is wrong"
- **Key**: **Cannot be repealed** once established
- **First Instance**: Election of first Khaleefa Abu Bakr
- **Status**: Unchallenged source of law
